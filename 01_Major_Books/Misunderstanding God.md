**Misunderstanding God: Worshiping a God We Barely Know**

*Acts 7:9–17*

**Introduction: The Most Common God in Church Today**

If you walk into most churches in America today and ask people, “Do you believe in God?” the overwhelming answer will be yes.

If you ask, “Do you worship God?” many will say, “Of course. I sing. I pray. I give. I serve.”

But if you push deeper—\
“What is God actually like?\
How does He really work in history?\
What does He do with suffering, with wicked rulers, with pain and famine and injustice?”—\
you will often find something troubling:

People are worshiping a God they do not actually know very well.

They know *about* Him.\
They know Bible stories.\
They know Christian phrases.

But the God they imagine is often not the God of Scripture.

- He is safer.

- He is more predictable.

- He is more aligned with our preferences.

- He feels more like a spiritual assistant than the sovereign Lord of history.

This isn’t new. This is exactly what Stephen confronted in Acts 7.

Stephen is standing before the Sanhedrin—the high council of Israel. These men know their Bibles far better than we do. They are the guardians of orthodoxy, the caretakers of the Temple, the interpreters of Moses. If anyone could claim to “know God,” it was them.

And yet Stephen’s entire sermon is making one devastating point:

You have the Scriptures, but you don’t really know the God behind them.\
You are worshiping a God you’ve reshaped in your own image.

Acts 7:9–17 is a key part of that argument. Stephen retells the story of Joseph, not as a children’s story, but as a theological mirror. He is saying to the Sanhedrin—and to us:

“You keep missing God because you don’t understand how He actually works.”

Let’s walk through this text and see what he shows us.

**I. A Council That Loved Scripture—and Misunderstood God**

Before we look at Joseph, we need to feel the weight of who Stephen is talking to.

The Sanhedrin was made up of chief priests, elders, and scribes—Pharisees and Sadducees. They knew the Law, the Prophets, and the Psalms by heart. They debated Greek words, parsed Hebrew verbs, and argued fine points of theology.

They were not biblical liberals. They were conservative, Scripture-quoting men.

But their view of God had been shaped by something more than Scripture.\
It had been shaped by:

- Nationalism: God must defend Israel’s status.

- Moralism: Blessing is always reward; suffering is always punishment.

- Religious control: God works through *our* structures, *our* systems, *our* leaders.

- Power: God must validate our authority, our Temple, our way of doing things.

So when Jesus came—humble, suffering, confronting them, welcoming sinners, healing on the Sabbath, refusing to play by their rules—He did not fit the God they thought they knew.

And that’s the irony:\
They rejected the Messiah in the name of faithfulness to God.

They weren’t atheists.\
They weren’t secular.\
They were worshiping a God they simply did not know.

This is why Stephen tells the story of Joseph. Joseph’s story is not random. It is a carefully chosen case study in how God actually works—and how religious people consistently misread Him.

**II. Joseph and the God Who Refuses to Fit Our Categories**

Luke records Stephen’s words in Acts 7:9:

“And the patriarchs, **jealous of Joseph**, sold him into Egypt; **but God was with him**.”

That small sentence is loaded with theology.

“The patriarchs” are the twelve sons of Jacob—the very foundations of Israel. In Jewish memory, these are the heroes. But Stephen reframes them. They are not wise patriarchs in this moment. They are jealous brothers resisting the will of God.

They “sold” Joseph into Egypt. The verb emphasizes a deliberate act of betrayal. They knew what they were doing.

Then Stephen adds the crucial phrase:\
“**But God was with him.**”

In Greek, it’s literally, *ho Theos ēn met’ autou*—“God was with him”—using an imperfect verb that suggests an ongoing reality. God’s presence with Joseph was not occasional, not intermittent. It was steady, unbroken, persistent—even as Joseph’s circumstances got worse.

Now put those two realities together:

At the same moment Joseph’s brothers are sinning against him,\
**God is with him**.

At the same moment Joseph’s life seems to be collapsing,\
**God’s plan is advancing**.

That’s the first way we misunderstand God.

We assume:\
“If circumstances are bad, God must be distant.”\
“If I’m suffering, God must be displeased.”\
“If I’m in a pit or a prison, God must have left me.”

But Stephen says the exact opposite:\
Joseph is betrayed, and God is with him.\
Joseph is enslaved, and God is with him.\
Joseph is falsely accused, and God is with him.\
Joseph is forgotten in prison, and God is with him.

In other words:

God’s nearness is not measured by how comfortable your life is.

This is where American Christianity struggles.

We live in a consumer culture.\
Our entire world trains us to evaluate everything—jobs, churches, relationships, even God—by one major question:\
“Is this working for me?”

If my health is good, my finances are stable, my relationships are smooth, then I feel like, “God must be with me.”

If things fall apart, if there is loss, if there is pain, we instinctively think, “What did I do wrong?” or “Where are You, God?”

We worship a God who exists mainly to keep our lives up and to the right.\
A God who functions like a divine life coach or brand enhancer.

The Sanhedrin had their own version of this:

In their minds, suffering was almost always a sign of sin. Blessing was almost always a sign of favor. You can hear this in the disciples’ question in John 9:2:

“Rabbi, who sinned, this man or his parents, that he was born blind?”

They can only imagine two categories:\
Blindness = curse.\
Curse = sin.

And Jesus says, “Neither.”\
“This happened that the works of God might be displayed in him.”

Lamentations 3:37–38 presses this even deeper:

“Who has spoken and it came to pass, unless the Lord has commanded it?\
Is it not from the mouth of the Most High\
that good and bad come?”

The Hebrew words there—*tov* (good) and *ra* (calamity, trouble)—are stark. The writer is saying:\
If anything happens—pleasant or painful—it has passed through the sovereign mouth of God.

He is not the author of evil. But He is not pushed aside by evil either. He is not passive.

That is not the God many Christians worship.\
We worship a God who is only “in” the good parts.\
The Bible reveals a God who is present and active in both.

**III. Affliction as Divine Testing, Not Divine Abandonment**

Stephen continues in Acts 7:10:

“…and \[God\] **rescued him out of all his afflictions** and gave him favor and wisdom before Pharaoh…”

The word “afflictions” here is **thlipsis**—a Greek term that means pressure, crushing, external and internal distress. It’s the word Paul uses in Romans 5:3 when he says, “We rejoice in our sufferings,” and in 2 Corinthians 4:17 when he talks about “this light momentary affliction.”

Psalm 105:17–19 gives us a divine commentary on Joseph’s story:

“He sent a man ahead of them,\
Joseph, who was sold as a slave.\
His feet were hurt with fetters;\
his neck was put in a collar of iron;\
**until what he had said came to pass,\
the word of the LORD tested him.**”

Notice the subject:\
“He sent… Joseph.”\
Not “He allowed Joseph to be sent.”\
“He sent.”

God dispatched Joseph into slavery.\
God wrote chains into the script.

Then it says, “the word of the LORD tested him.”\
The Hebrew word for “tested” (*tsaraph*) is used of refining metal—subjecting it to intense heat so impurities rise to the surface and are removed.

Joseph’s afflictions were not a sign that God had abandoned him.\
They were the furnace in which God was shaping him.

In American consumer Christianity, we almost have no category for this.\
If life is painful, our first instinct is to get out, fix it, escape it, or blame someone.

We pray away the very conditions God intends to use to deepen our faith.

Job understood something we often don’t. In Job 2:10, he says:

“Shall we receive good from God, and shall we not receive evil \[calamity\]?”

Job does not accuse God of sin, but he does not protect God from sovereignty either. He knows:\
If I only worship God when life goes well, I am not worshiping God. I am worshiping my circumstances.

Lamentations 3 goes even deeper. After describing bitter suffering, the author says in verse 32–33:

“Though He cause grief, He will have compassion…\
for He does not afflict from His heart…”

That phrase “from His heart” in Hebrew means God does not delight in afflicting. He does not enjoy it. But He does ordain it with purpose.

In other words:

God’s hand may bring grief; His heart is still full of compassion.

If we do not know this about God, two things will happen:

1.  In suffering, our worship will evaporate.

2.  In blessing, our worship will be shallow and self-centered.

We will sing to a God we expect to keep us comfortable, and when He does not, we will quietly resent Him while still using His name.

That is exactly what happened in Israel’s history.\
They loved God’s deliverance from Egypt.\
They grumbled under His discipline in the wilderness.\
They praised Him for the land.\
They worshiped other gods when His commands felt inconvenient.

They worshiped a God they did not really know.

**IV. Pharaoh, Politics, and the God Who Rules Through Wicked Kings**

Stephen adds another layer in Acts 7:10:

“\[God\] gave him favor and wisdom before **Pharaoh, king of Egypt**, who made him ruler over Egypt and over all his household.”

This is an astonishing statement. God gave Joseph “favor” and “wisdom” in the eyes of a pagan, polytheistic, oppressive ruler. Joseph becomes second-in-command in a corrupt empire.

God is not saying Egypt is righteous. He is not blessing Pharaoh’s idolatry. But He is using Pharaoh’s power and Egypt’s system to position Joseph exactly where He wants him.

The Bible is unembarrassed about this kind of sovereignty.

In Proverbs 21:1, we read:

“The king’s heart is a stream of water in the hand of the LORD;\
He turns it wherever He will.”

In Jeremiah 27:6, God calls Nebuchadnezzar—another wicked king—“My servant.”\
In Isaiah 45:1, He calls Cyrus, a Persian ruler, “My anointed.”

In Isaiah 10:5–7, God says Assyria is “the rod of my anger.”\
They intend conquest and cruelty. God intends discipline and purification.

By the time you reach Acts 4:27–28, the pattern reaches its climax:

“…Herod and Pontius Pilate, along with the Gentiles and the peoples of Israel, were gathered together **to do whatever Your hand and Your plan had predestined to take place.**”

Wicked men act freely, and yet they fulfill God’s predetermined purposes.

This is not fatalism.\
This is not God delighting in evil.\
This is God so sovereign that even rebellion cannot derail His design.

The Sanhedrin should have known this. Their Scriptures were full of it. But their concept of God had become nationalistic and defensive.

They wanted a God who would overthrow Rome, not a God who would use Rome.\
They wanted a Messiah who would crush their enemies, not one who would be crucified by them.

In American Christianity, we have a parallel temptation.

We subtly believe God’s sovereignty is only at work when our side wins, our policies pass, our cultural vision prevails. When the wrong candidates rise, when laws move in a direction we hate, we panic as though God has fallen off His throne.

But the Bible insists:\
God is not only sovereign when “our people” are in power.\
He is sovereign over Pharaoh, Nebuchadnezzar, Cyrus, Caesar, Herod, Pilate—and every administration that has ever existed.

If we don’t know this God, our worship will be held hostage by the news cycle. We will only be able to worship confidently when we feel culturally secure.

Stephen is saying to the Sanhedrin, and to us:\
“You do not actually believe in the God of Joseph if you cannot accept that God is working even through the very empire you fear.”

**V. Famine, Egypt, and the God Who Leads His People into Places They Would Never Choose**

Acts 7:11–15 presses the story forward:

“Now there came a famine throughout all Egypt and Canaan, and great affliction, and our fathers could find no food. But when Jacob heard that there was grain in Egypt, he sent out our fathers on their first visit… And Jacob went down into Egypt, and he died, he and our fathers…”

From a human standpoint, the story looks like this:

The covenant family is in the promised land.\
A famine hits.\
There is no food.\
They are forced to go to Egypt—back to the world of power, idolatry, and danger.\
Jacob dies outside the land God promised.

If you are reading your circumstances instead of God’s promises, this looks like failure.\
It looks like abandonment.\
It looks like the plan of God has broken down.

But Genesis 15:13–14 shows something different. Long before Joseph, God told Abraham:

“Your offspring will be sojourners in a land that is not theirs and will be servants there, and they will be afflicted for four hundred years.\
But I will bring judgment on the nation that they serve, and afterward they shall come out with great possessions.”

Egypt was not an accident in the story.\
Egypt was part of the script.

The famine was not outside God’s control.\
It was the instrument God used to move His people into the place where He would grow them into a nation and then display His power in the Exodus.

Now here is where this intersects directly with our worship:

If your functional theology is “God’s will is always comfortable, safe, and upward,” then Egypt will always feel like failure.

Any movement in your life that looks like a step backward—loss, disappointment, affliction, confusion—will be interpreted as, “God isn’t here,” or “God must be angry.”

But Scripture repeatedly shows God leading His people into:

- deserts,

- famines,

- exiles,

- prisons,

- storms.

Not because He is cruel, but because He is weaving a larger story than they can see.

When Israel is taken into Babylonian exile, it feels like total collapse. But in Jeremiah 29, God says:

“I sent you into exile.”\
Not, “Babylon was too strong for Me.”\
“I sent you.”

And then He tells them to build houses, plant gardens, seek the good of the city, and trust His long-term purposes.

Lamentations 3 can say:\
“He causes grief” and yet also say,\
“His steadfast love never ceases; His mercies never come to an end.”

If we don’t know this God, we will only worship Him for the parts of the story we like.

We’ll praise Him for Canaan and resent Him for Egypt.\
We’ll sing loudly at the Red Sea and sulk in the wilderness.\
We will love the resurrection and quietly ignore the cross.

We will worship a God we do not actually trust.

**VI. The Sanhedrin, American Christianity, and the God We’ve Edited**

When you step back from Acts 7:9–17, you see Stephen making a devastating comparison.

He is looking at the Sanhedrin—the guardians of Israel’s religion—and saying, in effect:

“You honor the patriarchs, but you ignore what their stories say about God.\
You quote Moses, but you refuse to accept how God worked through him.\
You memorize Joseph, but you don’t worship Joseph’s God.”

They had turned the living God into something more manageable:

- A God who only works through “insiders,” not outsiders.

- A God who always blesses obedience with immediate visible success.

- A God who would never use pagans, empires, or crosses in His plan.

- A God who exists to protect their religious status, their Temple, their power.

In other words, they had created a religiously acceptable, theologically sophisticated, culturally comfortable version of God.

And they worshiped that god in God’s name.

That is not far from American Christianity.

We have our own edited version of God:

- A God who mainly wants to improve our lives, not interfere with them.

- A God who fits snugly into our political ideologies.

- A God who would never ordain pain to advance holiness.

- A God who exists to affirm our dreams, not confront our idols.

- A God who blesses us as we are, rather than conforming us to the image of Christ.

We sing loud songs, talk about blessings, and use spiritual language—but beneath it, if we’re honest, many times we are worshiping a God who is mostly a projection of our preferences.

The Sanhedrin did the same—and it ended with them killing the Messiah while thinking they were defending God.

That is the danger:\
If you misunderstand who God is, you can oppose Him in the name of serving Him.

Jesus warned about this in John 16:2–3:

“Indeed, the hour is coming when whoever kills you will think he is offering service to God.\
And they will do these things because they have not known the Father, nor Me.”

They don’t know God.\
They don’t know Christ.\
But they are certain they are right.

They are worshiping a God they do not actually know.

**VII. Knowing the Real God: From Misunderstanding to Worship**

So where does this leave us?

Acts 7 is not written so we can feel superior to the Sanhedrin. It is written so we can repent of our own edited, manageable versions of God.

The invitation is not, “Try harder to worship.”\
The invitation is, “Let Scripture reshape your view of God so that your worship is actually aimed at the real One.”

That means letting texts like Acts 7:9–17 confront us:

- God chooses Joseph, not the older brothers.

- God is with Joseph in the pit and the prison.

- God sends famine to move His people.

- God uses Egypt and Pharaoh while remaining holy.

- God leads His people into places they would never choose, to accomplish purposes they could never imagine.

It means letting passages like Lamentations 3, Job 2, Isaiah 45, Jeremiah 27, and Acts 4 deepen our view of sovereignty.

It means listening to Jesus when He says suffering is not always punishment (John 9), and to Paul when he says “through many tribulations we must enter the kingdom of God” (Acts 14:22), and to Peter when he says not to be surprised at fiery trials (1 Peter 4:12).

Over time, as you submit your instincts to Scripture, something happens:

- Your view of God gets bigger.

- Your categories are disrupted.

- Your assumptions are challenged.

- Your idea of blessing expands beyond comfort.

- Your understanding of suffering deepens beyond “God must be mad at me.”

- Your worship becomes more honest, more robust, less tied to how life feels this week.

You begin to worship not a God who exists to decorate your life, but the God who rules heaven and earth—\
who wounds and heals,\
who humbles and exalts,\
who disciplines and comforts,\
who hides and reveals,\
who uses pits, prisons, Pharaohs, famines, crosses, and empty tombs to accomplish His purposes.

That God is worthy of worship when life is sweet\
and when life is bitter.

That God is big enough to carry you when your story makes sense\
and when it doesn’t.

And that God is the One Stephen died for.\
He knew Him well enough to trust Him while being stoned.

The question for us is not:\
“Do you believe in God?”\
The question is:

**Do you know the God you say you worship—\
or are you worshiping a safer version you’ve made along the way?**

My prayer is that the Spirit would do for us what Stephen tried to do for the Sanhedrin:\
tear down our edited versions of God,\
expose where our understanding is too small, too shallow, too American, too consumer-driven,\
and lead us into the joy—and the cost—of worshiping the God who truly is.

Not the God we wish existed,\
but the One who says:

“Be still, and know that **I** am God.” (Psalm 46:10)

Not your god.\
Not my god.\
Not America’s god.

The God of Joseph.\
The God of Stephen.\
The God and Father of our Lord Jesus Christ.

The only One worth worshiping.

Amen.
