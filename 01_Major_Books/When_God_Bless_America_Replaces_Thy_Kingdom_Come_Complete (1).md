**INTRODUCTION**

*Not Persecuted---Seduced*

> *"The church can survive decline. It cannot survive idolatry."*

• • •

**The Disease Beneath the Symptoms**

The crisis facing American Christianity is not primarily numerical. It

is not, at its core, about attendance decline, shrinking affiliation, or

the rise of the religious "nones." Those trends matter, but they are

symptoms, not the disease. The church has survived under Nero, under

Soviet communism, under Mao. It has endured regimes that imprisoned

pastors and burned Bibles. It will survive declining membership rolls.

American Christianity is not being persecuted. It is being seduced.

And seduction is far more dangerous than persecution.

Persecution clarifies allegiance. It forces a choice. It strips away

ambiguity. It makes the church decide whether Jesus is worth losing

everything for. Seduction does the opposite. Seduction confuses

allegiance. Seduction tells you that you can keep Jesus and keep power,

keep worship and keep control, keep the cross and keep the sword.

Seduction lets you keep everything.

We are not watching the church get crushed by the state. We are watching

parts of the church crawl into bed with it.

And the Bible has a word for this.

The prophets called it *zanah*.

In the book of Hosea, God accuses Israel not of atheism---but of

adultery. Israel still worshiped. They still sacrificed. They still

gathered in the temple. They still said the right words about covenant

faithfulness. But when fear rose---when Assyria threatened, when

political uncertainty came---they ran to foreign alliances for

protection. They looked to treaties, chariots, and kings to secure what

God had promised to secure.

And God said:

> *"You have played the whore... you have forgotten Me." ---Hosea 2:5; 8:9*

That Hebrew word---*zanah*---is not polite. It is visceral, relational

language. It is the language of betrayal, of divided loyalty, of a

spouse who keeps the marriage vows on paper while giving the heart away

elsewhere.

Israel did not stop believing in God.

They simply stopped believing He was enough.

They kept Yahweh---but added chariots. They kept worship---but trusted

Egypt. They kept the temple---but relied on treaties.

That was the betrayal. Not rejection. Relocated trust.

**Why Seduction Is Deadlier Than Persecution**

And that is why seduction is more dangerous than persecution.

Persecution forces you to choose. Seduction allows you to keep

everything---God and power, worship and control, faith and

legislation---until one becomes ultimate.

> *"Some trust in chariots and some in horses, but we trust in the name of the Lord our God." ---Psalm 20:7*

Chariots were not evil. Horses were not immoral. Political alliances

were not automatically sinful. They were simply not ultimate.

The problem was not political engagement. The problem was dependence.

And when dependence shifts, worship follows.

So when parts of the American church begin to look to political leaders

as protectors of Christianity... when legislation feels like

salvation... when national survival feels like Kingdom survival... we

are not merely being engaged citizens. We are flirting with *zanah*.

And the terrifying thing about *zanah* is that it feels necessary. It

feels wise. It feels protective. It feels like "responsible leadership."

It feels like "loving our children." It feels like "standing for truth."

It rarely feels like idolatry while you are doing it.

That is the seduction.

**What Happened to Us**

In the span of a single decade, political polarization in America has

not merely intensified; it has corroded the basic conditions that make

civic life possible. Ten years ago we disagreed. Now we despise.

When citizens stop viewing political opponents as neighbors and start

viewing them as threats---when disagreement becomes hatred---politics

stops being a forum for rigorous discussion and becomes a battlefield

for survival. And once politics becomes survival, integrity becomes

optional. Anything becomes permissible. Fear turns ethics into strategy.

The church has not simply observed this shift. The church has absorbed

it.

And if the church absorbs that mindset instead of confronting it, we are

not being prophetic. We are being discipled by fear.

That is what fear does. Fear does not patiently reason its way to

conclusions. Fear does not wait for careful theological reflection. Fear

reads one headline and reorganizes the soul.

When people feel like the world they grew up in is falling apart, they

do not say, "Let's gather for careful reflection." They panic. They want

protection. They want someone strong. They want someone who looks like

he can fix it.

And Christians do the same thing.

When we see culture shifting, when we feel like we are losing ground,

when we think morality is collapsing, our first instinct should be to

fall on our faces before God. But often it is not. We start looking for

power. For someone bold. Someone loud. Someone who promises to take it

back.

Something big enough. Strong enough. Decisive enough.

That instinct is not new. It is the same instinct that shaped

first-century Israel. Many in Israel did not reject Jesus because they

hated God. They rejected Him because He was not the kind of Messiah they

wanted. They wanted someone to take the nation back to God through

strength, sovereignty, and political restoration. They wanted Rome

pushed out. They wanted national revival. They wanted visible victory.

Instead, Jesus came announcing something far more unsettling. He said He

would not take the nation back to God by force, but that He would bring

God back to the nation through repentance. Not by seizing a throne, but

by carrying a cross. Not by crushing Rome, but by exposing the deeper

bondage---the bondage of sin in all men.

They wanted a sword. He brought a towel.

They wanted a liberator who would secure the borders. He came as a

Savior who would confront the heart.

And when the Messiah did not align with their political expectations,

many chose the strongman over the Servant.

That preference did not feel like apostasy. It felt patriotic. It felt

responsible. It felt necessary. And that is precisely what made it so

dangerous.

No one stood up and said, "We reject God." They simply preferred a

different version of how God should work.

We are not consciously rejecting Jesus. We are simply preferring a

different kind of Messiah. Sovereignty without surrender. Victory

without repentance. Power without a cross.

When that shift happens, rejection does not come in the form of atheism.

It comes in the form of substitution. We keep the "faith," but we adjust

the center. We say "Jesus is Lord," but we look for security elsewhere.

We preach the cross, but we trust the sword. We quote Scripture, but we

are governed by fear.

And that is when we are most vulnerable.

**The Theological Root: What Actually Preserves the Church?**

Before we rush to condemn political engagement altogether, we need to be

careful. Christianity has never taught withdrawal from public life. That

claim is historically ignorant and theologically lazy.

Augustine wrote *The City of God* in the shadow of Rome's collapse. But

notice what he does. He does not say, "Politics doesn't matter." He says

politics matters precisely because it is not ultimate. He refuses the

story Rome told about itself---that Rome was the guarantor of order, the

savior of civilization, the meaning of history. Augustine is doing

something more subversive than withdrawal; he is relativizing the

earthly city.

He is not shrinking civic life into irrelevance. He is putting civic

life in its place. He is telling Christians that the stability of Rome

is not the stability of the Kingdom, that the fate of an empire is not

the fate of the church, and that when Christians confuse the two, they

do not become faithful citizens---they become anxious idolaters.

Augustine's argument is devastating because it denies the emotional

center of imperial religion---the belief that Rome must endure or the

world falls apart. Augustine said no. Rome can fall. The world can

shake. History can humiliate your nation. And the Kingdom of God

remains.

That is the issue in America. We are struggling to accept that our

earthly city is penultimate. We keep acting as if the survival of the

nation is the survival of the church.

Aquinas developed a sophisticated account of natural law and governance,

insisting political authority has a legitimate role within God's

ordering of creation. Calvin worked to shape civic structures in Geneva.

Abraham Kuyper insisted Christ lays claim to every square inch of

existence---including politics.

So yes: political engagement is not foreign to Christianity. But neither

is political relativization.

The church has always engaged power. It has simply refused to worship

it. And that is the difference.

The issue is not whether Christians vote, advocate, or participate in

public life. The issue is ultimacy---what emotional and theological

weight we attach to those activities.

When we celebrate election results like resurrection... when we panic at

election losses like the tomb is still sealed... when we talk about

legislation as if it can redeem a nation's soul... that reveals

something. It reveals that we have attached hope to something that

cannot carry it.

Politics was never meant to bear the weight of salvation. And when we

put that weight on it, we do not merely distort politics. We distort

Christ. Because now Jesus is not the Savior; He is the mascot of our

national project. And that is not Christianity.

**The Shape of What Follows**

This book is written for people who love Jesus and are confused. It is

written for pastors who feel caught between their pulpit and the

political temperature of their congregation. It is written for young

adults who watched their parents' faith become indistinguishable from a

voting record and walked away. It is written for skeptics who suspect

the church has lost its way but want to see if anyone inside will say so

honestly.

I am not writing as a political commentator. I am writing as a pastor.

My concern is not which party wins. My concern is whether the church of

Jesus Christ is worshiping the right God.

By the time you finish this book, my hope is that you will be able to do

five things. First, recognize how fear and power can quietly rewrite

doctrine. Second, learn to "relativize" politics without withdrawing

from neighbor-love. Third, recover the early Christian

confession---"Jesus is Lord"---as politically disruptive rather than

partisan. Fourth, embrace a cross-shaped theology of power. And fifth,

rebuild discipleship, conscience, and witness for a post-Christian age.

This is not a book about who to vote for. It is a book about who to

worship.

And the first step toward answering that question honestly is admitting

we may have gotten it wrong.

So here is the diagnostic question I want you to hold in your mind

through every chapter that follows:

> ***What do you think preserves the church?***

If your honest answer involves courts, legislatures, cultural influence,

or political victory more than prayer, holiness, sacrificial love, and

courageous witness---then this book is for you.

Not because you are a bad person.

But because you may have been seduced.

And seduction does not announce itself. It simply rearranges your

affections until the idol feels indistinguishable from the God.

**CHAPTER ONE**

*Zanah: When You Keep the Vows and Give Away the Heart*

> *"Israel did not stop believing in God. They simply stopped believing He was enough."*

• • •

**The Language God Chose**

Of all the metaphors God could have used to describe Israel's

unfaithfulness, He chose the most intimate and devastating one

available: marriage.

Not contract law. Not military desertion. Not commercial fraud.

Marriage.

The entire book of Hosea is structured around this metaphor. God tells

the prophet Hosea to marry a woman named Gomer---a woman who will be

unfaithful to him---as a living parable of what Israel has done to God.

The relationship between Hosea and Gomer becomes a prophetic mirror of

the relationship between Yahweh and His people. Every act of adultery,

every wandering, every return---it is all there, written in the flesh of

a real marriage so that Israel cannot look away.

What makes this metaphor so searing is what it implies about the nature

of the betrayal. A business partner who breaks a contract is

frustrating. A soldier who deserts the army is dangerous. But a spouse

who sleeps with someone else while still coming home for dinner, while

still saying "I love you," while still occupying the same house and

sharing the same table---that is a particular kind of devastation.

It is the devastation of closeness without faithfulness. Of presence

without loyalty. Of saying the right words while your heart belongs

somewhere else.

That is *zanah*.

The Hebrew word appears dozens of times in the prophetic literature, and

it is almost always translated with sexual language: "playing the

whore," "prostituting oneself," "committing adultery." The prophets are

not being crude for shock value. They are being precise. They are saying

that what Israel has done to Yahweh is not casual negligence or mild

forgetfulness. It is relational betrayal of the most intimate kind---the

kind that can only happen between two parties who are supposed to be

bound to each other in exclusive, covenant love.

And this is the word---not a political term, not a policy label, not an

academic category---that best describes what is happening in parts of

the American church today. Not atheism. Not apostasy in the classical

sense of walking away from the faith entirely. But a quiet, respectable,

fear-driven relocation of the heart's deepest trust from God to

political power.

The vows are still spoken. The heart has moved.

**What Zanah Is Not**

This is where we need to slow down, because most people---including most

churchgoers---assume that the opposite of faithfulness is atheism. If

you are unfaithful to God, you must have stopped believing in Him. If

you have wandered from the Lord, you must have rejected His existence.

The assumption is that spiritual betrayal looks like a conscious

decision to walk out the door, slam it shut, and never come back.

But *zanah* is not atheism.

It is not the abandonment of faith. It is the supplementation of faith.

Israel kept worshiping. They kept the temple open. They kept the

festivals on the calendar. They kept the prayers in their mouths. They

kept the language of covenant on their lips. They did not deny Yahweh.

They did not tear down the altars or burn the scrolls or declare that

God was dead.

They simply decided that Yahweh, by Himself, was not enough to guarantee

their security.

So they added. They added alliances with Egypt. They added treaties with

Assyria. They added the military resources of foreign nations. They

added the gods of surrounding peoples---not because they loved those

gods, not because they found those religions intellectually compelling,

but because they feared their enemies. And fear always demands more

insurance than faith alone provides.

Listen to how God describes this in Hosea:

> *"For they have gone up to Assyria, a wild donkey wandering alone; Ephraim has hired lovers. Though they hire allies among the nations, I will soon gather them up." ---Hosea 8:9--10*

"Hired lovers."

That phrase should arrest us. God is not describing a nation that has

abandoned religion. He is describing a nation that has kept religion

while simultaneously pursuing security through political alliances. The

temple is still open. The sacrifices continue. The priests are still

employed. But the trust---the deep, functional, emotional trust that

determines how a people actually live---has shifted.

Israel has not divorced Yahweh. Israel has brought another partner into

the covenant bed and called it "strategy."

This is the anatomy of *zanah*: you do not throw God away. You simply

add insurance. You do not deny the covenant. You supplement it. You do

not leave the marriage. You bring someone else into the bed and tell

yourself it is for the good of the family.

And God's response is not indifference. It is not theological

detachment. It is the white-hot grief of a betrayed spouse who has given

everything and watched it treated as insufficient.

**Why Prophets Use Marital Language**

The covenant between God and Israel was not a business arrangement. It

was not a political contract with terms and penalties, like a treaty

between two nations of equal standing. It was a personal bond patterned

on the most intimate human relationship available---marriage. When God

brought Israel out of Egypt, He did not merely free a labor force. He

wed a bride.

Jeremiah captures this with heartbreaking beauty:

> *"I remember the devotion of your youth, your love as a bride, how you followed me in the wilderness, in a land not sown." ---Jeremiah 2:2*

God remembers the early days---when Israel trusted Him in the desert

with nothing. No armies. No treaties. No chariots. No backup plan. Just

manna falling from the sky each morning and a pillar of cloud leading

through the wilderness. That was the honeymoon.

And it was beautiful. Not because the desert was comfortable, but

because the dependence was total. Israel had no one to lean on but God,

and God proved sufficient. Every time. The water came from the rock. The

bread came from heaven. The enemies were defeated not by superior

military strategy but by divine intervention. Israel learned, in those

early years, what it meant to have God as their only security. And they

thrived.

And then came the land.

And with the land came prosperity. And with prosperity came options. And

with options came the quiet, reasonable, gradual migration of trust from

the God who provided to the means by which provision came. The harvests

were bountiful, so they began to credit the soil instead of the Sower.

The borders were secure, so they began to trust the walls instead of the

Wall-Builder. The economy was stable, so they began to rely on trade

networks instead of the Provider who made trade possible.

It is the oldest spiritual pattern in the world. Dependence breeds

intimacy. Prosperity breeds independence. Independence breeds

forgetfulness. And forgetfulness breeds betrayal.

Deuteronomy 8 warned about this pattern with almost eerie precision:

> *"Take care lest you forget the Lord your God... lest, when you have eaten and are full and have built good houses and live in them, and when your herds and flocks multiply and your silver and gold is multiplied... then your heart be lifted up, and you forget the Lord your God, who brought you out of the land of Egypt." ---Deuteronomy 8:11--14*

The warning is not against prosperity itself. It is against the

spiritual amnesia that prosperity produces. When life is comfortable, we

forget who made it so. When the chariots are plentiful, we forget who we

used to cry out to when we had none.

The prophets use marital language because nothing else captures the

offense. This is not a policy dispute between a government and its

advisor. This is a God who created, rescued, sustained, fed, defended,

and covenanted with a people---only to watch them hand their security to

another while still singing His worship songs on Saturday morning.

That is not a doctrinal error. That is heartbreak. And the marital

metaphor is the only container large enough to hold the grief.

**Translate to Today: Jesus Plus the Strongman**

Now here is where this stops being ancient history and starts being an

examination of the American church.

What does *zanah* look like in twenty-first-century Christianity?

It looks like a church that still preaches the gospel on Sunday but

spends the rest of the week convinced that political power is what will

actually preserve the faith. It looks like believers who pray fervently

but whose emotional stability rises and falls not with their

relationship with God but with their party's electoral prospects. It

looks like a Christianity that claims Jesus is Lord but acts as if the

nation's future depends on the right president occupying the right

office at the right time.

It looks like Jesus plus the strongman.

This is not a caricature. I have sat in rooms with sincere,

Bible-believing Christians who speak about political leaders with a

reverence and desperation that they do not bring to prayer meetings. I

have watched congregations weep over election results with more visible

emotion than they show during communion. I have heard pastors describe

political losses as though the tomb were still sealed---as though Christ

had not risen, as though the Spirit had not been poured out, as though

the gates of hell had somehow prevailed.

I have watched Christian social media accounts erupt with praise for a

political figure using language that should be reserved for the Messiah:

"Sent by God." "Chosen for this moment." "Our only hope." "The one who

will save us."

None of these people would say they worship a politician. That is the

whole point.

*Zanah* does not require conscious idolatry. It only requires relocated

trust. It only requires that something besides God becomes the thing you

cannot imagine living without. It does not require a formal declaration

of apostasy. It only requires that your functional hope---the thing that

actually governs your emotions, your decisions, your fears, and your

sense of security---has quietly migrated from the throne of Christ to

the platform of a political movement.

Ask yourself: When you think about the future of the church in America,

what comes to mind first? Is it the sovereignty of God, the power of the

Holy Spirit, the faithfulness of Christ to build His church? Or is it

the next election, the composition of the Supreme Court, the outcome of

a culture war, the legislative agenda of your preferred party?

I am not asking whether those political realities matter. They do.

Policies have consequences. Laws shape cultures. Justice requires

engagement.

I am asking which one is *ultimate* in your emotional life.

Because whatever is ultimate in your emotional life is functionally your

god.

And if that god is anything other than Jesus---if the thing you cannot

imagine living without, the thing whose loss would devastate you beyond

recovery, the thing that occupies the center of your hope---is a

political outcome rather than the risen Christ, then you have not

necessarily abandoned the faith.

You may have committed *zanah*.

And you may not even know it yet. Because the whole point of *zanah* is

that it happens while you are still going to church.

**Answering the Objection: "Isn't This Judging Motives?"**

At this point, someone will push back: "You cannot see my heart. You do

not know why I care about politics. You do not know whether my concern

is idolatrous or simply responsible. Stop judging my motives."

That is a fair instinct. And Jesus does warn against reckless,

hypocritical judgment. He tells us to take the log out of our own eye

before examining the speck in someone else's.

But Jesus also says, "By their fruits you will know them" (Matthew

7:20). And the prophets did not hesitate to name *zanah* when they saw

it---not because they could read hearts with omniscient precision, but

because the behavioral evidence was overwhelming. You do not need to be

omniscient to notice when a marriage is in trouble. The signs are

visible to anyone willing to look.

When a person says they trust God but panics at every political

development, that is evidence.

When a church preaches grace but excuses cruelty in leaders because

those leaders protect Christian interests, that is evidence.

When believers cannot distinguish between a threat to their nation and a

threat to their faith, that is evidence.

When the emotional temperature of a congregation is set not by the

preaching of Scripture but by the latest political headline, that is

evidence.

When Christians can tolerate almost any moral failure in a leader as

long as that leader delivers the right policy outcomes, that is

evidence.

I am not claiming the ability to read anyone's heart. I am saying that

the prophetic tradition---the tradition of Hosea, Jeremiah, Isaiah, and

Ezekiel---gives us the categories to name what we see. And what we see,

in too many corners of the American church, looks remarkably like what

Hosea described: a people who keep the worship and give away the trust.

The question is not "Are you political?"

The question is: "When you are afraid, where do you run first?"

If the honest answer is not "to God," then something has shifted. And

the first step toward repentance is admitting the shift has happened.

**How to Spot Relocated Trust in Your Emotional Reactions**

Idolatry is notoriously difficult to detect in yourself because it

always feels rational. No one thinks they are worshiping an idol. They

think they are being realistic. Responsible. Wise. Strategic. The idol

never introduces itself as an idol. It introduces itself as the obvious

and necessary thing to do given the circumstances.

But there are diagnostic markers---not designed to condemn, but to

invite honest self-examination. Consider these carefully:

Your emotional stability tracks with political outcomes more than

spiritual disciplines. If an election result can ruin your week but a

missed prayer time barely registers, something has shifted. If you spend

more time reading political commentary than reading Scripture, and if

the commentary produces more emotion in you than the Scripture does,

your heart is telling you where your functional trust resides.

You defend behavior in your preferred leaders that you would condemn

in your opponents.*\* This is perhaps the most visible symptom of* zanah

in American Christianity. It is the moral double standard that reveals

tribal loyalty has replaced principled conviction. When your side's

leader lies, it is "strategy." When the other side's leader lies, it is

"corruption." When your side's leader is cruel, it is "strength." When

the other side's leader is cruel, it is "tyranny." This inconsistency is

not merely hypocrisy. It is the fruit of idolatry. When the idol is your

tribe, you will protect the tribe's representatives regardless of their

character, because the idol demands protection.

**You cannot imagine the church thriving without political power.** If

the thought of Christianity without cultural influence, legal privilege,

or political access fills you with dread rather than historical

awareness---the early church had none of those things and turned the

world upside down---you have attached the church's survival to something

other than Christ. The church was born in a manger, grew under

persecution, and conquered the Roman Empire not through political

leverage but through the power of a resurrection. If you have forgotten

that, political power has replaced the Holy Spirit as your functional

source of confidence.

You treat criticism of your political position as an attack on your

faith. When political allegiance and spiritual identity become fused,

any critique of the former feels like blasphemy against the latter. If

someone can question your preferred candidate and you experience it as

an assault on the gospel, the fusion has already happened. Your politics

and your faith have become so entangled that you can no longer tell

where one ends and the other begins.

**You use apocalyptic language for political events.** If every election

is "the most important in history," if every cultural shift is "the end

of Christianity," if every political opponent is "evil" in an

ontological rather than moral sense---the eschatological temperature of

your language reveals that politics has become your horizon of hope.

When the next election feels more decisive than the return of Christ,

your eschatology has been quietly rewritten by your political

commitments.

**Your prayer life has been colonized by political anxiety.** If most of

your prayers are about elections, policies, and political enemies rather

than about your own sanctification, the needs of your neighbor, and the

advance of the gospel---your anxiety has reshaped your communion with

God. Prayer has become a form of political lobbying directed at heaven

rather than an act of surrender to the God who is sovereign over all

nations.

None of these markers are unforgivable. All of them are diagnostic. And

they all point to the same underlying condition: trust has been

relocated from God to a political project, and the relocation happened

so gradually that it felt like faithfulness.

That is the genius of seduction. It does not come as a stranger knocking

at the door with a competing religion. It comes dressed as wisdom,

wrapped in urgency, and speaking the language of responsibility. It

says, "Of course you still believe in God. But be practical."

And "be practical" is the phrase that has undone more spiritual

faithfulness than any atheist argument ever conceived.

**The God Who Runs Toward the Unfaithful**

But here is where the story turns. Because if Hosea only contained

accusation, it would be unbearable. If it were only diagnosis without

remedy, it would drive us to despair. But Hosea does not end with the

accusation of adultery.

It ends with the most extraordinary invitation in the Old Testament.

After all the indictments---after the graphic language of betrayal,

after the exposure of every alliance and every false lover, after the

naming of every idol and every misplaced trust---God does something that

no one in the ancient world would have expected a betrayed spouse to do.

He asks them to come home.

> *"Return, O Israel, to the Lord your God, for you have stumbled because of your iniquity. Take with you words and return to the Lord; say to him, 'Take away all iniquity; accept what is good, and we will pay with bulls the vows of our lips. Assyria shall not save us; we will not ride on horses; and we will say no more, 'Our God,' to the work of our hands. In you the orphan finds mercy.'" ---Hosea 14:1--3*

Read that repentance carefully. Notice its structure.

First: "Take away all iniquity." This is a confession that the problem

is real. Not exaggerated. Not merely a matter of perception. Real

iniquity---real unfaithfulness---that requires real forgiveness.

Second: "Assyria shall not save us." This is the renunciation of the

false savior. It is the explicit naming of the political alliance that

had functioned as a substitute for God's protection. It is Israel

saying, out loud, what they had been refusing to admit: "We looked to

Assyria for what only You can provide."

Third: "We will not ride on horses." This is the renunciation of

military self-sufficiency. It is the admission that the chariots and

horses---the instruments of political and military power---had become

objects of trust rather than tools of stewardship.

Fourth: "We will say no more, 'Our God,' to the work of our hands." This

is the deepest level of repentance. It is the admission that the things

they had built---the alliances, the systems, the structures of human

power---had been elevated to divine status in their hearts. They had

called the idol "God." And now they are refusing to do so any longer.

That is the repentance this book calls for. Not withdrawal from public

life. Not cynical disengagement. Not the abandonment of civic

responsibility. But the radical, uncomfortable, soul-level honesty of

admitting that we have given our hearts to something other than

God---and the courageous act of taking them back.

And look at how God responds:

> *"I will heal their apostasy; I will love them freely, for my anger has turned from them. I will be like the dew to Israel; he shall blossom like the lily; he shall take root like the trees of Lebanon." ---Hosea 14:4--5*

"I will heal their apostasy." Not "I will punish their apostasy." Not "I

will reluctantly tolerate their return." Heal. The word is restorative.

It implies that the unfaithfulness was a wound---a wound in the soul of

Israel and a wound in the heart of God---and that God's response to the

returning spouse is not grudging acceptance but extravagant healing.

"I will love them freely." The Hebrew word translated "freely" carries

the sense of generosity, spontaneity, and abundance. God's love for the

returning unfaithful is not measured, not cautious, not conditioned on a

probationary period. It is free. Lavish. Unreserved.

This is the gospel before the gospel had a name.

This is a God who watches His bride walk away, names the betrayal with

devastating honesty, and then---when the bride turns around---runs

toward her.

If that sounds familiar, it should. It is the same God who, centuries

later, told a story about a father who saw his prodigal son coming from

a long way off and ran to meet him (Luke 15:20). It is the same God who,

on a cross, looked at the people who had nailed Him there and said,

"Father, forgive them" (Luke 23:34). It is the same God who, in the

risen Christ, appeared to Peter---the disciple who had denied Him three

times---and said not "Explain yourself" but "Do you love me?" (John

21:15--17).

The God of Hosea is not a God who waits at the end of a long road of

self-improvement for His people to earn their way back. He is a God who

meets them in their mess, heals what is broken, and loves them back to

life.

That is the God we are called to return to. Not a taskmaster. Not a cold

judge. Not a distant sovereign. A jealous Lover who refuses to share His

bride but who will not stop pursuing her even when she wanders.

**The Way Back Begins Here**

The way back begins with naming the idol.

And the idol's name is not "politics." Politics is not the problem. The

idol's name is *relocated trust*. The idol is whatever you cannot

imagine living without that is not God. It is the thing that, if taken

away, would cause your faith to collapse---because your faith was

secretly resting on it rather than on Christ.

For some of us, the idol is a political party. For some, it is a

political leader. For some, it is the cultural influence of

Christianity. For some, it is the comfort of being in the majority. For

some, it is the sense that "our country" and "our faith" are the same

thing. For some, it is the feeling of power itself---the intoxicating

sensation that we are in control, that we can shape the future, that we

can win.

Name it. Be specific. Do not generalize. Do not say, "I trust God too

much in politics"---that is too vague to repent of. Say what it actually

is. Say, "I have treated this leader as if he were anointed by God in a

way that no politician is." Say, "I have attached the survival of my

faith to the survival of my political party." Say, "I have been more

afraid of losing an election than I am of losing my integrity."

Name it. Confess it. And return.

Because the God of Hosea is waiting. Not with folded arms and a lecture.

With open arms and a feast.

And the road home is shorter than you think.

It begins with one sentence:

> ***"Assyria shall not save us."***

Whatever your Assyria is---name it. Lay it down. And come home.

Because the God who named the adultery is the same God who heals it. And

He is not finished with you. He is not finished with the American

church. And He has no intention of sharing His bride with a political

idol---no matter how respectable that idol may appear.

**\*Key Takeaways\***

> *• Zanah is not atheism---it is spiritual adultery: keeping God while relocating trust to something else. The vows remain; the heart has moved. • God chose marital language because the covenant is intimate, personal, and exclusive---and betrayal within intimacy is the deepest wound. • Israel did not stop worshiping; they supplemented worship with political alliances driven by fear. They "hired lovers" while keeping the temple open. • The oldest spiritual pattern is: dependence breeds intimacy, prosperity breeds independence, independence breeds forgetfulness, forgetfulness breeds betrayal. • Modern zanah looks like Christians who claim Jesus is Lord but whose emotional stability, moral standards, and deepest hopes are functionally attached to political outcomes. • The diagnostic question is not "Are you political?" but "When you are afraid, where do you run first?" • Biblical repentance from zanah is not withdrawal from public life but the explicit renunciation of political trust as ultimate: "Assyria shall not save us." • The God who accuses Israel of adultery is the same God who invites them home---and promises to heal their apostasy freely, lavishly, and without condition.*

**CHAPTER TWO**

*Chariots Aren't Evil---But They Aren't Ultimate*

> *"When legitimate goods become ultimate goods, they become idols."*

• • •

**The Good Gift That Becomes a God Substitute**

Every idol begins as a gift.

This is the thing most people miss about idolatry, and it is the reason

the Bible's warnings about it are so persistently ignored. We imagine

idols as obviously evil things---golden calves, pagan statues, demonic

rituals. We picture idolatry as something primitive and absurd,

something no sophisticated modern person would fall into. And because we

picture it that way, we never see it coming.

But the most dangerous idols are not evil things. They are good things

that have been inflated beyond their design capacity.

Money is a gift. It provides for families, funds ministries, builds

hospitals, feeds the hungry. Sex is a gift. It binds marriages, produces

new life, expresses the deepest human intimacy. Work is a gift. It

cultivates creation, serves neighbors, exercises the stewardship mandate

that God gave humanity in the garden. Family is a gift. Friendship is a

gift. Health is a gift. Even politics---the ordering of common life for

the common good---is a gift from the God who cares about justice, order,

and human flourishing.

None of these things are evil. All of them become destructive when they

are treated as ultimate.

Tim Keller articulated this pattern with a clarity that has shaped a

generation of theological thinking. In *Counterfeit Gods*, he argued

that an idol is not typically a bad thing. It is a good thing that has

been made into an ultimate thing. It is a gift that has been promoted to

a position that only the Giver should occupy. When a good thing becomes

a god thing, it becomes a destructive thing---not because its nature has

changed, but because its position has.

John Calvin said it first, and more bluntly: the human heart is a

perpetual factory of idols. We do not need pagan temples to manufacture

false gods. We carry the factory with us. And the raw material we use is

almost always something good---something God gave us to enjoy, to

steward, to use in His service---that we have quietly, imperceptibly,

desperately elevated to the place where only God belongs.

This distinction matters enormously when we talk about politics.

Because the moment someone raises concerns about the church's

relationship to political power, the instinctive response is: "Are you

saying politics is evil? Are you saying Christians shouldn't be

involved? Are you saying we should just let the world burn?"

No.

I am saying that political engagement is a good gift. Voting is a good

gift. Advocacy, legislation, civic participation, the pursuit of justice

through governmental structures---these are legitimate and even

necessary expressions of neighbor-love. A Christian who ignores politics

is not holier than one who engages; in many cases, they are simply less

compassionate. The Samaritan did not walk past the beaten man and call

it "spiritual detachment." He got involved. He spent money. He used the

systems available to him to help.

The question has never been whether Christians should engage

politically.

The question is what *weight* that engagement carries in the life of the

soul.

And that question---the question of weight---is where everything turns.

**The Biblical Architecture: Psalm 20 and the Trust Distinction**

Psalm 20 is a royal psalm---a prayer offered for the king of Israel as

he prepares for battle. It is not a pacifist text. It does not condemn

military action. It does not pretend that warfare is irrelevant or that

national defense is unspiritual. It takes seriously the reality that

kings go to war, that nations face enemies, and that the survival of a

people sometimes depends on the courage and competence of its leaders.

But in the middle of this very practical, very political prayer, the

psalmist draws a line that echoes through the rest of Scripture:

> *"Some trust in chariots and some in horses, but we trust in the name of the Lord our God. They collapse and fall, but we rise and stand upright." ---Psalm 20:7--8*

This verse is one of the most important sentences in the Old Testament

for understanding the relationship between faith and politics. And it is

important precisely because of what it does *not* say.

It does not say, "Chariots are evil." It does not say, "Only fools use

horses." It does not condemn military preparedness as inherently

faithless or suggest that Israel should disband its army and wait for

miraculous intervention. The psalm is being prayed for a king who is

about to fight. The psalmist is not naive about the realities of power.

What the psalmist does is draw a distinction between *using* and

*trusting*. Chariots can be used. Horses can be ridden. Military

resources can be deployed. Political structures can be engaged. But none

of these things can be *trusted*---not in the way that God is trusted,

not as the foundation of hope, not as the thing that ultimately

determines whether you stand or fall.

That distinction---between use and trust, between engagement and

dependence, between the tool and the savior---is the theological key to

this entire book.

You can use political structures without trusting them. You can vote

without worshiping the outcome. You can advocate for justice without

believing that justice depends entirely on your advocacy succeeding. You

can engage the political process with passion, intelligence, and

conviction without making the political process the foundation of your

hope.

But the moment you cross from engagement to dependence---from using the

chariot to trusting the chariot, from participating in politics to being

emotionally sustained by politics---something has shifted in your soul.

And that shift is precisely what the psalmist warns against.

Because notice how the verse ends: "They collapse and fall, but we rise

and stand upright." The people who trust chariots eventually discover

that chariots are not ultimate. Chariots break. Horses stumble. Armies

lose. Political movements fail. Every human structure eventually proves

insufficient. And when it does, the people who built their hope on it

collapse with it.

But the people who trusted in the name of the Lord? They stand. Not

because they were passive. Not because they did nothing. But because

their standing was never dependent on the chariot in the first place.

That is the freedom the gospel offers to politically engaged Christians:

you can participate fully without being destroyed by the outcome. You

can care deeply without being consumed. You can engage the process

without being defined by the results.

But that freedom is only available to people who have made the trust

distinction. And making the trust distinction requires a kind of

spiritual honesty that most of us find deeply uncomfortable.

**Isaiah's Warning: The Egyptian Alliance**

Isaiah 31 provides an even more pointed illustration of the trust

distinction---and one that carries a devastating irony.

The historical situation is this: the kingdom of Judah faces military

threat from Assyria, the superpower of the north. Assyria has already

conquered the northern kingdom of Israel. Judah is next. The political

establishment in Jerusalem, understandably terrified, wants to form a

military alliance with Egypt---the superpower of the south. It is a

classic balance-of-power strategy. Egypt has chariots. Egypt has a

massive, professional army. Egypt can provide the military counterweight

that tiny Judah cannot muster on its own.

From a purely strategic perspective, the alliance makes sense. It is the

kind of decision any reasonable political advisor would recommend. It is

pragmatic, realistic, and "responsible."

And God condemns it.

> *"Woe to those who go down to Egypt for help and rely on horses, who trust in chariots because they are many and in horsemen because they are very strong, but do not look to the Holy One of Israel or consult the Lord!" ---Isaiah 31:1*

The accusation is specific: they "do not look to the Holy One of Israel

or consult the Lord." The problem is not that Egypt exists. The problem

is not that military alliances are available. The problem is the

direction of the gaze. The posture of the heart. The location of trust.

Judah has not consulted God. They have not prayed. They have not sought

divine guidance. They have assessed the political situation with their

own wisdom, identified the most powerful ally available, and made their

decision based on calculation rather than faith. They have looked at the

problem with entirely human eyes and reached an entirely human

conclusion.

And there is a devastating irony in this particular text that we must

not miss.

Egypt is where Israel was enslaved.

Egypt is where God demonstrated His power to save---through plagues,

through the parting of the sea, through the destruction of Pharaoh's

army. Egypt is where the chariots of the most powerful nation on earth

were drowned in the waters of the Red Sea while Israel walked through on

dry ground.

And now, generations later, Israel is running back to Egypt's chariots

for protection.

They have forgotten the Exodus. They have forgotten what God did. They

have forgotten who saved them from the very power they are now running

to for help.

Does that pattern sound familiar?

The American church was forged in a tradition that insisted on the

sufficiency of Christ, the power of the Holy Spirit, and the sovereignty

of God over nations. The great revivals that shaped American

Protestantism were not produced by legislative action. They were

produced by prayer, preaching, and the mysterious work of the Spirit.

The church grew not because it had political access but because it had

spiritual authority.

And now, in our anxiety, in our fear of cultural decline and moral

collapse, we are running to the very power structures we were never

meant to depend on---as though God's arm is too short to save without

Washington's help. As though the Spirit who raised Christ from the dead

needs a congressional majority to do His work.

We have forgotten our Exodus. And we are running back to Egypt.

**Fear: The Accelerant That Makes the Idol Feel Necessary**

Here is the crucial pastoral insight that holds this chapter together:

good things do not become ultimate things in a vacuum. The accelerant is

almost always fear.

No one sits down in a quiet moment of reflection and calmly, rationally

decides, "I am going to make politics my functional savior." That is not

how idolatry works. Idolatry happens under pressure. It happens in the

dark. It happens when the ground shifts beneath you and everything you

value feels like it is under threat. It happens when you watch the news

and your stomach tightens and your thoughts race and your blood pressure

spikes and you think, with every fiber of your being, "Something has to

be done."

That phrase---"something has to be done"---is not wrong in itself.

Christians should respond to injustice, should act in the face of evil,

should work for the good of their neighbors and communities. The

instinct to act is a gift from God.

But fear corrupts the instinct. Fear takes the legitimate desire to act

and transforms it into a desperate need to control. Fear says not merely

"something should be done" but "everything depends on what we do next."

Fear removes God from the equation and puts the entire weight of the

future on human shoulders---specifically, on the shoulders of whoever

promises to carry it.

Fear does not patiently reason its way to conclusions. Fear does not

wait for careful theological reflection. Fear does not pause to ask,

"What does Scripture say about this?" or "What does the history of the

church teach us about moments like this?" Fear reads one headline and

reorganizes the soul. It takes the hierarchy of your affections---God

first, then everything else in its proper order---and scrambles it in an

instant.

And once fear has reorganized the soul, the idol does not feel like an

idol. It feels like the only reasonable option.

This is why fear-driven Christianity is so spiritually dangerous. Not

because the fears are always irrational---some of them are legitimate.

Moral decline is real. Cultural hostility to Christianity is real. Legal

threats to religious liberty are real. The erosion of institutions that

once supported family life, community cohesion, and moral formation is

real.

But fear bypasses the spiritual discernment that would normally prevent

a good thing from becoming an ultimate thing. Under normal

circumstances, a healthy Christian can distinguish between caring about

an election and worshiping the outcome. But when fear enters the

picture---when the stakes feel existential, when the threat feels total,

when the enemy feels overwhelming---that distinction collapses.

When you are afraid, your threshold for idolatry drops dramatically.

Suddenly the chariot does not look like a useful tool. It looks like the

only thing standing between you and destruction. And once something

feels like the only thing standing between you and destruction, it has

become your functional savior---whether you call it that or not.

That is how political engagement crosses the line into political

idolatry. Not through a conscious decision to worship power. Not through

an explicit renunciation of faith. But through the gradual, fear-driven

elevation of political outcomes into objects of ultimate concern---so

that winning feels like salvation and losing feels like damnation.

And the terrifying thing is that the whole process feels responsible. It

feels like stewardship. It feels like "being realistic." It feels like

what any good Christian parent, any good pastor, any good citizen would

do.

Fear always makes the idol feel necessary. That is its most powerful

trick.

**1 Samuel 8: When Israel Wanted a King Like the Nations**

The pattern we are describing is older than Hosea. It is older than

Isaiah. It goes back to one of the most instructive and haunting

passages in the entire Old Testament: the moment when the people of

Israel demanded a king.

The context matters. Samuel, the last and greatest of the judges, is

aging. His sons, Joel and Abijah, have been appointed as judges after

him, but they are corrupt---taking bribes, perverting justice, failing

to walk in their father's ways. Meanwhile, the Philistines remain a

constant military threat. The nation feels vulnerable, leaderless, and

afraid.

And so the elders of Israel come to Samuel with a request that carries

the weight of a theological revolution:

> *"Appoint for us a king to judge us like all the nations." ---1 Samuel 8:5*

"Like all the nations."

That phrase is the key to understanding everything that follows. Israel

was not meant to be like the nations. Israel was meant to be set

apart---a people whose king was invisible, whose protector was divine,

whose security came not from military might or political institutions

but from the covenant faithfulness of God Himself. Their

distinctiveness---their strangeness, their refusal to organize

themselves the way other nations did---was supposed to be their witness

to the world that there was a God who could be trusted.

And now they want to be like everyone else. They want a visible king. A

human protector. A leader they can see, touch, follow, and point to when

the Philistines come.

Samuel is distressed. He prays. And God's response is stunning:

> *"Obey the voice of the people in all that they say to you, for they have not rejected you, but they have rejected me from being king over them. According to all the deeds that they have done, from the day I brought them up out of Egypt even to this day, forsaking me and serving other gods, so they are also doing to you." ---1 Samuel 8:7--8*

Notice: God does not say the request is merely pragmatically unwise. He

does not say, "This is a bad political strategy." He says it is a

spiritual rejection. The desire for a visible, powerful, human

protector---even if that protector is imperfect, even if the request is

driven by legitimate fear of legitimate enemies---is itself a rejection

of God's invisible kingship.

This is extraordinary. Israel is not asking for a pagan deity. They are

not building a golden calf. They are asking for a political

institution---a monarchy, like the other nations have. And God

identifies this request as idolatry. Not because monarchy is inherently

evil---God will later establish the Davidic monarchy and call it a

covenant blessing. But because the *motivation* behind the request is

fear-driven replacement of divine trust with human security.

God tells Samuel to warn them. And the warning is detailed and

devastating:

> *"These will be the ways of the king who will reign over you: he will take your sons and appoint them to his chariots and to be his horsemen... He will take your daughters to be perfumers and cooks and bakers. He will take the best of your fields and vineyards and olive orchards... He will take the tenth of your flocks, and you shall be his slaves." ---1 Samuel 8:11--17*

The king they are demanding will take. Their sons. Their daughters.

Their land. Their wealth. Their freedom. The protector will become the

predator. The savior will become the enslaver. The power they trust will

eventually consume them.

And then the most devastating line of all:

> *"And in that day you will cry out because of your king, whom you have chosen for yourselves, but the Lord will not answer you in that day." ---1 Samuel 8:18*

God grants the request. He gives them Saul. And the experiment unfolds

exactly as warned. Saul is tall, impressive, initially successful---and

ultimately a disaster. He is consumed by jealousy, paranoia, and

disobedience. He consults a medium instead of God. He sacrifices what is

sacred for what is expedient. He becomes everything God said a

fear-chosen king would become.

The pattern is consistent throughout Scripture, and it should terrify

every American Christian who has ever thought, "If we just get the right

person in office, everything will be okay."

When God's people demand political power as their security, God

sometimes gives it to them---not as a blessing, but as a judgment. He

lets them have what they asked for. And what they asked for eventually

devours them.

That should make us pause. Not because political participation is wrong.

But because the spirit in which we pursue political solutions---the

fear, the desperation, the sense that everything depends on *this*

leader or *this* election---may reveal that we have already made the

request Israel made. And God may already be granting it.

Answering the Objection: "Are You Saying Christians Shouldn't Be

Involved?"

Let me be as clear as I can, because this objection is the most common

one I hear, and it deserves a thorough and honest answer.

No. I am not saying Christians should withdraw from political life.

Christianity has never been a quietist religion. The claim that

Christians should retreat from public engagement is neither historically

accurate nor biblically supported. It is, in fact, a failure of love. If

you see injustice and do nothing because you are worried about "mixing

faith and politics," you have not achieved spiritual purity. You have

abandoned your neighbor.

Abraham Kuyper, the Dutch Reformed theologian who also served as prime

minister of the Netherlands, insisted that Christ lays claim to every

square inch of existence---including politics. Kuyper saw no conflict

between robust Christian conviction and vigorous political

participation. He believed that precisely *because* Jesus is Lord,

Christians have a responsibility to bring the implications of that

lordship into every sphere of public life.

Augustine engaged the political philosophy of his day at the highest

intellectual level. His *City of God* is not a call to political

disengagement. It is a massive, sophisticated work of political theology

that insists earthly politics matters---while simultaneously insisting

that it is not ultimate. Augustine did not withdraw from Rome. He

engaged Rome while relativizing Rome.

Calvin worked to shape civic structures in Geneva. The Reformers did not

hide in monasteries and wait for the Second Coming. They built schools,

shaped laws, reformed institutions, and insisted that the gospel had

public implications.

So the question has never been whether Christians should be involved in

politics. The historical and theological consensus is clear: they

should.

But---and this is where the conversation must become painfully,

uncomfortably honest---none of those great theologians made political

power the functional center of their hope.

Kuyper did not believe the church's survival depended on his political

success. When his party lost elections, he did not treat it as a crisis

of faith. Augustine did not panic when Rome fell. He wrote The City of

God specifically to argue that the fall of an earthly empire is not the

fall of the Kingdom of God. Calvin did not treat Geneva's government as

the guarantor of the gospel. He knew the gospel would outlast Geneva.

They engaged politics without worshiping it. They used the chariot

without trusting it. They participated in civic life without making

civic life ultimate. They held political convictions with deep

seriousness and loose hands. They cared about outcomes without being

defined by them.

That is the model. And it is a model that requires a level of spiritual

maturity, theological clarity, and faith that does not require visible

results to remain steady.

The question is not whether we are involved. The question is whether we

can be involved without being consumed. Whether we can care about

politics without making politics the load-bearing wall of our spiritual

house. Whether we can use the chariot without kneeling before it.

If we can---if our engagement is driven by love rather than fear, by

conviction rather than desperation, by hope in Christ rather than hope

in a candidate---then political engagement is an expression of

faithfulness.

If we cannot---if every election feels like the apocalypse, if every

political loss feels like a spiritual crisis, if our engagement is

driven more by terror than by love of neighbor---then something has

shifted. And the shift needs to be named before it can be healed.

**A Good Gift / God Substitute Diagnostic**

As a practical tool for discernment---something you can return to

regularly, not just once---consider these diagnostic questions about any

good thing in your life. They apply to money, career, relationships,

reputation, and family. But for the purposes of this book, apply them

specifically to your political engagement.

**If this were taken away, would I lose my sense of security?** Not

"would I be disappointed"---that is normal. But would I feel

existentially unsafe? Would my confidence in God's goodness collapse? If

the loss of a political outcome would shake your faith in God, that

outcome has been carrying weight that only God should carry.

Do I defend this with more passion than I defend the character of

Christ? If you will argue for hours about a political leader but fall

silent when Christ's name is mocked---if you will confront a coworker

over a policy disagreement but not over a blasphemous joke---your

passion reveals your priority.

Does this good thing require me to compromise my integrity to maintain

it? This is perhaps the sharpest question. If supporting your

political project requires you to excuse dishonesty, cruelty, sexual

misconduct, corruption, or the dehumanization of people made in God's

image---then the project has demanded a sacrifice that only an idol

requires. God never asks you to sin in order to serve Him. Only idols

make that demand.

Do I spend more emotional energy on this than on my relationship with

God? Measure it honestly. Not in theory---in practice. How much time

this week did you spend reading political commentary? How much time in

prayer? How much time arguing on social media? How much time in

Scripture? The allocation of your emotional energy is the most honest

map of your actual priorities.

Would I be willing to lose this for the sake of faithfulness to

Jesus? This is the ultimate test. Every good thing should be held with

open hands. If you could not imagine remaining faithful to Christ if

your political project failed---if the thought of political loss makes

following Jesus feel pointless or impossible---then the political

project has replaced Christ as the non-negotiable center of your life.

**Has my political engagement made me more like Jesus or less?** Look at

the fruit. Has your involvement in politics made you more patient, more

kind, more generous, more truthful, more humble? Or has it made you

angrier, more contemptuous, more tribal, more willing to dehumanize

people who disagree with you? The fruit reveals the root. If your

political engagement is producing the fruit of the Spirit, it is being

held properly. If it is producing rage, contempt, and

self-righteousness, something has gone wrong at the root level.

These questions are not designed to produce guilt. They are designed to

produce clarity. And clarity---the ability to see yourself honestly,

without defensiveness or rationalization---is the first gift of

repentance.

**Holding the Chariot Loosely**

So what does it look like to engage politically without making politics

ultimate? What does it look like to use the chariot without trusting it?

It looks like a Christian who votes with conviction and then sleeps well

regardless of the outcome---not because they do not care, but because

their security does not depend on winning.

It looks like a believer who advocates passionately for justice but does

not demonize people who disagree---because they know that the image of

God in their opponent is more fundamental than the policy difference

between them.

It looks like a church that engages the political process but refuses to

let the political process set the emotional temperature of its

worship---because the resurrection of Jesus Christ, not the latest poll

numbers, determines the mood of the people of God.

It looks like a pastor who speaks prophetically about public issues but

refuses to endorse candidates or align the church with a party---because

the church's prophetic voice depends on its independence from every

political faction.

It looks like a community of believers who can disagree politically

within the same congregation without treating each other as

enemies---because they know that baptism is a deeper bond than voter

registration.

It looks like the early church, which paid taxes to Caesar, prayed for

rulers it did not choose, served neighbors it could not control, and

refused to grant any earthly power the status of Lord---because that

title was taken.

This is not disengagement. It is the most engaged and most free form of

political participation available. It is engagement that is liberated

from fear, because the person who is not afraid of losing cannot be

manipulated by the threat of loss. It is engagement that is liberated

from desperation, because the person whose hope is in Christ does not

need the next election to vindicate their faith.

The chariot is a useful tool. Use it well. Use it wisely. Use it in

service of your neighbor and the common good.

But do not trust it. Do not worship it. Do not let anyone convince you

that the chariot is what stands between you and destruction.

Because the One who stands between you and destruction is not riding a

chariot. He is hanging on a cross. And three days later, He will be

standing in a garden, having defeated every power that has ever claimed

ultimacy---including the ones you are tempted to trust right now.

Trust Him. And hold the chariot loosely.

**\*Key Takeaways\***

> *• Every idol begins as a gift---not an evil thing but a good thing elevated beyond its design capacity. Political engagement is a good gift that becomes destructive only when treated as ultimate. • The trust distinction in Psalm 20 is the theological key: you can use chariots without trusting them, engage politics without worshiping outcomes. • Isaiah's warning about the Egyptian alliance carries a devastating irony: Israel ran back to the chariots of the very nation God had delivered them from. The American church risks the same pattern. • Fear is the accelerant that transforms good gifts into god substitutes. Under pressure, the chariot stops looking like a tool and starts looking like the only thing between you and destruction. • Israel's demand for a king "like the nations" was not pragmatically foolish---it was spiritually idolatrous. God identified it as a rejection of His kingship and granted it as a judgment. • The great Christian political theologians---Kuyper, Augustine, Calvin---engaged politics vigorously without making politics the functional center of their hope. That is the model. • The "Good Gift / God Substitute" diagnostic reveals where trust has been relocated by examining emotional stability, moral consistency, and willingness to lose. • Holding the chariot loosely means engaging politics with conviction and freedom---free from fear, free from desperation, and free from the need to win in order to maintain faith.*

**CHAPTER THREE**

*The Messiah You Want vs. the Messiah You Need*

> *"They wanted a sword. He brought a towel."*

• • •

**The Crowd That Tried to Make Jesus King**

In the sixth chapter of John's Gospel, something remarkable

happens---something that reveals, more clearly than almost any other

moment in the New Testament, the distance between what people want from

God and what God actually came to do.

Jesus has just performed one of His most famous miracles. With five

loaves of bread and two fish, He has fed five thousand men---plus women

and children. The crowd is astonished. They have seen supernatural power

on display. They have tasted provision that defies the laws of economics

and agriculture. And they reach a conclusion that feels inevitable:

> *"When the people saw the sign that he had done, they said, 'This is indeed the Prophet who is to come into the world!'" ---John 6:14*

They are referencing the prophecy in Deuteronomy 18, where Moses

promises that God will raise up a prophet like himself---a new

liberator, a new lawgiver, a new leader who will do for Israel what

Moses did. And they believe they have found Him. Here is a man with the

power to feed armies, heal the sick, command nature,

and---presumably---drive out the Romans and restore Israel to its former

glory.

The crowd is ready to make Him king. Not metaphorically. Not in some

spiritualized, internal sense. Literally. They want to take Him by force

and put Him on a throne.

And then John records one of the most startling verses in the Gospels:

> *"Perceiving then that they were about to come and take him by force to make him king, Jesus withdrew again to the mountain by himself." ---John 6:15*

Jesus ran from the crown.

Let that sink in. The crowd is offering Him exactly what any political

messiah would want: popular support, enthusiastic followers, a mandate

to rule. And Jesus does not accept it. He does not negotiate terms. He

does not say, "Let me think about it." He withdraws. He leaves. He

climbs a mountain alone.

Not because He was not a king. He was---and is---the King of Kings.

Not because He lacked authority. He possessed all authority in heaven

and on earth.

But because the crowd wanted the wrong kind of king. They wanted a

messiah who would use supernatural power to solve their political

problems. They wanted sovereignty without surrender. Victory without

repentance. A throne without a cross. National restoration without

personal transformation.

And Jesus knew that if He accepted the crown on their terms, He would

become exactly what the devil had offered Him in the wilderness---a

ruler of earthly kingdoms who had bypassed the way of suffering, a

savior who delivered political freedom while leaving the soul in

bondage.

The crowd was not wrong to want a king. They were wrong about what kind

of king they needed.

And that mistake---wanting the right thing in the wrong way, wanting

God's power deployed according to human expectations---is not a

first-century problem. It is a perennial problem. It is our problem.

The Wilderness Temptation: The Shortcut That Never Stops Being

Offered

To understand why Jesus withdrew from the crown, we need to go back to

the beginning of His public ministry and the temptation in the

wilderness. Because the temptation the crowd is offering in John 6 is

the same temptation Satan offered in Matthew 4. The packaging is

different. The substance is identical.

Matthew records three temptations. The first is about provision: turn

stones into bread. The second is about spectacle: throw yourself off the

temple and let the angels catch you. The third is the one that matters

most for our purposes, because it is explicitly political:

> *"Again, the devil took him to a very high mountain and showed him all the kingdoms of the world and their glory. And he said to him, 'All these I will give you, if you will fall down and worship me.'" ---Matthew 4:8--9*

Read this carefully. What Satan is offering is not merely temptation to

personal sin. It is a theological proposition. It is a competing vision

of how the world gets fixed.

Satan is saying: You want to save the world? Here it is. All the

kingdoms. All the power. All the political authority. You can have

it---right now. No cross. No suffering. No slow, patient, agonizing work

of redemption through death and resurrection. No thirty years of

obscurity followed by three years of rejection followed by a humiliating

public execution. Skip all of that. Take the shortcut. I will give you

what you want---just worship me.

And Jesus said no.

> *"Then Jesus said to him, 'Be gone, Satan! For it is written, "You shall worship the Lord your God and him only shall you serve."'" ---Matthew 4:10*

Jesus did not refuse because political authority is evil. He will later

tell His disciples that all authority in heaven and on earth has been

given to Him (Matthew 28:18). He did not refuse because He was

uninterested in the kingdoms of the world. He came precisely to redeem

them.

He refused because political authority obtained through compromise with

evil---even for good ends, even for redemptive purposes, even to "save

the world"---is a betrayal of the Father's plan. The shortcut is always

a betrayal. The means matter. The method of conquest matters. And God's

method is not coercion, not power brokering, not political maneuvering.

God's method is a cross.

That temptation did not end in the wilderness. It followed Jesus

throughout His entire public ministry. Every time the crowd wanted to

crown Him. Every time the disciples argued about who would sit at His

right and left hand in the coming kingdom. Every time zealots tried to

recruit Him for their cause. Every time the people expected a military

Messiah who would throw off Roman oppression and restore Israel to

national greatness.

The temptation was always the same: bypass the cross, take the shortcut,

use power to accomplish what only suffering can achieve.

And Jesus refused. Every single time.

Now here is the question for the American church: Are we refusing it? Or

are we accepting the very deal Jesus turned down?

When we align the church with political power in exchange for

protection, influence, and cultural dominance---when we accept the

bargain of "all these kingdoms, if you will just support me"---are we

following Jesus, or are we accepting the offer He rejected?

The question is not comfortable. But it is inescapable.

**The Kind of Messiah They Rejected**

To understand the first-century rejection of Jesus, we need to

understand the first-century expectation. Because the people who

rejected Jesus were not godless pagans. Many of them were devout,

Scripture-reading, synagogue-attending, prayer-praying believers. They

loved God. They believed His promises. They were waiting for the Messiah

with genuine hope.

They just had a very specific idea of what the Messiah would do.

The dominant messianic expectation in first-century Judaism was

political. The Messiah would be a new David---a warrior king who would

restore Israel's political sovereignty, drive out the occupying Romans,

reestablish the Davidic throne in Jerusalem, and inaugurate an era of

national greatness under God's visible rule. This was not a fringe hope.

It was the mainstream expectation, rooted in real biblical texts and

reinforced by centuries of foreign occupation, humiliation, and longing

for deliverance.

The promises to David in 2 Samuel 7. The victory songs of the psalms.

The prophetic visions of a restored kingdom in Isaiah, Ezekiel, and

Daniel. The expectation of a "Son of Man" who would receive dominion and

an everlasting kingdom. All of these texts were in their Bibles, and all

of them could be read---and were read---as predictions of a politically

triumphant Messiah.

What they missed---or what they chose not to see---was the other thread.

The thread of the Suffering Servant in Isaiah 52--53, who would be

"despised and rejected by men," who would bear the iniquities of the

people, who would be "like a lamb that is led to the slaughter."

The thread of the humble king in Zechariah 9:9, who would come "riding

on a donkey, on a colt, the foal of a donkey"---not on a war horse, not

in a chariot, not at the head of an army, but on the most humble animal

available.

The thread of the shepherd struck down in Zechariah 13:7, whose death

would scatter the sheep before God's refining work could begin.

The thread of the psalmist in Psalm 22, who cries out, "My God, my God,

why have you forsaken me?"---a psalm that reads like a detailed preview

of crucifixion written centuries before crucifixion was invented.

All of these texts were in their Scriptures. But the political messiah

was so much more appealing---so much more satisfying to the national

ego, so much more aligned with the desire for visible victory---that the

suffering thread was minimized, spiritualized, or simply ignored.

When Jesus arrived, He was not what they expected.

He did not raise an army. He did not storm Herod's palace. He did not

negotiate with Rome. He did not organize a political movement. Instead,

He washed feet. He touched lepers. He ate with tax collectors and

prostitutes. He welcomed children. He confronted the religious

establishment. He wept over Jerusalem. He told parables about seeds and

soil instead of strategy and siege warfare.

He announced that the Kingdom of God was at hand---and then defined that

Kingdom in ways that baffled, disturbed, and eventually infuriated

everyone who heard Him.

The Kingdom belongs to the poor in spirit. The meek. The mourners. The

peacemakers. The persecuted. Not the powerful, the triumphant, the

well-connected, the politically astute.

No wonder they were confused. No wonder many of them eventually turned

away. No wonder, when the choice was laid before them between Jesus and

Barabbas---between the suffering servant and the violent

revolutionary---the crowd chose Barabbas.

They chose the strongman over the servant. And they have not stopped

choosing.

**The Triumphal Entry That Wasn't**

Luke 19 records what the church has traditionally called the "Triumphal

Entry"---Jesus' arrival in Jerusalem on what we celebrate as Palm

Sunday. It is one of the most misunderstood moments in the Gospels, and

the misunderstanding reveals everything about the distance between human

expectations and divine intention.

The scene looks triumphant. The crowd is ecstatic. They are shouting,

"Blessed is the King who comes in the name of the Lord! Peace in heaven

and glory in the highest!" They are throwing their cloaks on the

road---a gesture of royal honor. The atmosphere is electric with

expectation. This is the moment. The Messiah is finally going to take

His throne. Jerusalem will be restored. Rome will be humbled. God's

people will be free.

But look at what Jesus does as He approaches the city:

> *"And when he drew near and saw the city, he wept over it, saying, 'Would that you, even you, had known on this day the things that make for peace! But now they are hidden from your eyes. For the days will come when your enemies will set up a barricade around you and surround you and hem you in on every side and tear you down to the ground, you and your children within you. And they will not leave one stone upon another in you, because you did not know the time of your visitation.'" ---Luke 19:41--44*

The crowd is celebrating. Jesus is weeping.

They think He is coming to conquer. He knows He is coming to die.

They believe this entry will lead to a throne. It will lead to a cross.

They are shouting about glory. He is grieving about

destruction---because He knows that within a generation, Rome will do

exactly what He describes: surround the city, tear it down, and leave

not one stone upon another. The very political catastrophe the crowd

hopes the Messiah will prevent is the catastrophe Jesus sees coming

precisely because they have rejected His kind of messiahship.

And the reason He weeps is contained in that devastating phrase: they do

not know "the things that make for peace."

They think peace comes through military victory and political

restoration. Jesus knows peace comes through repentance, reconciliation,

and the self-giving love of God poured out on a cross. They want the

peace that comes from defeating your enemies. He offers the peace that

comes from loving them. They want the peace of power. He brings the

peace of surrender.

The distance between what the crowd wanted and what Jesus came to do is

the same distance that exists today between what many American

Christians expect from political power and what God actually provides

through the gospel.

We keep looking for a triumphal entry---a moment when our side wins, our

values prevail, our enemies are humbled, and the kingdom feels like it

has arrived.

God keeps offering a crucifixion.

And we keep being confused about why He won't fight the way we want Him

to.

**Mark 8: The Rebuke That Defines Everything**

If there is one passage in the Gospels that exposes the spiritual danger

of wanting the wrong kind of Messiah, it is Mark 8:27--38. This passage

is the hinge of Mark's entire Gospel, and its implications for the

American church are staggering.

The scene begins with a question. Jesus asks His disciples: "Who do

people say that I am?" They offer various answers---John the Baptist,

Elijah, one of the prophets. Then Jesus makes it personal: "But who do

you say that I am?"

Peter answers with the confession that defines Christianity: "You are

the Christ." The Messiah. The anointed one. The King we have been

waiting for.

It is the right answer. And everything that follows reveals how

catastrophically Peter misunderstands what it means.

Because immediately after Peter's confession, Jesus begins to explain

what kind of Christ He will be:

> *"And he began to teach them that the Son of Man must suffer many things and be rejected by the elders and the chief priests and the scribes and be killed, and after three days rise again. And he said this plainly." ---Mark 8:31--32*

Suffer. Be rejected. Be killed. Rise again. That is the messianic

program. That is the plan. Not political conquest. Not military victory.

Not cultural dominance. Suffering, rejection, death, and resurrection.

Peter cannot accept it.

> *"And Peter took him aside and began to rebuke him." ---Mark 8:32*

Let the audacity of this sink in. Peter has just confessed Jesus as the

Christ---the anointed king of Israel, the one sent by God to save the

world---and within sixty seconds, he is rebuking Him. Correcting Him.

Telling God's Messiah that God's plan is wrong.

Peter is not rejecting Jesus. He is editing Jesus. He is keeping the

title "Christ" while insisting on a different job description. He wants

a Messiah who wins through strength, who conquers through power, who

restores Israel through political and military triumph. A suffering

Messiah is a contradiction in terms. A crucified King is an absurdity. A

Savior who dies is no savior at all.

And Jesus' response is the most devastating rebuke in the entire Bible:

> *"But turning and seeing his disciples, he rebuked Peter and said, 'Get behind me, Satan! For you are not setting your mind on the things of God, but on the things of man.'" ---Mark 8:33*

Jesus calls Peter "Satan."

Not an enemy. A disciple. Not someone who denies Christ. Someone who

confesses Christ---and then tries to edit the kind of Christ he is

willing to follow.

And the reason for the rebuke is not that Peter is stupid or that his

concern is trivial. The reason is that Peter is promoting a theology of

power that is satanic in origin. The desire for a Messiah who wins

through strength rather than suffering, who conquers through force

rather than sacrifice, who delivers the nation through political triumph

rather than personal transformation---that desire is not merely

mistaken. It is the same offer Satan made in the wilderness. It is the

same temptation Jesus has already rejected. And now it is coming from

inside the inner circle.

The application to our moment should be unmistakable.

When the American church confesses "Jesus is Lord" but then insists that

the way forward is through political power, cultural dominance,

legislative control, and the defeat of enemies through coercion rather

than love---we are doing exactly what Peter did.

We are confessing the right Christ and demanding the wrong mission.

We are saying "You are the Messiah" and then rebuking Him when His plan

does not look like what we wanted.

And Jesus' response has not changed. It is the same today as it was on

the road to Caesarea Philippi: "You are not setting your mind on the

things of God, but on the things of man."

Answering the Objection: "But Jesus Confronts Evil---What About

Justice?"

This is an important objection, and it deserves more than a dismissive

wave. Because the portrait of Jesus I am painting---a Jesus who rejects

political power, who suffers rather than conquers, who dies rather than

fights---can be misread as a portrait of passivity. And Jesus is not

passive.

Jesus overturns tables in the temple. He calls the Pharisees

"whitewashed tombs" and "brood of vipers." He speaks truth to the face

of every power structure He encounters---religious, political, economic.

He does not avoid conflict. He walks into it. He does not tolerate

injustice. He names it.

So the objection is fair: if Jesus confronts evil, why can't we use

political power to confront evil too?

The answer is not that Christians should never use political tools to

pursue justice. As we established in the previous chapter, political

engagement is a legitimate expression of neighbor-love. Laws matter.

Policies matter. The vulnerable need protection, and sometimes that

protection comes through legislation, courts, and governmental action.

The answer is in *how* Jesus confronts evil---and what He refuses to do

in the process.

Jesus confronts evil without seizing political control. He speaks truth

to Pilate without negotiating for a position in the Roman government. He

rebukes the Pharisees without organizing a competing religious party. He

cleanses the temple without establishing a permanent enforcement

mechanism. His confrontation of evil is fierce, direct, and

prophetic---but it is never coercive. It never depends on having the

political power to enforce compliance.

Jesus confronts evil without dehumanizing His enemies. He calls the

Pharisees hypocrites, but He also weeps over Jerusalem. He rebukes

Peter, but He also restores him. He is arrested by soldiers, but He

heals the ear of the one Peter struck. The confrontation of evil in the

Gospels never slides into the contempt, cruelty, or dehumanization that

characterizes so much of our political engagement. Jesus can name sin

without destroying the sinner. That is a skill the church has largely

forgotten.

Jesus confronts evil by absorbing it rather than retaliating against it.

This is the heart of the matter. On the cross, Jesus does not defeat

evil by exercising superior force. He defeats evil by taking it into

Himself, bearing its full weight, and refusing to let it reproduce

through Him. He breaks the cycle of violence not by winning the fight

but by refusing to fight on evil's terms. "When he was reviled, he did

not revile in return; when he suffered, he did not threaten, but

continued entrusting himself to him who judges justly" (1 Peter 2:23).

And the resurrection that follows is God's vindication of that

path---not the path of the sword, but the path of self-giving love. The

resurrection does not come through military revolt. It comes through

dying and being raised. The victory is real. The kingdom is established.

Evil is defeated. But the method of defeat is sacrifice, not coercion.

So when we insist that the church needs political power to accomplish

justice, we are implicitly saying something about the cross: we are

saying it was not enough. We are saying God's strategy of conquering

through suffering is insufficient and needs to be supplemented by the

strategies of the world. We are saying what Peter said---"Surely this

cannot be the plan"---and receiving the same rebuke.

Justice in the way of Jesus does not bypass suffering. It passes through

it. And any vision of justice that requires the church to become cruel,

manipulative, dishonest, or power-hungry in order to achieve its goals

has already departed from the way of Christ---no matter how righteous

the cause.

**Cross-Shaped Courage and Non-Anxious Presence**

So what does this mean practically? If the church is not supposed to

seize political power to impose righteousness, and if the church is not

supposed to withdraw into passivity and irrelevance---what is the church

supposed to do?

The answer is cross-shaped courage. And it looks like nothing the world

expects.

A cross-shaped church does not need to win every culture war to maintain

its identity. Its identity is not derived from cultural dominance but

from union with Christ. A cross-shaped church can lose every political

battle and still be the church. It can be marginalized, ridiculed,

legally constrained, and culturally irrelevant---and still proclaim the

resurrection with authority and joy.

Why? Because its confidence was never in its cultural position. Its

confidence was in a tomb that is empty.

A cross-shaped church is free---genuinely, radically free---from the

tyranny of outcomes. It can work for justice without being destroyed by

injustice. It can advocate for truth without being devastated when truth

is rejected. It can engage the political process without being defined

by the political results. Its future is secured not by electoral

victories but by the resurrection of Jesus Christ from the dead.

This is not passivity. It is the most active and most dangerous thing

the church can be. Because a church that is not afraid of losing is a

church that cannot be controlled. A church that does not need political

power is a church that can speak truth to political power without

flinching, without hedging, without calculating the political cost.

The reason so many churches cannot speak truth right now is precisely

that they are afraid of losing---losing access, losing influence, losing

protection, losing tax exemptions, losing cultural centrality, losing

the seat at the table. They have become dependent on the chariot. And

the chariot's owner always demands cooperation in exchange for rides.

But a church that has already died---a church that has taken up its

cross and followed Jesus into death and resurrection---has nothing left

to lose. And a church with nothing left to lose is the most powerful

force on earth. Not powerful in the way Rome was powerful. Powerful in

the way the resurrection is powerful: an authority that does not come

from the sword but from the empty tomb.

Edwin Friedman, the family systems therapist, coined a phrase that has

become invaluable for understanding leadership in anxious times:

"non-anxious presence." The idea is that in a system flooded with

anxiety---a family, an organization, a nation---the most transformative

thing a leader can do is refuse to be governed by the anxiety. Not to

ignore it. Not to dismiss it. But to be present in the middle of it

without being controlled by it.

That is what the church is called to be in America right now: a

non-anxious presence. Not indifferent to the crises around us. Not naive

about the real threats and real suffering in our culture. But refusing

to let anxiety set the agenda. Refusing to let fear rewrite our

theology. Refusing to let the temperature of the political moment

determine the posture of the people of God.

The anxious church grasps for power. The non-anxious church extends open

hands.

The anxious church demonizes its enemies. The non-anxious church prays

for them.

The anxious church measures its health by political influence. The

non-anxious church measures its health by faithfulness to Christ.

The anxious church asks, "Are we winning?" The non-anxious church asks,

"Are we faithful?"

Cross-shaped courage means being willing to lose the chariot in order to

keep the King. It means choosing the towel over the sword, the cross

over the throne, the way of Jesus over the way of the world---even when

the way of the world looks like it is winning and the way of Jesus looks

like it is losing.

Because the way of Jesus always looks like it is losing right up until

Sunday morning. And then everything changes.

**Which Messiah Are You Following?**

Let me close this chapter with a question that is meant to be

uncomfortable.

Which Messiah are you actually following?

Not which Messiah do you say you follow. Not which Messiah appears on

your doctrinal statement or your church's website. Which Messiah governs

your actual behavior, your actual emotional life, your actual response

to the political moment you are living in?

If the Messiah you are following promises political victory, cultural

dominance, and the defeat of your enemies through the exercise of

power---that is not Jesus. That is the messiah the crowd wanted. That is

the messiah Peter demanded. That is the messiah Satan offered in the

wilderness.

If the Messiah you are following calls you to suffer for righteousness

rather than seize power for convenience; to love your enemies rather

than destroy them; to speak truth without cruelty; to pursue justice

without becoming unjust in the process; to hold political engagement

with open hands and ultimate trust in closed hands around the

cross---that is the Jesus of the Gospels. That is the Messiah who was

rejected, crucified, and raised on the third day.

The first messiah fills arenas. The second fills tombs---and then

empties them.

The first messiah offers you the kingdoms of the world. The second

offers you a cross---and then, through the cross, gives you the

universe.

The first messiah makes you feel powerful. The second makes you free.

The question is not whether you believe in Jesus. The question is which

Jesus you believe in. And the answer is revealed not by your theology

but by your behavior---by what you are willing to lose, what you are

willing to suffer, and who you become when you do not get what you want.

If losing a political battle destroys your faith, your faith was in the

wrong messiah.

If being marginalized makes you cruel, you have been following the wrong

king.

If the only future you can imagine for the church requires political

power, you have been worshiping at the wrong throne.

The true Messiah does not need your political project to succeed. He

needs your faithfulness. And faithfulness looks like a cross---not a

crown.

At least, not yet.

**\*Key Takeaways\***

> *• The crowd in John 6 wanted to make Jesus king by force; He withdrew because they wanted sovereignty without surrender, power without a cross. • Satan's wilderness temptation was a theological proposition: political authority without suffering. Jesus refused the shortcut because the means of victory matter to God. • First-century Israel rejected Jesus not because they hated God but because He did not match their expectation of a politically triumphant Messiah. • The "Triumphal Entry" reveals the distance between human expectation and divine intention: the crowd celebrated while Jesus wept, because they did not know the things that make for peace. • Peter's rebuke of Jesus and Jesus' response ("Get behind me, Satan") reveals that demanding a theology of power over suffering is satanic in origin---even when it comes from a sincere disciple. • Jesus confronts evil fiercely but never coercively---He absorbs evil on the cross rather than retaliating, and the resurrection vindicates this path. • Cross-shaped courage means the church is free from the tyranny of outcomes, able to speak truth without fear because its future is secured by resurrection, not elections. • The question is not whether you believe in Jesus but which Jesus you believe in---the one who offers a crown without a cross, or the one who offers a cross that leads to a crown.*

**CHAPTER FOUR**

*"Jesus Is Lord"---The Most Dangerous Sentence in the Empire*

> *"To confess 'Jesus is Lord' was not a private spiritual sentiment. It was a public act of political defiance."*

• • •

**The Sentence That Could Kill You**

In the modern American church, the phrase "Jesus is Lord" is the most

familiar and least understood confession in all of Christianity.

We say it in worship songs. We print it on coffee mugs. We embroider it

on throw pillows. We post it on social media with sunset backgrounds and

curated fonts. It appears on bumper stickers and T-shirts and church

marquees. It is so ubiquitous, so comfortable, so thoroughly

domesticated that it has lost virtually all of its original power.

And that loss of power is not accidental. It is, in fact, the most

successful act of theological neutralization in the history of the

church.

Because in its original context, "Jesus is Lord" was not a worship

lyric. It was not a devotional sentiment. It was not a statement of

personal spiritual preference. It was a political claim that could---and

did---get you killed.

To understand why, you need to understand the world in which the

earliest Christians lived. You need to understand what the word *Lord*

meant before it became a hymnal cliché. And you need to understand what

it cost to say it.

In the first-century Roman Empire, there was one sentence that every

citizen, every subject, every conquered people was expected to affirm.

It was not a request. It was the price of participation in Roman

society. And the sentence was this:

> *"Kaiser kyrios." Caesar is Lord.*

This was not merely a political statement, though it was certainly that.

It was a theological claim. The Roman emperor was not simply a political

ruler. He was *divus*---divine. Beginning with Augustus, the imperial

cult presented the emperor as a god, or at least as the son of a god,

worthy of worship, offering, and absolute allegiance. Temples were built

to the emperor. Festivals celebrated his divine status. Incense was

burned before his image. The phrase *Kaiser kyrios* was the loyalty oath

of the empire---the verbal act of submission that marked you as a good

Roman, a loyal subject, a person who knew your place in the cosmic

order.

And into that world, the earliest Christians walked with a

counter-confession that constituted treason:

> *"Iesous kyrios." Jesus is Lord.*

Same word. Same grammatical construction. Directly and deliberately

contradictory. To say "Jesus is Lord" in the first century was to say,

simultaneously and necessarily, "Caesar is *not* Lord." It was not a

statement about your personal devotional life. It was a public

declaration that the ultimate authority in the universe was not the man

on the throne in Rome but the man on the cross outside Jerusalem---and

that this crucified man had been raised from the dead and was now seated

at the right hand of God, ruling over every principality and power,

including the one that thought it ruled the world.

That is what the earliest Christians meant when they said "Jesus is

Lord." And the Roman Empire understood perfectly.

**Treason in Thessalonica: Acts 17 and the Political Gospel**

The book of Acts gives us a window into exactly how the Roman world

heard the early Christian message. And what they heard was not a private

religion. They heard a political revolution.

In Acts 17, Paul and Silas arrive in Thessalonica. Paul does what he

always does: he goes to the synagogue and reasons from the Scriptures,

explaining that the Messiah had to suffer, die, and rise again, and that

"this Jesus, whom I proclaim to you, is the Christ" (Acts 17:3). Some

Jews are persuaded. Some Greeks are persuaded. And a mob forms.

The opponents drag some of the new believers before the city

authorities. And the accusation they bring is one of the most revealing

sentences in the entire New Testament:

> *"These men who have turned the world upside down have come here also, and Jason has received them, and they are all acting against the decrees of Caesar, saying that there is another king, Jesus." ---Acts 17:6--7*

Read that accusation carefully. The opponents of the gospel do not say,

"These men are teaching a new philosophy." They do not say, "These men

have an interesting private spirituality." They say, "These men are

acting against the decrees of Caesar" and "saying that there is another

king."

They understood the gospel perfectly. Better, in fact, than many modern

Christians do.

The accusation is that the early Christians are proclaiming a rival

king. Not a rival spiritual guru. Not a rival moral teacher. Not a rival

source of personal peace and inner fulfillment. A rival *king*---one

whose authority supersedes Caesar's, whose kingdom renders the Roman

Empire penultimate, whose decrees override the decrees of the most

powerful government on earth.

The phrase "turned the world upside down" is itself revealing. The Greek

word is *anastatoo*---to disturb, to unsettle, to cause political

upheaval. These are not people engaging in quiet spiritual devotion.

They are people whose message has the power to restructure the political

order. And the Roman authorities know it.

Luke, the author of Acts, records this accusation without correcting it.

He does not say, "Of course, they misunderstood---the gospel is not

political." He does not explain that the kingdom of God is purely

spiritual and has no implications for earthly power. He simply records

the accusation and moves on, because the accusation is essentially

correct. The early Christians *were* proclaiming another king. They

*were* announcing a kingdom that relativized every earthly government.

They *were* living in a way that undermined the absolute claims of the

Roman state.

The difference---and this is the critical distinction for our

moment---is in *how* they were doing it. They were not forming a

political party. They were not running candidates. They were not

lobbying for legislative influence. They were not building a voting

bloc. They were not making alliances with sympathetic Roman officials in

exchange for protection and cultural power.

They were worshiping. They were sharing meals. They were caring for the

poor. They were telling the truth about who was actually in charge. And

the empire found that more threatening than an army.

**The Hymn That Dethroned Caesar: Philippians 2**

Philippians 2:5--11 is one of the most magnificent passages in the New

Testament. It is often called the *Carmen Christi*---the Hymn of

Christ---and it may well be the oldest piece of Christian liturgy we

possess, predating even Paul's letter. It is a theological masterpiece.

And it is an act of political subversion disguised as a worship song.

To hear it the way the Philippians heard it, you need to know one thing

about Philippi: it was a Roman colony. Not just a city in the empire---a

*colonia*, a settlement of Roman military veterans who had been given

land as a reward for service. Philippi was proud of its Roman identity.

Its citizens had Roman rights. Its public life was saturated with Roman

culture, Roman law, and Roman religion---including the imperial cult. To

live in Philippi was to live surrounded by reminders that Caesar was

lord.

And into that context, Paul writes this:

> *"Have this mind among yourselves, which is yours in Christ Jesus, who, though he was in the form of God, did not count equality with God a thing to be grasped, but emptied himself, by taking the form of a servant, being born in the likeness of men. And being found in human form, he humbled himself by becoming obedient to the point of death, even death on a cross." ---Philippians 2:5--8*

This is already a radical claim. Jesus, who possessed divine equality,

did not *grasp* at it---did not use it as a tool of domination, did not

leverage it for power. Instead, He emptied Himself. He became a servant.

He submitted to the most humiliating form of execution the Roman Empire

had devised---a death reserved for slaves, rebels, and the lowest of

criminals.

But it is the next verses that would have sent a shockwave through the

Roman colony:

> *"Therefore God has highly exalted him and bestowed on him the name that is above every name, so that at the name of Jesus every knee should bow, in heaven and on earth and under the earth, and every tongue confess that Jesus Christ is Lord, to the glory of God the Father." ---Philippians 2:9--11*

*Every knee will bow. Every tongue will confess. Jesus Christ is Lord.*

In a Roman colony where every citizen was expected to bend the knee to

Caesar, Paul is announcing that there is a name higher than Caesar's. In

a city built on imperial loyalty, he is declaring that the ultimate

confession of the universe is not *Kaiser kyrios* but Iesous Christos

kyrios. In a culture that exalted power, military conquest, and

political domination, Paul says the one who was exalted above all things

is the one who *emptied Himself* and *became a servant* and died on a

cross.

This is not merely theology. It is a complete inversion of the Roman

value system. Rome said greatness comes through power. Paul says

greatness comes through service. Rome said the path to lordship is

domination. Paul says the path to lordship is self-emptying. Rome said

the cross is the ultimate shame---the mark of defeat, powerlessness, and

subhumanity. Paul says the cross is the doorway to the highest

exaltation in the cosmos.

And the claim that every knee will bow---not just willing knees, not

just believing knees, but *every* knee, in heaven and on earth and under

the earth---is a direct challenge to the totality of Caesar's claims.

Caesar demanded universal allegiance. Paul says someone else has it.

Caesar claimed divine authority. Paul says the truly divine one chose

humiliation. Caesar built his empire on the sword. Paul says the true

kingdom was built on a cross.

The Philippian Christians would have heard this not as abstract theology

but as a manifesto. They would have understood, with perfect clarity,

that to sing this hymn was to commit an act of allegiance that put them

at odds with the most powerful empire on earth. And they sang it anyway.

**The True King of Kings: Revelation's Counter-Imperial Vision**

If Acts shows how the Roman world *heard* the Christian confession, and

Philippians shows how Christians *sang* it, then Revelation shows how

they *hoped* in it---especially under the pressure of active

persecution.

The book of Revelation was written during a period of intense conflict

between the early church and the Roman state. The precise date is

debated, but the political reality is clear: Christians were being

pressured---sometimes by social exclusion, sometimes by economic

penalty, sometimes by imprisonment and death---to participate in the

imperial cult. To burn incense before the emperor's image. To say

*Kaiser kyrios*. And they were refusing.

Into that context, John writes an apocalyptic vision that is, among many

other things, the most sustained piece of political resistance

literature in the Bible. And at its climax, John sees the true ruler of

the world:

> *"Then I saw heaven opened, and behold, a white horse! The one sitting on it is called Faithful and True, and in righteousness he judges and makes war... On his robe and on his thigh he has a name written, King of kings and Lord of lords." ---Revelation 19:11, 16*

*King of kings. Lord of lords.*

These were not original phrases. They were imperial titles. The Roman

emperor styled himself as the supreme ruler over all lesser kings and

lords. Revelation takes those titles and reassigns them---not to the man

on the throne in Rome, but to the Lamb who was slain. The entire book is

structured as a counter-narrative to imperial propaganda. Where Rome

says, "We bring peace through military conquest," Revelation says the

true peace comes through a slaughtered Lamb. Where Rome says, "The

emperor is the savior of the world," Revelation says the savior was

crucified by the empire. Where Rome says, "Our kingdom will last

forever," Revelation says Babylon---Rome's code name---will fall.

But here is what makes Revelation so important for our discussion: the

weapon the King of Kings carries is not a sword wielded in violence. It

is a sword proceeding from His mouth---His word, His truth, His

declaration of how things really are. The conquering is not done through

military force or political maneuvering. It is done through the

proclamation of truth and the faithfulness of the saints who refuse to

bow to the beast, even when it costs them everything.

The martyrs in Revelation conquer through their testimony and through

the blood of the Lamb (Revelation 12:11). They overcome not by seizing

political power but by refusing to surrender their confession. They win

not by electing a friendlier emperor but by maintaining their allegiance

to a different King. Their political action is worship. Their resistance

is faithfulness. Their revolution is the refusal to say "Caesar is lord"

even when saying "Jesus is Lord" means death.

That is the political vision of the New Testament. And it looks nothing

like what American Christianity has become.

**How the Most Dangerous Sentence Became the Safest**

So how did we get from there to here? How did "Jesus is Lord"---a

confession that overthrew empires, sustained martyrs, and functioned as

the most subversive political statement in the ancient world---become a

bumper sticker?

The story is complex, but the trajectory is clear, and it begins with

Constantine.

In 313 AD, the Emperor Constantine issued the Edict of Milan, ending the

persecution of Christians and granting them legal toleration. Within a

generation, Christianity went from a persecuted minority faith to the

official religion of the Roman Empire. And everything changed.

On one level, this was an extraordinary gift. Persecution ended.

Churches could be built. The Scriptures could be copied and distributed

without fear. Theological councils could meet to clarify doctrine. The

gospel could spread without the constant threat of imprisonment or

death.

But on another level, something was lost---something that the church has

never fully recovered. When Christianity became the religion of the

empire, the confession "Jesus is Lord" lost its edge. Because when

Caesar himself claims to be a Christian, saying "Jesus is Lord" no

longer implies "Caesar is not." The confession is no longer a

counter-claim. It is a reinforcement of the existing order. Jesus is

Lord *and* Caesar is His representative. The kingdom of God *and* the

kingdom of Rome are on the same side. The cross *and* the sword serve

the same master.

This is what historians call the Constantinian settlement, and its

effects have shaped Western Christianity for seventeen centuries. The

church gained access to power, protection, and cultural influence. In

exchange, it gradually lost the ability to speak prophetically to

power---because it was *in* power. It lost the ability to critique the

empire---because it was *the empire's religion*. It lost the ability to

say "Caesar is not lord"---because Caesar was sitting in the front pew.

The domestication deepened over centuries. In medieval Christendom, the

church and the state became so thoroughly intertwined that it was

virtually impossible to distinguish between them. The pope crowned

emperors. Emperors enforced theological orthodoxy. The sword served the

altar, and the altar blessed the sword. "Jesus is Lord" no longer

functioned as a claim that relativized all earthly power. It functioned

as the religious foundation *for* earthly power.

The Reformation challenged aspects of this arrangement but did not

overthrow it. Luther depended on German princes for protection. Calvin

shaped civic structures in Geneva. The Church of England was a state

church by design. Reformation Christianity, for all its theological

radicalism, remained largely Constantinian in its understanding of the

relationship between church and political power.

And when European Christians came to America, they brought the

Constantinian instinct with them. The specific arrangements varied---the

First Amendment formally separated church and state in ways that were

genuinely revolutionary. But the cultural assumption persisted: America

is a Christian nation. Our government reflects Christian values. Our

laws are built on Christian foundations. "Jesus is Lord" means "America

is blessed." The confession no longer challenges the nation. It baptizes

it.

That is how we arrived at our current moment---where "Jesus is Lord" can

appear on the same flag as a political candidate's name without anyone

sensing a contradiction. Where a confession that once meant "I will die

before I grant ultimate allegiance to any earthly ruler" now means "My

preferred candidate shares my values." Where the most subversive

sentence in the history of the world has been reduced to a tribal

identifier.

The domestication is complete. And it is killing us.

**What We Lost When We Tamed the Confession**

When "Jesus is Lord" stopped meaning "Caesar is not," we lost several

things that the American church desperately needs to recover.

**We lost the ability to critique our own nation prophetically.** If

"Jesus is Lord" and "America is a Christian nation" are functionally the

same confession, then criticizing America feels like criticizing Jesus.

Patriotism and discipleship merge. National interest and Kingdom

interest become indistinguishable. And the prophetic tradition that runs

from Moses to Amos to Jesus---the tradition that says "Thus says the

Lord" to the nation's face, calling it to repentance---becomes

impossible. You cannot prophetically challenge a system you have

sacralized.

The Old Testament prophets stood *within* Israel but *against* its

unfaithfulness. They loved the nation precisely by refusing to bless its

sins. They were patriots of the deepest kind---patriots who cared more

about the nation's soul than its comfort, more about its covenant

faithfulness than its military success. But their patriotism was

subordinated to their loyalty to God. When the two conflicted, God won.

Always.

A domesticated church cannot do this. A church that has identified the

Kingdom of God with the American project cannot prophetically challenge

the American project without feeling like it is challenging the Kingdom

of God. And so it remains silent when it should speak, compliant when it

should resist, comfortable when it should be grieved.

**We lost the independence that makes the church's voice credible.** The

early church's moral authority came precisely from its independence.

When Christians cared for plague victims while Roman elites fled the

cities, the world noticed---not because the church was in power, but

because it acted with sacrificial love while *out* of power. When

Christians refused to participate in the imperial cult even under threat

of death, their testimony carried weight---because there was obviously

nothing to be gained by it except suffering.

A church that is aligned with political power loses this credibility.

When Christians speak about justice while holding the levers of cultural

influence, the world hears self-interest disguised as righteousness.

When the church advocates for policies that happen to benefit its own

position, even genuinely good policies lose their moral force because

the advocacy looks strategic rather than sacrificial. The church's voice

is most powerful when it has the least to gain from being heard.

We lost the freedom to hold all political arrangements with open

hands. The early church survived---and often thrived---under emperors

who were hostile, indifferent, or friendly. It did not depend on the

political environment for its vitality. It could function under Nero,

under Marcus Aurelius, under Diocletian, under Constantine. Its life was

not contingent on having the right person in power, because its life

came from the resurrected Christ, not from the sitting emperor.

A domesticated church has lost this flexibility. It has become so

intertwined with a particular political arrangement that the loss of

that arrangement feels like the loss of the faith itself. When cultural

influence wanes, the church panics---not because the gospel is

threatened (the gospel cannot be threatened by an election), but because

the particular political environment in which the church is comfortable

is changing. And the panic reveals the depth of the dependency.

**We lost the scandal of the cross as a political statement.** In the

first century, saying "Jesus is Lord" was scandalous because it claimed

that ultimate authority belonged to a crucified man---not a military

conqueror, not a political genius, not a wielder of coercive force, but

someone Rome had executed as a criminal. The cross was the empire's

ultimate statement of power: "We can do this to you." And the

resurrection was God's ultimate counter-statement: "Yes, you can. And it

does not matter."

When "Jesus is Lord" is domesticated, the scandal of the cross

disappears. The crucified Lord becomes a mascot for cultural power. The

one who was executed by the state is reimagined as the patron saint of

the state. The one who refused political power is invoked to legitimate

political power. And the fundamental meaning of the confession---that

God's power is made perfect in weakness, that the last shall be first,

that the way up is the way down---is quietly discarded in favor of a

Jesus who looks a lot like Caesar with a cross necklace.

**Recovering the Subversive Edge Without the Culture War Posture**

So how do we recover the original power of "Jesus is Lord" without

turning it into another weapon in the culture wars? How do we restore

the confession's subversive edge without adopting the very posture of

power that the confession was meant to overthrow?

This is the question, and it requires extraordinary care. Because the

danger on one side is a domesticated faith that challenges nothing. And

the danger on the other side is a weaponized faith that challenges

everything except itself.

The recovery begins with understanding what *kind* of Lord Jesus is. And

the Philippians hymn gives us the answer: He is the Lord who emptied

Himself*. He is the Lord who* took the form of a servant. He is the

Lord who *humbled Himself to the point of death*. He is the Lord who

received the name above every name not by seizing power but by

surrendering it. Not by climbing to the top but by descending to the

bottom. Not by crushing His enemies but by being crushed for them.

This means that when we confess "Jesus is Lord," we are not merely

saying that Jesus has authority. We are saying that authority *itself*

has been redefined. The nature of lordship has been transformed by the

cross. Power that dominates is not the Lord's kind of power. Authority

that coerces is not the Lord's kind of authority. A kingdom that

requires the weapons of the world to advance is not the Lord's kind of

kingdom.

To confess "Jesus is Lord" is therefore to commit yourself to a

particular *way* of engaging the world---a way marked by service rather

than domination, by truth-telling rather than propaganda, by enemy-love

rather than enemy-destruction, by willingness to suffer rather than

willingness to inflict suffering.

That means the recovery of the confession is not accomplished by being

louder, angrier, or more politically aggressive. It is not accomplished

by "taking back" culture or "reclaiming" institutions or "fighting" for

influence. All of those strategies assume that the way to confess Jesus

as Lord is to exercise the kind of lordship Caesar exercised---to

dominate, to control, to win. But the confession itself declares that

the Lord conquered by being conquered. He won by losing. He reigned by

serving.

The subversive power of "Jesus is Lord" is recovered when the church

lives so differently from the surrounding culture that people have to

ask why. When Christians love their enemies so conspicuously that it

baffles the tribalized world. When the church serves the poor so

lavishly that secular observers cannot explain it by self-interest. When

believers speak truth so consistently---to both political parties, to

both sides of every cultural divide---that no faction can claim them.

The early church did not conquer Rome by outmaneuvering it politically.

It conquered Rome by *out-loving* it, *out-dying* it, and *out-hoping*

it. It offered a community that was more compelling than anything the

empire could provide---a community where slaves and masters ate at the

same table, where ethnic divisions were healed rather than exploited,

where the sick and abandoned were cared for rather than discarded. The

confession "Jesus is Lord" was credible because the community that

confessed it was visibly different from the world that rejected it.

That is the recovery we need. Not a church that confesses Jesus is Lord

and then acts like Caesar is the model. A church that confesses Jesus is

Lord and then acts like *Jesus* is the model---which means service,

sacrifice, truth, and love, even when those things bring no political

advantage whatsoever.

**The Confession That Costs Something**

In the early church, to confess "Jesus is Lord" was to accept a cost. It

could mean social exclusion---losing your place in the guild, losing

business relationships, being shut out of civic life. It could mean

economic loss---forfeiting the commercial advantages that came with

participating in the imperial cult. It could mean imprisonment. It could

mean death.

The early Christians did not confess "Jesus is Lord" because it was

convenient or because it aligned with their cultural identity or because

it reinforced their political preferences. They confessed it because it

was true. And they were willing to pay whatever price that truth

demanded.

Now consider the American context. What does it cost to say "Jesus is

Lord" in the contemporary United States?

In most settings, the honest answer is: nothing. It costs nothing to say

"Jesus is Lord" in a church that already believes it. It costs nothing

to post it on social media to an audience that already agrees with you.

It costs nothing to wear it on a T-shirt in a culture where

Christianity, though declining in influence, is still the dominant

religious tradition.

But here is where the confession begins to cost something again: When it

means refusing to grant ultimate allegiance to any political movement.

When it means declining to treat a candidate as a savior. When it means

speaking truth that your own party does not want to hear. When it means

caring for people your tribe has labeled as enemies. When it means

losing relationships because you will not subordinate your confession to

your politics.

The confession costs something when it actually functions as it was

designed to function---as a declaration that relativizes every other

claim to ultimate authority.

If "Jesus is Lord" does not challenge your politics, it is not

functioning. If it only challenges the *other side's* politics, it is

being weaponized, not confessed. If it sits comfortably alongside your

partisan loyalty without creating any tension, then it has been

domesticated---tamed and declawed and turned into a mascot for something

that is not the Kingdom of God.

The test of whether you are truly confessing "Jesus is Lord" is not

whether you say the words. The test is where the confession creates

friction. Because if Jesus is actually Lord---if His authority actually

supersedes every earthly power---then His lordship will inevitably

conflict with your preferred political arrangement at some point. It

will demand something of you that your party does not demand. It will

prohibit something that your movement celebrates. It will call you to

love someone your side has decided to hate.

If your confession of Jesus' lordship never creates friction with your

political identity, one of two things is true: either you have perfectly

aligned your politics with the Kingdom of God (which no one in history

has managed to do), or you have subordinated the lordship of Christ to

the lordship of your political tribe.

The early Christians faced a choice: Caesar or Jesus. The choice was

clear, the cost was high, and they chose Jesus.

The American church faces a more subtle version of the same choice. Not

Caesar or Jesus---but a particular vision of America, or Jesus. A

particular political movement, or Jesus. A particular cultural

arrangement that feels safe and familiar and *right*, or the crucified

and risen Lord who transcends every culture, every nation, and every

political project.

The choice should be just as clear. But it is not. Because we have been

saying "Jesus is Lord" for so long without it costing us anything that

we have forgotten what the words mean.

It is time to remember.

It is time to say "Jesus is Lord" and mean it---not as a tribal slogan,

not as a political weapon, not as a comfortable affirmation of the

status quo, but as the most radical, dangerous, costly, and hopeful

sentence a human being can utter.

It is time to confess that the crucified carpenter from Nazareth is the

rightful King of the universe---and then to let that confession reshape

everything: our politics, our allegiances, our loves, our enemies, our

hopes, and our willingness to suffer for the truth.

Because if Jesus is truly Lord, then nothing else can be. Not America.

Not a party. Not a candidate. Not a movement. Not an ideology.

And if nothing else can be Lord, then you are free. Free from the

tyranny of political tribalism. Free from the fear that the wrong

election will undo the Kingdom of God. Free from the desperate need to

control the levers of power in order to feel safe.

You are free because the Lord you serve does not need your political

project to succeed. He has already conquered. He has already been

exalted. He has already been given the name above every name. And every

knee will bow---not because the church amassed enough political capital

to force compliance, but because the crucified King has been raised from

the dead and is seated at the right hand of God.

That is the confession. It cost the early church everything. It should

cost us something too.

**\*Key Takeaways\***

> *• "Jesus is Lord" was not a private devotional sentiment in the first century---it was a direct, deliberate counter-confession to "Caesar is Lord," and it could get you killed. • Acts 17:6--7 reveals that the Roman world heard the gospel as a political claim: "They are saying there is another king, Jesus." The opponents of the gospel understood its implications better than many modern Christians do. • Philippians 2:5--11, written to a Roman colony saturated with imperial cult, declares that the name above every name belongs not to Caesar but to the crucified servant---inverting the entire Roman value system. • Revelation reassigns the imperial titles "King of kings" and "Lord of lords" to the slaughtered Lamb, and the saints conquer not by seizing power but by maintaining their confession even unto death. • The Constantinian settlement---when Christianity became the empire's religion---domesticated the confession by making "Jesus is Lord" compatible with, rather than subversive of, earthly political power. • We lost four critical things when the confession was tamed: prophetic critique of our own nation, independence that makes the church's voice credible, flexibility to thrive under any political arrangement, and the scandal of the cross as a political statement. • Recovery does not come through louder culture war but through living so differently that the confession becomes visible---through service, enemy-love, truth-telling, and willingness to suffer without political advantage. • The test of whether you truly confess "Jesus is Lord" is not whether you say the words but whether the confession creates friction with your political identity.*

**CHAPTER FIVE**

*Civil Religion: When a Nation Borrows Sacred Words*

> *"The most dangerous idol is the one that looks like worship."*

• • •

**The Prayer That Feels Like Scripture**

There is a moment in the life of nearly every American church that

reveals, with almost surgical precision, how deeply national identity

has been woven into the fabric of Christian worship. It usually happens

on the Sunday nearest the Fourth of July, or on the Sunday before

Memorial Day, or sometimes on the Sunday after a national tragedy. The

sanctuary is decorated with flags. The worship set includes a patriotic

hymn. Someone reads a passage about God's faithfulness to "this nation."

A veteran is honored. A prayer is offered for "our troops" and "our

leaders" and "our way of life."

And in that moment, something happens that most people in the room do

not notice. The line between worship of God and celebration of nation

becomes invisible. The emotional register of the room---the reverence,

the gratitude, the sense of being in the presence of something

sacred---is identical whether the congregation is singing "Holy, Holy,

Holy" or "God Bless America." The tears that fall during a patriotic

tribute feel the same as the tears that fall during communion. The lump

in the throat when the flag is presented feels the same as the lump in

the throat when the cross is lifted.

That emotional fusion---that seamless blending of national feeling and

spiritual devotion---is not an accident. It is the product of something

that has been growing in American culture for centuries. And it has a

name.

Sociologists call it *civil religion.*

And if you want to understand how patriotism became a practical savior

for millions of American Christians---if you want to understand how "God

Bless America" quietly replaced "Thy Kingdom Come" as the functional

prayer of the church---you have to understand what civil religion is,

where it came from, and why it is so spiritually lethal precisely

because it feels so spiritually natural.

**What Civil Religion Is---and What It Is Not**

The term "civil religion" was given its modern form by sociologist

Robert Bellah in a landmark 1967 essay, "Civil Religion in America."

Bellah observed that alongside the formal separation of church and state

enshrined in the First Amendment, America had developed its own

quasi-religious national narrative---a set of beliefs, symbols, rituals,

and sacred texts that functioned like a religion even though they were

not housed in any particular church or denomination.

This national religion had its own sacred history: the founding of the

nation as an act of divine providence, the pilgrims as a new Israel

crossing into a promised land, the Revolution as a righteous struggle

for God-given liberty, the Civil War as a blood sacrifice that purified

the nation, and the world wars as crusades against evil in which America

served as God's chosen instrument.

It had its own sacred texts: the Declaration of Independence, the

Constitution, the Gettysburg Address, the "I Have a Dream" speech. These

documents are treated with a reverence that, in practice, feels

canonical---not merely as important political writings but as

expressions of transcendent moral truth.

It had its own sacred rituals: the Pledge of Allegiance, the singing of

the national anthem, the observance of Memorial Day and Veterans Day,

the presidential inauguration with its hand on the Bible and its

invocation of God's blessing.

It had its own prophets and saints: Washington, Jefferson, Lincoln,

King. These figures are not merely respected as political leaders. They

are venerated as moral and spiritual exemplars---men who discerned and

enacted God's purposes for the nation.

And it had its own core conviction: that America occupies a special

place in God's plan. That this nation, more than any other, has been

blessed, guided, and commissioned by divine providence to serve as a

beacon of liberty, justice, and moral order in the world.

Now here is what makes civil religion so difficult to identify and so

dangerous to confront: most of it is not wrong. At least, not obviously

wrong.

It is not wrong to be grateful for the freedoms Americans enjoy. It is

not wrong to honor those who sacrificed to defend them. It is not wrong

to recognize the genuine influence of Christian thought on the nation's

founding documents and moral traditions. It is not wrong to pray for the

nation and its leaders. It is not wrong to love your country.

The problem is not that civil religion says false things. The problem is

that civil religion assigns sacred weight to national things. It borrows

the language, the emotions, and the categories of biblical faith and

applies them to a political entity. It takes words that belong to the

covenant between God and His people---words like "chosen," "covenant,"

"providence," "redemption," "sacrifice," "salvation"---and wraps them

around a flag.

And once those words are wrapped around a flag, the flag begins to feel

holy. The nation begins to feel covenantal. The political project begins

to feel redemptive. And any threat to the nation begins to feel like a

threat to God's kingdom itself.

That is not patriotism. That is theology. And it is a theology that the

Bible explicitly rejects.

**The Idol That Speaks the Language of Zion**

The prophets of the Old Testament had a particular horror of idolatry

that borrowed the language of Yahweh worship. They were alarmed not only

by the worship of foreign gods---the Baals and Asherahs of Canaan---but

by the subtler and far more dangerous tendency to take the vocabulary of

covenant faithfulness and attach it to something other than God.

Isaiah confronted this directly:

> *"I am the Lord; that is my name; my glory I give to no other, nor my praise to carved idols." ---Isaiah 42:8*

God's jealousy is not petty. It is protective. He refuses to share His

glory because He knows what happens when His people assign sacred status

to created things: the created thing begins to demand what only the

Creator can rightly receive. It begins to demand ultimate allegiance.

Ultimate trust. Ultimate devotion. And the people who give it end up

shaped by the idol rather than by the God whose language the idol has

borrowed.

This is precisely what civil religion does. It does not present itself

as a rival to Christianity. It presents itself as Christianity's

ally---its political arm, its cultural expression, its patriotic face.

It speaks the language of Zion. It invokes the name of God. It quotes

Scripture. It prays publicly. It declares that this nation is "under

God."

And because it sounds like faith, it is almost impossible for many

Christians to recognize it as a competitor to faith.

But consider what civil religion actually teaches, when you strip away

the reverent tone and examine the functional theology:

It teaches that America has a covenant with God---a special, binding

relationship in which God has chosen this nation for a unique redemptive

purpose. But no New Testament text assigns covenantal election to any

modern nation-state. The people of God in the New Testament are not

defined by a border but by baptism, not by citizenship but by union with

Christ. The covenant community is the church---composed of every tribe,

tongue, people, and nation---not any single country, no matter how

historically significant.

It teaches that America's founding was an act of divine providence

comparable to the Exodus. But the founding of the United States, however

remarkable, was a political event carried out by fallen human beings

with mixed motives, many of whom enslaved other human beings while

writing documents about liberty. To narrate the founding as a sacred

event on par with God's redemptive acts in Scripture is to confuse

political history with salvation history---and to render invisible the

victims of the very "providence" being celebrated.

It teaches that national decline is equivalent to spiritual

apostasy---that when America loses cultural influence, moral consensus,

or global standing, something has gone wrong in the spiritual realm. But

the Kingdom of God has never been contingent on the health of any

particular nation. The gospel flourished when Rome collapsed. The church

grew under Mao. Christianity exploded in the Global South precisely as

it declined in Western Europe. National decline is not apostasy. It may,

in fact, be the very soil in which the church rediscovers its dependence

on God rather than on empire.

It teaches that defending the nation is functionally equivalent to

defending the faith. But the faith does not need a nation to defend it.

The faith has a Defender. His name is Jesus. And He has never needed a

military budget, a Supreme Court, or a congressional majority to

preserve His church. He told Peter, "On this rock I will build my

church, and the gates of hell shall not prevail against it" (Matthew

16:18). He did not say, "On this constitution I will build my nation,

and the gates of secularism shall not prevail against it."

When civil religion teaches these things---and it teaches them not

through formal catechesis but through hymns, holidays, political

rhetoric, and the slow accumulation of cultural habit---it creates a

functional theology that competes with the gospel. It does not announce

itself as a rival religion. It wraps itself in the language of the faith

and presents itself as the faith's most loyal friend. And that is what

makes it so devastatingly effective.

Because the idol that speaks the language of Zion is the idol you never

think to question.

**America Is Not Israel**

This needs to be said plainly, because it is the theological fault line

beneath the entire civil religion edifice: America is not Israel.

The United States of America does not occupy a redemptive-historical

role analogous to ancient Israel. It has not received a divine covenant.

It has not been promised a land by direct revelation. It is not the

vehicle through which God is bringing salvation to the world. It is a

nation---one of many nations---governed by common grace, subject to

common judgment, and populated by citizens who bear the image of God no

more and no less than citizens of Brazil, Nigeria, South Korea, or any

other country on earth.

This is not anti-American sentiment. It is basic Christian theology. And

the failure to affirm it has produced an extraordinary distortion in

American Christianity---a distortion in which the fate of the nation is

emotionally and theologically conflated with the fate of the church.

The confusion runs deep, and it has deep roots. The Puritans who settled

New England did, in fact, use explicitly covenantal language to describe

their project. John Winthrop's famous "City on a Hill" sermon drew

directly from the rhetoric of Israel's covenant with God, presenting the

Massachusetts Bay Colony as a new Israel entering a new promised land

with a sacred obligation to live faithfully before God and the watching

world.

That language was powerful. It was also, in important ways,

theologically mistaken.

Winthrop's use of covenant language was not unique to him; it was part

of a broader Puritan conviction that God makes national covenants with

particular peoples. And that conviction has had an extraordinarily long

life in American Christianity. It echoes through the rhetoric of

manifest destiny, through the abolitionist movement, through the civil

rights era, through the religious right, and through contemporary

Christian nationalism. The specific content has changed. The structural

claim has not: America has a special relationship with God, and that

relationship carries obligations and blessings that are analogous to

Israel's covenant.

But the New Testament does not support this claim. The New Testament is

remarkably uninterested in the spiritual status of nations qua nations.

Jesus does not establish a national covenant. Paul does not assign

covenantal election to Rome or Greece or any other political entity. The

book of Acts narrates the gospel crossing every border without

sacralizing any of them. The epistles address churches---not countries.

And Revelation, far from depicting any nation as God's chosen vessel,

portrays all earthly empires as penultimate at best and beastly at

worst.

Peter, writing to scattered Christians across Asia Minor, uses language

that is directly relevant to this discussion:

> *"But you are a chosen race, a royal priesthood, a holy nation, a people for his own possession, that you may proclaim the excellencies of him who called you out of darkness into his marvelous light." ---1 Peter 2:9*

Notice who the "holy nation" is. It is not Rome. It is not Israel in its

political form. It is the church---the community of believers called out

of every tribe, tongue, and people. The covenantal language that was

once applied to ethnic Israel has been transferred, in the New

Testament, to the multi-ethnic, transnational body of Christ. The

"chosen race" is not a race at all in the biological or national sense.

It is a community constituted by baptism, faith, and the indwelling of

the Holy Spirit.

Peter then adds a phrase that should function as a check on every form

of civil religion:

> *"Beloved, I urge you as sojourners and exiles to abstain from the passions of the flesh, which wage war against your soul." ---1 Peter 2:11*

*Sojourners and exiles.* Not citizens in the ultimate sense. Not people

who are at home in any earthly nation. Residents, yes. Participants,

yes. Tax-payers and neighbor-lovers and civic contributors, yes. But not

people whose deepest identity is national. Not people whose ultimate

allegiance is patriotic. Sojourners---people who live in a place without

belonging to it ultimately. Exiles---people who know that their true

citizenship is elsewhere.

Paul says the same thing to the Philippians---who, remember, lived in a

Roman colony where imperial identity was everything:

> *"But our citizenship is in heaven, and from it we await a Savior, the Lord Jesus Christ." ---Philippians 3:20*

Our citizenship is in heaven. Not "in addition to" our earthly

citizenship. Not "as a spiritual supplement to" our national loyalty.

Our primary, defining, identity-shaping citizenship is in heaven. Every

other citizenship---American, Roman, British, Chinese---is secondary.

Temporary. Penultimate.

This does not mean Christians should be bad citizens. Peter and Paul

both instruct believers to honor governing authorities, to pay taxes, to

pray for rulers, and to live peaceably within the civic structures of

their time. But the posture is always one of loving participation within

a clear hierarchy of allegiance. God first. The church's mission first.

The Kingdom of Heaven first. And then---within that framework,

subordinated to that ultimate loyalty---the responsibilities of earthly

citizenship.

Civil religion inverts this hierarchy. It takes the secondary loyalty

and wraps it in the language of the primary. It makes national identity

feel covenantal. It makes patriotic devotion feel like worship. It makes

the defense of the nation feel like the defense of the faith. And once

that inversion is complete, the sojourner forgets he is a sojourner. The

exile forgets she is an exile. And the earthly city begins to feel like

the City of God.

Augustine warned against exactly this. In *The City of God,* he drew a

sharp distinction between the *civitas Dei*---the City of God---and the

*civitas terrena*---the earthly city. Both exist simultaneously. Both

make claims on the loyalty of their citizens. But they are not the same

city. And the Christian's task is to live faithfully within the earthly

city while never forgetting that it is not ultimate---that it is passing

away, that its glories are temporary, and that no earthly nation, no

matter how great, can satisfy the longing of a heart made for the

Kingdom of God.

Civil religion collapses this distinction. It tells Christians that the

earthly city and the City of God are, in America's case, essentially the

same project. And that collapse is the foundation on which Christian

nationalism is built.

**The Cross and the Flag: A Fusion That Corrupts Both**

When the cross and the flag become visually, emotionally, and

theologically inseparable, both are corrupted.

The cross is corrupted because it is reduced from a universal symbol of

God's self-giving love for the entire world to a tribal symbol of one

nation's identity. The cross of Christ is for *every* people, *every*

nation, *every* tribe, *every* tongue. It is the place where God

reconciled *the world* to Himself, not the place where God endorsed a

particular political project. When the cross is draped in a national

flag---whether literally or emotionally---it loses its universality. It

becomes the possession of one tribe. And the gospel it represents is

heard by the rest of the world not as good news for all people but as

the religious branding of a particular nation's power.

The flag is corrupted because it is elevated from a legitimate symbol of

national identity to a sacred object that demands reverence rather than

mere respect. A flag is a political symbol. It represents a nation's

history, ideals, and common life. It deserves honor. It does not deserve

worship. And when the emotional response to the flag becomes

indistinguishable from the emotional response to the cross---when

disrespecting the flag feels like blasphemy---something has gone wrong

not only theologically but patriotically. Because genuine patriotism

includes the freedom to critique, to dissent, to hold the nation

accountable to its own highest ideals. A flag that demands the reverence

of a sacred object has become an idol---and idols, by their nature,

cannot tolerate criticism.

The Exodus command is absolute:

> *"You shall have no other gods before me. You shall not make for yourself a carved image, or any likeness of anything that is in heaven above, or that is in the earth beneath, or that is in the water under the earth. You shall not bow down to them or serve them, for I the Lord your God am a jealous God." ---Exodus 20:3--5*

A "carved image" is not limited to a wooden statue. It is anything

created---any thing, any institution, any nation, any symbol---that

receives the devotion, the trust, and the emotional allegiance that

belongs to God alone. When a nation becomes the object of that kind of

devotion---when its preservation feels like the preservation of the

faith, when its enemies feel like God's enemies, when its success feels

like God's vindication---the nation has become a carved image. It has

entered the space that belongs only to the Lord. And the Lord has made

it clear that He does not share that space.

Answering the Objection: "But Wasn't America Founded on Christian

Values?"

This is the most common objection raised whenever someone challenges the

civil-religion narrative, and it deserves a careful, honest,

non-dismissive answer.

The short answer is: yes, in part. And also: no, in part. And both parts

matter.

Yes, Christian thought genuinely influenced the intellectual and moral

foundations of the American experiment. The concept of human dignity

rooted in the *imago Dei*---that every person is made in the image of

God and therefore possesses inherent, inalienable worth---shaped the

Declaration of Independence's assertion that "all men are created equal"

and are "endowed by their Creator with certain unalienable Rights." The

Reformation's emphasis on the authority of conscience before God

contributed to the development of religious liberty. The Puritan work

ethic, covenant theology, and emphasis on education shaped the cultural

soil in which American institutions grew. To deny this influence is

historically dishonest.

But the story is more complicated than the civil-religion version

allows. The founders were not uniformly Christian. Some, like Samuel

Adams and John Jay, were devout believers. Others, like Thomas Jefferson

and Benjamin Franklin, were Deists who respected Christianity's moral

framework while rejecting core doctrines like the divinity of Christ and

the authority of Scripture. The founding documents are not Christian

confessions. They are Enlightenment-era political documents that borrow

selectively from Christian and classical sources. The God invoked in the

Declaration is "Nature's God"---a deistic concept compatible with

Christianity but not identical to the Triune God of the Nicene Creed.

More importantly, the nation that was founded on the principle that "all

men are created equal" simultaneously enslaved millions of people made

in the image of God. The nation that proclaimed "liberty and justice for

all" systematically denied liberty and justice to Indigenous peoples,

African Americans, women, and other marginalized groups for centuries.

The providential narrative of American founding---the "city on a hill"

story---is true only if you ignore the people standing in the hill's

shadow.

This is not an attack on America. It is an insistence on telling the

truth about America---the full truth, including the parts that

complicate the sacred narrative. And it is precisely the kind of

truth-telling that civil religion cannot tolerate, because civil

religion depends on a simplified, sacralized version of national history

that resists complication.

Christians, of all people, should be committed to telling the full

truth---because the God we worship is a God of truth, and because the

gospel itself is a story that refuses to hide the ugliness of human sin.

The Bible does not sanitize Israel's history. It records David's

adultery and murder. It exposes Solomon's idolatry. It narrates the

injustices of Israel's kings in unflinching detail. The prophets loved

Israel precisely by refusing to let Israel tell a flattering lie about

itself.

If American Christians cannot bring the same honesty to American

history---if we can only tell the story of the nation in hagiographic,

providential terms---then we have adopted a standard of truth lower than

the Bible's own standard. And that should concern us deeply.

So yes, Christian values influenced the founding. But the founding was

also shaped by Enlightenment rationalism, by economic self-interest, by

the brutal realities of slavery and colonialism, and by the complex,

fallen humanity of the men who designed it. Acknowledging this does not

diminish gratitude for the genuine goods the American experiment has

produced. It simply refuses to confuse a political founding with a

divine covenant.

**Healthy Patriotism vs. Covenantal Confusion**

The point of this chapter is not to destroy patriotism. It is to save

it---from the idolatry that will eventually consume it.

There is a form of patriotism that is entirely compatible with Christian

faithfulness. It looks like gratitude for the genuine goods of your

nation---its freedoms, its ideals, its opportunities---without

attributing those goods to a divine covenant that does not exist. It

looks like working for the common good of your fellow

citizens---pursuing justice, serving your neighbor, contributing to the

health of your community---without treating the nation as the vehicle of

God's redemptive purposes. It looks like loving your country the way you

love your family: deeply, honestly, with open eyes about its flaws, and

with the willingness to confront those flaws precisely because you love

it too much to let it persist in self-deception.

This patriotism is modest. It does not claim divine favoritism. It does

not treat national symbols as sacred objects. It does not panic when the

nation's cultural influence wanes, because it does not confuse cultural

influence with the Kingdom of God. It does not demand reverence---it

earns respect. And it can hold a flag in one hand and a cross in the

other without confusing which one is ultimate.

Covenantal confusion, by contrast, is patriotism that has crossed the

line into theology. It is the belief---sometimes explicit, more often

felt rather than articulated---that America's relationship with God is

structurally analogous to Israel's. That God has made promises to this

nation. That the nation's obedience brings blessing and its disobedience

brings judgment---not in the general providential sense that applies to

all nations, but in the specific, covenantal sense that applied uniquely

to Israel in the Old Testament.

This confusion produces a distinctive set of spiritual pathologies:

It makes national decline feel like covenant judgment, which produces

not repentance but rage. If America's cultural shifts are signs of

divine displeasure, then the people responsible for those shifts are not

merely political opponents---they are enemies of God. And you do not

negotiate with enemies of God. You crush them. The covenantal framework

turns political disagreement into holy war.

It makes patriotic devotion feel like spiritual obedience, which makes

criticism of the nation feel like sin. If loving America is part of

loving God, then questioning America's mythology is tantamount to

questioning God's faithfulness. Dissent becomes heresy. Prophetic

critique becomes betrayal. And the tradition of the prophets---who loved

Israel enough to tell it the truth---is silenced in the name of a

loyalty that God Himself never demanded.

It makes political engagement feel like spiritual warfare in the

ultimate sense, which strips politics of its penultimacy and loads it

with eschatological weight it cannot bear. If every election is a battle

for God's covenant with America, then every election is ultimate. Every

loss is catastrophic. Every opponent is demonic. And every compromise is

unthinkable---because you do not compromise in a holy war.

These pathologies are visible throughout the American church. They

explain the apocalyptic tone of so much Christian political rhetoric.

They explain the inability of many Christians to engage political

opponents with any semblance of love or respect. They explain the fusion

of political and spiritual identity that makes it impossible for some

believers to distinguish between a threat to their party and a threat to

their faith.

And they all flow from a single theological error: the belief that

America is Israel.

It is not. And the sooner the American church accepts that, the sooner

it can recover the freedom to be what it was always meant to be: a

sojourning people, a heavenly-citizened community, a body whose ultimate

allegiance is to a King who transcends every nation---including this

one.

**The Freedom of a Church That Belongs to No Nation**

When the church disentangles itself from civil religion---when it stops

confusing the American project with the Kingdom of God---it does not

lose something. It gains something extraordinary.

It gains the freedom to love America without worshiping it. A church

that knows the difference between patriotism and idolatry can celebrate

the genuine goods of the nation without sacralizing them, can serve the

common good without treating the nation as ultimate, and can grieve the

nation's failures without experiencing them as threats to the faith.

It gains the freedom to critique America without guilt. When national

identity and spiritual identity are fused, criticism of the nation feels

like apostasy. But when the church knows that its citizenship is in

heaven, it can bring the full weight of prophetic truth to bear on the

nation's sins---its racism, its materialism, its exploitation of the

vulnerable---without fearing that it is betraying God's covenant.

Because God's covenant is with the church, not with the nation. And the

church serves the nation best by telling it the truth.

It gains the freedom to welcome all nations. A church bound to one

nation's identity is, by definition, alienating to people from other

nations. But the church of Jesus Christ is, by design, transnational.

Its doors are open to every tribe and tongue. Its gospel is for every

people. Its Table has seats for every nation. And when the church is

visibly and joyfully free from the entanglement of civil religion, it

becomes what it was always meant to be: a preview of the Kingdom where

every nation gathers before the throne of the Lamb.

It gains the freedom to endure any political outcome without despair.

This is perhaps the most important freedom of all. A church that has

confused its identity with a nation's fortunes is a church that will be

destroyed by that nation's decline. But a church that knows it belongs

to a Kingdom that cannot be shaken---a Kingdom secured not by elections

but by resurrection---can face any political outcome with sorrow, yes,

with concern, yes, but never with despair. Because despair is only

possible when the thing you have lost is ultimate. And for the church of

Jesus Christ, no nation is ultimate.

The writer of Hebrews knew this. He was writing to a community that was

losing everything---social standing, economic security, political

protection---and he said to them:

> *"Therefore let us be grateful for receiving a kingdom that cannot be shaken, and thus let us offer to God acceptable worship, with reverence and awe." ---Hebrews 12:28*

A kingdom that cannot be shaken. Not a nation that cannot be

shaken---nations shake all the time. Not an empire that cannot

fall---empires fall regularly. A *kingdom*---God's Kingdom---that is

founded on something no political earthquake can touch.

That is the invitation. Not to stop caring about America. Not to abandon

civic responsibility. Not to retreat into apolitical passivity. But to

stop treating the American project as though it were the Kingdom of God.

To stop wrapping the cross in the flag. To stop borrowing sacred

language for national purposes. And to trust that the God who has

sustained His church through the fall of Rome, the splintering of

Christendom, the rise and fall of empires, and the upheaval of every

civilization in history is more than capable of sustaining it through

whatever happens to the United States of America.

The church does not need a nation to survive.

The church needs a King.

And it already has one.

His name is Jesus. He is Lord. And His kingdom will have no end.

Let "Thy Kingdom Come" be the prayer of the church. And let "God Bless

America" return to what it was always meant to be: a humble request, not

a theological confession.

**\*Key Takeaways\***

\- Civil religion is a quasi-religious national narrative---complete

with sacred history, sacred texts, rituals, prophets, and a core

conviction of divine favor---that borrows the language and emotions

of biblical faith and applies them to the nation.

\- The danger of civil religion is not that it says false things but

that it assigns sacred weight to national things---turning patriotic

devotion into something that feels indistinguishable from worship.

\- America is not Israel. No New Testament text assigns covenantal

election to a modern nation-state. The "holy nation" of 1 Peter 2:9

is the church, not any country.

\- Christians are "sojourners and exiles" (1 Peter 2:11) whose primary

citizenship is in heaven (Philippians 3:20)---not people whose

deepest identity is national.

\- The cross-and-flag fusion corrupts both: the cross loses its

universality and becomes a tribal symbol; the flag is elevated from

a political symbol to a sacred object that cannot tolerate critique.

\- Christian values genuinely influenced the American founding, but the

founding was also shaped by Enlightenment rationalism, economic

self-interest, and the brutal realities of slavery---and telling the

full truth is not anti-American but pro-truth.

\- Covenantal confusion---the belief that America has an Israel-like

covenant with God---produces spiritual pathologies: political

disagreement becomes holy war, national decline feels like divine

judgment, and patriotic devotion feels like obedience.

\- A church disentangled from civil religion gains extraordinary

freedom: to love without worshiping, to critique without guilt, to

welcome all nations, and to endure any political outcome without

despair.

**CHAPTER SIX**

*How Christian Nationalism Quietly Rewrites Doctrine*

> *"The most dangerous heresies are not the ones that contradict the faith openly. They are the ones that rearrange it subtly---so that everything sounds right but nothing works the way it should."*

• • •

**The Mutation No One Announced**

Christian nationalism does not arrive with a heresy trial. It does not

publish a revised creed. It does not convene a council and announce, "We

have decided to alter the faith." It does something far more effective:

it rearranges the *weight* of existing doctrines until the structure of

the faith has shifted---while all the original vocabulary remains in

place.

This is what makes it so difficult to confront and so easy to deny. If

you ask a Christian nationalist whether Jesus is Lord, they will say

yes. If you ask whether salvation is by grace through faith, they will

affirm it. If you ask whether the Bible is authoritative, they will

agree enthusiastically. The words have not changed. The doctrinal

statements have not been revised. The confessions remain on the church's

website, untouched.

But something has shifted underneath. The load-bearing walls of the

faith have been quietly relocated. And the result is a structure that

looks like historic Christianity from the outside but functions as

something else entirely on the inside.

This chapter is about that shift. It is about how Christian

nationalism---not as a conscious ideology but as an emotional and

cultural formation---quietly rewrites doctrine at multiple levels. Not

by removing doctrines from the confession but by changing which

doctrines *matter most* in the lived experience of the believer. Not by

denying the faith but by reshaping its priorities until the faith serves

a different master.

The mutation operates at four levels: eschatology, soteriology,

ecclesiology, and ethics. Each of these is a foundational pillar of

Christian theology. And each of them is being subtly but decisively

altered by the gravitational pull of nationalist anxiety.

If that sounds like an overstatement, I ask only that you read carefully

and test what follows against your own experience. Because the whole

point of a quiet mutation is that it does not feel like a mutation. It

feels like faithfulness.

**Eschatology Rewritten: When the Horizon of Hope Shrinks**

Eschatology is not merely about end-times charts and rapture theories.

At its deepest level, eschatology answers the question: Where is history

going, and what are we ultimately waiting for? It is about the shape of

Christian hope---what we believe the future holds, what we believe God

is doing in history, and what we believe will ultimately set things

right.

Historic Christian eschatology is centered on the return of Christ. The

final hope of the church is not a political arrangement, not a cultural

golden age, not a restored civilization. It is the personal, visible,

triumphant return of Jesus Christ, who will judge the living and the

dead, make all things new, and establish a Kingdom that will have no

end. That hope is what sustains the church through suffering, sustains

it through marginalization, sustains it through every form of cultural

loss---because the church's future is not contingent on history

cooperating. It is contingent on Jesus keeping His promise.

Paul captured this in Philippians 3:20: "Our citizenship is in heaven,

and from it we await a Savior, the Lord Jesus Christ." The word

"citizenship" is deliberately political. Paul is writing to a Roman

colony, to people who took enormous pride in their Roman citizenship.

And he is saying: your ultimate political identity is not Roman. It is

heavenly. Your deepest allegiance is not to the empire. It is to the

returning King.

That does not mean Christians should be indifferent to the present. The

New Testament is relentlessly practical about how believers should live

in the here and now---serving neighbors, doing justice, caring for the

poor, speaking truth. But the emotional center of gravity, the thing

that anchors the soul when everything else shakes, is the return of

Christ---not the return of a political majority.

Christian nationalism quietly rewrites this.

Under the gravitational pull of nationalist anxiety, the horizon of hope

shrinks. It contracts from the cosmic to the national, from the eternal

to the electoral. The decisive battle for the future is no longer the

return of Christ but the next election. The decisive question is no

longer "Will Jesus keep His promise?" but "Will our side win in

November?" The emotional energy that should be directed toward "Watch

and pray" gets redirected toward "Vote and panic."

This does not happen through a formal theological revision. No one

announces, "We have decided that the return of Christ is no longer the

center of our hope." It happens through emphasis, through emotional

investment, through the slow migration of urgency from one horizon to

another.

Consider the evidence. How much time does the average politically

engaged Christian spend thinking about the return of Christ compared to

the next election? How much emotional energy is directed toward eternal

hope compared to political anxiety? When was the last time you heard a

sermon on the second coming that produced more visible emotion in the

congregation than a sermon touching on politics?

The answers to those questions reveal the rewrite. When the next

election feels more urgent than the next advent---when political loss

produces more grief than spiritual complacency---eschatology has been

functionally revised. The hope has shrunk. The horizon has contracted.

And the result is a faith that cannot endure loss, because it has tied

its sense of the future to something that can be taken away in a single

Tuesday in November.

This is why fear-shaped Christianity produces believers who live in

constant emergency. If your eschatology is centered on the return of

Christ, you can suffer political loss without despair, because the

decisive event in history has already happened *(the resurrection)* and

the decisive event to come *(the return)* is guaranteed by the character

of God, not by the competence of a political movement. But if your

eschatology has been rewritten---if the decisive event is always the

next election---then every loss is catastrophic, every setback is

existential, and peace becomes impossible.

The emergency never ends. Because there is always another election.

Always another threat. Always another crisis that demands total

mobilization. And the soul that is formed by permanent emergency is a

soul that cannot rest, cannot worship without distraction, cannot trust

without visible evidence that "our side" is winning.

That is not Christian hope. That is political anxiety dressed in

eschatological clothing. And it produces a people who, as Paul warned,

are "without hope and without God in the world" (Ephesians 2:12)---not

because they have stopped believing in God, but because they have

functionally stopped hoping in Him.

**Soteriology Rewritten: When Salvation Becomes National Restoration**

Soteriology is the doctrine of salvation. It answers the most

fundamental question in all of theology: How are sinful human beings

made right with a holy God? And the historic Christian answer---the

answer that runs from Paul through Augustine through Luther through the

Reformers to the present day---is that salvation is accomplished by the

atoning work of Jesus Christ on the cross and received by grace through

faith. It is not earned. It is not legislated. It is not achieved

through cultural influence or political dominance. It is a gift, offered

to sinners who have nothing to bring except their need.

Paul stated it with crystalline clarity: "For by grace you have been

saved through faith. And this is not your own doing; it is the gift of

God, not a result of works, so that no one may boast" (Ephesians

2:8--9).

Christian nationalism does not openly contradict this. No one stands in

a pulpit and announces, "Salvation is actually by political victory."

The vocabulary of grace remains. The language of the cross persists. The

altar calls continue.

But listen carefully to the emotional language of the movement. Pay

attention not to the doctrinal statements but to the actual

rhetoric---the words that generate the most passion, the phrases that

produce the most urgency, the sentences that people repeat to each other

in moments of fear:

*"We have to save the country."*

*"This is our last chance."*

*"If we don't win, we lose everything."*

*"Our children won't have a future."*

*"We need to take our country back."*

Each of these sentences, taken individually, might express a legitimate

concern. Policies do matter. Laws do shape cultures. The welfare of the

next generation is a genuine responsibility.

But taken together, and heard as a system of meaning, something has

shifted. The *meaning* of salvation has migrated. Salvation is no longer

primarily about Christ saving sinners from the guilt and power of sin.

It is about "our team" saving the nation from cultural collapse.

Redemption is no longer a work of the Spirit through the proclaimed

gospel. It is a project of cultural control through political

mobilization.

The cross becomes a symbol of "our people" rather than the instrument of

God's atoning work for all people. Grace becomes a word we use in

worship while our actual emotional life runs on the fuel of power. The

gospel shifts from "Repent and believe" to "Panic and mobilize."

This is why Hosea remains so devastatingly relevant. *Zanah* is not

atheism. It is supplementation. "God is good, but we need Egypt too."

"Jesus saves, but we need Washington too." That is the logic. And the

logic is so reasonable, so practical, so responsible-sounding that it

never triggers the alarm systems that are designed to detect heresy.

Because it is not a heresy of denial. It is a heresy of addition. It

keeps everything and adds the one thing that changes everything: the

belief that God's salvation is insufficient without political

reinforcement.

The Reformers fought and died over the principle of solus

Christus---Christ alone. Salvation is through Christ alone. Not Christ

plus works. Not Christ plus the sacramental system. Not Christ plus

human merit. Christ alone. And now, in a tragic irony, churches that

proudly display the five solas on their walls are living as though

salvation is Christ plus the right president, Christ plus the right

Supreme Court, Christ plus the right cultural arrangement.

The sola has been quietly abandoned---not on the doctrinal statement,

but in the emotional life of the congregation. And the emotional life of

the congregation is where theology actually lives.

**Ecclesiology Rewritten: When the Church Becomes a Political Caucus**

Ecclesiology is the doctrine of the church---what the church is, what it

is for, and how it relates to the surrounding culture. And here the

mutation may be most visible, because it shows up not in abstract

theology but in the lived experience of congregational life.

The New Testament describes the church with extraordinary language. The

church is the body of Christ (1 Corinthians 12). It is the temple of the

Holy Spirit (1 Corinthians 3:16). It is the bride of Christ (Ephesians

5:25--27). It is a "holy nation" and a "royal priesthood" (1 Peter

2:9)---language that deliberately mirrors the terms God used for Old

Testament Israel but applies them to a community defined not by

ethnicity or geography but by faith in Jesus Christ.

The church is universal. It spans every nation, every language, every

culture, and every political system. Paul insists in Galatians 3:28 that

in Christ "there is neither Jew nor Greek, there is neither slave nor

free, there is no male and female, for you are all one in Christ Jesus."

The deepest identity of the church is not national. It is baptismal. The

church's unity is not found in shared citizenship but in shared

confession: *Jesus is Lord.*

Christian nationalism rewrites this. It does not deny that the church is

universal---not explicitly. But it functionally treats the American

church as the church that matters most, American Christianity as the

version of the faith that must be preserved at all costs, and the health

of American political institutions as the precondition for the health of

the global body of Christ.

The result is a church whose boundaries start to look less like baptism

and more like voter registration. Where the question "Are you one of

us?" is answered not by confession of faith but by political alignment.

Where someone who shares your theology but not your politics feels less

welcome than someone who shares your politics but could not articulate

the gospel.

I have watched this happen in real congregations. I have watched

churches where a visitor who expressed doubt about a political candidate

was treated with more suspicion than a visitor who expressed doubt about

the resurrection. I have watched congregations where the emotional bond

between members was forged more by shared political outrage than by

shared communion at the Lord's Table. I have watched small groups where

the discussion of a political controversy generated more passion, more

tears, and more self-disclosure than any discussion of Scripture ever

did.

When that happens, the church has not been destroyed. It has been

replaced. The form remains---the building, the worship service, the name

on the sign. But the substance has shifted. It is no longer the body of

Christ gathered around Word and Sacrament. It is a political caucus that

opens with a prayer.

And the most devastating consequence of this ecclesiological mutation is

the loss of the church's prophetic independence. A church that has fused

its identity with a political movement cannot prophetically critique

that movement. It cannot hold its preferred leaders accountable, because

accountability would feel like betrayal. It cannot speak truth to power,

because it has become the chaplain of power. It cannot say "Thus says

the Lord" to the nation, because it has confused the nation's interests

with the Lord's purposes.

The Old Testament prophets were able to confront Israel precisely

because they stood within the covenant community but *outside* the

political establishment. Amos was a shepherd, not a court prophet.

Jeremiah was persecuted by the very government he addressed. Elijah

confronted Ahab at enormous personal cost. Their independence was the

source of their authority. The moment the prophet becomes the king's

chaplain, the prophetic voice dies---because you cannot challenge a

system that you depend on for your position.

A church that has merged its identity with a political faction has

voluntarily surrendered the most powerful tool in its arsenal: the

freedom to say what God says, regardless of what the powerful want to

hear.

**Ethics Rewritten: When Character Becomes Optional**

The final level of mutation is perhaps the most visible and the most

damaging to the church's witness: the systematic erosion of moral

consistency in the name of political expediency.

Christian ethics---rooted in the character of God revealed in Scripture

and embodied in the life of Jesus Christ---has always insisted that

means matter as much as ends. The Bible does not merely ask *what* you

are pursuing; it asks *how* you pursue it. The good and righteous God

does not accept worship that comes through injustice (Amos 5:21--24). He

does not bless victory achieved through dishonesty (Proverbs 12:22). He

does not honor power maintained through cruelty (Micah 6:8). The method

of the work matters to the God who is Himself the standard of the work.

Paul stated this with striking directness in Romans 3:8 when he

confronted the idea that we might "do evil that good may come." His

response was not a nuanced "well, it depends on the circumstances." It

was condemnation: *"Their condemnation is just."*

Christian nationalism erodes this. Not by openly arguing that character

does not matter---almost no one would say that in plain language. But by

creating a system of moral reasoning in which character becomes

subordinate to outcomes. In which the question "Is this person honest,

kind, just, and self-controlled?" is replaced by the question "Will this

person deliver the results we need?"

The mutation works like this: First, a legitimate threat is identified.

Moral decline. Cultural hostility to Christianity. Legal threats to

religious liberty. The erosion of institutions. The threat is real. No

honest observer would deny it entirely.

Second, the threat is escalated to existential status. It is no longer

merely a concerning trend. It is the end of everything. The survival of

the church, the future of our children, the fate of civilization

itself---all of it hangs in the balance.

Third, the existential stakes justify extraordinary measures. Because

the threat is total, the response must be total. Normal moral

constraints---honesty, consistency, kindness, fairness---become luxuries

we cannot afford. We are at war. And in war, you do not stop to ask

whether your general is a gentleman. You ask whether he can win.

And so character becomes optional. Dishonesty is reframed as "fighting

back." Cruelty is reframed as "strength." The dehumanization of

opponents is reframed as "telling it like it is." Sexual misconduct is

excused because "we're not electing a pastor." Corruption is tolerated

because "at least he's on our side."

And the moral double standard becomes total. When our leader lies, it is

strategy. When their leader lies, it is corruption. When our leader is

cruel, it is strength. When their leader is cruel, it is tyranny. The

standard is no longer the character of Christ. The standard is tribal

loyalty. And tribal loyalty, once it becomes the governing ethical

principle, will excuse anything---absolutely anything---that serves the

tribe's interests.

This is not merely hypocrisy, though it certainly produces hypocrisy. It

is a doctrinal mutation at the level of ethics. It is the functional

abandonment of the principle that God cares about *how* things are done,

not merely *whether* they are accomplished. It is the replacement of

Christian moral reasoning---which holds that certain actions are wrong

regardless of the outcome---with consequentialist reasoning, which holds

that the outcome justifies the means.

And the church has been here before. Every historical moment in which

the church has aligned itself with unjust power---the Crusades, the

Inquisition, the defense of slavery, the silence during the

Holocaust---was defended by exactly this logic: the stakes are too high

for moral purity. We cannot afford to be principled right now. The ends

justify the means.

And every single time, history has delivered the same verdict: the

church that trades its moral integrity for political protection does not

preserve itself. It loses itself. And the damage takes generations to

repair.

**The Colossians Corrective: The Christ Who Holds All Things Together**

If Christian nationalism quietly dismantles the architecture of the

faith, the antidote is not merely political disengagement. The antidote

is theological reconstruction---a return to the Christ who is, as Paul

writes in Colossians, the one in whom "all things hold together"

(Colossians 1:17).

Colossians 1:15--20 is one of the most comprehensive statements about

the identity and supremacy of Jesus Christ in the entire New Testament.

And it is written to a church that is in danger of supplementing

Christ---adding other spiritual powers, other sources of security, other

mediators between themselves and God. The parallel to our situation is

striking.

Paul writes: "He is the image of the invisible God, the firstborn of

all creation. For by him all things were created, in heaven and on

earth, visible and invisible, whether thrones or dominions or rulers or

authorities---all things were created through him and for him. And he is

before all things, and in him all things hold together" (Colossians

1:15--17).

Notice the political language: thrones, dominions, rulers,

authorities. Paul is not speaking abstractly. He is naming the very

structures of political power that his readers might be tempted to fear

or to trust. And he is saying: Christ created them. Christ precedes

them. Christ sustains them. And Christ will outlast them. They are real,

but they are not ultimate. They have a place in God's ordering of the

world, but that place is subordinate to the one in whom all things hold

together.

And then the passage reaches its climax: "And he is the head of the

body, the church. He is the beginning, the firstborn from the dead, that

in everything he might be preeminent. For in him all the fullness of God

was pleased to dwell, and through him to reconcile to himself all

things, whether on earth or in heaven, making peace by the blood of his

cross" (Colossians 1:18--20).

*In everything he might be preeminent.* That is the corrective. Not

merely that Christ is important---everyone in the church will affirm

that. But that Christ is preeminent. First. Central. Supreme. Not one

voice among many. Not one source of security among several. Not one

factor in the equation alongside political power, cultural influence,

and legislative strategy. Preeminent. In *everything.*

The question Christian nationalism forces upon us is whether we actually

believe that. Not whether we can affirm it in a doctrinal statement. But

whether we live as though it is true. Whether the preeminence of Christ

is the functional operating principle of our lives---or whether it is a

theological conviction that we hold in theory while our actual

decisions, emotions, and allegiances are governed by something else.

Because if Christ is truly preeminent, then no political loss can

ultimately threaten us. If Christ truly holds all things together, then

the fraying of our preferred cultural arrangement is not the unraveling

of reality. If the fullness of God dwells in Christ, then we do not need

to find that fullness in a political movement, a cultural institution,

or a national identity.

Colossians does not say politics is irrelevant. But it says politics is

penultimate. It says the thrones and authorities that feel so powerful,

so permanent, so decisive are themselves creatures---made by Christ,

sustained by Christ, and accountable to Christ. They will serve their

purpose and pass away. And Christ will remain.

A church that has internalized the Colossians vision of Christ does not

need Christian nationalism. It does not need the nation to be

"Christian" in order to maintain its faith. It does not need political

power to feel secure. It does not need cultural dominance to be

confident. It has something better. It has a Lord in whom all things

hold together---including the things that feel like they are falling

apart.

**Answering the Objection: "You're Exaggerating"**

This is the most common objection I receive, and it deserves a

thoughtful response. Because the critic has a point: most Christians who

hold nationalist-adjacent views are not consciously rewriting doctrine.

They are not sitting in their living rooms thinking, "Today I will

revise my eschatology and subordinate my soteriology to a political

agenda." They are sincere. They love Jesus. They are afraid. And they

are trying to be responsible in a world that feels increasingly hostile

to their faith.

I do not doubt their sincerity. I question the formation.

Because the entire argument of this chapter is that doctrinal mutation

does not require conscious intent. It requires only sustained exposure

to a narrative that rearranges priorities without announcing the

rearrangement. And the narrative of Christian nationalism---that America

is uniquely chosen, that political power is the primary means of

preserving the faith, that the right leader can restore what has been

lost, that the survival of the church depends on the survival of a

particular political arrangement---is powerful enough to reshape the

soul of a believer without that believer ever realizing what has

happened.

Think of it this way. If you immerse yourself in political media for

three hours a day and Scripture for fifteen minutes, your soul is being

formed primarily by the political media---not because you have chosen to

prioritize it theologically, but because *formation follows attention.*

The narrative you spend the most time inhabiting is the narrative that

will shape your reflexes, your emotional responses, your instinctive

reactions to threat. And if that narrative is one of permanent crisis,

existential stakes, and political salvation, then your theology will

slowly, silently conform to it---even if your doctrinal statement

remains unchanged.

James K. A. Smith has argued persuasively that human beings are shaped

more by their practices than by their propositions---more by what they

do habitually than by what they believe intellectually. We are, in his

phrase, "loving animals" before we are "thinking animals," and our loves

are formed by the rituals, habits, and narratives that we inhabit every

day.

If that is true---and I believe both Scripture and experience confirm

that it is---then the question is not merely "What do you believe about

salvation?" but "What narrative is forming your affections?" Not merely

"What is your eschatology?" but "What does your emotional life reveal

about where your hope actually resides?"

So no, I am not exaggerating. I am describing a formation that is

happening in plain sight---in churches, on social media, at kitchen

tables, in the anxious prayers of believers who love God and are

terrified of what they see happening in their country. The formation is

real. Its effects are measurable. And its consequences are devastating.

But let me be equally clear: the diagnosis is not a condemnation. It is

an invitation. Because the same God who named Israel's adultery in Hosea

also invited Israel home. The same Christ who rebuked Peter for

promoting a theology of power also restored Peter after his denial. The

same Spirit who reveals our idolatry also empowers our repentance.

The mutation can be reversed. The doctrine can be recovered. The heart

can come home. But only if we are willing to name what has

happened---honestly, specifically, and without self-protective

rationalization.

**Signs Your Theology Has Been Fear-Edited**

As a practical tool for self-examination---not for condemning others,

but for honest personal inventory---consider these diagnostic questions.

They are designed not to produce guilt but to produce clarity. And

clarity, as we have said before, is the first gift of repentance.

Does the return of Christ feel less urgent to you than the next

election? If the second coming produces less emotional response in you

than a midterm result, your eschatology has been rewritten. The church

that lives in the light of Christ's return is a church that can endure

any political season. The church that lives in the shadow of the next

election is a church that will be consumed by every one.

Do you speak about "saving the country" with more passion than you

speak about the gospel saving sinners? Listen to your own language.

Not the language you use in church on Sunday morning, but the language

you use in conversation, on social media, at the dinner table. Where is

the urgency? Where is the fire? If national salvation has more emotional

weight than personal salvation, the center has shifted.

Has your view of political opponents moved from "neighbor" to "enemy"

to "subhuman"? Fear always dehumanizes. It starts by making the other

side wrong. Then it makes them dangerous. Then it makes them evil. And

finally, it makes them less than human---objects of contempt rather than

bearers of the image of God. If your political engagement has taken you

on that trajectory, fear has rewritten your anthropology. You no longer

believe, in practice, that every person is made in God's image. You

believe that the people on your side are made in God's image, and the

people on the other side are obstacles to be defeated.

Can you name a political position you hold that you adopted from your

tribe rather than derived from Scripture? This is not a trick

question. It is an honesty question. All of us have political positions

that we inherited from our cultural context rather than discovered

through careful biblical reflection. The test of spiritual maturity is

whether we are willing to hold those positions up to the light of

Scripture and allow them to be corrected---or whether we have so fused

our tribal identity with our faith that the very idea of reexamination

feels threatening.

**Would your faith survive the complete loss of political power?** This

is the ultimate question. If every law went against you. If every court

ruled contrary to your convictions. If every cultural institution

marginalized your voice. If the political project you have invested in

collapsed entirely---would your faith in Jesus Christ survive? Would

your hope endure? Would your worship continue? Would you still believe

that the tomb is empty and the King is coming? If the answer is yes,

your faith is in Christ. If the answer is uncertain, something else has

been bearing the weight that only Christ should carry.

**The Way Forward: Theological Reformation, Not Political Withdrawal**

The answer to doctrinal mutation is not political withdrawal. It is

theological reformation. The answer is not to stop caring about the

world but to start caring about it from the right center---from the

preeminence of Christ rather than the supremacy of a political project.

Reformation begins with what the Reformers themselves insisted on: a

return to Scripture as the ultimate authority. Not Scripture as filtered

through the priorities of Fox News or MSNBC. Not Scripture as curated by

a political movement. Not the verses that support your policy

preferences while the uncomfortable ones are quietly set aside.

Scripture---the whole counsel of God---read honestly, in community, with

a willingness to be corrected.

Reformation continues with the recovery of worship as the church's

primary political act. When the church gathers to confess that Jesus is

Lord, to break bread in remembrance of His death and resurrection, to

sing the story of God's faithfulness, to pray for enemies and rulers

alike---that is not a retreat from politics. It is the most subversive

political act imaginable. It is the declaration that there is a King

whose authority supersedes every earthly government, and that this

King's people are formed by His story, not by the story the culture is

telling.

Reformation means recovering the church's prophetic independence---the

freedom to speak truth to every political faction, including the one

that many of its members support. A church that cannot criticize its own

party is a church that has lost its voice. A church that reserves

prophetic speech only for the other side has become a propaganda arm,

not a prophetic community. The test of genuine prophetic courage is not

whether you can challenge your opponents. That is easy. The test is

whether you can challenge your allies. That is costly. And it is

precisely the cost that gives the prophetic word its authority.

And reformation means rebuilding the practices that form the soul around

Christ rather than around anxiety. Prayer. Scripture. Sabbath. Fasting.

Silence. Confession. Communion. These are the practices that anchor the

church's identity in something deeper than the news cycle. They are the

spiritual disciplines that create the kind of people who can engage

politics without being consumed by it---who can care about the world

without making the world their god.

The mutation is real. But the cure is available. And the cure is not a

new political strategy. It is an old theological conviction, recovered

with fresh urgency:

*In Christ all the fullness of God was pleased to dwell.* And that

fullness is enough.

**\*Key Takeaways\***

> *• Christian nationalism does not openly deny doctrine---it rearranges the weight of existing doctrines until the faith's functional center shifts from Christ to political outcomes. • Eschatology is rewritten when the horizon of hope shrinks from the return of Christ to the next election, producing believers who live in permanent emergency rather than resurrection confidence. • Soteriology is rewritten when the emotional language of salvation migrates from "Christ saves sinners" to "our side saves the nation"---supplementation, not denial. • Ecclesiology is rewritten when the church's boundaries begin to track political alignment rather than baptismal confession, and the church loses its prophetic independence. • Ethics are rewritten when character becomes subordinate to outcomes and the moral double standard of tribal loyalty replaces the consistent standard of Christ's character. • Colossians 1:15--20 provides the corrective: Christ is preeminent over every throne, dominion, ruler, and authority---they are penultimate; He alone holds all things together. • Doctrinal mutation does not require conscious intent---it requires only sustained formation by a narrative of political crisis that slowly reshapes priorities without announcing the change. • The cure is not political withdrawal but theological reformation: a return to Scripture, the recovery of worship as the church's primary political act, and the rebuilding of spiritual practices that anchor identity in Christ.*

**CHAPTER SEVEN**

*Constantine's Bargain: Protection and Its Price*

> *"The church that gains the world's protection often loses the ability to challenge the world's assumptions."*

• • •

**The Day Everything Changed**

In 313 AD, the Emperor Constantine issued the Edict of Milan, and

Christianity's relationship with political power was permanently

altered.

Before that edict, to be a Christian was to accept a cost. It meant

potential exclusion from civic life. It meant economic disadvantage. It

meant social suspicion. It could mean imprisonment. In some periods and

some places, it meant death. The early church did not grow because it

was convenient or culturally advantageous to be a Christian. It grew

because the gospel was so compelling, and the community it produced was

so visibly different, that people were willing to risk everything to be

part of it.

And then, almost overnight, the entire calculus changed.

Constantine did not merely tolerate Christianity. He favored it. He

returned confiscated property to the churches. He exempted clergy from

certain taxes and civic duties. He funded church construction. He

convened the Council of Nicaea in 325 to resolve theological

disputes---with imperial authority backing the proceedings. Within a

generation, Christianity moved from the margins to the center of Roman

life. By 380 AD, under Emperor Theodosius I, Nicene Christianity became

the official religion of the Roman Empire.

For many believers, this felt like vindication. After centuries of

suffering, God had finally delivered His people. The prayers of the

martyrs had been answered. The church had endured the furnace, and now

it was being crowned.

And in many ways, the shift was genuinely good. Persecution ended. The

Scriptures could be copied and distributed without fear. Churches could

be built openly. Theological councils could meet to clarify doctrine.

The gospel could be preached without the constant threat of violence.

Hospitals, orphanages, and charitable institutions could be established

with public support. The dignity of the human person---an idea the

church championed in a world that discarded the weak---began to reshape

Roman law.

These were not small things. They were real blessings. And anyone who

tells you that Constantine simply "ruined" Christianity in one stroke is

offering lazy history and bad theology. God is sovereign over emperors,

and the Constantinian moment produced genuine goods that the church

should acknowledge with gratitude.

But there was a price.

And the price was so subtle, so gradual, and so wrapped in the language

of blessing that it took centuries to fully understand what had been

lost.

**What Changed When the Church Gained Power**

The most important thing that changed was not a doctrine. It was a

posture.

When the church was persecuted, it had to rely on spiritual authority.

Its leaders were formed in prayer, courage, and suffering. Its community

was purified by cost. Hypocrisy was expensive---you did not join a

persecuted church for social advantage. The people who gathered for

worship in catacombs and house churches did so because they genuinely

believed that Jesus was risen, that His kingdom was real, and that His

lordship was worth more than their comfort, their careers, and their

lives.

That is a particular kind of church. It is lean. It is courageous. It is

free---free from the need to please the powerful, free from the fear of

losing cultural standing, free from the temptation to confuse the

Kingdom of God with the stability of any earthly empire.

When the church became privileged, something shifted at the deepest

level of its instincts. It began to rely not on spiritual authority but

on institutional authority. It began to assume cultural centrality. It

began to fear losing access. It began to make decisions not solely

around truth and faithfulness but around stability, influence, and the

preservation of its protected position.

This shift did not happen all at once. It happened gradually, almost

imperceptibly, the way a person who moves from a small apartment into a

mansion slowly begins to feel entitled to the space. At first, the

mansion feels like an extraordinary gift. Then it feels normal. Then it

feels necessary. And eventually, the thought of returning to the

apartment feels not just uncomfortable but threatening---as though the

apartment were a punishment rather than the place where your character

was formed.

The church's social location changed. And when your social location

changes, your instincts change with it.

A church that is out of power asks, "How do we remain faithful?" A

church that is in power asks, "How do we remain in power?" Those are

very different questions. And they produce very different communities.

**The Cross Moves from Shame to Symbol of Empire**

Nothing illustrates the shift more vividly than what happened to the

cross.

In the first three centuries, the cross was a sign of shame, suffering,

and self-giving love. It was the instrument Rome used to execute slaves,

rebels, and criminals. To say "Jesus is Lord" while pointing to a cross

was to make the most counterintuitive claim in the ancient world: that

God's power was revealed not through military conquest but through

voluntary suffering. That the King of the universe had submitted Himself

to the empire's most humiliating punishment---and that this submission,

not Rome's armies, was the true power that would reshape the world.

The cross was the great inversion. Rome said power flows from the top

down. The cross said power flows from the bottom up. Rome said the

strong dominate the weak. The cross said the Strong One chose to become

weak so that the weak could be made strong. Rome said the cross was

defeat. The resurrection said the cross was victory.

And then Constantine put the cross on his soldiers' shields.

According to tradition, before the Battle of Milvian Bridge in 312,

Constantine saw a vision of the cross with the words In hoc signo

vinces---"In this sign, conquer." He ordered the Christian symbol

painted on his soldiers' shields, won the battle, and attributed his

victory to the Christian God.

Set aside the question of whether the vision was genuine. Focus instead

on what happened to the meaning of the cross.

The cross---the instrument of voluntary suffering, the sign of power

made perfect in weakness---became a military emblem. A tool of conquest.

A symbol painted on shields and carried into battle. The sign that once

said "God conquers through self-giving love" now said "God conquers

through our army."

The theological implications are staggering. When the cross becomes a

symbol of imperial victory rather than sacrificial love, the entire

grammar of Christian faith is altered. The church that carried the cross

as a sign of its willingness to suffer now carried the cross as a sign

of its power to dominate. The church that had been crucified with Christ

now crucified in the name of Christ.

This is not a minor aesthetic shift. It is a theological

revolution---one that quietly reversed the meaning of the very event at

the center of the Christian faith.

The Structural Problem: When the Church's Stability Depends on the

Empire's Stability

Here is where Constantine's bargain becomes most relevant to our present

moment.

Once the church's institutional stability became tied to the empire's

stability, a new and devastating logic took hold: threats to the empire

became threats to the church.

This sounds abstract, so let me make it concrete.

Before Constantine, if Rome fell, the church would grieve for the

suffering of its neighbors---but it would not grieve for the loss of its

own position, because the church had no privileged position to lose. The

church's life came from the risen Christ, not from Roman favor. The

church could function under any emperor, any regime, any political

arrangement, because its vitality was not contingent on its cultural

standing.

After Constantine, the church had buildings funded by the state, clergy

exempted from taxes, theological councils backed by imperial authority,

and a social position that depended on the ongoing favor of political

power. Lose the emperor's favor, and you lose your tax exemptions, your

buildings, your influence, your legal protections.

And once you have something to lose, your relationship to power

fundamentally changes.

The church that has nothing to lose can speak truth without calculation.

It can confront the emperor to his face, as Ambrose of Milan confronted

Theodosius after the massacre at Thessalonica, because even if the

confrontation costs everything, the church's survival does not depend on

the emperor's goodwill.

But the church that has everything to lose---buildings, budgets,

cultural influence, legal protections, tax-exempt status, access to

power---begins to calculate. It begins to ask not only "What is true?"

but "What can we afford to say?" It begins to weigh prophetic

faithfulness against institutional survival. And when those two

considerations conflict---as they inevitably will---the church that is

tied to power will almost always choose survival over truth.

Not because the leaders are wicked. Not because they have consciously

chosen cowardice. But because the structural incentives of privilege

push toward compliance. When your position depends on the favor of the

powerful, you develop an instinct for saying what the powerful want to

hear. You may not even notice it happening. But the prophetic edge

dulls. The challenging sermon becomes the safe sermon. The hard truth

becomes the convenient truth. The church that once said "Thus says the

Lord" to the face of the empire begins to say "Thus says the Lord" only

when the Lord happens to agree with the empire's agenda.

That is Constantine's bargain. Protection in exchange for compliance.

Privilege in exchange for silence. A seat at the table in exchange for

not disrupting the meal.

And the most insidious feature of the bargain is that it never feels

like a bargain at all. It feels like blessing.

**1 Samuel 8 Revisited: Protection as Judgment**

We examined 1 Samuel 8 in Chapter Two, but this passage demands a second

look in the context of Constantine's bargain, because the structural

dynamic is identical.

Israel demands a king. Not because they have rejected God

intellectually. Not because they have embraced paganism. But because

they are afraid. The Philistines are a real threat. Samuel's sons are

corrupt. The nation feels vulnerable. And so the elders come to Samuel

and say, "Appoint for us a king to judge us like all the nations" (1

Samuel 8:5).

God's interpretation of this request is devastating: "They have not

rejected you, but they have rejected me from being king over them" (1

Samuel 8:7).

The request for a visible, powerful, human protector---even a legitimate

one, even one that God Himself will eventually work through---is

identified as a rejection of God's invisible kingship. Not because

political leadership is inherently evil. But because the motivation is

fear-driven replacement of divine trust with human security.

And then God grants the request---but not as a straightforward blessing.

He grants it as a judgment. He gives them what they asked for. And what

they asked for eventually consumes them.

The parallel to the Constantinian moment is striking. The early church

did not need imperial protection to thrive. It had thrived under

persecution. It had grown precisely because it was not aligned with

power, because its moral authority came from its willingness to suffer,

because the community it produced was so compelling that people joined

it despite the cost.

And then the church asked for a king like the nations. It accepted

imperial favor. It received protection. And over time, the protection

reshaped the church's character. The church that had been purified by

suffering became softened by comfort. The church that had been sharpened

by opposition became dulled by alliance. The church that had spoken

truth to power became power's chaplain.

God granted the request. And the gift, over centuries, revealed itself

to contain a cost that the church is still paying.

**The Medieval Entanglement: When Church and State Became Inseparable**

The Constantinian settlement did not remain static. It deepened over

centuries into what historians call Christendom---the complete

intertwining of church and state that characterized medieval Europe.

In Christendom, the church crowned emperors and emperors enforced

theological orthodoxy. The pope claimed authority over kings, and kings

claimed divine sanction for their wars. Heresy was not merely a

theological error; it was a crime against the state. Dissent was not

merely a spiritual concern; it was a political threat. The sword served

the altar, and the altar blessed the sword.

The results were complex. On one hand, Christendom produced

extraordinary goods: the preservation of learning, the building of

universities, the development of hospitals, the cultivation of art and

architecture that still takes the breath away. The claim that

Christendom was simply a dark age of religious tyranny is historically

illiterate.

But on the other hand, the fusion of church and state produced a

Christianity that could not distinguish between serving Christ and

serving the empire. When the Crusaders marched to Jerusalem under the

sign of the cross, they believed they were serving the Lord. When the

Inquisition tortured suspected heretics, it believed it was protecting

the faith. When European colonizers subjugated indigenous peoples while

planting crosses, they believed they were advancing the Kingdom of God.

The tragedy is not that these were evil people doing evil things. Many

of them were sincere believers acting according to the logic of a system

that had fused spiritual authority with coercive power. They could not

see what they were doing because the fusion had become so total that the

distinction between God's work and the empire's work had disappeared.

That is the ultimate cost of Constantine's bargain. Not that it produces

consciously wicked Christians, but that it produces Christians who can

no longer tell the difference between the Kingdom of God and the kingdom

they happen to inhabit. When the fusion is complete, every act of

national self-interest can be baptized as divine will. Every war can be

a holy war. Every policy can be "God's plan." And the cross---the sign

of self-giving love---becomes a weapon.

**The Reformation: A Partial Break**

The Protestant Reformation challenged aspects of the medieval

arrangement, but it did not overthrow the Constantinian instinct.

Luther depended on German princes for protection. Without Frederick the

Wise, the Elector of Saxony, Luther would likely have been executed as a

heretic. Luther's survival---and the survival of the

Reformation---depended on political patronage. And that dependence

shaped Luther's political theology in ways that had long-lasting

consequences. His doctrine of the two kingdoms provided a theological

framework for distinguishing spiritual and temporal authority, but in

practice, the Lutheran churches became deeply intertwined with the

German state---a fact that would have devastating consequences in the

twentieth century.

Calvin worked to shape civic structures in Geneva, building what many

have called a "holy commonwealth." His vision was not a theocracy in the

crude sense---Calvin insisted on the distinction between civil and

ecclesiastical authority. But Geneva's experiment demonstrated the

gravitational pull of the Constantinian instinct: when the church has

access to civic power, it is extraordinarily difficult to resist the

temptation to use that power to enforce conformity.

The Church of England was a state church by design. The monarch was the

head of the church. Political and ecclesiastical authority were

structurally fused in a way that made prophetic critique of the state

all but impossible from within the established church.

The Reformation produced extraordinary theological gains---the recovery

of justification by faith, the authority of Scripture, the priesthood of

all believers. But on the question of the church's relationship to

political power, the Reformers largely replicated the Constantinian

arrangement in Protestant form. The church remained tied to the state.

And the instinct---the deep, structural instinct to depend on political

protection for the church's survival---remained intact.

**The American Experiment: A New Arrangement, an Old Instinct**

When European Christians came to America, they brought the Constantinian

instinct with them. But the American arrangement was genuinely

different---at least on paper.

The First Amendment formally separated church and state in a way that

was revolutionary for its time: "Congress shall make no law respecting

an establishment of religion, or prohibiting the free exercise thereof."

No state church. No official denomination. No religious test for public

office. The government would neither sponsor nor suppress religion.

This was, in many ways, a gift to the church. The separation meant that

the church's authority would have to be earned, not imposed. It meant

that Christianity would have to persuade rather than coerce. It meant

that the church's growth would depend on the power of the gospel and the

quality of its community rather than on legal compulsion.

And for much of American history, the church thrived under this

arrangement. The great revivals that shaped American Protestantism---the

First Great Awakening under Edwards and Whitefield, the Second Great

Awakening, the explosive growth of evangelical Christianity in the

nineteenth and twentieth centuries---were not produced by legislation.

They were produced by preaching, prayer, and the mysterious work of the

Holy Spirit. The church grew not because it had political access but

because it had spiritual authority.

But the Constantinian instinct persisted beneath the surface. While the

First Amendment separated church and state institutionally, the cultural

assumption that America was a "Christian nation" remained deeply

embedded. The structures were separated, but the hearts were still

entangled.

For as long as American culture broadly aligned with Christian

values---as long as the Ten Commandments hung in courtrooms, as long as

prayer was expected in public schools, as long as Christian moral

assumptions shaped the legal and cultural landscape---the entanglement

was invisible. You could not see the dependency because you did not need

to. The chariot and the faith were moving in the same direction. There

was no friction.

But when American culture began to shift---when prayer was removed from

public schools, when the sexual revolution reshaped social norms, when

secularization accelerated, when Christianity moved from cultural

majority to cultural contest---the dependency was suddenly exposed.

And the church panicked.

Not because the gospel was threatened. The gospel cannot be threatened

by a Supreme Court decision. Not because the Spirit had been quenched.

The Spirit does not require cultural privilege to do His work. But

because the cultural position the church had grown accustomed to---the

comfortable assumption that America and Christianity were naturally

allied---was eroding.

And the panic revealed the truth: the church's confidence had been

resting not on the resurrection but on cultural privilege. Not on Christ

but on Constantine's bargain---the American version.

The buildings were different. The emperors wore different clothes. But

the bargain was the same: protection in exchange for alignment.

Privilege in exchange for usefulness. A seat at the table in exchange

for not upsetting the arrangement.

And now that the arrangement is changing, we are discovering how deeply

we depended on it.

**Answering the Objection: "Isn't Political Favor a Blessing?"**

At this point, someone will push back: "But God does bless nations

through favorable governance. Isn't it a good thing when rulers support

the church? Wasn't Constantine's conversion an answer to prayer? Isn't

it right to thank God when political conditions are favorable to the

gospel?"

Yes. Absolutely.

It is a good thing when governments protect religious liberty. It is a

good thing when laws reflect justice. It is a good thing when rulers

support the common good. Paul instructs Timothy to pray for kings and

all who are in authority, "that we may lead a peaceful and quiet life,

godly and dignified in every way" (1 Timothy 2:2). Favorable governance

is something to pray for and be grateful for.

But there is a vast difference between receiving a blessing and

depending on it.

The Israelites in the wilderness received manna every morning. It was a

gift. It was provision. It was grace. But if they tried to hoard it---if

they tried to stockpile it out of fear that God might not provide

tomorrow---it rotted (Exodus 16:20). The gift was meant to be received

daily, with open hands and trust. The moment they tried to secure it, to

guarantee it, to make it permanent, it became a source of decay.

Political favor is like manna. When it comes, receive it gratefully. Use

it wisely. Steward it for the good of your neighbor and the advance of

justice. But do not hoard it. Do not build your identity around it. Do

not let it become the foundation of your confidence. Because it can be

taken away. And if your faith cannot survive without it, then your faith

was never in the God who provides---it was in the provision itself.

The question is not whether political favor is a blessing. The question

is whether you can flourish without it.

Can your church worship with joy if the tax exemptions are revoked? Can

your community maintain its mission if the cultural wind turns hostile?

Can your faith remain steady if the government is indifferent---or even

opposed---to your convictions?

If the answer is yes, then political favor is being held with open

hands. It is a gift received, not a god worshiped.

If the answer is no---if the loss of political favor would feel like the

loss of the faith---then Constantine's bargain has done its work. The

church has become dependent on the very thing it was never meant to

need.

**How to Live Without Privilege---and Without Bitterness**

So what does it look like for the American church to disentangle from

Constantine's bargain? What does it look like to hold political favor

with open hands? What does it look like to be the church without

cultural privilege?

It does not look like bitterness. The church that has lost privilege and

responds with resentment, victimhood, and nostalgia for a vanished

golden age has not actually escaped the bargain. It is still defined by

the privilege---it has simply moved from enjoying it to mourning it. The

emotional center has not changed. The church is still oriented around

cultural power rather than around Christ.

It does not look like withdrawal. The church that responds to cultural

marginalization by retreating into isolation, building walls around its

community, and refusing to engage the public square has confused

faithfulness with fear. The early church did not withdraw from Roman

society. It participated in it---paying taxes, serving neighbors,

praying for rulers---while refusing to grant Rome ultimate status.

Engagement without ultimacy is the model, not retreat.

It looks like the rediscovery of the church's original power source.

The early church did not have tax exemptions. It did not have political

access. It did not have cultural centrality. It did not have legal

protections. It did not have buildings, budgets, or media platforms.

What it had was the Holy Spirit.

What it had was a community of believers whose love for one another was

so visible, so radical, and so countercultural that the watching world

could not explain it.

What it had was a message so powerful that it turned the Roman Empire

upside down---not through political maneuvering but through

proclamation, service, and the willingness to suffer.

What it had was a crucified and risen Lord whose resurrection guaranteed

that no earthly power---not Rome, not Nero, not the gates of

hell---could ultimately prevail against it.

That is still what the church has. None of it depends on political

favor. None of it requires cultural privilege. None of it can be granted

or revoked by any government on earth.

The question is whether we believe it.

Because if we genuinely believe that the Holy Spirit is

sufficient---that the gospel is powerful enough, that the community of

Christ is compelling enough, that the resurrection is real enough---then

the loss of cultural privilege is not a crisis. It is a clarification.

It strips away the scaffolding and reveals whether the building can

stand on its own.

And the church of Jesus Christ can stand on its own. It has always been

able to stand on its own. It was designed to stand on its own. It was

born in a manger, grew under persecution, and conquered the most

powerful empire in history without a single political alliance.

We do not need Constantine's bargain.

We need the Spirit of the living God.

And the Spirit has never needed an emperor's permission to do His work.

**Key Takeaways**

\- Constantine's Edict of Milan (313 AD) produced genuine goods---the

end of persecution, the freedom to build churches, the ability to

convene theological councils---but it also initiated a structural

shift that changed the church's instincts about power.

\- When the church gained privilege, it moved from relying on spiritual

authority to relying on institutional authority---and began to fear

losing access more than losing faithfulness.

\- The cross---once a sign of voluntary suffering and self-giving

love---was placed on soldiers' shields, transforming its meaning

from divine weakness into imperial conquest.

\- The most dangerous structural consequence of Constantine's bargain

is this: once the church's stability depends on the empire's

stability, threats to the empire become threats to the church---and

defending the state becomes "defending the faith."

\- The Reformation challenged medieval Christendom theologically but

largely replicated the Constantinian arrangement in Protestant

form---Luther depended on princes, Calvin shaped Geneva, the Church

of England fused crown and church.

\- America's First Amendment separated church and state

institutionally, but the cultural assumption that America was a

"Christian nation" kept the Constantinian instinct alive beneath the

surface.

\- Political favor is like manna: receive it gratefully, steward it

wisely, but do not hoard it or build your identity around

it---because if your faith cannot survive without it, your faith was

in the provision, not the Provider.

\- The church was born without political privilege, grew without it,

and conquered the Roman Empire without it. The Spirit has never

needed an emperor's permission to do His work.

**CHAPTER EIGHT**

*Germany's Warning: Baptized Nationalism and Moral Catastrophe*

> *"The church that trades its integrity for protection does not preserve itself. It loses itself."*

• • •

**A Nation Looking for a Savior**

Germany in the 1920s and early 1930s was a nation in agony. The

humiliation of the Versailles Treaty had stripped the country of

territory, military capacity, and national pride. The Weimar Republic,

Germany's experiment with parliamentary democracy, was riddled with

instability---coalition governments rose and fell with dizzying speed,

hyperinflation destroyed the savings of ordinary families, and the Great

Depression of 1929 delivered an economic blow that left millions

unemployed and desperate.

For German Christians---and Germany was an overwhelmingly Christian

nation, both Protestant and Catholic---the crisis felt not merely

economic or political. It felt civilizational. It felt like the moral

foundations of their society were crumbling. Sexual permissiveness in

Berlin's cabarets scandalized traditional believers. Marxist movements

threatened the church's social position. Secularism was advancing in

universities and public institutions. The old certainties of

Christendom---church, family, moral consensus---seemed to be

disintegrating under the weight of modernity.

The fear was real. The threats were not imaginary. Moral decline was not

a figment of conservative paranoia. The cultural ground genuinely was

shifting under the feet of German Christians, and the institutions they

had trusted to maintain social order were visibly failing.

And into that fear stepped a man who promised restoration.

Adolf Hitler did not initially rise to power by attacking Christianity.

He rose by presenting himself as its defender. He invoked providence. He

spoke of restoring Germany's soul. He promised to protect the church,

defend the family, restore national greatness, and push back against the

moral decay that Christians found so alarming. He offered anxious

believers exactly what they wanted: protection without repentance, power

without humility, national renewal without self-examination.

And millions of German Christians accepted the offer.

Not because they were uniquely evil. Not because they lacked sincere

faith. But because they were afraid. And fear, as we have seen

throughout this book, makes the idol feel necessary. Fear makes the

chariot look like the only option. Fear makes the strongman's promises

sound like the voice of God.

**The "German Christians": When Theology Serves the State**

The movement known as the *Deutsche Christen*---the "German

Christians"---represents one of the most instructive and disturbing

episodes in church history. It was not a pagan movement. It was not

composed of atheists who infiltrated the church to destroy it from

within. It was a movement of sincere Protestants who believed, with

genuine conviction, that God was working through Adolf Hitler to restore

Germany's Christian soul.

The German Christians argued that national revival and spiritual renewal

belonged together. They believed that the German *Volk*---the people,

the nation, the race---was part of God's created order, and that

defending the nation was therefore a form of obedience to God. They saw

no conflict between loyalty to Christ and loyalty to the Führer. In

their theology, the two loyalties reinforced each other.

And that is precisely what made the movement so dangerous. It was not

paganism. It was baptized nationalism---a Christianity that had absorbed

the nation's story so thoroughly that it could no longer distinguish

between God's purposes and Germany's ambitions.

The theological consequences were devastating. The German Christians

attempted to remove the Old Testament from Christian worship because it

was "too Jewish." They sought to purge the church of pastors with Jewish

ancestry. They rewrote hymns to align with Nazi ideology. They adopted

the *Führerprinzip*---the leadership principle---into church governance,

arguing that the church, like the state, needed a single strong leader

rather than democratic structures. They draped the cross in the swastika

and called it faithfulness.

The doctrine was quietly, systematically rewritten. Not all at once. Not

through a single dramatic act of apostasy. But gradually, under the

pressure of national crisis, through the slow accommodation of theology

to political necessity, one compromise at a time.

That is how it always happens. Not with a bang but with a thousand small

concessions, each one feeling reasonable at the time.

**Barmen: The Line That Had to Be Drawn**

Not every German Christian accepted the fusion. In May 1934,

representatives of the Confessing Church---a movement of Protestants who

rejected the German Christians' capitulation---gathered in the city of

Barmen to draft what would become one of the most important theological

documents of the twentieth century: the Barmen Declaration.

The Barmen Declaration did not address specific political policies. It

did not endorse a political party or call for armed resistance. What it

did was far more fundamental: it drew a theological line. It declared

that Jesus Christ, as He is attested in Holy Scripture, is the one Word

of God whom we must hear, trust, and obey in life and in death. And it

explicitly rejected the claim that the church should recognize "other

events, powers, historic figures, and truths as God's revelation"

alongside or apart from Jesus Christ.

In other words, Barmen said: the church has one Lord. Not two. Not

Christ and the Führer. Not the gospel and the national destiny. Not

Scripture and the ideology of the state. One Lord. One Word. One source

of ultimate authority. And any theology that supplements Christ with a

political savior---no matter how urgent the crisis, no matter how

compelling the leader, no matter how real the threat---is a false

theology.

Karl Barth, the primary author of the declaration, understood that the

German church's failure was not primarily political. It was theological.

The church had not merely made a strategic error in aligning with

Hitler. It had committed a doctrinal error---a failure to maintain the

exclusive lordship of Christ over every sphere of life, including

national identity and political allegiance.

Dietrich Bonhoeffer, who would eventually be executed by the Nazis for

his involvement in a conspiracy against Hitler, put it even more

starkly. He argued that the church had entered a crisis not because the

state was attacking it, but because the church had surrendered its own

identity. It had become so entangled with the national project that it

could no longer speak a word from God that the nation did not want to

hear. It had lost the one thing that made it the church: the freedom to

proclaim Christ as Lord over and against every earthly power.

The Confessing Church was a minority. Most German Protestants either

actively supported the German Christian movement or quietly accommodated

themselves to the new order. The pastors who resisted paid a steep

price---imprisonment, exile, execution. The majority who cooperated were

not punished by the state. They were rewarded with stability,

institutional survival, and the comfortable illusion that they were

being faithful.

History has rendered its verdict on who was actually faithful.

**The Moral Catastrophe: How the Church Lost Its Voice**

The consequences of the German church's compromise extend far beyond

politics. What happened in Germany between 1933 and 1945 was not merely

a political failure. It was a moral catastrophe of civilization-altering

proportions---and the church's complicity in that catastrophe remains

one of the most devastating indictments in Christian history.

The Holocaust was enacted in a nation with a Christian majority. The

laws that stripped Jewish citizens of their rights, that segregated

them, that deported them, that ultimately murdered six million of

them---these laws were enacted in a country where Sunday church

attendance was the cultural norm. Pastors who preached sermons about

loving your neighbor on Sunday watched Jewish neighbors hauled away on

Monday and said nothing. Churches that sang hymns about the justice of

God remained silent while injustice was institutionalized at every level

of society.

How did this happen? How did a nation of churchgoers become complicit in

industrial-scale murder?

The answer is not that Germans were uniquely wicked. The answer is that

the church had already surrendered its prophetic independence before the

worst atrocities began. By the time the moral crisis demanded courageous

resistance, the church had already traded its voice for the security of

political alignment. It had already decided that national stability was

more important than moral clarity. It had already accepted the premise

that the nation's enemies were the church's enemies, and that defending

the nation was the same thing as defending the faith.

Once those categories collapsed, the church could not speak. Not because

it lacked the theological resources---the Bible is relentless in its

defense of the vulnerable, the stranger, the oppressed. But because

speaking would have required the church to challenge the very political

power it had embraced as its protector. And you cannot prophetically

confront a power you depend on for your survival.

That is the price of protection. Not just moral compromise in the

moment, but the loss of the capacity to resist when the moment demands

it. The church did not become complicit in the Holocaust overnight. It

became complicit one accommodation at a time, one silence at a time, one

rationalization at a time---until the accumulated weight of compromise

had made prophetic speech unthinkable.

**Answering the Objection: "Are You Calling People Nazis?"**

This is the objection I need to address directly, because if I do not,

everything in this chapter will be dismissed by the people who most need

to hear it.

No. I am not calling American Christians Nazis. I am not saying that

contemporary Christian nationalism is equivalent to National Socialism.

I am not suggesting that supporting a political candidate is the same as

supporting genocide. That kind of rhetorical escalation is lazy,

manipulative, and morally irresponsible, and it deserves to be rejected

whenever it appears---from any direction.

But the rejection of lazy comparison does not excuse us from learning

what history teaches.

And what history teaches is not that patriotism produces fascism. It is

something more subtle, more uncomfortable, and more directly relevant to

our moment: when fear combines with theological confusion, churches can

rationalize alliances that later horrify them.

The German Christians who supported Hitler did not wake up one morning

and decide to become complicit in atrocity. They made a series of

smaller decisions---each one seemingly reasonable, each one driven by a

legitimate concern---that cumulatively led them to a place they never

intended to go. They believed their moment was exceptional. They

believed the stakes justified the compromise. They believed that this

time, the ends would justify the means. They believed they were being

responsible, prudent, realistic.

Every generation tells itself the same story: "This time is different.

This time the stakes are higher. This time compromise is necessary. This

time we have to hold our nose and make the pragmatic choice."

And history keeps rendering the same verdict: the church that trades its

integrity for protection does not preserve itself. It loses itself.

The lesson of Germany is not "you are Nazis." The lesson of Germany is

"you are not immune." No generation of Christians is immune to the

seduction of political power. No church is so theologically

sophisticated, so culturally aware, so spiritually mature that it cannot

be led astray by fear. The German church was one of the most educated,

most theologically rich, most historically rooted churches in the

world---and it failed catastrophically.

If they could fall, so can we. And the first step toward falling is the

conviction that you cannot.

**The "Lesser of Two Evils" and Moral Anesthesia**

One of the most common defenses for political compromise in the American

church is the "lesser of two evils" framework. The reasoning is

straightforward: in a fallen world, choices are rarely pure. Christians

must often choose between imperfect options. Voting is not ordination.

Supporting a candidate is not a declaration that they are morally

righteous. It is simply a pragmatic calculation about which option will

produce less harm.

That reasoning is not wrong in itself. Christians do live in a fallen

world. Choices are often between imperfect options. Moral purity in

political decision-making is not always available, and pretending

otherwise is its own kind of self-deception.

What is dangerous is when "lesser evil" becomes moral anesthesia---when

it stops functioning as a sober acknowledgment of fallenness and starts

functioning as a blanket excuse for tolerating whatever your preferred

side does.

When we stop calling evil "evil" because it is our side's evil. When we

begin defending behavior we would condemn in our opponents. When we

excuse corruption, cruelty, and dishonesty because the person committing

them delivers the policy outcomes we want. When we find ourselves

constructing elaborate justifications for conduct that, in any other

context, we would recognize as disqualifying.

At that point, we are not navigating moral complexity. We are

recalibrating morality around outcomes. We are doing precisely what Paul

warned against when he wrote:

> *"Why not do evil that good may come?"---their condemnation is just. ---Romans 3:8*

Paul's answer to ends-justify-means reasoning is not nuanced. It is

condemnation. God never said, "Break My commands if it helps your

cause." God never said, "Moral integrity is negotiable when the

political stakes are high enough." God never said, "Character matters in

private life but not in public leadership."

And here is where the German parallel becomes directly instructive---not

as a one-to-one comparison, but as a pattern.

The German Christians who supported Hitler did not begin by supporting

genocide. They began by accepting the premise that Germany's crisis was

so severe that normal moral standards could be temporarily suspended.

They began by telling themselves that the ends---national restoration,

economic recovery, the defense of Christian civilization---justified

tolerating a leader whose methods were sometimes distasteful. They began

by calling evil "necessary" and cruelty "strength" and ruthlessness

"leadership."

And with each accommodation, the moral threshold dropped. Each

compromise made the next compromise easier. Each rationalization

expanded the category of what was "acceptable." Until the church found

itself standing in a place it never intended to stand---complicit in

horrors it never imagined it would tolerate---and unable to explain how

it got there.

The "lesser of two evils" framework is spiritually dangerous not because

it acknowledges moral complexity, but because it can train the

conscience to tolerate more and more evil over time. It can function as

a ratchet that only turns in one direction---toward greater

accommodation, greater excuse-making, greater moral numbness---until the

Christian who started by reluctantly tolerating imperfection finds

themselves enthusiastically defending what they once would have

denounced.

The question is not whether you can tolerate imperfection in a political

leader. Of course you can. The question is whether your tolerance has a

floor---a point beyond which you will say, "This I cannot support,

regardless of the political cost." If you cannot name that floor---if

there is no behavior, no level of dishonesty, no degree of cruelty that

would cause you to withdraw your support---then the "lesser of two

evils" framework is no longer functioning as moral reasoning. It is

functioning as moral surrender.

**Character Matters: Nathan, Elijah, and the Baptist**

Another common objection in this conversation is: "Presidents aren't

pastors. We are not electing a national chaplain. We are electing

someone to govern, and governance requires a different standard than

pastoral ministry."

There is a kernel of truth in this. Political leadership and pastoral

ministry are different vocations with different qualifications. We

should not expect identical character profiles from a head of state and

a shepherd of a congregation.

But Scripture does not draw the line where this objection draws it.

Scripture consistently holds rulers accountable for justice, integrity,

and character---not merely for policy outcomes.

Nathan confronted David---not for a policy failure, but for adultery and

murder (2 Samuel 12). Elijah confronted Ahab---not for raising taxes,

but for stealing Naboth's vineyard and shedding innocent blood (1 Kings

21). John the Baptist confronted Herod---not for his political platform,

but for his sexual immorality---and lost his head for it (Mark

6:17--29).

In every case, the prophet does not say, "Well, he's not a pastor." The

prophet says, "You are a ruler, and God holds rulers accountable for

their character, not just their legislation."

Micah summarizes the standard with devastating simplicity:

> *"He has told you, O man, what is good; and what does the Lord require of you but to do justice, and to love kindness, and to walk humbly with your God?" ---Micah 6:8*

That standard is not limited to clergy. It is addressed to rulers, to

nations, to every human being who exercises authority. Justice.

Kindness. Humility. If those qualities are absent from a leader---if the

leader is characterized instead by injustice, cruelty, and

arrogance---then the Christian who supports that leader must at least be

honest about what they are tolerating and why.

Political office does not excuse moral corruption. If anything, it

raises the stakes, because power magnifies what is already there. A

dishonest person with power is more dangerous than a dishonest person

without it. A cruel person with authority does more damage than a cruel

person without it. Character is not a luxury in leadership. It is a

load-bearing wall. And when the wall collapses, everything built on top

of it comes down.

So why are parts of the church pretending now that character does not

matter?

Because we are afraid. And fear makes us practical in ways that are

actually faithless. Fear convinces us that the situation is so dire that

we cannot afford the luxury of moral standards. Fear says, "Yes, the

leader is flawed, but the alternative is worse." And before long, fear

has redefined "flawed" to include things that Scripture calls sin---and

we have stopped noticing the shift.

**What Germany Teaches About the Cost of Silence**

Perhaps the most haunting aspect of the German church's failure was not

what it did but what it did not do. It was not primarily the active

collaboration that damns the historical record---though that was real

and indefensible. It was the silence. The vast, institutional,

Sunday-morning silence of a church that knew something was wrong and

said nothing.

Martin Niemöller, the German pastor who initially supported Hitler

before becoming one of the most prominent leaders of the Confessing

Church, captured this failure in words that have become some of the most

quoted of the twentieth century:

> *"First they came for the socialists, and I did not speak out---because I was not a socialist. Then they came for the trade unionists, and I did not speak out---because I was not a trade unionist. Then they came for the Jews, and I did not speak out---because I was not a Jew. Then they came for me---and there was no one left to speak for me."*

The silence was not passive. It was a choice. It was the choice to

prioritize institutional survival over prophetic faithfulness. It was

the choice to protect the church's relationship with the state rather

than protect the people the state was destroying. It was the choice to

say, "This is not our concern," about matters that the gospel makes

every believer's concern.

And the silence had a theology behind it. The theology said: our primary

responsibility is to maintain the church's stability. Our job is to keep

the institution running, the congregations intact, the buildings open.

If speaking truth would jeopardize our position, then silence is not

cowardice---it is stewardship.

That theology is alive and well in the American church today. It is the

theology of the pastor who knows something is wrong but will not say it

because it would split the congregation. It is the theology of the

church leader who privately laments the moral compromises of their

political allies but publicly remains silent because speaking would cost

them influence. It is the theology that confuses institutional

preservation with faithfulness to Christ---and chooses the institution

every time.

But Jesus did not promise to preserve institutions. He promised to build

His church. And the church He builds is not defined by buildings,

budgets, or political access. It is defined by its willingness to

confess Him as Lord---even when confession costs everything the

institution holds dear.

> *"My kingdom is not of this world. If my kingdom were of this world, my servants would have been fighting, that I might not be delivered over to the Jews. But my kingdom is not from the world." ---John 18:36*

Jesus said this while standing before Pilate---the representative of the

most powerful political authority on earth. And He did not flinch. He

did not calculate the political cost. He did not moderate His claims to

avoid offending the state. He spoke the truth about the nature of His

kingdom, knowing it would lead to His execution.

That is the model. And it is a model that requires the church to value

truth more than survival, faithfulness more than stability, and the

lordship of Christ more than the approval of any earthly power.

**The Pattern We Must Recognize**

Let me be precise about what I am asking you to take from this chapter,

because precision matters when the stakes are this high.

I am not asking you to accept a facile comparison between the

contemporary United States and Nazi Germany. The political situations

are different. The institutional structures are different. The specific

threats are different. History does not repeat itself in photocopy form.

But history does reveal patterns. And the pattern that the German

experience illuminates is this: when a church becomes so entangled with

national identity that it cannot distinguish between God's purposes and

the nation's ambitions, it loses the ability to resist when resistance

is required. When a church accepts political protection at the cost of

prophetic independence, it gains security in the short term and loses

its soul in the long term. When a church allows fear to rewrite its

theology---making the nation's enemies into God's enemies, the nation's

preservation into the church's mission, the nation's leader into God's

anointed---it has already departed from the gospel, even if it continues

to use the gospel's language.

That pattern is not unique to Germany. It has appeared in every era

where the church has traded its independence for the comfort of

political alignment. It appeared in Constantine's Rome. It appeared in

medieval Christendom. It appeared in colonial Christianity. It appeared

in apartheid South Africa. It appeared in the American church's defense

of slavery and segregation.

And it is appearing now.

Not in identical form. Not with identical consequences. But with the

same underlying dynamic: fear drives the church to seek political

protection, protection demands theological accommodation, and

accommodation gradually erodes the church's ability to speak truth,

pursue justice, and confess Christ as Lord over every earthly

power---including the one it has embraced.

The question is not whether this could happen to the American church.

The question is whether we are honest enough to recognize where it is

already happening, and courageous enough to repent before the cost

becomes catastrophic.

Because the road from accommodation to complicity is never as long as we

think. And the church that cannot name the idol while it is still being

formed will not be able to resist the idol once it is fully established.

Paul's instruction to the Roman church remains as urgent now as it was

two thousand years ago:

> *"Do not be conformed to this world, but be transformed by the renewal of your mind, that by testing you may discern what is the will of God, what is good and acceptable and perfect." ---Romans 12:2*

Non-conformity is not a posture of arrogance. It is the posture of a

people who know they serve a King whose kingdom is not of this

world---and who refuse to let any earthly kingdom claim the loyalty that

belongs to Him alone.

**\*Key Takeaways\***

\- Germany's church did not fall because it abandoned faith---it fell

because it fused faith with national identity until the two became

indistinguishable, and the church could no longer speak a word the

nation did not want to hear.

\- Hitler rose not by attacking Christianity but by presenting himself

as its defender---offering protection without repentance, power

without humility, national restoration without self-examination.

\- The Barmen Declaration drew the essential theological line: Jesus

Christ is the one Word of God, and no political leader, national

destiny, or historical crisis can be recognized as a supplementary

source of divine authority.

\- The Holocaust was enacted in a majority-Christian nation---not

because Christians were uniquely evil, but because the church had

already surrendered its prophetic independence before the worst

atrocities began.

\- The lesson of Germany is not "you are Nazis" but "you are not

immune." Every generation of Christians is vulnerable to the

seduction of political power when fear combines with theological

confusion.

\- The "lesser of two evils" framework becomes spiritually dangerous

when it functions as moral anesthesia---training the conscience to

tolerate more evil over time rather than maintaining a clear moral

floor.

\- Scripture holds rulers accountable for character, not merely

outcomes---Nathan confronted David, Elijah confronted Ahab, John the

Baptist confronted Herod. Political office does not excuse moral

corruption.

\- The church's greatest failure in Germany was not active

collaboration but institutional silence---the choice to prioritize

stability over truth. That theology of silence is alive in the

American church today.

**CHAPTER NINE**

*The Generational Cost: When Hypocrisy Makes the Gospel Unbelievable*

> *"Hypocrisy does not merely make the gospel less attractive. It makes the gospel unbelievable."*

• • •

**The Audience That Never Left the Room**

There is a generation watching.

They are sitting in the back pews of churches they are not sure they

belong to anymore. They are scrolling through social media feeds where

pastors they once trusted share content that sounds more like campaign

propaganda than the gospel. They are sitting across the dinner table

from parents whose faith they once admired but whose political behavior

has become indistinguishable from the tribalism they hear condemned from

the pulpit every Sunday.

And they are drawing conclusions.

Not first about politics. Not first about culture. Not first about which

party is right or which candidate deserves their vote.

About God.

This is the part of the conversation that pastors and parents need to

hear, and it is the part that makes everyone uncomfortable. Because the

generational exodus from the church in America is not primarily a story

about secularism winning. It is not primarily a story about universities

corrupting the young. It is not primarily a story about social media,

pornography, or the seductive power of progressive ideology---though all

of those factors are real and should not be dismissed.

It is, at its deepest level, a story about credibility.

When the people who taught you that character matters excuse the most

visible character failures because the person in question delivers the

right political outcomes---you do not merely question their politics.

You question their theology. You question whether they ever believed

what they said. You question whether the faith they handed you was real

or whether it was always, underneath the Sunday language, a vehicle for

something else.

And once that question takes root, it does not go away easily. Because

the question is no longer "Is this political position right?" The

question becomes "Is any of it true?"

That is the generational cost of what we are doing. And the bill is

already coming due.

**The Data and the Stories Behind It**

The research on religious disaffiliation among young adults in America

has been extensive, and the findings are consistent across multiple

studies and methodologies. The number of Americans under forty who

identify as religiously unaffiliated---the so-called "nones"---has grown

dramatically over the past two decades. The Pew Research Center has

documented this trend repeatedly. The General Social Survey has tracked

it for even longer. The numbers are not in dispute.

What is in dispute is the explanation.

The most common conservative explanation runs something like this: young

people are leaving because secular culture is too appealing, because

universities undermine faith, because social media exposes them to ideas

that erode belief, because the sexual revolution made the church's moral

demands inconvenient. In this telling, the problem is entirely external.

The culture is corrupting our children. The solution is more insulation,

more apologetics, more cultural engagement, more political power to push

back against the secular tide.

There is partial truth in that explanation. The cultural pressures on

young believers are real. The intellectual challenges are real. The

sexual ethics of historic Christianity do create genuine tension with

contemporary cultural norms. None of that should be minimized.

But that explanation, by itself, is dangerously incomplete. Because it

conveniently ignores the most uncomfortable factor: the behavior of the

church itself.

David Kinnaman's research at the Barna Group, published in books like

*unChristian* and *You Lost Me*, found something that should have

alarmed every church leader in America. Young adults who left the church

did not primarily describe the faith as intellectually implausible. They

described it as hypocritical. They described it as judgmental. They

described it as politically entangled. They described it as more

interested in power than in love. They described a faith that said one

thing and did another---and they found the contradiction intolerable.

The word that came up again and again in the research was not "false."

It was "inauthentic."

That distinction matters enormously. A generation that thinks the gospel

is intellectually false might be reached with better arguments. A

generation that thinks the church is fundamentally inauthentic requires

something far more costly: repentance.

**What Hypocrisy Actually Communicates**

We tend to think of hypocrisy as a personal moral failing---an

individual who says one thing and does another. And it is that. But when

hypocrisy becomes systemic---when entire communities normalize a gap

between their stated values and their actual behavior---it communicates

something far more devastating than individual moral weakness.

Systemic hypocrisy communicates that the stated values were never real.

When a church preaches that character matters and then rallies behind a

leader whose character is visibly, publicly, undeniably deficient---not

in the way that all sinners are deficient, but in ways that the church

itself would have condemned without hesitation in any other

context---the message received by the watching world is not "Christians

are imperfect." Everyone already knows that. The message received is:

"Character never actually mattered to them. It was always about power."

When a church preaches that truth matters and then tolerates, defends,

or celebrates dishonesty because the dishonest person advances their

political interests, the message received is not "Christians are

navigating a complex world." The message received is: "Truth was always

negotiable. It was only a weapon they used against other people."

When a church preaches love for enemies and then dehumanizes, mocks, and

demonizes political opponents with language that would shame a high

school bully, the message received is not "Christians are passionate

about justice." The message received is: "The love they preached was

conditional. It only applied to people who agreed with them."

When a church preaches humility and then celebrates arrogance, cruelty,

and domination as "strength," the message received is not "Christians

value strong leadership." The message received is: "Jesus was a mascot.

Power was the real god all along."

This is not a progressive critique. This is not a politically motivated

complaint from people who simply dislike conservative politics. This is

a theological catastrophe of the first order. Because the gospel's power

to persuade depends, in part, on the community that proclaims it being

recognizably shaped by it. When the community is not recognizably

different from the tribalized, fear-driven, power-hungry culture around

it---when the only distinguishing mark of the church is which political

tribe it belongs to rather than whose image it bears---the gospel does

not merely lose its appeal. It loses its plausibility.

Jesus said it plainly:

> *"By this all people will know that you are my disciples, if you have love for one another." ---John 13:35*

The world was supposed to recognize Christ's people by their love.

Instead, an entire generation now recognizes Christ's people by their

politics. And that recognition is not leading them toward faith. It is

leading them away.

**Jesus on Hypocrisy: The Sin He Condemned Most Fiercely**

It is worth pausing to notice something about the Gospels that should

unsettle every church leader in America: Jesus reserved His most

devastating language not for pagans, not for the sexually immoral, not

for tax collectors or prostitutes---but for the religious establishment.

Matthew 23 is the longest sustained rebuke in the Gospels. Seven times

Jesus pronounces "woe" on the scribes and Pharisees. And the recurring

charge is not theological error in the abstract. It is the specific sin

of saying one thing and living another:

> *"The scribes and the Pharisees sit on Moses' seat, so do and observe whatever they tell you, but not the works they do. For they preach, but do not practice." ---Matthew 23:2--3*

They preach but do not practice. They bind heavy burdens on others but

will not lift a finger themselves. They do everything to be seen. They

love the places of honor. They love being called "teacher" and "leader."

They tithe mint and cumin but neglect justice, mercy, and faithfulness.

They clean the outside of the cup while the inside is full of greed and

self-indulgence.

And the metaphor Jesus reaches for is devastating:

> *"Woe to you, scribes and Pharisees, hypocrites! For you are like whitewashed tombs, which outwardly appear beautiful, but within are full of dead people's bones and all uncleanness." ---Matthew 23:27*

Whitewashed tombs. Beautiful on the outside. Dead on the inside.

Religious performance masking spiritual rot.

Now notice: Jesus does not say the Pharisees' theology is entirely

wrong. He even tells the crowd to do what they teach. The content of

their teaching is largely correct. What is catastrophically wrong is the

disconnect between their teaching and their lives. They have the right

words but the wrong character. The right doctrine but the wrong spirit.

The right positions but the wrong posture.

And Jesus says this about them:

> *"You travel across sea and land to make a single proselyte, and when he becomes a proselyte, you make him twice as much a child of hell as yourselves." ---Matthew 23:15*

That verse should terrify every Christian leader in America. Because

Jesus is saying that hypocritical religious leadership does not merely

fail to produce disciples. It actively produces the opposite. The

converts produced by hypocritical leaders are worse off than they were

before---not because the theology is false, but because the hypocrisy

teaches them that theology is a tool for manipulation rather than a call

to transformation.

When the church normalizes the gap between its words and its behavior,

it does not produce a generation that simply drifts away. It produces a

generation that is actively inoculated against the gospel---because they

have seen what they believe to be the gospel lived out, and what they

saw was not good news. It was tribal power dressed in religious

language.

If we are producing young people who look at the church and see not the

beauty of Christ but the ugliness of partisan tribalism---if the fruit

of our "engagement" is a generation that finds the gospel unbelievable

not because of its claims but because of its claimants---then we need to

stop blaming the culture and start examining ourselves.

**The Specific Hypocrisies That Are Costing Us**

Let me be specific, because vague accusations of hypocrisy are easy to

dismiss. The hypocrisies that are driving the next generation away are

concrete, visible, and well-documented.

**The character reversal.** For decades, evangelical churches taught

that character is the most important qualification for leadership---in

the home, in the church, and in public office. This was not a

controversial position. It was the position. Sermons were preached about

it. Books were written about it. It was the stated reason for political

engagement: we need leaders of character, leaders who reflect biblical

values, leaders whose personal integrity matches their public promises.

And then, in the span of a single election cycle, that position was

abandoned. Not revised. Not nuanced. Abandoned. Character stopped being

the primary criterion and became an afterthought---something to be

excused, explained away, or simply ignored because the political stakes

were deemed too high.

The watching generation noticed. And what they concluded was not that

the church had made a difficult pragmatic calculation. What they

concluded was that the church had never meant it in the first place. The

"character matters" conviction was exposed, in their eyes, as a weapon

deployed selectively rather than a principle held consistently.

**The compassion inversion.** The Jesus of the Gospels is consistently

portrayed as one who moves toward the vulnerable: the sick, the outcast,

the foreigner, the poor, the powerless. The church has historically been

at its most beautiful when it has done the same---building hospitals,

establishing schools, feeding the hungry, welcoming refugees, visiting

prisoners.

But when political alignment demands hostility toward the

vulnerable---when immigrants become "invaders," when the poor become

"lazy," when refugees become "threats," when compassion is reframed as

weakness and cruelty is celebrated as strength---the watching generation

sees a faith that has betrayed its own Savior. They do not see a church

navigating complexity. They see a church that has chosen its political

tribe over its theological identity.

**The truth crisis.** Christianity is built on truth claims. The

resurrection is either historically true or it is not. The Scriptures

are either trustworthy or they are not. The character of God is either

faithful or it is not. The entire apologetic enterprise depends on the

claim that truth matters---that reality is knowable, that lies are

destructive, that integrity in speech reflects integrity of soul.

And then the watching generation sees churches---and church

leaders---tolerating, repeating, and sometimes enthusiastically

promoting claims that are demonstrably false. Conspiracy theories are

shared without scrutiny. Misinformation is spread without correction.

Leaders who lie repeatedly and provably are defended not by arguments

for their truthfulness but by arguments for why their lying doesn't

matter because the other side is worse.

When the community that claims to serve the God of truth becomes a

community that tolerates untruth whenever it is politically convenient,

the foundation of every other truth claim---including the gospel

itself---is undermined. Because if truth is only valued when it serves

the tribe, then truth is not actually valued. It is instrumentalized.

And a generation that has watched truth be instrumentalized will not

easily believe that the truth claims of the gospel are anything

different.

**The enemy-love gap.** Jesus said, "Love your enemies and pray for

those who persecute you" (Matthew 5:44). This is not an optional

addendum to the faith. It is one of the most distinctive and radical

commands in the entire New Testament. It is what sets Christian ethics

apart from every other ethical system in the ancient world. It is the

command that made pagans stare in astonishment at the early church.

And the watching generation sees a church that has largely abandoned it.

Not in theory---no one has officially removed enemy-love from the

doctrinal statement. But in practice, political opponents are spoken

about with a contempt, mockery, and dehumanization that bears no

resemblance to anything Jesus ever modeled. The people we are supposed

to love are the people we most visibly hate. And the generation that

notices this does not think, "Well, enemy-love is aspirational." They

think, "They don't actually believe any of this."

Answering the Objection: "They're Leaving Because of Culture, Not the

Church"

This is the objection I hear most frequently from church leaders, and it

needs to be addressed with care, because it contains a genuine insight

wrapped in a dangerous deflection.

The genuine insight: cultural pressures on young believers are real and

significant. The ubiquity of pornography has affected sexual ethics and

relational expectations. Social media has created comparison cultures

that breed anxiety and dissatisfaction. Universities have, in some

cases, created environments hostile to religious conviction. The

entertainment industry often portrays Christianity as bigoted, outdated,

or irrelevant. The sexual revolution has made the church's teaching on

sexuality the most socially costly position a young person can hold. All

of this is true.

The dangerous deflection: using these real cultural pressures as a

shield against self-examination. Because the logic runs like this: "If

young people are leaving because of TikTok and universities, then the

church doesn't need to change. The culture needs to change. And the way

to change the culture is more political power."

That logic is a closed loop. It starts with the assumption that the

church is fine and the culture is the problem, and it ends by demanding

more of the very thing---political power and cultural control---that is

alienating the next generation in the first place.

The honest truth is that both factors are at work simultaneously.

Cultural pressures are pulling young people away from the church. And

the church's own behavior is pushing them. The pull factors are real.

But the push factors are the ones we can actually do something

about---because they are ours to repent of.

Here is what pastors know in their bones but are often afraid to say out

loud: a young person who sees the church living out the gospel with

integrity, compassion, honesty, and sacrificial love can withstand

enormous cultural pressure. The faith is not so fragile that a college

philosophy class can destroy it---if it has been formed by a community

that embodies what it preaches. What makes the cultural pressures

devastating is not their strength. It is the weakness of the church's

witness. When the culture says "Christianity is hypocritical" and the

young person's lived experience in the church confirms it, the cultural

critique sticks. But when the culture says "Christianity is

hypocritical" and the young person's lived experience in the church

contradicts it, the critique bounces off.

The church's greatest apologetic has never been its arguments. It has

been its life. And when the life of the church validates the world's

critique rather than refuting it, no amount of apologetics, youth

programming, or political activism will keep the next generation.

**The Witness of 1 Peter: Beauty in a Hostile World**

The New Testament provides a remarkable guide for exactly this

situation---a situation where the surrounding culture is hostile,

skeptical, and predisposed to think the worst of the church. And the

strategy it prescribes is not political power. It is visible, undeniable

goodness.

The apostle Peter, writing to Christians scattered across a hostile

Roman world, does not tell them to seize political influence. He does

not tell them to fight for cultural dominance. He does not tell them to

find a sympathetic Caesar. He tells them to live so beautifully that

even their critics will be silenced:

> *"Keep your conduct among the Gentiles honorable, so that when they speak against you as evildoers, they may see your good deeds and glorify God on the day of visitation." ---1 Peter 2:12*

Peter's strategy is extraordinary. He does not deny that the world will

misrepresent Christians. He assumes it. He expects slander, false

accusations, and hostility. But his response is not outrage, not

political maneuvering, not culture war. His response is: live so well

that the accusation becomes absurd. Let your conduct be the rebuttal.

Let your love be the apologetic. Let the sheer quality of your life

together make the slander unsustainable.

He continues:

> *"But even if you should suffer for righteousness' sake, you will be blessed. Have no fear of them, nor be troubled, but in your hearts honor Christ the Lord as holy, always being prepared to make a defense to anyone who asks you for a reason for the hope that is in you; yet do it with gentleness and respect, having a good conscience, so that, when you are slandered, those who revile your good behavior in Christ may be put to shame." ---1 Peter 3:14--16*

Notice the sequence. First, honor Christ as holy in your hearts---that

is, make sure your ultimate allegiance is rightly ordered. Second, be

prepared to give a reason for your hope---not for your political

platform, not for your cultural agenda, but for your *hope*. Third, do

it with gentleness and respect. Fourth, maintain a good conscience so

that the slander has nowhere to land.

This is the early church's strategy for credibility in a hostile world.

And it could not be more different from what the American church is

currently doing.

We are not giving people a reason for our hope. We are giving them a

reason for our outrage. We are not demonstrating gentleness and respect.

We are demonstrating contempt and tribal aggression. We are not

maintaining a good conscience. We are excusing behavior that violates

our stated convictions because the political stakes feel too high.

And the result is exactly what Peter's strategy was designed to prevent:

the watching world---including the watching next generation---has

credible grounds for dismissing the church. Not because the gospel is

false, but because the community that proclaims it is not living as

though it is true.

**Titus 2 and the Doctrine That Depends on Living**

Paul's letter to Titus provides one of the most striking connections

between conduct and doctrine in the entire New Testament. Writing to a

young pastor on the island of Crete---a culture that was famously

difficult and morally chaotic---Paul instructs Titus to teach sound

doctrine. But he ties the teaching to something unexpected: the behavior

of the community.

He tells older men to be self-controlled and sound in faith. He tells

older women to be reverent in behavior and to teach what is good. He

tells younger men to be models of good works. He tells slaves to be

trustworthy in every way. And then he gives the reason---and the reason

is stunning:

> *"...so that in everything they may adorn the doctrine of God our Savior." ---Titus 2:10*

Adorn the doctrine. The Greek word is *kosmeō*---from which we get

"cosmetic." It means to make beautiful, to arrange attractively, to

display in a way that draws admiration. Paul is saying that the behavior

of the community is what makes the doctrine *beautiful* to the watching

world.

The doctrine, by itself, is true. But truth that is not embodied is not

compelling. Truth that contradicts the lives of those who proclaim it is

not merely unpersuasive---it is repulsive. The doctrine needs to be

"adorned"---dressed in the clothing of a community that actually lives

it---before it becomes attractive to the world.

Now apply this to our moment. What is the American church's conduct

currently "adorning"? What does the world see when it looks at us?

If what the world sees is a community defined by political tribalism,

moral inconsistency, selective outrage, and a willingness to compromise

integrity for political gain---then we are not adorning the doctrine. We

are defacing it. We are taking the most beautiful message in the history

of the world and making it ugly.

And the generation that sees the ugliness is not wrong to recoil. They

are responding to exactly what Paul said they would respond to: the

doctrine, when lived badly, repels rather than attracts. The problem is

not with the doctrine. The problem is with the community that has made

the doctrine unrecognizable.

**A Credibility Rebuild Plan**

If the diagnosis is credibility loss, then the prescription is not

better marketing, more engaging youth programs, or trendier worship

services. The prescription is repentance that produces visible,

sustained, costly change.

What would a credibility rebuild look like for the American church? It

would require at least the following:

**Consistency of moral standards regardless of political alignment.**

The church must hold the same moral standards for leaders on "its side"

as it holds for leaders on the other side. If dishonesty disqualifies an

opponent, it must disqualify an ally. If cruelty is condemned in the

other party, it must be condemned in ours. If sexual misconduct matters

when the other side does it, it must matter when our side does it. This

is not political neutrality. It is moral integrity. And it is the bare

minimum of prophetic credibility.

**Public repentance for specific, named failures.** Vague confessions of

"imperfection" are not repentance. The watching generation has seen

specific behaviors, specific endorsements, specific compromises. They

need to hear specific acknowledgments. "We were wrong to excuse this."

"We should not have tolerated that." "We compromised our witness here,

and we are repenting of it." The specificity of the repentance must

match the specificity of the failure, or it will be dismissed as public

relations rather than genuine contrition.

A recovery of the church's independence from partisan

identification. The church must become a community that no political

party can claim. Not because the church has no political

convictions---it does, and it should---but because its convictions are

derived from Scripture and the lordship of Christ rather than from a

party platform. When the church's positions line up with the left on

some issues and with the right on others---when it is consistently

inconsistent with every earthly faction because its allegiance is to a

heavenly King---then and only then will its prophetic voice regain

credibility.

**Visible, sacrificial love that cannot be explained by self-interest.**

The early church gained credibility by caring for people who could not

benefit them---plague victims, abandoned infants, widows, prisoners. The

watching generation needs to see the same. They need to see a church

that serves immigrants regardless of their legal status, that feeds the

hungry regardless of their voting record, that visits prisoners

regardless of their crimes, that cares for the poor regardless of

whether the poor are "deserving." When the church's love is

indiscriminate---when it mirrors the God who "sends rain on the just and

the unjust" (Matthew 5:45)---the gospel becomes visible in a way that no

sermon, no book, and no political victory can achieve.

Honest engagement with the generation's questions rather than

dismissal of their concerns. The next generation is not asking these

questions because they are rebellious, lazy, or corrupted by culture.

Many of them are asking because they love Jesus and are grieved by what

they see. They deserve to be heard, not lectured. They deserve honest

answers, not defensive deflections. They deserve a church that treats

their departure as a wound to be healed rather than a betrayal to be

punished.

**What Is at Stake**

Let me be as direct as I can about what is at stake.

We are not merely losing a generation to secularism. We are losing a

generation to disillusionment---and disillusionment is far harder to

overcome than doubt. A person who doubts the gospel can be reached with

evidence, with argument, with testimony, with encounter. A person who is

disillusioned with the church has already seen the testimony---and found

it wanting. They are not asking "Is this true?" They are saying "I

watched people who claimed it was true, and it didn't change them."

That is a different problem. And it requires a different solution.

The solution is not more information. The solution is not better

programs. The solution is not political victory that will make the

church culturally dominant again so that the next generation has no

choice but to participate.

The solution is a church that looks like Jesus.

A church that tells the truth even when it is costly. A church that

loves its enemies even when it is ridiculed. A church that holds moral

standards consistently even when it is inconvenient. A church that cares

for the vulnerable even when there is no political reward. A church that

repents publicly even when it is humiliating. A church that would rather

lose every political battle than lose its integrity. A church that knows

the difference between winning an election and winning a soul.

That kind of church has always been the most powerful apologetic in the

world. Not because it is perfect---no church is. But because it is

honest about its imperfection, relentless in its pursuit of

faithfulness, and visibly shaped by the Savior it proclaims.

The next generation is not looking for a perfect church. They are

looking for a real one. And "real" means a community where the gap

between confession and conduct is acknowledged, lamented, and actively

closed---not defended, justified, or dismissed.

Paul told the Corinthians that when an unbeliever walks into a faithful

assembly and encounters genuine worship and authentic community, the

response should be to fall on their face and declare, "God is really

among you" (1 Corinthians 14:25).

When the next generation walks into our assemblies, are they falling on

their faces? Or are they walking out the door?

The answer depends not on our doctrine---our doctrine is fine. It

depends on whether our lives match what our doctrine claims.

And if they do not, then the most urgent task before the American church

is not to win the next election. It is to become the kind of community

that makes the next generation say, against all cultural pressure, "God

is really among you."

That is the generational cost of hypocrisy: not merely empty pews, but a

generation that has concluded the pews were empty of God long before

they were empty of people.

And that conclusion, however painful, may be a gift---if we have the

courage to hear it as a call to repentance rather than a reason for

resentment.

**Key Takeaways**

> *• The generational exodus from the church is not primarily a story about secularism winning---it is a story about credibility lost. Young adults describe the church as hypocritical and politically entangled, not merely intellectually implausible. • Systemic hypocrisy communicates that the church's stated values were never real---that character, truth, love, and humility were always tools for tribal power rather than genuine convictions. • Jesus reserved His fiercest condemnation for the Pharisees' hypocrisy---not because their theology was wrong, but because the gap between their teaching and their lives made them factories of disbelief (Matthew 23:15). • The specific hypocrisies driving the next generation away are concrete: the character reversal, the compassion inversion, the truth crisis, and the abandonment of enemy-love in practice. • Blaming culture alone for the exodus is a dangerous deflection---the pull factors are real, but the push factors (the church's own failures) are the ones we can repent of. • Peter's strategy for credibility in a hostile world is not political power but visible, undeniable goodness---conduct that silences slander because it cannot be denied (1 Peter 2:12). • Paul teaches that the community's conduct "adorns" the doctrine (Titus 2:10)---making it beautiful or ugly depending on whether the lives match the words. • The most urgent task before the American church is not winning the next election but becoming the kind of community that makes the next generation say, "God is really among you."*

**CHAPTER TEN**

*Cross and Sword: Two Theologies of Power*

> *"Propaganda can fill arenas. It cannot raise the dead."*

• • •

**The Question Behind Every Question**

We have spent nine chapters diagnosing a disease. We have named it

*zanah*---relocated trust. We have traced it through Israel's alliances,

through the crowd's demand for a political messiah, through the

domestication of the confession "Jesus is Lord," through the

Constantinian bargain, through Germany's catastrophe, through the

generational wreckage of hypocrisy. We have watched the same pattern

repeat across centuries and continents: fear drives the people of God

toward political power, political power promises protection, and the

church trades its prophetic soul for a seat at the table.

But beneath every chapter, beneath every historical example and every

scriptural text, there has been one question pulsing like a heartbeat:

> ***How does God actually conquer?***

That is the question this chapter must answer. Because everything

else---every diagnostic marker, every reflection question, every call to

repentance---depends on whether we believe God's method of victory is

sufficient. If we do not trust the way God conquers, we will inevitably

supplement it with the way the world conquers. If we believe that the

cross is God's definitive revelation of power, then the entire posture

of the church in relation to political authority changes. If we do

not---if we secretly believe the cross was a necessary but insufficient

step that must be supplemented by legislation, cultural dominance, and

the machinery of coercion---then we will keep running back to Egypt no

matter how many times the prophet calls us home.

So this is where the argument arrives at its climax. Not a political

strategy. Not a policy recommendation. A theology of power.

Specifically, the question of whether the church operates by the logic

of the cross or the logic of the sword---and what happens when it

confuses the two.

**Two Kingdoms, Two Logics**

The New Testament does not pretend that political authority is

irrelevant or illegitimate. Paul's letter to the Romans includes one of

the most discussed passages on government in all of Scripture:

> *"Let every person be subject to the governing authorities. For there is no authority except from God, and those that exist have been instituted by God\\.. For he is God's servant for your good. But if you do wrong, be afraid, for he does not bear the sword in vain. For he is the servant of God, an avenger who carries out God's wrath on the wrongdoer." ---Romans 13:1, 4*

This passage is important, and it is routinely abused in two opposite

directions. On one side, it is flattened into an unconditional command

to obey whatever the government says---a reading that would have made

nonsense of the early church's refusal to worship Caesar, of Peter's

declaration that "we must obey God rather than men" (Acts 5:29), and of

the entire book of Revelation, which portrays the Roman state as a beast

demanding illegitimate worship. On the other side, it is dismissed as

situational advice with no enduring theological significance---a reading

that ignores Paul's deliberate, theological grounding of political

authority in God's ordering of creation.

The passage does something more nuanced than either extreme allows. It

acknowledges that the state has a God-given role: the restraint of evil,

the maintenance of order, the protection of the vulnerable through the

exercise of coercive force. The state bears the sword. That is its

vocation. And within its proper limits, that vocation is legitimate.

Christians can affirm this without embarrassment.

But notice what Paul does *not* say. He does not say the state bears the

cross. He does not say the state's method is the church's method. He

does not say that because government uses coercion to restrain evil, the

church should use coercion to advance the gospel. He does not collapse

the distinction between how the state operates and how the church

operates.

The state restrains evil through force. The church overcomes evil

through witness, service, sacrifice, and the power of the Holy Spirit.

These are not the same logic. They are not interchangeable methods. And

the confusion of the two---the assumption that the church can or should

adopt the state's tools to accomplish the church's mission---is one of

the most persistent and destructive errors in the history of

Christianity.

Paul himself makes the distinction explicit in his second letter to the

Corinthians:

> *"For though we walk in the flesh, we are not waging war according to the flesh. For the weapons of our warfare are not of the flesh but have divine power to destroy strongholds." ---2 Corinthians 10:3--4*

The weapons of our warfare are not of the flesh. That sentence should be

tattooed on the conscience of every Christian who has ever believed that

the church needs the instruments of political coercion to accomplish its

mission. Paul is not saying the church does not fight. It fights

fiercely. But the weapons are different. The strategy is different. The

entire logic of engagement is different.

The state uses the sword. The church uses the word. The state compels.

The church persuades. The state enforces compliance. The church invites

transformation. The state punishes wrongdoing. The church absorbs

it---and then rises from the dead.

These two logics can coexist. A Christian can affirm the state's

legitimate use of force while simultaneously insisting that the church's

mode of operation is fundamentally different. You can support the police

while refusing to police the conscience. You can affirm the necessity of

national defense while insisting that the church does not need military

metaphors to describe its mission---at least, not the kind that involve

actual weapons.

The problem arises when the church forgets which logic it operates

under. When the church begins to believe that it needs the sword to

survive---that without legislative protection, cultural dominance, and

political leverage, the gates of hell will prevail---it has adopted the

state's logic and abandoned its own. It has traded the weapons of divine

power for the weapons of the flesh. And in doing so, it has implicitly

declared that the cross was not enough.

**The Cross as God's Definitive Act of Power**

This is where we must stop being sentimental about the cross.

In much of American Christianity, the cross has been reduced to a symbol

of personal forgiveness---the mechanism by which individual sinners

receive a ticket to heaven. And it is that. The cross is the place where

the sins of the world were borne by the sinless Son of God, where the

penalty that justice demanded was paid in full, where the wrath of God

against human rebellion was absorbed by the one person who did not

deserve it. That is glorious, and it is true, and it is the heart of the

gospel.

But the cross is more than a mechanism of personal salvation. The cross

is God's definitive revelation of how He conquers.

Rome used crucifixion to dominate. It was the empire's most eloquent

statement of power: "We can do this to you. We can strip you naked, nail

you to wood, and leave you hanging at the intersection where the most

people will see your agony. We can turn your body into a public service

announcement about what happens to those who resist." Crucifixion was

not merely execution. It was political theater. It was the state's

ultimate argument: comply, or this is what awaits you.

And God took Rome's most powerful instrument of domination and turned it

into the instrument of redemption.

The resurrection did not come through military revolt. It did not come

through political negotiation. It did not come through the careful

accumulation of cultural influence. It came through a man hanging on a

cross, absorbing the full weight of human evil into Himself, refusing to

retaliate, and then walking out of a tomb three days later.

That is how God conquers. Not by exercising superior force. Not by

out-coercing the coercers. Not by assembling a more powerful army or

winning a more decisive election. God conquers by absorbing evil,

bearing its full weight, and refusing to let it reproduce through Him.

He breaks the cycle of violence not by winning the fight on evil's terms

but by refusing to fight on evil's terms altogether.

Fleming Rutledge, in her magisterial work *The Crucifixion*, argues that

the cross is not God's concession to weakness. It is God's revelation of

strength. The power of God is not demonstrated by the avoidance of

suffering but by the transformation of suffering into the instrument of

redemption. The cross does not display God's inability to fight back. It

displays His refusal to fight back on the terms the world

offers---because fighting on the world's terms would reproduce the very

evil He came to destroy.

Paul understood this with devastating clarity:

> *"For the word of the cross is folly to those who are perishing, but to us who are being saved it is the power of God\\.. For the foolishness of God is wiser than men, and the weakness of God is stronger than men." ---1 Corinthians 1:18, 25*

The cross is foolishness to the perishing because it does not look like

power. Power, in the world's estimation, looks like Rome. It looks like

chariots. It looks like a congressional majority. It looks like cultural

dominance and the ability to enforce your will on those who resist. The

cross looks like none of those things. The cross looks like defeat. It

looks like weakness. It looks like the end.

And that is precisely why it is the power of God. Because it defeats

evil in a way that evil cannot understand, cannot anticipate, and cannot

replicate. You can out-fight a warrior. You can out-strategize a

politician. You can out-spend an empire. But you cannot defeat a man who

is willing to die rather than become what you are. You cannot conquer a

community whose response to persecution is forgiveness, whose response

to hatred is love, whose response to death is the unshakable confidence

that the grave is not the final word.

The early church understood this. That is why they conquered the Roman

Empire---not by out-powering it, but by *out-dying* it, *out-loving* it,

and *out-hoping* it. They offered something the empire could not

provide: a community where love was stronger than fear, where death had

been defeated, where the powers of this world had been relativized by

the power of a resurrection.

**Revelation 5: The Lamb Who Conquered**

The book of Revelation provides the most vivid and theologically dense

portrait of Christ's mode of conquest in the entire New Testament. And

the portrait is deliberately, shockingly subversive.

In Revelation 5, John sees a scroll sealed with seven seals---a scroll

that represents God's plan for the redemption of all things. No one in

heaven or on earth is found worthy to open the scroll. John weeps. The

plan of God seems stuck, the future locked shut.

Then one of the elders speaks:

> *"Weep no more; behold, the Lion of the tribe of Judah, the Root of David, has conquered, so that he can open the scroll and its seven seals." ---Revelation 5:5*

A lion. The imagery is unmistakable. Power, ferocity, royal authority,

military conquest. The Lion of Judah has conquered. John turns to

look---expecting, as anyone in the ancient world would, to see a

creature of overwhelming strength.

And what does he see?

> *"And between the throne and the four living creatures and among the elders I saw a Lamb standing, as though it had been slain." ---Revelation 5:6*

John hears "Lion." He sees "Lamb." He hears the language of military

triumph. He sees the evidence of sacrificial death.

This is not a mistake. It is the most important interpretive key in the

book of Revelation---and arguably in the entire New Testament. The Lion

*is* the Lamb. The conqueror *is* the slain one. The one who has

triumphed over every power in the cosmos has done so not by wielding a

sword but by being slaughtered. Victory and sacrifice are not opposites.

In God's economy, sacrifice *is* victory. Death *is* the means of

conquest. The Lamb who was slain is the only one worthy to open the

scroll and execute God's plan for history.

Richard Bauckham, one of the foremost scholars of Revelation, argues

that this Lion-Lamb juxtaposition redefines the concept of power for the

entire Christian community. When you hear "power" and expect a lion but

see a lamb, the word "power" itself has been transformed. You can no

longer use the word the way Rome uses it. You can no longer use the word

the way political movements use it. Power, in the Kingdom of God, looks

like a slaughtered Lamb standing in the throne room of the universe.

And notice: the Lamb is *standing*. It has been slain, but it is not

dead. It bears the marks of its suffering, but it is alive. This is the

resurrection---the vindication of the path of sacrifice. God did not

rescue Jesus *from* the cross. He vindicated Jesus *through* the cross.

The suffering was not a detour on the way to victory. The suffering

*was* the victory. And the resurrection is the Father's declaration that

this is how He conquers, this is how He redeems, this is how He

establishes His Kingdom---not through the sword, but through the

self-giving love of the Lamb.

Then heaven responds:

> *"Worthy is the Lamb who was slain, to receive power and wealth and wisdom and might and honor and glory and blessing!" ---Revelation 5:12*

Power belongs to the Lamb. Not to the empire. Not to the political

machine. Not to the cultural movement that promises to restore Christian

influence. Power---real, ultimate, universe-shaping power---belongs to

the one who was slaughtered. And the elders and the living creatures do

not debate this or qualify it or add footnotes about political strategy.

They worship.

That is the theology of power the church is called to embody. And it is

a theology that the politically captive church has largely forgotten.

**How the Saints Conquer: Revelation 12**

If Revelation 5 shows how Christ conquers, Revelation 12 shows how His

people do. And the mode of victory is the same.

> *"And they have conquered him by the blood of the Lamb and by the word of their testimony, for they loved not their lives even unto death." ---Revelation 12:11*

Three weapons. Not one of them is a sword.

The blood of the Lamb. The word of their testimony. The willingness to

die.

The saints in Revelation do not conquer the dragon by organizing a more

effective political coalition. They do not conquer by electing a

sympathetic emperor. They do not conquer by accumulating enough cultural

capital to marginalize their opponents. They conquer by holding fast to

their confession---"Jesus is Lord"---even when that confession costs

them everything.

Their political action is worship. Their resistance is faithfulness.

Their revolution is the refusal to say "Caesar is Lord" even when the

alternative is death.

This is not passivity. Revelation 12:11 is a victory statement. "They

have conquered." The verb is *enikēsan*---they overcame, they prevailed,

they won. But the manner of their victory is what stuns the imagination.

They won by dying. They prevailed by refusing to save themselves. They

overcame the dragon by loving faithfulness more than survival.

Now ask yourself: Does that sound like the American church right now?

Does the church in America look like a community that has conquered its

fear of death? Does it look like a people who love not their lives even

unto death? Or does it look like a people who are terrified of losing

cultural influence, political access, tax-exempt status, and the

comfortable assumption that America is "our" country?

A church that is afraid of losing cannot conquer in the manner of

Revelation 12. Because Revelation 12's victory depends on the

willingness to lose everything except the confession. And a church that

has made political power essential to its survival has, by definition,

added a requirement that the New Testament never imposes.

The early church did not need a friendly emperor to be faithful. It did

not need legislative protection to proclaim the gospel. It did not need

cultural dominance to transform lives. It needed the blood of the Lamb,

the word of its testimony, and the courage to die.

That is still all it needs.

**What Happens When the Church Picks Up the Sword**

History provides devastating evidence of what happens when the church

abandons the logic of the cross and adopts the logic of the sword. We do

not need to speculate. We only need to remember.

The Crusades were launched in the name of Christ and drenched the Holy

Land in blood. The Inquisition used the instruments of state coercion to

enforce theological conformity and tortured those who dissented. The

wars of religion that followed the Reformation---Catholic against

Protestant, Protestant against Protestant---killed millions in the name

of doctrines that were supposed to bring peace. The colonial missions of

the sixteenth through nineteenth centuries used the cross as a vanguard

for imperial conquest, so that to this day, in vast portions of the

global South, Christianity is associated with cultural destruction,

slavery, and the theft of land.

Every one of these catastrophes occurred when the church adopted the

state's logic---when it decided that the way to advance the Kingdom was

through coercion, control, and the exercise of political power. Every

one of them represents a betrayal of the Lamb's mode of conquest. And

every one of them did incalculable damage to the credibility of the

gospel.

The argument is not that every Christian who engages in politics is

heading toward the Crusades. That is a caricature, and caricatures do

not persuade. The argument is that there is a structural logic at work:

when the church treats political power as essential to its mission, the

methods of political power inevitably reshape the church's character.

You cannot wield the sword without being shaped by the sword. You cannot

adopt the state's tools without absorbing the state's instincts.

Jesus warned about this in the most direct terms possible:

> *"Put your sword back into its place. For all who take the sword will perish by the sword." ---Matthew 26:52*

He said this to Peter---the same Peter who had confessed Him as the

Christ, the same Peter who would later deny Him, the same Peter who

would eventually die a martyr's death. Peter reached for the sword

because he wanted to protect Jesus. The motive was understandable. The

love was genuine. The instinct was human and even admirable.

And Jesus rebuked it. Not because defending the innocent is always

wrong. But because in that moment, the sword represented an alternative

to the cross. It represented the belief that what God was about to

accomplish through suffering could be improved upon by violence. It

represented the same temptation that had followed Jesus from the

wilderness to Caesarea Philippi to Gethsemane: bypass the cross, take

the shortcut, fight the battle on the world's terms.

The church that picks up the sword may win battles. It may secure

cultural influence. It may preserve its tax exemptions and its social

respectability and its access to the halls of power. But it will lose

something far more valuable than anything it gains: it will lose its

resemblance to Jesus. And a church that no longer resembles Jesus is no

longer the church, regardless of how many seats it holds in Congress.

**The Church That Chooses the Cross**

So what does it look like, practically, for the church to operate by the

logic of the cross rather than the logic of the sword? What does

cross-shaped power look like in the real world, in real communities, in

real political engagement?

It looks like a church that tells the truth when truth is costly. The

politically captive church has learned to be silent when silence is

strategic---to overlook the sins of its preferred leaders, to mute its

prophetic voice when speaking up might jeopardize access. A cross-shaped

church tells the truth to both parties, both movements, both sides of

every cultural divide. It speaks with the specificity that makes

everyone uncomfortable, because the prophetic tradition does not deal in

vague generalities. Nathan did not say to David, "Some people have done

some bad things." He said, "You are the man." A church that cannot name

specific sins in its own political tribe has been captured by that

tribe.

It looks like a church that loves its enemies visibly and concretely.

Not in theory. Not in sermon illustrations. In practice. It means

Christians across the political divide worshiping together, serving

together, and refusing to allow political disagreement to become

relational destruction. It means caring for people your party has

labeled as threats. It means pursuing the good of communities that did

not vote the way you did. It means demonstrating, in the flesh, that

baptism creates a deeper bond than voter registration.

It looks like a church that serves the vulnerable without leveraging

that service for political gain. When the church cares for the poor,

visits the prisoner, feeds the hungry, and welcomes the stranger, it

must do so because Jesus commanded it---not because it produces good

optics, not because it builds a platform, not because it positions the

church as a useful partner for whatever administration happens to be in

power. Service that is contaminated by political strategy is no longer

service. It is marketing. And the watching world can tell the

difference.

It looks like a church that suffers without retaliating. This is the

hardest and most essential mark of cross-shaped power. When the church

is criticized, marginalized, mocked, or legally constrained, its

response reveals which theology of power it actually holds. If the

response is outrage, litigation, political retaliation, and the

mobilization of cultural warfare, the church is operating by the sword's

logic. If the response is patient endurance, continued faithfulness,

undiminished love, and the quiet confidence that the tomb is

empty---that is the logic of the cross.

Peter, the same disciple who once reached for the sword, eventually

learned the difference:

> *"For to this you have been called, because Christ also suffered for you, leaving you an example, so that you might follow in his steps. He committed no sin, neither was deceit found in his mouth. When he was reviled, he did not revile in return; when he suffered, he did not threaten, but continued entrusting himself to him who judges justly." ---1 Peter 2:21--23*

When He was reviled, He did not revile in return. When He suffered, He

did not threaten. He entrusted Himself to the one who judges justly.

That is cross-shaped power. It is not weakness. It is the most radical

form of strength available to human beings: the strength to absorb evil

without reproducing it, to endure suffering without becoming the kind of

person who inflicts it, to trust that God's judgment is more reliable

than your retaliation.

And it is the only kind of power that the resurrection vindicates.

**Answering the Objection: "This Sounds Like Surrender"**

Let me anticipate the strongest objection to everything I have written

in this chapter. Someone is reading this and thinking: "This is naive.

This is pie-in-the-sky idealism. This is the theology of people who have

never had to protect their families, their churches, or their

communities from real threats. In the real world, you need power. In the

real world, the cross does not stop legislation. In the real world, you

cannot love your enemies into policy change."

I want to take that objection seriously, because it is not trivial. Real

evil exists. Real injustice exists. Real threats to religious liberty,

to the vulnerable, to the common good---these are not imaginary. And the

Bible itself affirms that the state has a role in restraining evil

through the exercise of force. Romans 13 is in the canon. It is not

going away.

But notice what the objection assumes. It assumes that the only

alternative to political power is passivity. It assumes that if the

church does not wield the sword, it must be sitting on its hands. It

assumes that the options are either political dominance or total

withdrawal.

That is a false binary. And it is the false binary that has trapped the

American church for decades.

The early church was not passive. It was the most active, most

transformative, most culturally disruptive movement in the history of

the ancient world. It fed the hungry. It cared for plague victims when

Roman elites fled the cities. It welcomed slaves and masters to the same

table. It created communities of radical equality, sacrificial

generosity, and stubborn hope in the face of imperial power. It told the

truth about who was really in charge. And it did all of this without a

single seat in the Roman Senate.

The early church did not have political power. It had something better:

spiritual authority. And spiritual authority came not from its proximity

to the throne but from its proximity to the cross.

So the question is not whether the church should act. Of course it

should act. The question is whether the church's action must take the

form of political domination in order to be effective. And the New

Testament's answer is no. The church's most powerful action has always

been its witness---its willingness to live so differently, love so

extravagantly, speak so truthfully, and die so courageously that the

watching world cannot explain it by any category other than "God is

among them."

That is not surrender. That is the most dangerous thing the church can

be. Because a church that operates by the logic of the cross cannot be

controlled by the logic of the sword. A church that does not need

political power cannot be manipulated by the promise of political power.

A church that has already died---that has taken up its cross and

followed Jesus into death and resurrection---has nothing left to lose.

And a church with nothing left to lose is, paradoxically, the most

powerful force on earth.

**Repent from Trusting, Not from Voting**

So here is the call. And it is a call to repentance---not the kind of

repentance that withdraws from the world, but the kind that reorders the

soul.

Repent from trusting. Not from voting.

Repent from the belief that the church needs political power to survive.

It does not. The church was born without it, grew without it, and has

repeatedly been purified by losing it.

Repent from the belief that the right president, the right Congress, the

right Supreme Court composition can secure what only Christ can secure.

They cannot. They never could. Every political arrangement in history

has been temporary. The Kingdom of God is not.

Repent from the fear that drives you to compromise your integrity for

political outcomes. Fear is not a strategy. Fear is a confession that

you have forgotten who is on the throne. And the one on the throne is

not sitting in the Oval Office. He is seated at the right hand of God,

and every knee will bow to Him---not because the church amassed enough

political capital to force compliance, but because He is the risen Lord

of the universe.

Repent from the contempt that your political engagement has produced. If

your involvement in politics has made you crueler, more tribal, more

willing to dehumanize people made in the image of God---then your

politics have been discipling you more effectively than your faith. The

fruit of the Spirit is love, joy, peace, patience, kindness, goodness,

faithfulness, gentleness, and self-control. If your political engagement

is producing rage, contempt, and self-righteousness, the root is

diseased, no matter how righteous the cause.

Repent from the substitution---the quiet, gradual, fear-driven

replacement of Christ as the center of your hope with a political

project, a political leader, or a political vision. Name it. Be

specific. Do not spiritualize it. Say what it is. Say, "I have treated

this movement as though it were the Kingdom." Say, "I have defended this

leader as though he were anointed in a way that no politician is." Say,

"I have made winning feel like salvation and losing feel like death."

And then do what Hosea 14 says:

> *"Assyria shall not save us; we will not ride on horses; and we will say no more, 'Our God,' to the work of our hands." ---Hosea 14:3*

Put the idol down. Stop calling it wisdom. Stop calling it realism. Stop

calling it responsible leadership. Call it what it is: relocated trust.

And come home.

Because the God who named the adultery is the same God who heals it:

> *"I will heal their apostasy; I will love them freely, for my anger has turned from them." ---Hosea 14:4*

Freely. Lavishly. Without a probationary period. Without a lecture. The

God of Hosea does not wait for you to clean yourself up before He

welcomes you back. He meets you in your mess. He heals what is broken.

He loves you back to life.

That is the God we return to. Not a policy platform. Not a cultural

vision. Not a political messiah. The crucified and risen Christ, who has

already defeated every power that has ever claimed ultimacy---including

the ones you are tempted to trust right now.

**What Is Actually Preserved by the Cross**

The church's future is not preserved by presidents, congresses, courts,

or cultural legislation.

The church's future is preserved by holiness---the kind of holiness that

makes the watching world curious about the God who produces it.

The church's future is preserved by prayer---not the kind of prayer that

lobbies heaven for partisan outcomes, but the kind that surrenders to

the God who is sovereign over every nation, every election, and every

empire that has ever risen or fallen.

The church's future is preserved by truth---spoken with courage, with

specificity, with consistency to both left and right, and with the

willingness to accept the cost of saying what no one wants to hear.

The church's future is preserved by the love of enemies---the one

command of Jesus that no political movement on earth has ever been

willing to endorse, the one practice that has no strategic value

whatsoever, and the one thing that most clearly marks a community as

belonging to the crucified God.

The church's future is preserved by gospel courage---the willingness to

proclaim the death and resurrection of Jesus Christ as the decisive

event in the history of the world, to call all people to repentance and

faith, and to do so without apology, without political calculation, and

without the need for any earthly power to validate the message.

These are the weapons of divine power. They are not impressive by the

world's standards. They do not fill arenas. They do not trend on social

media. They do not produce the rush of adrenaline that comes from

political victory.

But they raise the dead.

And a church that can raise the dead does not need to win an election to

prove it is alive.

**The Closing Word**

Propaganda can fill arenas. It cannot raise the dead.

A church that chooses propaganda over resurrection power may keep its

influence for a moment. But it will lose its soul.

A church that chooses the cross will lose---in the world's estimation.

It will be mocked. It will be marginalized. It will be told it is

irrelevant, naive, and weak. It will be tempted, every single day, to

pick up the sword and fight the battle on the world's terms.

But the way of Jesus always looks like it is losing right up until

Sunday morning.

And then everything changes.

So put down the sword. Pick up the cross. And trust the Lamb.

Because the one who stands between you and destruction is not riding a

chariot. He is not occupying an office. He is not building a coalition.

He is standing in a throne room, bearing the marks of His slaughter, and

every creature in heaven and on earth and under the earth is singing:

> ***"Worthy is the Lamb who was slain."***

That is the power. That is the victory. That is the only King who will

never disappoint, never betray, and never need your political project to

succeed.

Trust Him.

And let the sword go.

**Key Takeaways**

\- Romans 13 affirms the state's legitimate use of the sword to

restrain evil, but Paul never gives the church the sword---the

church's weapons are "not of the flesh" but have "divine power to

destroy strongholds" (2 Corinthians 10:3--4).

\- The cross is not merely the mechanism of personal salvation---it is

God's definitive revelation of how He conquers: by absorbing evil

rather than retaliating, by suffering rather than coercing.

\- Revelation 5 redefines power: John hears "Lion" but sees "Lamb." The

conqueror of the cosmos is the slain one. Victory and sacrifice are

the same act in God's economy.

\- The saints in Revelation 12 conquer by the blood of the Lamb, the

word of their testimony, and the willingness to die---not by

political coalitions, cultural dominance, or legislative strategy.

\- Every historical catastrophe where the church adopted the

sword---Crusades, Inquisition, wars of religion, colonial

missions---demonstrates that wielding the state's tools reshapes the

church's character.

\- The alternative to political power is not passivity---it is

spiritual authority: the church's most transformative moments have

come when it had the least political power and the greatest

proximity to the cross.

\- Repentance means not withdrawing from political engagement but

renouncing political trust as ultimate: "Assyria shall not save us."

\- The church's future is preserved by holiness, prayer, truth-telling,

enemy-love, and gospel courage---weapons that do not fill arenas but

can raise the dead.

**Reflection Questions**

> 1\. When you think about the church's future, does your confidence rest

primarily on the power of the Spirit or on the outcome of the next

election? What does your emotional response to political news reveal

about which theology of power you actually hold?

> 2\. Can you identify a specific instance where your church or community

adopted the logic of the sword---coercion, manipulation,

dehumanization of opponents---while claiming to serve the Kingdom of

God? What was the fruit?

> 3\. If the church lost all political influence tomorrow---no access, no

cultural clout, no legislative leverage---what would remain? Would

it be enough? Does the thought fill you with dread or with freedom?

> 4\. Read 1 Peter 2:21--23 slowly. When you are reviled, do you revile in

return? When you suffer political loss, do you threaten? Or do you

entrust yourself to the one who judges justly? Where is the gap

between Peter's description and your actual practice?

> 5\. What would it look like for your church to be the most loving, most

truthful, most generous community in your city---without any

political advantage whatsoever? Would that mission feel like enough?

> 6\. Has your political engagement over the past decade made you more

like the Lamb or more like the beast? Be honest about the fruit:

patience or rage, humility or contempt, faithfulness or compromise.

**Practices**

\- Personal: Read Revelation 5 every morning this week. Each day,

journal one way the Lion-Lamb vision challenges how you think about

power, victory, and the church's mission. Ask: "Where have I been

expecting a lion when God is sending a lamb?"

\- Personal: Identify one area where you have adopted the logic of the

sword---where you have responded to opposition with coercion,

contempt, or the desire to dominate rather than serve. Confess it

specifically. Ask God to replace the sword with the cross in that

area of your life.

\- Family: Read Matthew 26:47--56 together---Peter's use of the sword

and Jesus' rebuke. Discuss: Where has our family reached for the

"sword" (anger, retaliation, political hostility) when Jesus was

calling us to a different response? What would it look like to put

the sword back?

\- Church: Ask your congregation or small group to articulate the

church's mission using only the categories of Revelation 12:11---the

blood of the Lamb, the word of testimony, and the willingness to

suffer. Does the mission survive this filter? What would need to

change if it does not?
