\# INTRODUCTION: The Argument That Broke the American Church

\## Why Critical Race Theory Exposed What We Were Already Hiding

The American church did not have a race problem because Critical Race Theory arrived. The church had a race problem. CRT simply exposed it, and when people saw what was underneath, they panicked.

Some panicked on the right. They saw CRT as a Marxist assault on Christianity itself—an ideology that would reduce people to racial categories, weaponize guilt, and destroy the church from within. They mobilized. They passed resolutions. They drew lines. They said no.

Others panicked on the left. They saw CRT as finally naming what white Christianity had been hiding for four hundred years. They adopted it wholesale. They expected the white church to confess, repent, and dismantle itself. They said yes to everything they heard.

Both responses were panic. Neither was thinking.

This book refuses both.

\## What This Book Does Not Do

This is not a defense of Critical Race Theory. CRT has genuine problems. It operates from anthropological assumptions that contradict the Christian vision of the human person. It often settles for justice without reconciliation. It flattens complexity in service of a metanarrative. The Bible's vision of justice is more searching than CRT—not less—which means the Christian response to CRT cannot be capitulation.

This is also not a dismissal of CRT. The academics who built CRT—Derrick Bell, Kimberlé Crenshaw, Richard Delgado—were making specific claims about how law and social systems embed racial bias. Those claims deserve to be understood before they are rejected. Most people who attack CRT have never read it. That is intellectual cowardice dressed as conviction.

This book is something else. It asks: What does Scripture actually teach about race, ethnicity, justice, and the image of God? How did the American church—a church built on the gospel of reconciliation—become the institution that defended slavery, maintained segregation, and then panicked when confronted with its own history? And what would it look like if the church offered the world something more searching than either progressive ideology or conservative denial?

\## The Real Crisis

The crisis is not that CRT exists. The crisis is that when CRT arrived, the American church had no credible alternative. We had no theology of justice forged in suffering. We had no prophetic tradition that could speak with authority. We had a four-hundred-year history we had never honestly reckoned with.

So when CRT showed up saying, "Your systems are rigged, and you have been lying about it," the white church's response was not, "The Bible speaks more deeply to this than CRT does." The response was either defensive (CRT is Marxism, don't listen) or confused (Maybe CRT is right, we don't know what else to do).

Both responses revealed the same thing: the American church had lost its own voice on racial justice.

That is the real crisis.

\## What Scripture Says About This

The Bible has its own justice theology. It is not found in CRT textbooks. It is found in Exodus, in the prophets, in Jesus's reading of Isaiah in the synagogue, in Paul's letter to Philemon. It says that God cares about systems that oppress the vulnerable. It says that ethnicity is real and beautiful and never grounds for hierarchy. It says that the cross breaks every power structure—including racial ones. It says that justice must precede reconciliation, and reconciliation is possible only through the blood of Christ.

That vision is more demanding than CRT. It demands more. It offers more. And the American church has largely abandoned it.

The conservatives abandon it by denying systemic injustice exists. The progressives abandon it by adopting a secular structure that offers no power to actually reconcile divided people. Both fail to read their own Scripture.

\## Who This Book Is For

This book is for the Christian who is confused. You have heard that CRT is a threat. You have also heard that the church's silence on racial justice is a sin. You don't know who to trust. You want to think clearly about this. You want to know what Scripture actually says. You want to hold both prophetic conviction and pastoral love. You want to refuse both cheap conservatism and uncritical progressivism.

This book is for you.

It is also for the pastor who senses that his congregation is divided on this issue and he has no structure to lead them. It is for the Christian parent raising children in a world confused about race. It is for the person who has been harmed by the church's racial history and wants to know if there is anything in Scripture that offers something better than denial on one side or bitterness on the other.

We will start by actually understanding what CRT says. Not the caricature. Not the political attacks. What the academics actually wrote. You need to understand what you are disagreeing with.

Then we will turn to Scripture. What does the Bible teach about race and ethnicity? What does it say about systemic injustice? What does it demand of the people of God regarding the vulnerable and the oppressed?

Then we will do what the American church has largely refused to do: we will look honestly at our own history. How did the church defend slavery? What theology was used to justify segregation? When did evangelicals realign around race? What did we do during the civil rights movement? The answers are not comfortable. But you cannot understand the present crisis without understanding that history.

Then we will look at what CRT gets right—not because CRT is in the end true, but because the Bible also names these truths. We will look at where CRT gets it wrong—where it contradicts the gospel's anthropology, where it offers justice without reconciliation, where it flattens the complexity of the human person.

Finally, we will ask: What does the church owe the world on race? Not performative diversity. Not guilty self-flagellation. Not denial. But genuine justice pursued through the power of the gospel, leading to genuine reconciliation made possible only through Christ's blood.

\# CHAPTER 1: What Critical Race Theory Actually Says

\## A Honest Summary Before the Debate

Before you can honestly engage with Critical Race Theory, you have to know what it actually says. Not the caricature. Not what conservative or progressive activists claim it says. What the academics who created it wrote.

Most people have never read a single work by a CRT scholar. They have read Twitter attacks on CRT. They have heard sermons warning against it. They have seen listicles claiming it is Marxism. They have adopted or rejected an entire intellectual structure without understanding what is in it.

That is intellectual laziness. That is not thinking. That is panic.

If you are going to disagree with something, you owe it to yourself and to everyone listening to disagree with what it actually says.

\## The Origins: Derrick Bell and the Law School

Critical Race Theory was born in American law schools in the 1980s. Derrick Bell, a Black law professor at Harvard, was the intellectual father. Bell was not trying to create a revolutionary ideology. He was trying to answer a legal question: Why, despite the Civil Rights Act of 1964, did racial inequality persist in America?

The conservative answer is simple: the law is neutral now. Racism is individual. If you work hard, you will succeed. Any ongoing racial inequality is personal failure, not systemic bias.

Bell looked at the legal record and rejected this. He saw patterns. He saw that even after formal laws changed, the racial outcomes did not change proportionally. He saw that institutions continued to embed racial bias into their structures—not always through explicit rules, but through facially neutral rules that had racial effect.

This became Bell's central insight: racism is not merely an attitude individual people hold. Racism is embedded in how systems work.

That is not a sweeping claim in 2026. But it was distinctive when Bell made it in 1980.

\## The Second Wave: Kimberlé Crenshaw and Intersectionality

Derrick Bell's work was primarily about race and law. In the early 1990s, Kimberlé Crenshaw expanded the system. Her key contribution was a concept called "intersectionality."

Crenshaw observed something simple: a Black woman does not experience racism the way a Black man does. She does not experience sexism the way a white woman does. Her experience is not racism plus sexism. It is a distinct experience created by the intersection of race and gender together.

Crenshaw's argument was that people have multiple identities, and systems of power operate through multiple axes—race, gender, class, sexuality, disability. A person's lived experience depends on where they sit in relation to all of these axes simultaneously.

Again, this is not inherently revolutionary. It is an observation about complexity. But it became revolutionary when Crenshaw made it clear: if you want to understand injustice, you cannot study race without studying gender. You cannot study gender without studying class. The systems are interlocking.

\## The Expansion: Richard Delgado and Jean Stefancic

Richard Delgado and Jean Stefancic took CRT from the law school into a broader cultural analysis. They looked at how race had been understood in American law, American history, and American culture.

Their work identified several claims that together form what people call "Critical Race Theory":

\*\*Claim One: Race is a social construct, not a biological reality.\*\*

This is widely misunderstood. When CRT scholars say race is a "social construct," they do not mean race is not real. They mean that the categories "white," "Black," "Asian," etc. are human inventions. They were created to serve a function—to organize power. The biological reality is human genetic variation. The social reality is that we organized that variation into racial categories and built hierarchies on top of them.

This is historically true. The concept of whiteness, as we understand it, did not exist in the seventeenth century. It was constructed. Irish people were not "white." Jews were not "white." Mexicans were not "white." Over time, boundaries shifted. People moved in and out of whiteness. That is the definition of a social construct—it is human-made, not natural.

The Bible teaches the same thing. Genesis 10 lists nations. The prophets speak of tribes. Revelation 7 celebrates diverse peoples. Nowhere does Scripture teach that racial hierarchy is natural or God-ordained. If it has been constructed to justify injustice, it can be deconstructed.

\*\*Claim Two: Racism is systemic, not merely individual.\*\*

This is the claim that most threatens the conservative narrative. CRT scholars argue that racism is not primarily about individual prejudice. Racism is about power structures embedded in how institutions function.

An example: a bank denies a loan to a Black applicant. The loan officer might have no conscious racial animus. The bank's lending criteria might be written without any mention of race. But if the criteria privilege credit history, and if historical discrimination created a pattern of Black communities having less accumulated credit history, then the facially neutral criteria perpetuate racial outcomes.

Is that discrimination? Legally, it depends on intent. The bank did not intend racial discrimination. But the effect is racial discrimination.

CRT scholars say: the effect matters more than the intent. If your neutral systems produce racial outcomes, your systems are serving racism, whether or not your heart is racist.

This is where CRT becomes more searching than the law allows. The law asks: did you intend discrimination? CRT asks: do your systems perpetuate racial inequality? Those are different questions.

\*\*Claim Three: Lived experience of marginalized people is a legitimate source of knowledge.\*\*

This is the epistemological claim. It says: if you want to understand how racism actually operates in America, you should listen to people who have experienced it. Their lived experience is not anecdotal. It is data.

The Bible teaches this same thing. Psalm 82 says God hears the cry of the afflicted. Proverbs 31 says open your mouth for the mute. The prophets constantly say: you have not listened to the poor, the widow, the stranger. Their witness matters.

CRT scholars argue that American law and American policy have been designed by people in the majority group without adequately listening to people in the marginalized group. The result is that policy often misses the actual impact of systems on the people they affect.

\*\*Claim Four: The law can be an instrument of racial oppression.\*\*

This is obvious when you read American legal history. The law once explicitly protected slavery. The law created segregation. The law allowed redlining—the explicit denial of mortgages based on race. The law created Jim Crow.

But CRT's claim goes deeper. CRT scholars argue that even after formal laws changed, the legal system can continue to serve racial oppression through facially neutral mechanisms. The war on drugs, sentencing disparities, qualified immunity for police—these are not explicitly racial laws. But they have racial effects.

This is not a fringe argument. It is supported by mountains of data. Sentencing disparities between Black and white defendants for identical crimes are real. The racial impact of drug laws is real. The data is overwhelming.

\## What CRT Does NOT Say

Before we move on, let me name what CRT scholars do not actually claim, because this is where the caricature departs from reality.

CRT does not say all white people are oppressors. CRT does not say all Black people are victims. CRT does not say racism is genetic or that white people are inherently racist. CRT does not claim that individual prejudice does not matter. CRT does not say that wealth disparities are solely the result of racism (though racism is one significant factor among many).

CRT scholars are making a narrower claim: racial categories were created to organize power, systems continue to distribute that power along racial lines, and understanding this requires looking at the structures, not just the attitudes, that perpetuate inequality.

That is an empirical claim. It can be debated. But the debate should be about what CRT actually claims, not about a cartoon version of what someone thinks CRT claims.

\## Where Christians Should Listen

The Bible teaches several things that align with CRT's diagnosis, even if CRT's solutions are inadequate.

The Bible teaches that God cares about systems that oppress the vulnerable. Exodus is not just the story of individual redemption—it is the story of liberation from an economic system that enslaved people. The prophets do not just call individuals to stop exploiting the poor. They call for jubilee—structural debt forgiveness. They call for the return of land. They call for systems that protect the vulnerable.

The Bible teaches that sin has corporate dimensions. Nehemiah confessed the sins of his ancestors. Daniel confessed the sins of his people. The concept of corporate sin—that a people can share guilt for institutional injustice—is thoroughly biblical.

The Bible teaches that lived experience matters in bearing witness. Job's friends came to comfort him, but when they heard his testimony, they had to reconsider their theology. The disciples had to hear the witness of women before they believed in the resurrection. Peter had to hear Cornelius's witness before he understood that the gospel was for gentiles. When people tell you what they have experienced, you do not get to dismiss it because it does not fit your theory.

These are not CRT arguments. These are biblical arguments. CRT is naming things the Bible already named. The question is: why did the American church need a secular structure to name what Scripture already said?

\## Where Christians Must Question

But CRT's structure also conflicts with Christian anthropology in important ways.

CRT tends to see identity as primarily group-based and primarily defined by power relationships. A person's essence is their position in the system—oppressor or oppressed. CRT sees society as at its core organized around these power categories.

Christianity sees identity as primarily rooted in the image of God. Every person, regardless of race or status, bears the divine image. That image cannot be taken away by power or privilege. That image cannot be given by victimization. My identity is not my racial category or my oppressor status. My identity is God's beloved, redeemed through Christ.

This is not a minor difference. It is anthropological. It changes how we understand justice. CRT often works backward from systems to see what people are. Christianity works backward from the image of God to see what every person is—and therefore what justice demands.

CRT can name systems accurately. Christianity can offer transformation that CRT cannot.

\## The Point

The point of this chapter is simple: understand what you are debating. Read Derrick Bell. Read Kimberlé Crenshaw. Read Richard Delgado's introduction to CRT. Then you can make an informed judgment about where CRT is right and where it is wrong.

The American church's default position of dismissing CRT without reading it is embarrassing. It suggests we are so confident in our own worldview that we do not need to understand the worldview we are attacking. That is intellectual cowardice. That is not how people of truth operate.

And if you find yourself uncritically accepting CRT as the full solution to racial injustice, ask yourself: have I read the scholars? Do I understand their anthropological assumptions? Do I understand where they might be right and where they might contradict Scripture?

You owe yourself that work. You owe the person you are speaking with that work. The conversation is worth having. But it is worth having with clarity, not panic.

Read the texts. Then think. Then speak.

\# CHAPTER 2: What the Bible Actually Says About Race, Ethnicity, and the Image of God

\## The Foundation

If you are going to think biblically about race, you have to start with the absolute foundation: every human being bears the image of God.

> *"So God created mankind in his own image, in the image of God he created them; male and female he created them." Genesis 1:27.*

That is the non-negotiable premise. Not "males bear the image." Not "people of certain races bear the image." All mankind. The imago Dei is universal. It is not earned. It is not taken away by circumstances or power. It is constitutive of what it means to be human.

This is where we have to start because everything else flows from this. If a person does not bear the image of God—if they are somehow less fully human—then hierarchy and exploitation become logical. If they do bear the image of God, then every attempt to reduce them to a category, to extract labor from them, to deny them dignity is an assault on the image of God himself.

The American church defended slavery by trying to carve out exceptions to the imago Dei. Cursed Hamites, they said. Natural slaves, they said. Less developed, they said. Everything was an attempt to argue that some humans bore the image more fully than others. Scripture will not allow that argument.

The image of God is not partial. It is not hierarchical. It is not dependent on race, intelligence, ability, or status.

\## Ethnicity Is Real

But here is where Christian teaching on race gets complicated in the best way: ethnicity is real, and it is good.

In Acts 17, Paul preaches to the Areopagus. He tells the Athenians: "From one man he made all the nations, that they should inhabit the whole earth; and he marked out their appointed times in history and the boundaries of their lands."

God made nations. God marked out their boundaries. God appointed their times. Ethnicity is not a mistake that needs to be erased. It is intentional. It is part of creation.

Later Paul writes, "There is neither Jew nor Gentile, neither slave nor free, nor is there male and female, for you are all one in Christ Jesus" (Galatians 3:28). People often quote this verse to argue that ethnicity does not matter. But that is not what Paul is saying. Paul is not denying that Jew and Gentile are different. He is saying that in Christ, those differences do not create hierarchy.

The final vision of Scripture is not a homogenized humanity. The vision in Revelation 7 is "a great multitude that no one could count, from every nation, tribe, people and language, standing before the throne and before the Lamb."

Not "no nations." Every nation. Not "no cultures." Cultures preserved. Not "all the same." A multitude, diverse, celebrating difference before the throne of God.

Ethnicity is real. Ethnicity is beautiful. Ethnicity is part of God's creation design. And ethnicity will matter in the new creation. What will not matter is ethnicity as the grounds for hierarchy, for exploitation, for marginalization.

\## The Curse of Ham: What Scripture Says and What the Church Invented

The most consequential lie the American church told about Scripture was the doctrine of the "curse of Ham."

Here is the biblical text. Noah gets drunk. He lies naked in his tent. His son Ham sees his nakedness and tells his brothers. His brothers cover him without looking. Noah wakes up and curses Canaan, the son of Ham.

"Cursed be Canaan! The lowest of slaves will he be to his brothers." Genesis 9:25.

That is the entire text. It is a sparse, strange story. Noah curses Canaan because Ham violated his dignity.

Now listen to what the American church did with this text. American preachers took the curse of Canaan and mapped it onto Black Africans. They argued that Canaan was the ancestor of African peoples. They argued that God had cursed Black people to be slaves. They made this curse the biblical justification for the entire enterprise of American slavery.

Every major theological defense of American slavery relied on the curse of Ham.

This is not a minor misreading. This is a fundamental distortion of Scripture to serve an evil practice. Here is what makes it so pernicious: the curse is real. The text is in Scripture. But the application is a lie.

First, the curse of Canaan has nothing to do with Africa or with the descendants of Ham generally. Canaan is the son of Ham. The curse is on Canaan specifically, and historically, the Canaanites were the people who lived in the land that became Israel. The curse explains the conquest of Canaan in Joshua. It has nothing to do with Africa.

Second, even if it did apply to African peoples, the curse would be the opposite of biblical. The curse says, "cursed be Canaan." The imago Dei says, "in God's image he made them." The curse is overridden by the foundation. When the foundation is the image of God, no curse justifies slavery.

Third, the arc of Scripture is the curse is broken. Jesus comes to reverse curses. He comes to restore what was broken. He comes to make clear that the image of God cannot be cursed, cannot be enslaved, cannot be reduced to property.

The doctrine of the curse of Ham is the American church's most explicit sin against Scripture. We invented it. We weaponized it. We used the word of God to justify the enslavement of millions. That history has consequences that are still playing out.

\## What Scripture Says About Systemic Injustice

If you want to understand what the Bible says about what we now call systemic injustice, you have to read the prophets.

Amos stands up and says:

> *"Hear this, you who tread upon the poor and who trample upon the needy... The Lord has sworn by the Pride of Jacob: 'I will never forget anything they have done.'" Amos 8:4-7.*

Amos is not saying individual rich people are bad people. He is naming a system—treading, trampling—that produces poverty. The system has human agents, but the judgment is on the system itself.

Isaiah says:

> *"Woe to those who enact unjust decrees and write oppressive laws, depriving the poor of justice and robbing the needy of their rights... What will you do on the day of reckoning, when disaster comes from afar?" Isaiah 10:1-3.*

Isaiah is talking about law. Unjust law. Laws that look neutral on their face but that produce oppression. Laws designed by the powerful to benefit the powerful while leaving the poor defenseless.

Micah says:

> *"'Hear, you heads of Jacob and rulers of Israel... You hate good and love evil; you tear the skin from my people and the flesh from their bones... Will you not be ashamed of your shameful deeds?'" Micah 3:1-7.*

This is not individual sin. This is systemic violence. This is a system of power that benefits some while destroying others.

The prophets read the structures of their societies and said: these structures serve injustice. God will judge not just the individuals who perpetrate injustice but the systems that embed and perpetuate it.

This is where the Bible's vision becomes more searching than CRT. CRT can name systems. The Bible names systems and offers power to transform them. But the Bible's starting point is the same: systems matter. Structures matter. It is not enough to change hearts if the structures remain that perpetuate harm.

\## Corporate Sin and Inherited Guilt

One of the strangest parts of Scripture for Western Christians is the concept of corporate sin—the idea that a people can bear guilt for the sins of previous generations or for the sins embedded in their systems.

Nehemiah does this explicitly:

> *"I prayed: 'Lord... We have acted very wickedly toward you. We have not obeyed the commands, decrees and laws you gave your servant Moses.'" Nehemiah 1:5-7.*

Nehemiah was not personally responsible for the sins he is confessing. His ancestors were. But he stands before God and confesses as if he personally carried the guilt. He identifies with his people's sin.

Daniel does the same thing:

> *"I prayed to the Lord my God... while I was speaking and praying, confessing my sin and the sin of my people Israel..." Daniel 9:4, 20.*

Again, Daniel is confessing the sins of his people, not personal individual sins. He is standing in the gap between what his people have done and what God's judgment will be.

This is thoroughly biblical. It is also antithetical to American individualism. We have been taught that sin is individual, that guilt is individual, that you are only responsible for your own actions. Scripture teaches something more complex: you can bear corporate responsibility for systems and structures you did not create but you benefit from.

This does not mean white Americans alive today are personally guilty of slavery. It means that if you benefit from systems that were built through slavery, you have a corporate responsibility to understand that history and to help dismantle systems still shaped by it.

The Bible's ethical vision is larger than the individual conscience. It includes the conscience of a people, the integrity of systems, the justice of structures.

\## Jubilee and Structural Justice

One of the most subversively ignored parts of Scripture is the jubilee laws in Leviticus 25.

Every fiftieth year, the jubilee came. All debts were forgiven. All enslaved people were freed. All land returned to its original owners. The entire social structure reset.

This was not charity. This was not individual generosity. This was a structural mechanism designed into the law itself to prevent the permanent accumulation of wealth and power. It recognized a truth the Bible makes clear: without structural intervention, the wealthy accumulate more wealth, the poor accumulate more debt, and eventually you have a permanent underclass.

The jubilee was God's answer: no. Every fifty years, we reset. We remember that land belongs to God, not to us. We remember that people are not property. We remember that the accumulation of power and wealth without limit is against God's design.

Has the American church ever attempted to apply jubilee theology to racial injustice? Has any major Christian denomination called for the structural reset that would correspond to jubilee—reparations, land return, debt forgiveness? Has the American church recognized that the wealth gap between Black and white families is not individual failure but the result of systematic exclusion from wealth-building mechanisms? Has it attempted to imagine what jubilee would look like in response to four hundred years of slavery and another hundred years of segregation?

The answer is no. The American church has ignored jubilee. It has treated justice as individual righteousness rather than structural transformation.

\## The Summary

The Bible teaches:

\- Every person bears the divine image and cannot be reduced to a category or enslaved.

\- Ethnicity is real and beautiful and will matter in the new creation.

\- God cares about systems and structures that oppress the vulnerable.

\- The prophets read societies structurally, not just individually.

\- Corporate sin is real—people can bear responsibility for inherited injustice.

\- God designs structural mechanisms to prevent permanent oppression.

All of this is in Scripture. All of this aligns with the reality that racism is systemic. All of this says that the church's response cannot be merely individual conversion. It has to include structural transformation.

The irony is that when CRT showed up naming systemic racism, the American church was shocked. The American church had abandoned its own prophetic tradition. We had become so individualistic that we could no longer read our own Scripture.

That is the real scandal. Not that CRT exists. That we needed CRT to wake us up to what our own Bible had been saying all along.

\# CHAPTER 3: The American Church's Racial History

\## Slavery, Segregation, and the Theology That Made It Possible

You cannot understand the current crisis in the American church on race without understanding what the church actually did for four hundred years. Not what it said it believed. What it actually did. The theology it created to justify what it did.

This will be painful to read if you are white. Good. It should be. It should also be clarifying if you are Black—a moment when someone finally says out loud what you already knew.

\## The Biblical Defense of Slavery

In 1831, Thomas Stringfellow, a Virginia Baptist minister, published "A Brief Examination of Scripture Testimony on the Institution of Slavery." Stringfellow was not a fringe figure. He was a respected pastor and theologian. His work became the standard biblical defense of slavery among Southern religious leaders.

Stringfellow's argument was this: slavery appears throughout Scripture. God permitted it in the Old Testament. Jesus did not abolish it in the New Testament. Therefore, slavery is not sinful. To oppose slavery is to oppose Scripture.

Stringfellow quoted the Levitical laws about slavery. He pointed out that Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob—the patriarchs—owned slaves. He noted that Paul told slaves to obey their masters. He argued that if God had wanted to abolish slavery, he would have done so explicitly. Since he did not, slavery must be consistent with God's design.

Here is what Stringfellow did not do: he did not engage the difference between indentured servitude in ancient Israel and the racial chattel slavery that defined America. He did not engage the texts about letting slaves go in the sabbath and jubilee years. He did not engage the ethical vision of Exodus—God liberating enslaved people. He did not engage the prophets' judgment against those who exploited the vulnerable.

Stringfellow was selective. He was also effective. His theology shaped Southern preaching for decades.

But Stringfellow was not alone. In 1856, James H. Thornwell, a Presbyterian theologian and the president of South Carolina College, published "Report on the Religious Instruction of the Negroes, in the Presbyterian Church." Thornwell argued that slavery was a "providential arrangement" of God. He said the races had different capacities and that slavery was the proper condition for Black people.

Thornwell's theology was explicit: Black people were not fully equal image-bearers. Their proper condition was subjugation.

In 1864, Robert Lewis Dabney, a Confederate chaplain and theological giant, published "A Defence of Virginia and through her, of the South." Dabney argued that slavery was consistent with Christianity. He argued that abolition was not a Christian cause but a progressive delusion.

These were not fringe voices. These were the major theological voices of the Southern church. They shaped what evangelical churches believed about slavery.

And it was not just the South. Northern churches were complicit too. The churches of the North grew rich on the back of slavery—financing slave ships, insuring slavery ventures, engaging in the triangle trade. Northern churches did not defend slavery theologically as vigorously as Southern churches. But Northern churches did nothing to stop it either. Northern churches facilitated it. Northern churches profited from it.

The complicity was universal.

\## The Curse That Was Invented

We already looked at the curse of Ham. But it is worth seeing how systematically the American church weaponized it.

Theologians and preachers took the brief text about Noah cursing Canaan and built an entire racial theology on it. They argued that all of Ham's descendants were cursed. They mapped this onto the African peoples. They claimed the curse explained and justified slavery.

This theology dominated the American pulpit for two hundred years. Children were raised hearing that God had cursed African peoples to slavery. Ministers preached it. Missionaries carried it. It became doctrine.

The curse of Ham was so effective as a justification for slavery that when slavery ended, the curse lived on. If African peoples were cursed by God, then segregation was not a violation of God's design—it was obedience to it. If African peoples were marked by God, then separate accommodations and separate schools and separate entrances were biblical.

The curse of Ham became the theological foundation for two hundred years of American racism. It was completely made up. It had no basis in Scripture. But it was so effective that generations of Christians believed it.

That history matters. That history is in the bones of American Christianity. If you do not know this history, you do not understand why race is so intractable in American churches.

\## Slavery's Defenders and the People Who Said Nothing

There were Christians who opposed slavery. William Lloyd Garrison was a Christian abolitionist. Harriet Beecher Stowe was a Christian abolitionist. There were white abolitionists in the churches.

But they were in the minority. And they were opposed. Southern churches disfellowshipped them. Northern churches soft-pedaled their message. Mainline denominations split over slavery—North and South branches that did not reunite until the 1960s, and some not even then.

The historical reality: the American church did not lead on abolition. The American church lagged. The American church fought against it. The American church provided theological cover for slavery long after the general culture had moved against it.

This is not a minor thing. This is the defining failure of American Christianity. We had the gospel of liberation. We had the story of Exodus. We had the prophetic witness. We had the cross that broke every hierarchy. And we used Scripture to defend slavery instead.

The shame of that should never fully leave us.

\## Segregation: A Voluntary Choice, Not Forced

Here is another fact that gets minimized: segregation in American churches was not primarily forced by law. It was chosen.

After slavery ended, the Southern church could have chosen integration. The law did not require segregation in churches. The law required it in schools, in public accommodations, in housing. But churches could have integrated.

They did not. They chose segregation. They chose it consciously. They chose it on theological grounds.

The theology of segregation said this: God made the races different. God intended for them to remain separate. Integration was against God's design. The church should model the separation God intended.

Baptist churches. Methodist churches. Pentecostal churches. Holiness churches. All chose segregation. Some established "colored" churches as a supposedly benevolent way to let Black people worship in their own way. But those colored churches received a fraction of the resources. Those colored churches remained under white denominational control. The benevolence masked the hierarchy.

Black churches themselves were created partly by necessity and partly by choice—choosing to have control over their own spiritual lives, free from white domination. The Black church tradition became one of the most spiritually vital traditions in American Christianity. But it was created against the backdrop of white churches choosing segregation.

This matters for understanding the current moment. The segregation was not external imposition. The segregation was internal choice. The American church chose it. That choice shaped everything—church architecture, theology, worship styles, biblical interpretation, pastoral priorities.

To this day, American churches remain largely segregated. That is not accident. That is inheritance.

\## Billy Graham and the Complicated Legacy

Billy Graham is often held up as an example of evangelical race progress. Graham did some things right. In the 1950s, when it was genuinely countercultural, Graham desegregated some of his crusades. He invited Black pastors to participate in his events. He did not make a huge public stand on civil rights, but he did move in the right direction.

But Graham also preached to segregated audiences. He held crusades in the South where Black people had to sit in separate sections. He did not use his massive platform to prophetically confront segregation. He preached the gospel in a way that left systems unchallenged.

Graham was better than most. He was also not good enough. He was a transitional figure—pointing the direction the church should have gone while not going there fully.

What makes Graham's legacy complicated is this: evangelicals have used Graham's minimal steps toward integration to claim that evangelicalism was actually progressive on race. We have used Billy Graham as cover for our own failures. We have said, "Look, evangelicals cared about integration," pointing to Graham, while ignoring that most evangelicals remained segregated.

That is spiritual dishonesty. That is using a good man's partial steps to excuse our own inaction.

\## The Civil Rights Movement and Evangelical Silence

The civil rights movement of the 1960s was, in many ways, a Christian movement. Martin Luther King was a pastor. John Lewis was a minister. James Lawson was an ordained elder. The movement was rooted in theology, in the prophetic tradition, in the Exodus story.

Where were the evangelicals?

Largely silent. Evangelical churches did not march. Evangelical leaders did not speak. Some evangelical churches actively resisted integration. Some evangelical pastors said that civil rights was a communist plot. Some evangelical theologians argued that race relations were a matter for individual conscience, not for churches or government to address.

The historical record is clear: evangelicalism was, by and large, on the wrong side of the civil rights movement.

There were exceptions. John Perkins was an evangelical Christian who committed his life to racial reconciliation. Some evangelical churches integrated. Some evangelical leaders supported the movement quietly, even if they did not support it publicly.

But the institutional evangelical church—the denominations, the major churches, the theological voices—was silent or opposed during the moral moment of the twentieth century.

That silence had consequences. It signaled to Black Christians that evangelicalism was not their home. It signaled that evangelical Christianity was comfortable with white supremacy. It created a breach between Black and white evangelicalism that never fully healed.

Black evangelicals exist. But evangelicalism as a whole is experienced by Black Christians as a white religion. That is not accident. That is history.

\## The Unfinished Business

We are now in a moment where the American church is being confronted with this history. Some churches are trying to reckon with it. Some churches are ignoring it. Some churches are saying the reckoning itself is the problem.

But the history does not go away because we refuse to face it. The history lives in the fabric of American Christianity. It lives in predominantly white churches in neighborhoods that were redlined. It lives in church leadership structures where Black voices are absent. It lives in theological grids that have been shaped by centuries of racial hierarchy.

The question is not whether the American church has a racial history. The question is what the American church will do with that history.

Will we face it? Will we confess it? Will we repent of it in ways that actually cost us something? Or will we pretend it was a different era, a different church, not our responsibility?

The prophetic tradition says: you cannot move forward while carrying a lie. You cannot be reconciled while denying what you did. You cannot claim the gospel while maintaining systems built on injustice.

The American church built systems on injustice. The American church defended slavery. The American church chose segregation. The American church remained silent during the fight for civil rights.

That history is not behind us. It is in us. The question is what we will do with it.

\# CHAPTER 4: Martin Luther King's Theology

\## What the Church Claims to Honor But Refuses to Read

Martin Luther King is quoted at every American church during Black History Month. His "I Have a Dream" speech is celebrated. His name is invoked. His image appears in Sunday school materials. Every mainstream evangelical church in America now honors Martin Luther King Jr.

But almost no white evangelical church has actually read what King wrote and said. If they had, they would have to confront the fact that King was far more demanding than contemporary evangelicalism is willing to be.

King was not asking the church to do diversity training. He was not asking for representation in leadership. He was asking for something far more searching: he was asking the American church to actually believe the gospel it claimed to believe.

\## The Letter from Birmingham Jail

In April 1963, King was arrested in Birmingham, Alabama for leading protests against segregation. While in jail, he wrote his most important theological document: "Letter from Birmingham Jail."

The letter is addressed to white clergy who had published a statement calling King's actions "unwise and untimely." The clergy were not defending segregation. They were asking King to slow down. Wait for the right time. Work within the system. Be patient.

King's response was theologically devastating.

He wrote: "I must confess that I am not afraid of the word 'tension' in this connection. I have earnestly opposed violent tension, but there is a type of constructive, nonviolent tension which is necessary for growth."

King was saying: you are asking me to stop creating tension about injustice. You want me to maintain the peace. But the peace you want is the peace of continuing injustice. That is not peace. That is complicity.

Then King wrote the line that should haunt every white Christian who has ever preached about social order:

> *"I must make two honest confessions to you, my Christian and Jewish brothers. First, I must confess that over the past few years I have been gravely disappointed with the white moderate. I have almost reached the regrettable conclusion that the Negro's great stumbling block in his stride toward freedom is not the White Citizen's Counciler or the Ku Klux Klansman, but the white moderate, who is more devoted to 'order' than to justice."*

This is the thing white Christianity has never forgiven King for saying. Not that white racists were bad. Of course white racists were bad. But that white moderates—white people who claimed to believe in equality but who would not act, who valued order more than justice—were actually the greater obstacle to freedom.

The white moderate is the pastor who says, "I believe in civil rights, but we need to be careful not to offend people." The white moderate is the church leader who says, "I support equality, but not right now, not like this." The white moderate is the Christian who agrees that segregation is wrong while continuing to benefit from it.

King named the white moderate as the problem because the moderate was preventing change. The moderate was the one saying, "Be patient. Work within the system. Don't be too aggressive." The moderate was the one with just enough legitimacy to slow down justice.

Then King went further. He said: "Actually, time itself is neutral... There is 'time for all things.' It is often said that time will tell. Actually, time is always on the side of the oppressor, not on the side of the justice-seeker."

This is crucial. King is saying that waiting, patience, working slowly—these all favor the people in power. The longer you wait for justice, the more the people in power can organize against you. The people who are being harmed cannot afford to wait.

Then came the theological hammer. King said: "We should never forget that everything Adolf Hitler did in Germany was 'legal.' It was illegal to aid and comfort a Jew in Hitler's Germany. But I would have aided and comforted my Jewish brothers."

King was comparing segregation laws to Nazi law. He was saying: the law is not the same as justice. Unjust law is not binding. There is a higher law than the law of man, and when human law contradicts that higher law, obedience goes to the higher law.

The American church heard this and largely rejected it. The American church wanted to believe that law equals order equals God's design. King was saying: no. When law serves injustice, it is you who must resist the law.

\## King's Radical Theology

Most people know King's theology from one source: the "I Have a Dream" speech. The speech is beautiful. It is hopeful. It imagines a day when people will be "judged not by the color of their skin but by the content of their character."

But if that is all you have read of King, you do not know his theology. The Dream speech is a moment in a much larger body of work.

Read King's sermon "Why Jesus Called a Man a Fool." Read his sermon "A Knock at Midnight." Read his book "Where Do We Go from Here: Chaos or Community?" In these texts, King's theology is searching and prophetic. He is not just calling for integration. He is calling for a fundamental restructuring of American economic and political life.

In "Where Do We Go from Here," King directly addresses the problem of poverty. He says:

> *"Something is wrong with capitalism... the profit motive alone is not adequate to meet the needs of every member of the society... true compassion is more than flinging a coin to a beggar; it comes to see that an edifice which produces beggars needs restructuring."*

King is not arguing for minor reforms to help poor people. He is arguing that capitalism itself—the system that produces poverty—needs to be at its core restructured. He is saying the foundation is wrong.

King was also profoundly critical of militarism. His sermon "Beyond Vietnam" in 1967 said America needed to undergo a subversive reordering of its priorities, its foreign policy, its commitment to war.

King was also clear about what he was willing to die for. In his final sermon, delivered the night before his assassination, he said: "Well, I don't know what will happen now. We've got some difficult days ahead. But it doesn't matter with me now."

He was not afraid. He was not hedging. He was willing to die for justice.

\## What Evangelicalism Did With King

Evangelical Christianity has adopted King's name while rejecting his theology. We quote his dream while ignoring his critique of capitalism. We celebrate his nonviolence while maintaining our own structures of violence and exploitation. We have turned King into a safe figure—a man of the past who said nice things about equality—when King was actually a revolutionary.

If the American evangelical church had actually listened to King, we would have to ask ourselves:

\- Do our churches benefit from unjust economic systems?

\- Do we love order more than justice?

\- Do we ask oppressed people to wait for their freedom?

\- Are we more devoted to comfort than to prophetic witness?

\- What systems in our own time are we defending when we should be resisting?

We have not asked ourselves these questions. We have quoted King's dream while maintaining our nightmares.

\## King's Eschatology

One of the things that made King powerful was his eschatological conviction. He believed that God had already decided the outcome of history. That the arc of history bent toward justice. That God's kingdom would come whether America cooperated or not.

This is crucial. King was not optimistic about American progress. He was not believing in human ingenuity or American goodness. He was believing in God. He was believing that God had already decided. His work was to align himself with what God was already doing.

This is the faith the American church has largely lost. We have become pragmatists. We calculate what is possible. We work within existing systems. We do not believe that God might be asking us to do something that looks impossible from a human perspective.

King believed in impossible things because he believed in God. He believed that segregation would fall because God had decided it would. He believed that justice would come because God was bringing it. His work was to obey God, not to calculate success.

The difference between King's faith and the faith of the contemporary American church is the difference between conviction and compromise.

\## The Challenge to White Christianity

King's real challenge to white Christianity is this: you say you believe in the gospel. You say you believe that all people are created in God's image. You say you believe that Jesus broke down the wall between Jew and Gentile. You say you believe that the cross has power to reconcile enemies.

But you do not live as if you believe it. Your churches are segregated. Your leadership is segregated. Your money flows to people who look like you. Your theology has been shaped by the systems you benefit from.

The question King posed is still unanswered: if you actually believed the gospel, how could you maintain segregation? How could you stay silent while your brothers and sisters were beaten, jailed, and killed for asking for basic human dignity?

You cannot. If you actually believed the gospel, you could not.

That is King's indictment. That is why the American church has had to domesticate King, to turn him into a dreamer who asked nicely for equality, rather than a prophet who demanded that the American church repent of its complicity with injustice.

\## What We Owe King

The American church owes King something. We owe him the acknowledgment that he was right and we were wrong. We were silent. We were comfortable. We chose order over justice. We remained in segregated churches and segregated theological institutions and segregated leadership structures while King was being jailed and beaten for prophetically calling us to actually believe what we claimed to believe.

We owe him the work of reading him honestly. Not the sanitized King of Black History Month. The real King. The King who was killed because he threatened too much. The King who was calling American capitalism to account. The King who was calling American militarism to account. The King who was calling American Christianity to account.

And we owe him the work of asking: what would King be calling the contemporary church to account for? If he were alive today, what would he be saying about the systems we maintain, the people we marginalize, the theology we have constructed to justify the unjust?

That is the real work of honoring King. Not quoting him. Hearing him. Actually hearing what he said. Actually changing in response to it.

\# CHAPTER 5: Is Systemic Racism Real?

\## What the Bible Says About Corporate Sin and Structural Injustice

The most common conservative argument against understanding racism as systemic goes like this: the Bible teaches that sin is individual. Only individuals sin. Only individuals can repent. Therefore, we cannot speak of systemic racism. We can only speak of individual racists.

This is not biblical. It is a modern individualism read backward into Scripture. The Bible has a strong theology of corporate sin.

\## Nehemiah's Confession

In the book of Nehemiah, the rebuilt Jerusalem is struggling. Ezra the scribe reads the law to the people, and as they hear it, they realize how much they have violated it. The people gather to pray. Nehemiah leads them in confession.

Listen to what Nehemiah confesses:

> *"We have acted very wickedly toward you. We have not obeyed the commands, decrees and laws you gave your servant Moses." Nehemiah 1:7.*

Later, in chapter 9, the confession becomes more elaborate: "They were disobedient and rebelled against you; they put your law behind their backs. They killed your prophets, who had admonished them in order to turn them back to you."

Here is the crucial thing: Nehemiah is confessing sins committed by his ancestors. He was not alive when his ancestors broke the law. He did not personally kill the prophets. Yet he confesses these sins as if he personally committed them.

Why? Because he understands that sin is not merely individual. Sin has a corporate dimension. A people can be responsible for the systems and patterns that their ancestors created. A person can inherit guilt for continuing those systems.

This is not the only place this happens. When the Israelites conquered Achan in Joshua 7, they executed not just Achan but his entire family. The sin of one person involved his whole household. The judgment fell on the corporate entity, not just the individual.

\## Daniel's Confession

Daniel is an even clearer example. Daniel is a righteous man. He has not personally participated in the sins he is about to confess. But in Daniel 9, he confesses:

> *"I prayed to the Lord my God and confessed: 'O Lord, the great and dreadful God, who keeps his covenant of love with all who love him and obey his commands, we have sinned and done wrong. We have been wicked and have rebelled; we have turned away from your commands and laws. We have not listened to your servants the prophets.'"*

Daniel includes himself in a collective guilt for sins he did not personally commit. He stands with his people in their sin. He confesses on behalf of the nation.

Why? Because Daniel understands that nations and peoples have a corporate existence. The sins of the nation are the sins of its people—not individually, but collectively.

This is not a minor biblical teaching. This is throughout Scripture. Joshua confesses Israel's sins. Jeremiah speaks for Israel. Nehemiah identifies with Israel's sin. The pattern is consistent.

\## Corporate Sin in the Prophets

The prophets are even clearer about corporate responsibility. Amos speaks to the nation as a whole:

"They sell the innocent for silver, and the needy for a pair of sandals." (Amos 2:6)

Amos is not naming individual evil-doers who sold innocent people. He is naming a system. The system of the nation of Israel engaged in this practice. The nation as a corporate entity was complicit.

Isaiah says:

> *"Woe to those who make unjust laws, to those who issue oppressive decrees, to deprive the poor of their rights... What will you do on the day of reckoning?" (Isaiah 10:1-3)*

Again, Isaiah is not just condemning individual wicked lawmakers. Isaiah is condemning the structure—the laws themselves. The laws, even though enacted through legal processes, are unjust. The nation that enacted them is responsible.

Micah says:

> *"Hear, you leaders of Jacob, you rulers of Israel... You eat the flesh of my people and tear away their skin." (Micah 3:1-3)*

Micah is naming structural violence. The leaders—the corporate power structure—are consuming the people. Not individually. As a system.

\## The Difference Between Individual and Systemic Sin

Here is what needs to be clear: naming systemic sin does not erase individual responsibility. An individual racist is still individually guilty. An individual who benefits from unjust systems while doing nothing to change them is still individually guilty.

But systemic sin is real in addition to individual sin. A system can be unjust even if the people operating within it are individually well-meaning. A system can perpetuate harm even when the people in the system are not consciously trying to do harm.

An example: a bank has lending criteria that look neutral. The criteria ask for credit history, income, collateral. Nothing explicitly about race. But if that bank's lending criteria systematically result in denying loans to Black applicants at rates significantly higher than white applicants, the system is racist—regardless of whether the loan officer has racial animus.

Is the loan officer individually guilty of racism? Maybe not. The loan officer might be personally colorblind. But the loan officer is participating in a system that perpetuates racism. And that system is real.

The prophets recognized both levels. They condemned the oppressor—the individual who actively exploited the poor. But they also condemned the system that enabled oppression. They recognized that you could have a system of injustice even if most of the people in the system did not think of themselves as unjust.

\## The Evidence That Systemic Racism Exists

By now, this is not a theoretical question. It is an empirical question. And the evidence is overwhelming.

Housing: The Federal Housing Administration explicitly refused to insure mortgages in neighborhoods with Black residents. This was federal policy. The FHA literally had maps marking "hazardous" areas that included any neighborhood with Black residents. This meant Black families could not access government-backed mortgages. White families could. Over decades, this created a massive wealth gap. White families built equity in homes. Black families could not. That gap persists today.

Criminal justice: Black people receive longer sentences than white people for identical crimes. The data is consistent. A Black man convicted of a felony receives a sentence on average 20% longer than a white man convicted of the same felony. Is this because individual judges are consciously racist? Some might be. But the pattern is systemic. It is how the system operates.

Employment: Studies where researchers send identical resumes—one with a "white-sounding" name and one with a "Black-sounding" name—consistently find that the resume with the white-sounding name gets more callbacks. Same qualifications. Same resume. Different name. Different outcome. This is systemic.

Education: Schools in predominantly Black neighborhoods receive significantly less funding than schools in predominantly white neighborhoods, even within the same district. Facilities are worse. Teachers are less experienced. This is not by accident. This is how the system is structured.

Policing: Black people are killed by police at a rate three times higher than white people, even adjusting for crime rates. Black people are more likely to be stopped, searched, and arrested for the same behaviors that white people engage in with impunity.

These are not individual racist acts. These are patterns. These are how systems work. This is systemic racism.

The Bible would call this structural sin. A system is operating in a way that produces racial injustice. The system itself is unjust.

\## Where Conservatives Get Confused

Conservative Christianity often says: systemic racism implies that individual white people are guilty just for being white. That is not what systemic racism means. Systemic racism means systems are structured in ways that benefit white people and harm Black people. Individual white people benefit from those systems whether or not they consciously chose to benefit.

An analogy: if you inherit a house built on stolen land, you are benefiting from an injustice you did not personally commit. You did not steal the land. But you are living in the house. That does not make you personally guilty of theft. But it does mean you have a responsibility to understand that your house is built on injustice, and to consider what you owe to the people whose land was taken.

Individual white people are not guilty of slavery. We did not enslave anyone. But we benefit from systems that were built through slavery, that maintained segregation, that still operate in ways that privilege white people and disadvantage Black people. That is not guilt. That is reality.

\## Corporate Repentance

If corporate sin is real—and Scripture teaches that it is—then corporate repentance must be real too. A nation can repent. A people can repent. A system can be repented of and changed.

Nehemiah modeled this. He did not say, "I am not personally guilty, so I will not confess." He confessed on behalf of his people. He identified with their sin. He led the people in turning from it.

This is what the American church has failed to do. The American church has not corporately confessed its history of defending slavery, maintaining segregation, and remaining silent during civil rights. Individual white Christians might have admitted that individuals were wrong. But the church as a whole has not engaged in genuine corporate repentance.

That is why the work remains undone. That is why racial division persists in the church. You cannot move forward carrying a lie.

\## The Summary

Systemic racism is real. Scripture affirms that systems can be unjust. Scripture affirms that corporate bodies can be responsible for injustice. Scripture affirms that repentance of systemic injustice is possible.

The American church needs to:

\- Acknowledge that American Christian systems benefited from slavery and segregation.

\- Confess that the church defended these systems theologically.

\- Recognize that many contemporary systems still carry the weight of that history.

\- Commit to changing those systems in ways that cost something.

Not as individuals claiming personal innocence. As a people confessing corporate sin and committing to corporate transformation.

That is what Nehemiah did. That is what Daniel did. That is what the prophets called for.

The question is: will the American church do it?

\# CHAPTER 6: Where CRT Gets It Right

\## What Christians Can Learn from Secular Justice Movements

CRT is not the gospel. CRT does not have the power to transform human hearts. CRT does not offer reconciliation. But CRT is making some observations about how American systems function that are accurate. These are observations the Bible also makes.

A Christian can disagree with CRT's ultimate solutions while acknowledging where CRT's diagnosis is correct. That is not capitulation. That is honesty.

\## CRT's Correct Diagnosis: Systems Embed Power

CRT scholars observe that institutions—law, education, economics, criminal justice—are not neutral. They were built by people in power. They were built to serve people in power. They continue to serve people in power.

This is not a subversive observation. It is historical fact. American law was written by men. American law protected male property rights and male political power. That is not judgment. That is history.

American criminal law was written by people who benefited from slavery. The Thirteenth Amendment abolished slavery—except as punishment for crime. American criminal law has always been shaped by the fact that it was an instrument of racial control.

American housing policy was written by people who wanted to segregate America by race. The FHA's redlining was not accidental. It was policy. It was intentional. It was designed to keep Black families out of white neighborhoods.

CRT scholars say: look at the history of these institutions. They were designed by people in power to serve people in power. Even if the language has changed, the function remains.

This is correct. You can read the historical record. You can see how institutions were designed. You can trace the continuities from the past to the present.

The Bible teaches something similar. The prophets looked at the institutions of their time and asked: who benefits? Who is harmed? When Amos looked at the law courts of Israel, he saw that the poor could not get justice. The law was designed in favor of the wealthy.

When Isaiah looked at the kings of Israel, he saw that they were stealing land from the poor. The economic structures were designed to enrich the rich and impoverish the poor.

CRT scholars are doing what the prophets did: asking whether the institutions that claim neutrality actually serve justice or injustice.

\## Lived Experience as a Source of Knowledge

CRT scholars say that people who have experienced injustice have legitimate knowledge about how injustice works. A Black person who has been stopped by police repeatedly has knowledge that data collection cannot fully capture. A person denied a mortgage has knowledge about housing discrimination that statistics do not convey.

This is also biblical. The psalms are full of people crying out because they have experienced injustice. God calls the people to listen: "Defend the weak and the fatherless; uphold the cause of the poor and the oppressed." (Psalm 82:3)

How do you know the cause of the poor and the oppressed? By listening to them. By taking their testimony as a primary source of knowledge.

Proverbs says: "The first to speak in court seems right, until someone comes forward and questions him." (Proverbs 18:15)

The point is that you do not fully understand an issue until you have heard from people who are affected by it. A man who has never been denied a mortgage does not understand housing discrimination the way a person does who has been repeatedly denied. A white person who has never been stopped by police for "matching a description" does not understand police profiling the way a Black person does.

This does not mean that individuals cannot understand systemic injustice—of course they can. It means that lived experience is a legitimate form of knowledge. It is not the only form. But it is a form that should be included in the conversation.

The American church has failed to do this. White churches have made decisions about race without adequately listening to Black Christians. White pastors have preached about racial justice without consulting Black pastors. White theologians have written about race without engaging Black theological traditions.

CRT is simply saying: listen to people who have experienced the injustice you are trying to address. That is also what Scripture says.

\## Interest Convergence: When Majority Groups Change

Derrick Bell made an observation called "interest convergence." Bell looked at Brown v. Board of Education, the Supreme Court decision that declared segregation in schools unconstitutional. This was, on its face, a victory for racial justice. The Court said segregation is wrong.

But Bell noted something: the timing of Brown coincided with America's need to project an image of racial progress to the world during the Cold War. America was competing with the Soviet Union for influence globally. Soviet propaganda pointed out that America had segregation. America needed to show the world that America was progressive on race.

Brown came down at a moment when ending segregation served America's interests in global competition. Bell's insight: progress on race happens not just because justice demands it, but because it serves the interests of the majority group.

Is this cynical? Maybe. But it is also historically accurate. Emancipation came after a brutal war. The Thirteenth Amendment passed when the North had military victory. The Civil Rights Act passed when the country was shaken by the assassination of Kennedy and the moral power of the civil rights movement had made silence impossible.

Progress happens when justice serves the interest of the majority group. Before that moment, the majority group resists. After the moment passes, the majority group often tries to reverse the progress.

This is observable in America's racial history. The American church integrated somewhat during the civil rights era when the nation was integrating and integration seemed inevitable. But as soon as integration became inconvenient—as soon as white flight became possible, as soon as charter schools became available—the church re-segregated.

The church did not change its theology about race. The church's behavior changed because the circumstances changed. The interest convergence ended, and the church returned to segregation.

This is not a judgment that people are evil. It is an observation about how systems work. Systems change when the people in power find it advantageous. Systems persist when they continue to benefit the people in power.

CRT scholars are making a sociological observation. The Bible makes a similar observation. The prophets note that the wealthy resist justice because justice threatens their wealth. "They hate the one who reproves in court and despise him who tells the truth." (Amos 5:10)

The wealthy resist justice. That is what CRT scholars observe and what the Bible also teaches.

\## Naming Patterns Without Naming Villains

One of CRT's strengths is that it allows you to name patterns without requiring that every person in the pattern be a conscious villain.

A white banker in 2026 is not defending slavery. The banker is probably a decent person. But the banker is operating within a system that still carries the weight of centuries of racial exclusion from wealth-building. The banker is part of a pattern.

Naming that pattern is not accusing the banker of being racist. It is observing that the system the banker operates within perpetuates racial inequity.

This distinction matters because it allows white people to have honest conversations about race without having to defend their own virtue. You do not have to argue that you are not racist. You can simply acknowledge: the systems I benefit from have racial histories and continue to produce racial outcomes. What should I do about that?

The Bible teaches the same distinction. God judges nations and systems, not just individuals. When God says through Amos, "This is what the Lord says: For three sins of Israel... I will not turn back my wrath," God is not saying every individual Israeli is evil. God is saying the nation as a whole has sinned. The system operates unjustly.

An individual can acknowledge corporate sin without personalizing it as an attack on their individual virtue.

\## The Limits of CRT (For Christians)

Now, having said where CRT is right, let me be clear about where CRT's structure has limits that Christianity must transcend.

CRT sees humans primarily as situated in relationships of power. You are what you are because of where you sit in the system—oppressed or oppressor. That becomes your essence.

Christianity sees humans primarily as image-bearers. That is your essence. Your oppressor status or oppressed status is real, but it is not who you are. You are God's beloved. That is where your identity comes from.

This difference matters because it shapes what you believe is possible. If a person's essence is defined by their position in a power system, then changing the system might change their position but not their essence. But if a person's essence is being God's beloved, then transformation is possible. A person can move from being shaped by injustice to being shaped by God's love.

This is the gospel's promise. This is what CRT cannot offer.

\## The Summary

CRT scholars are observing real things about American systems:

\- Systems are not neutral; they were designed by people in power and continue to serve people in power.

\- Lived experience of marginalized people is a source of knowledge.

\- Systems change when the interests of the majority group align with the change.

\- You can name patterns without requiring that every person in the pattern be consciously evil.

All of these observations are consistent with Scripture. The difference is that Scripture not only names the systems; Scripture offers power to transform them and power to transform the people within them.

The church does not need to choose between naming injustice and offering transformation. The church needs to do both. The prophets did both. Jesus did both. He named the hypocrisy of the religious system and offered forgiveness to the woman caught in sin.

CRT can help us see the systems more clearly. The gospel can help us transform them.

\# CHAPTER 7: Where CRT Gets It Wrong

\## The Gospel's Justice vs. Secular Identity Politics

CRT gets some things right about American systems. But CRT's ultimate structure contradicts Christian anthropology in ways that matter profoundly. A Christian cannot adopt CRT wholesale. A Christian can learn from CRT without being captured by it.

\## The Anthropological Problem

CRT's understanding of human identity is that people are primarily defined by their position in a system of power. Who you are is determined by what group you belong to and what power relationships that group has. Identity is group-based and power-based.

Christianity teaches something radically different. A human being is primarily a being created in God's image, loved by God, redeemed by God. That is your identity. Not your race, not your gender, not your class, not your oppressor status. Your identity is being God's beloved.

This is not semantic. This is foundational. It changes everything about how you understand yourself and how you engage others.

If identity is primarily power-based, then the goal of justice is to invert the power relationship. The oppressed should become the oppressors. The marginalized should become the centered. Justice becomes about who gets to be in power.

If identity is primarily rooted in being God's beloved, then the goal of justice is not to invert hierarchies but to eliminate them. The goal is a community where no one is in power over anyone else, where everyone is equally beloved, where everyone serves each other.

These are not the same thing.

\## The Problem of Unforgiveness

One of the limitations of CRT as a structure for justice is that it often embeds unforgiveness into the system. If a person's identity is determined by their oppressor status, then that person cannot be healed of that identity. They are locked in the relationship with the system that damaged them.

Christianity offers something different. It offers forgiveness. Not forgetting. Not pretending harm did not happen. But forgiveness—the possibility of moving beyond the harm that was done.

In the CRT structure, a person who has been harmed carries that harm as part of their identity. In the Christian structure, a person can be healed of that harm and can move forward freed from the identity of victim.

This is not to say that acknowledging harm is unhealthy. Acknowledging harm is necessary. But being defined by harm is not freedom. Being defined by harm is remaining in bondage to the harm.

Jesus offers something different. Jesus offers the possibility of being healed and freed. "For freedom Christ has set us free; stand firm therefore, and do not submit again to a yoke of slavery." (Galatians 5:1)

This verse is often quoted in individual contexts—freedom from sin, freedom from guilt. But it applies to systems too. You do not have to remain in the identity the system tried to give you. You can be freed from it.

\## CRT and Reconciliation

CRT is concerned with justice. Justice is important. But CRT often settles for justice without reconciliation. CRT can name the perpetrator and demand restitution. CRT can change systems and redistribute power. But CRT struggles with the question of what happens after justice: can the perpetrator and victim actually become one people again?

Christianity insists on reconciliation. Not at the expense of justice. Justice must come first. But reconciliation is the goal—that people who have been enemies can become family again.

Philemon is a short letter in the Bible. Paul is writing to Philemon about Onesimus, a slave who ran away from Philemon's household. Paul is asking Philemon to receive Onesimus back, not as a slave but as a beloved brother.

This is wild. A slave owner, in the Roman system, had the legal right to execute a runaway slave. A slave, in the Roman system, had no legal recourse, no dignity, no chance.

And Paul, operating within that system, asks for something that the system does not allow: reconciliation. He asks Philemon to receive Onesimus as a brother. He says, "Perhaps the reason he was separated from you for a little while was that you might have him back forever—no longer as a slave, but better than a slave, as a beloved brother."

Paul is not asking Philemon to do nothing about the injustice of slavery. But Paul is asking for more than justice. Paul is asking for love. He is asking for the possibility of these two people actually becoming family.

This is what Christianity offers that CRT does not: the possibility that people who have been divided can actually be reconciled.

\## The Problem of Guilt Without Redemption

CRT often leaves white people with guilt but no path to redemption. You have benefited from unjust systems. That is true. You carry the benefit of centuries of injustice. That is true. But now what?

CRT can say you owe restitution. CRT can say you need to dismantle the systems. CRT can say you need to acknowledge your complicity. But CRT struggles to answer: can you actually be forgiven? Can you actually move beyond the guilt?

In a structure where identity is determined by oppressor status, a white person is locked in that status. The white person cannot escape being an oppressor because that is what the system has made them.

Christianity offers forgiveness. Genuine forgiveness. Not getting off the hook. Not pretending the harm didn't happen. But actual forgiveness that opens the possibility of being freed from the guilt.

This matters because without the possibility of forgiveness, reconciliation is impossible. If a person is locked in guilt with no way out, they cannot genuinely meet the other person. They can only meet them as someone locked in their own shame.

\## Where Justice Without Reconciliation Fails

There are examples throughout history of justice without reconciliation. After World War I, the Treaty of Versailles imposed such harsh terms on Germany that the German people felt humiliated and hungry for revenge. They wanted to overturn the terms. They felt the justice was not justice at all—it was oppression. The result was resentment that festered and contributed to the rise of Hitler.

After the Rwanda genocide, there could have been simply retribution. The perpetrators could have been killed. But Rwanda chose something different: Truth and Reconciliation. Perpetrators admitted what they had done. Victims heard the admissions. Communities worked toward healing. It was not simple. It was not easy. But it opened the possibility of people actually living together again.

Justice without reconciliation breeds resentment. Justice with reconciliation opens the possibility of actually healing the breach.

\## The Missing Piece in CRT: Transformation

CRT's structure assumes that systems are the problem and that changing systems is the solution. If you redistribute power, change laws, and embed justice in structures, you have solved the problem.

But systems are not the only problem. People are. People carry sin. People carry brokenness. People, even in just systems, can still choose injustice.

The gospel offers transformation of people. Not just systems. The gospel says that you can be made new. "Therefore, if anyone is in Christ, the new creation has come: The old has gone, the new is here!" (2 Corinthians 5:17)

This is not positive thinking. This is not ignoring systems. But this is claiming that human hearts can actually change. That people can repent. That people can turn from injustice.

CRT does not have tools for this. CRT can identify who is responsible for systemic injustice. But CRT cannot offer power to repent and change.

\## The Incompleteness of CRT for a Christian Vision

To be clear: I am not saying CRT is evil. CRT is a tool. CRT can help you see systems more clearly. CRT can help you name what you might not have named before. For a Christian, CRT can be a useful ally in naming systemic sin.

But CRT cannot be your structure for understanding justice. Because CRT's ultimate concern is power—who has it, who doesn't, how to redistribute it. Christianity's ultimate concern is not power. It is wholeness. It is healing. It is reconciliation.

A Christian should be able to say:

\- I see what CRT names about systems. That is real.

\- I also see what Christianity offers beyond that. That is more real.

\- I am committed to both naming injustice and offering transformation.

\- I am committed to both justice and reconciliation.

\- I believe that systems should change and that people should be made new.

That is not compromising the gospel. That is fulfilling the gospel.

\## The Specific Problems with Contemporary CRT Application

In its most extreme contemporary applications, CRT becomes something other than what the original scholars intended. In some applications, CRT becomes:

\- Viewing all white people as inherently oppressive regardless of their actions.

\- Viewing all Black people as inherently victims regardless of their agency.

\- Refusing to recognize that individuals have choices within systems.

\- Making identity categories absolute and untraversable.

\- Offering no hope for genuine reconciliation or transformation.

These applications have moved away from CRT as an academic analysis and toward something closer to ideology. And ideology—whether it comes from the left or the right—is dangerous in the church.

The gospel is not ideology. The gospel is a story of God's redemption that transcends any human system of thought.

\## The Path Forward

A Christian can engage CRT critically. A Christian can learn from CRT what is true while rejecting what contradicts the gospel. A Christian can name systemic injustice while offering hope for systemic transformation and personal redemption.

This requires holding things in tension:

\- Systems are real and unjust systems must be named and changed.

\- People are real and people can be transformed and redeemed.

\- Justice must happen, and reconciliation is the goal.

\- Power structures should be flattened, and all people are equally beloved.

These are not contradictions. These are the fuller vision of what the gospel offers compared to what any secular structure can offer.

\# CHAPTER 8: White Evangelicalism's Blind Spot

\## Why the Loudest Critics of CRT Have the Least Credibility

There is an irony that should not be missed: the loudest voices attacking Critical Race Theory from evangelical pulpits and platforms are coming from institutions and traditions that have never honestly reckoned with their own racial history.

You cannot credibly critique CRT while maintaining the very systems that CRT is attempting to name. You cannot credibly warn people about the dangers of critical theory while continuing the theology that defended slavery. You cannot credibly claim to value truth while refusing to tell the truth about your own history.

Yet this is exactly what white evangelicalism is doing.

\## The Southern Baptist Convention's Founding

The Southern Baptist Convention was founded in 1845. It was founded to defend slavery.

Before 1845, Baptists operated in a single denomination—American Baptists. But tensions rose over slavery. Northern Baptists were beginning to move toward abolition. Southern Baptists were committed to slavery. The Southern Baptist Convention was created specifically to allow Southern Baptists to maintain and defend slavery.

This is not interpretation. This is history. The convention was literally created as a religious defense of slavery.

For the next 120 years, the SBC defended slavery, and later segregation, and later resisted integration with vigor. The SBC did not integrate its seminaries until the 1980s. The SBC elected its first non-white denomination-wide leader only recently, many decades after the civil rights era.

The SBC has, in recent years, officially apologized for its stance on slavery and segregation. That apology is good. But an apology is not the same as fundamental change. The SBC's leadership structure remains overwhelmingly white. The wealth gap between white and Black Baptist communities remains vast, a direct inheritance of centuries of slavery and segregation.

Yet SBC leaders are among the loudest voices attacking CRT.

The credibility problem is obvious. You cannot credibly attack a structure that names systemic racism while maintaining an institution that was literally founded to defend racial slavery. You cannot credibly warn about systemic injustice embedded in institutions while refusing to acknowledge the systemic injustice your own institution perpetuated and continues to perpetuate.

\## The Evangelical Realignment Around Segregation

After Brown v. Board of Education declared segregation unconstitutional in 1954, white evangelicals had a choice. They could accept integration. They could lead on racial justice. They could say the gospel compels us to move toward reconciliation.

They did not. Instead, white evangelicals created a massive network of "segregation academies"—private schools designed specifically to allow white children to avoid integrated public schools.

These were not fringe institutions. These were mainline evangelical institutions. Bob Jones University. Oral Roberts University. Hundreds of Christian schools founded specifically to maintain segregation.

Not integration academies. Not schools designed to provide Christian education while modeling reconciliation. Segregation academies. Schools whose purpose was explicitly to allow white families to maintain racial separation.

Many of these schools maintained explicit rules against interracial dating until the 1980s and 1990s. Bob Jones University did not remove its ban on interracial dating until 2000. Two thousand. Not 1960. Not 1970. 2000.

This is the history of white evangelicalism. We created a massive infrastructure to maintain racial separation in the name of Christian education. We did this not in the 1920s. We did this in the 1950s and 1960s, in the very moment when the nation was beginning to integrate.

\## The Religious Right's Origin

The contemporary religious right is often credited with beginning around abortion. The claim is that evangelicals, alarmed by Roe v. Wade in 1973, mobilized politically.

This is wrong. The religious right began around segregation. The religious right was mobilized to defend segregation academies and to preserve racial separation.

In 1981, Jerry Falwell said: "The time has come to call our nation back to God, back to morality, back to sensibility, and back to conservatism." Falwell's moment of "calling the nation back" was happening specifically when segregation academies were under legal threat for their discriminatory policies.

Falwell was defending segregation. That was the origin. Abortion came later. Abortion became the public face, but segregation was the origin.

This matters because it means white evangelicalism's mobilization into politics was built on defending racial separation. The religious right was birthed as a resistance movement to integration.

Once you know this history, when you see white evangelicals today attacking CRT, you see what is happening: evangelicals are defending their own racial systems while attacking the system that names them.

\## The Hypocrisy of Attacking CRT

Here is what makes the attack on CRT from white evangelical pulpits hypocritical:

CRT is attempting to name how systems perpetuate racial injustice. CRT is arguing that institutions embed bias in ways that are not always visible. CRT is saying that people can benefit from unjust systems without being consciously malicious.

This is exactly what white evangelicalism has done. White evangelicalism benefited from unjust racial systems. White evangelicalism created and maintained segregation academies. White evangelicalism remains overwhelmingly white in its leadership. White evangelicalism has built its wealth and influence on racial separation and racial hierarchy.

CRT is naming exactly what white evangelicalism is guilty of.

So when white evangelical leaders attack CRT, what they are really saying is: "Do not name the systems we benefit from. Do not point out how our institutions perpetuate racial hierarchy. Do not force us to reckon with our history."

That is what the attack on CRT is. It is not intellectual critique. It is institutional self-protection.

\## The Alternative That Evangelicalism Failed to Offer

The devastating thing is that the Bible offers a vision of racial justice that is more searching than CRT. The gospel offers reconciliation where CRT can only offer justice. The gospel offers transformation where CRT can only offer structural change.

If white evangelicalism had offered this vision—if evangelicals had said, "The gospel demands that we integrate our churches, our schools, our communities, and we are leading the way"—evangelicalism would have credibility to critique CRT.

Instead, evangelicalism did the opposite. Evangelicalism built systems of segregation and created theology to justify them. When CRT showed up and named those systems, evangelicalism panicked because evangelicalism had no answer.

The answer evangelicalism should have had was: "Yes, systems matter. Yes, we have embedded racial bias in our institutions. Yes, we need to repent and change. But the gospel offers more than CRT. The gospel offers the power to actually transform hearts and reconcile enemies."

But evangelicalism could not say that because evangelicalism had not done that work. Evangelicalism had not repented. Evangelicalism had not changed its institutions. Evangelicalism had just tried to hide its history.

\## The Problem of Selective Memory

White evangelicalism has engaged in selective memory about its racial history. Many white evangelicals know about Billy Graham's minor steps toward integration. Many white evangelicals can point to individual evangelical leaders who opposed racism. But many white evangelicals do not know about the segregation academies. Many do not know that the religious right was founded to defend segregation. Many do not know that SBC seminaries were segregated into the 1980s.

This selective memory allows white evangelicals to feel like they are actually progressive on race while maintaining systems that perpetuate racial injustice. They can quote MLK while remaining in predominantly white churches. They can oppose racism in principle while refusing to integrate their institutions in practice.

CRT is attempting to make this impossible. CRT is saying: you cannot claim to be colorblind while benefiting from systems that sort by color. You cannot claim to be anti-racist while maintaining segregated institutions. You cannot ignore systemic injustice.

This is what threatens evangelicals. Not the truth of CRT's analysis. The fact that CRT won't let us hide anymore.

\## The Intellectual Dishonesty

The loudest evangelical attacks on CRT come from people who have made no serious attempt to understand CRT. They have not read Derrick Bell. They have not read Kimberlé Crenshaw. They are attacking a caricature.

This is intellectually dishonest. And it is particularly dishonest coming from people who have never honestly reckoned with their own history.

If you are going to critique CRT, you should at least know what CRT says. You should at least be able to quote CRT accurately and engage with what it actually argues. Most evangelical attacks on CRT fail this basic test.

But more than that: if you are going to critique CRT while maintaining your own unjust systems, you should expect to be called hypocritical. And you should be.

\## What Real Credibility Would Require

If white evangelicalism wanted credibility to critique CRT, white evangelicalism would need to:

\- Honestly reckon with the history of segregation academies. Acknowledge what they were and what they were for.

\- Honestly account for why evangelical institutions remain overwhelmingly segregated. Give reasons that are not just "it happened that way."

\- Commit to integration of leadership in ways that cost something. Not token diversity, but substantive change.

\- Teach the actual history of evangelicalism and race to the next generation. Not the sanitized version. The whole story.

\- Support Black-led institutions and Black evangelical voices without trying to control them.

\- Commit to structural change that redistributes resources and power.

This is the work of genuine repentance. This is the work that would give white evangelicalism the moral authority to speak prophetically about the problems with CRT.

But evangelicalism has not done this work. So when evangelicals attack CRT, they sound like people trying to silence a voice that is naming their own sin.

Because that is exactly what is happening.

\## The Irony

The final irony is that if white evangelicalism actually believed what it claims to believe about the gospel—that Christ breaks down walls between Jew and Gentile, that Christ reconciles enemies, that Christ's blood makes us one—evangelicalism would not need CRT to be proved wrong. Evangelicalism would have already integrated. Evangelicalism would have already modeled reconciliation. Evangelicalism would have already offered the world a better vision than CRT.

The fact that evangelicalism did not do this. The fact that evangelicalism built segregation academies. The fact that evangelicalism created the religious right to defend segregation. The fact that evangelicalism remains segregated and leadership-segregated. The fact that evangelicalism is now panicking about CRT.

All of this proves that white evangelicalism does not actually believe what it claims to believe.

That is the real crisis. Not CRT. The crisis is that white evangelicalism has abandoned the gospel's vision of reconciliation and continues to defend systems that perpetuate racial division.

You cannot credibly attack CRT while maintaining that history. You cannot credibly warn about systemic injustice while benefiting from your own. You cannot credibly claim the gospel while refusing to live it.

That is not politics. That is spiritual accountability.

\# CHAPTER 9: Black Church Theology

\## What White Christianity Missed and Still Refuses to Learn

There is a theological tradition in America that is deeper, more searching, more prophetic than white evangelical theology has any claim to. It is the theology of the Black church. It is a theology forged in suffering. It is a theology that understood systemic injustice centuries before Critical Race Theory gave it academic language.

White Christianity has largely ignored this tradition. White seminaries did not teach it. White theologians did not engage it. White churches did not listen to it. And in that refusal, white Christianity impoverished itself and refused the gift that Black Christianity was offering.

\## The Exodus Theology of the Black Church

The Black church understood Exodus as the central biblical narrative. Exodus is not just the story of Israel escaping Egypt. Exodus is the story of God liberating enslaved people. When Black Christians read Exodus, they read their own story.

> *"Let my people go." That was the word. That was God's word to the slaveholder. Let them go. You do not own them. They are God's people. They must be free.*

White Christianity had effectively abandoned Exodus theology. White Christianity had read the Bible in terms of individual salvation—Jesus died for your sins, repent and believe. That is true theology. But it is incomplete theology. The white church had minimized the liberationist element of Scripture.

The Black church never abandoned it. The Black church understood that faith without liberation is not faith. That God cares about systems of oppression. That the God of Exodus is the God who breaks chains.

This is not radicalism. This is biblical. But white Christianity had so thoroughly domesticated the gospel that liberation theology felt subversive when Black Christians named it.

\## James Cone and the Theology of Liberation

James Cone was the first major Black systematic theologian in America. Cone wrote in the 1960s and 1970s, in the midst of the civil rights movement and the Black Power movement. He asked a question that white Christianity was not asking: what does the gospel say to Black people who are being oppressed?

Cone's answer was subversive: God is on the side of the oppressed. God's liberation is the central theme of Scripture. The Cross is not just about individual forgiveness; it is about liberation. Jesus's victory over death is a victory for the oppressed over systems of death.

Cone said: if the gospel is not good news to the oppressed, it is not the gospel. If Jesus did not come to liberate the enslaved, then Jesus is not the Jesus of Scripture. If the church does not side with the oppressed against the oppressor, then the church is not the church of Jesus.

This was too demanding for white Christianity. White Christianity wanted a gospel that worked for the middle class, that affirmed American values, that did not challenge the systems that benefited white people.

Cone offered something different. Cone offered a gospel that made sense of suffering. That explained why God would allow oppression—not to teach the oppressed a lesson, but to call the oppressed to liberation, and to call the oppressor to repentance.

This is not the gospel white Christianity was teaching.

\## Howard Thurman and the Spiritual Discipline of Suffering

Howard Thurman was a generation before Cone, a pastor and theologian who worked with both white and Black audiences. Thurman wrote about Jesus and the disinherited—what the gospel meant for people who had been excluded and marginalized.

Thurman's insight was that the oppressed have spiritual resources that the oppressor does not. Because the oppressed have to face the deepest questions—How do I survive this? How do I maintain my humanity in a system designed to strip it from me? How do I find joy in a context of suffering?—the oppressed develop spiritual depths that the comfortable do not reach.

This is not to romanticize suffering. Suffering is not good. Oppression is not good. But suffering can deepen a person's capacity for faith, for hope, for love. The oppressed, having nothing else to depend on, depend on God. The oppressed, having no other comfort, find comfort in prayer and in community.

White Christianity, uncomfortable with this truth, largely ignored Thurman. But Thurman's theology is deep. Thurman is saying: listen to people who suffer. They have something to teach you about faith.

This is what the Black church has always done. The Black church gathered people who had suffered, and in that gathering, it created a space where suffering could be named, where community could be experienced, where God could be met.

\## The Spirituals: Theology Forged in Suffering

One of the most deep sources of Black church theology is the spirituals—the songs enslaved people created. These are not entertainment. These are theology set to music.

> *"I Want to Go Home" speaks of heaven, but the "home" is freedom. Heaven is not just spiritual escape. Heaven is the freedom the enslaved could not find on earth.*
>
> *"Go Down Moses" is a call to liberation. The song recounts the Exodus story and calls the contemporary enslaver to let the people go.*
>
> *"Wade in the Water" tells enslaved people how to escape—follow the North Star, wade in the water so the dogs cannot track you. It is survival theology. It is practical wisdom. It is faith that trusts God while taking action.*
>
> *"Swing Low, Sweet Chariot" speaks of a chariot that will carry the singers home. It speaks of death, but it also speaks of hope—that death is not the end, that there is something beyond this suffering, that God has not abandoned the people.*

These songs are theology. They are deeper than most theological treatises because they come from the place where faith has to be real. These songs come from people who could have given up on God, who could have concluded that God did not care about their suffering. But instead, they sang. They believed. They testified that God sees them, God loves them, God will act.

White Christianity has largely treated these spirituals as entertainment. We have sung them in concert. We have taught them in school as cultural artifacts. But we have not learned from them. We have not allowed them to teach us about the God who sides with the oppressed.

\## The Preaching Tradition of the Black Church

The preaching in the Black church is different from the preaching in the white church. White evangelical preaching tends to be expository or topical. It explains the text or explains a doctrine. It is cerebral.

Black church preaching is prophetic. It reads the Scripture in the context of the congregation's reality. It names what is happening in the world—injustice, suffering, oppression—and it speaks God's word to that reality. The preacher is not standing above the congregation explaining truth. The preacher is standing with the congregation, naming their suffering, and calling them to hope and action.

This tradition of prophetic preaching is biblical. It is what the prophets did. It is what Jesus did. Jesus stood up in the synagogue and read Isaiah: "The Spirit of the Lord is on me, because he has anointed me to proclaim good news to the poor... to proclaim freedom for the prisoners... to set the oppressed free." Then Jesus said, "Today this scripture is fulfilled in your hearing." (Luke 4:18, 21)

Jesus was reading the Scripture into the context of his people's reality. Jesus was saying, God is speaking to your situation right now.

The Black church has maintained this tradition. The Black church reads Scripture as a living word that speaks to the congregation's actual life. The Black church preaches as if it matters, as if God cares about what the people are facing, as if the gospel is not a spiritual abstraction but a concrete word for a concrete people.

White churches have largely abandoned this. White evangelical churches preach as if the gospel is timeless and contextless. We preach as if the congregation has no particular reality that the gospel needs to address. We preach as if the gospel is about the spiritual realm and has little to do with justice, systems, economics, politics.

This is not biblical preaching. This is domesticated preaching.

\## What White Christianity Lost

When white Christianity refused to listen to Black church theology, white Christianity lost:

\- A living connection to the liberating God of Exodus.

\- An understanding of how faith actually works in contexts of suffering.

\- A prophetic tradition that reads Scripture as a living word for the congregation's reality.

\- An appreciation for how the oppressed develop spiritual depths that the comfortable do not reach.

\- A vision of God that is not domesticated, not patriotic, not comfortable, but revolutionary.

White Christianity gained:

\- Comfort.

\- Respectability.

\- Access to power.

\- The ability to domesticate the gospel to serve the status quo.

That was a bad trade. That was a betrayal of the gospel.

\## The Ongoing Refusal

Even now, even as white Christianity is beginning to acknowledge that it got race wrong, white Christianity remains largely ignorant of Black church theology. White seminary students do not study Cone as part of their core curriculum. White evangelical churches do not invite Black pastors to teach about the theology of liberation. White denominational leaders do not consult Black theologians as authorities on what the gospel means.

The refusal continues. The white church continues to treat Black church theology as a supplement, as something to learn "about" Black people, not as a central source of Christian wisdom.

This is racism. Not overt racism. Institutional racism. The refusal to recognize that Black church theologians are authorities on what Christianity should be. The refusal to treat the Black church as an equal dialogue partner.

As long as this refusal continues, the white church will remain impoverished. As long as the white church treats Black theology as supplemental, the white church will not be fully Christian.

\## The Prophetic Word

The Black church has a prophetic word for white Christianity. That word is: you have domesticated the gospel. You have turned it into a religion that serves your comfort. You have made it possible to be Christian while maintaining oppression. You have created theology that justifies injustice.

This is not a new word. The prophets have been saying this to the people of God for thousands of years. But the Black church is saying it specifically and prophetically to white Christianity.

The question is: will white Christianity listen? Or will we continue to refuse the gift that the Black church is offering?

The answer will determine whether Christianity in America can be prophetic, or whether it will remain domesticated.

\# CHAPTER 10: Racial Reconciliation Is Not Enough

\## Why the Bible Demands Justice Before Unity

There is a problem that has plagued the American church's approach to racial reconciliation. The problem is that we have tried to reconcile without first doing justice.

The church has said: let's just love each other. Let's just be brothers and sisters in Christ. Let's just focus on our common faith and move past our racial division.

This is cheap reconciliation. This is not biblical. The Bible is clear: justice must precede peace. Reconciliation built on injustice is not reconciliation. It is complicity.

\## Cheap Reconciliation

Cheap reconciliation is what happens when you ask the person who has been harmed to forgive without the person who did the harm confessing and making restitution.

In a marriage, if one spouse has been unfaithful, cheap reconciliation sounds like: "I did wrong, but I don't want to talk about it. Let's just move forward. Let's just focus on loving each other now." The unfaithful spouse refuses to do the work of confession and restitution. The faithful spouse is asked to forgive without receiving apology.

This does not work in marriage. It does not work in race.

Cheap reconciliation in race sounds like: "Let's not focus on what happened in the past. Let's just focus on being united now. Let's just be colorblind. Let's not bring up racial divisions."

This is asking Black people to set aside the injustice they have experienced. This is asking Black people to forgive without the white church confessing and changing. This is asking Black people to pretend that centuries of slavery, segregation, and marginalization do not matter.

This is not reconciliation. This is silencing. This is a way for the people in power to maintain the status quo while claiming that they want unity.

\## The Prophetic Demand for Justice First

The prophets are clear. Justice must come before peace.

Isaiah says: "Justice will dwell in the desert and righteousness will live in the fertile field. The fruit of that righteousness will be peace; its effect will be quietness and confidence forever." (Isaiah 32:16-17)

Notice the order. Justice first. Righteousness first. Then peace. Then quietness and confidence. You cannot reverse the order. You cannot have peace without justice. Attempting to do so produces a false peace that is really just oppression with a friendly face.

Amos says: "Let justice roll on like a river, righteousness like a never-failing stream!" (Amos 5:24)

Amos is called a justice movement where justice is the primary thing. Justice rolling on like a river. That is the biblical vision. Not peaceful coexistence. Justice.

Micah says: "What does the Lord require of you? To act justly and to love mercy and to walk humbly with your God." (Micah 6:8)

Justice is primary. Justice is what is required. Mercy comes after justice. You cannot have mercy without first having justice.

The prophetic tradition is clear: any peace not founded on justice is a false peace.

\## Reconciliation Without Repentance Is Cheap Grace

Dietrich Bonhoeffer distinguished between cheap grace and costly grace. Cheap grace is grace without discipleship, grace without repentance, grace without cost. It is grace that says you are forgiven but you do not have to change.

Reconciliation without repentance is cheap grace applied to race.

True reconciliation requires:

1\. The person who did the harm confesses the harm.

2\. The person who did the harm takes steps to change the structures that produced the harm.

3\. The person who did the harm makes restitution when possible.

4\. The person who was harmed receives genuine apology and compensation.

5\. Both parties commit to the new relationship.

Only then is reconciliation real.

The American church has wanted to skip steps 1-4 and go straight to step 5. We have wanted reconciliation without the work of justice. This does not work.

When the white church says to the Black church, "Let's just be reconciled," without confessing what the white church did, without changing white church structures, without making restitution, the Black church rightly hears: "I want you to shut up about injustice and pretend everything is fine so I can feel good about myself."

That is cheap grace. That is not gospel.

\## What Justice Actually Requires

Justice in the context of racial harm requires several things.

\*\*First, honest historical reckoning.\*\* The white church must tell the full story of what it did. Not the sanitized version. The full version. The segregation academies. The defense of slavery. The silence during civil rights. The ongoing segregation of institutions and leadership.

Many white churches will not do this. Many white churches have rewritten their history to minimize their complicity. They tell stories about how progressive they were, about how they were ahead of their time. But the record does not support this.

Honest reckoning means telling the truth, even when the truth is uncomfortable.

\*\*Second, institutional change.\*\* It is not enough to change hearts if you do not change structures. A white church can feel bad about its racial history while maintaining all-white leadership, all-white staff, and all-white congregations.

Justice requires actual structural change. It requires hiring Black leaders. It requires investing in Black communities. It requires dismantling the systems that maintain segregation.

\*\*Third, resource redistribution.\*\* The wealth of the white church was built partly through benefiting from systems of injustice. Justice requires redistributing resources. It requires funding Black churches. It requires supporting Black businesses. It requires closing the wealth gap.

This will cost something. Real justice costs. If your reconciliation costs you nothing, it is not reconciliation. It is performance.

\*\*Fourth, changed power dynamics.\*\* As long as white people remain in power and Black people remain marginalized, there is no reconciliation. Real reconciliation requires that power be shared. That Black voices are not just heard but are trusted to make decisions. That Black leaders are not consultants brought in to give color to white leadership but are actual leaders.

\## The Danger of Premature Reconciliation

Premature reconciliation is dangerous because it prevents the kind of deep healing that is necessary. If you reconcile before doing justice, you reconcile around the injustice. The injustice remains embedded in the relationship.

Then later, the injustice will resurface. The person who was harmed will feel the harm again. The person who was not held accountable will feel entitled to maintain their power.

Reconciliation that is premature is reconciliation that will not hold.

\## The Example of South Africa

South Africa provides a crucial example of what real reconciliation requires. After apartheid, South Africa could have chosen simple retribution (killing the perpetrators) or premature reconciliation (just moving on without addressing the past).

Instead, South Africa chose the Truth and Reconciliation Commission. Perpetrators of apartheid violence admitted what they had done. Victims heard the admissions. Communities worked toward healing. Some perpetrators were held accountable. Some were granted amnesty if they confessed fully.

This was not perfect. But it attempted to combine justice (truth-telling, accountability) with reconciliation (the possibility of moving forward together).

The American church has not attempted anything like this. The American church has wanted reconciliation without truth. Reconciliation without accountability. Reconciliation without cost.

Until that changes, reconciliation will not be real.

\## The Gospel's Vision of Reconciliation

The gospel does offer genuine reconciliation. But reconciliation built on the foundation of justice.

Jesus teaches that if you come to make an offering at the altar and remember that your brother has something against you, leave your offering and go be reconciled to your brother first. (Matthew 5:23-24)

The order matters. Reconciliation comes before the offering. But notice: you are the one who has done wrong. You must go be reconciled. You must initiate the reconciliation. You must make it right.

Paul writes to Philemon about Onesimus. But Paul does not ask Philemon to just forgive and move on. Paul asks Philemon to receive Onesimus "no longer as a slave, but better than a slave, as a beloved brother." Paul is asking for a fundamental change in the relationship. No more slave-master dynamic. Now brother-brother.

That is reconciliation built on justice. The injustice (slavery) is ended. The relationship is transformed. Now reconciliation is possible.

\## The Work the American Church Must Do

If the American church wants genuine racial reconciliation, the church must:

\- Tell the full truth about its racial history.

\- Change its structures so that Black people have real power and voice.

\- Redistribute resources to close racial wealth gaps.

\- Support Black-led churches and institutions without trying to control them.

\- Make reparative justice a priority, not an afterthought.

\- Accept that reconciliation will take generations, not years.

This is the costly work. This is the work that will actually produce reconciliation.

Without this work, there will be no reconciliation. There will only be racial management. There will only be the appearance of progress while injustice persists.

The gospel demands justice. The gospel demands that the person who has wronged others do the work of restitution and change. The gospel demands that reconciliation be built on this foundation of justice.

Until the American church does this work, the American church is not reconciled. The American church is still divided. And the division is still rooted in injustice.

\# CHAPTER 11: Raising Children to See Race Biblically

\## Parenting Beyond Colorblindness and Critical Theory

Your child is going to notice that people have different skin colors. Your child is going to have questions about why some people are treated differently. Your child will notice that some people are underrepresented in positions of power. Your child will learn from peers, from school, from media, from the world.

The question is: what will you, as a Christian parent, teach your child about race?

Most Christian parents have operated from one of two frameworks. The colorblind structure says: we do not notice race, we treat everyone the same, race does not matter. The critical structure says: race is the primary lens through which we understand everything, power dynamics are rooted in race, you must actively dismantle racism.

Neither is biblical. Neither is adequate.

\## The Problem with Colorblindness

Colorblindness sounds beautiful. It sounds like the dream of Dr. King—that people would be "judged not by the color of their skin but by the content of their character."

But colorblindness is not biblical. The Bible does not teach colorblindness. The Bible teaches that God saw the color, God saw the ethnicity, and God called it good.

> *"From one man he made all the nations... he marked out their appointed times in history and the boundaries of their lands." (Acts 17:26)*

God made nations. God made diversity. God looked at what he had made and "it was very good."

Colorblindness teaches your child to not see something that God created as good. Colorblindness teaches your child to pretend that race does not matter when, in fact, race produces real consequences in the world.

When a child asks, "Why do all the presidents look like grandpa?" and you say, "We do not notice those things," you are teaching your child to not see. You are teaching your child to not notice patterns. You are teaching your child to not ask questions about why the world is ordered the way it is.

This is not protecting innocence. This is creating blindness. This is preventing your child from developing the capacity to see injustice.

Colorblindness also puts a burden on your child of color. If you tell a child of color that you do not notice race, you are telling that child that you do not notice an important part of their identity. You are telling that child that their experience of the world—which includes their race—is something you will not acknowledge.

Colorblindness is not a virtue. Colorblindness is a failure to see.

\## The Problem with Uncritical Adoption of CRT

On the other hand, if you adopt the system of Critical Race Theory wholesale, you teach your child that identity is primarily about power. You teach your child to see themselves and others primarily through the lens of oppression or oppression.

This creates different problems. It can teach a child of color that their primary identity is victim. It can teach a white child that their primary identity is oppressor. It prevents children from seeing the full complexity of human identity.

More at its core, you are teaching your child that the human person is primarily a product of systems. You are not teaching your child that every person bears the image of God. You are teaching your child that what matters is what group you belong to.

This is not biblical either.

\## What Biblical Parenting About Race Looks Like

Biblical parenting about race starts with one foundational claim: every person bears the image of God.

Not the white person. Not the Black person. Every person. Male and female. Rich and poor. Powerful and marginalized. Every person is God's beloved. Every person is created in God's image.

From that foundation, you can teach your child several things:

\*\*First, ethnicity is real and beautiful.\*\*

You can teach your child about different cultures. You can celebrate the food, the music, the traditions, the beauty of different peoples. You can teach your child that ethnicity is a gift, not a problem.

If your child is of color, you can teach them to be proud of their heritage. You can teach them about the contributions their people have made. You can teach them about the strength of their traditions.

If your child is white, you can teach them about their heritage too. Not as superior. But as theirs. Not as the default human. But as one particular way of being human.

\*\*Second, race creates real consequences in the world.\*\*

You need to teach your child that people are treated differently based on race. You need to name this. You need to explain it in age-appropriate ways.

If your child comes home and says, "The principal is always a white man," you do not say, "We do not notice those things." You say, "You are right. Most principals in America are white men. That is because of history. Here is why."

You teach your child to see patterns. You teach your child to ask questions. You teach your child to notice when certain groups are overrepresented in positions of power.

\*\*Third, being benefited by injustice does not make you bad.\*\*

If your child is white and you are teaching them about racial injustice, you must be careful not to teach them that they are bad because they are white. This is not biblical. This creates shame instead of responsibility.

You can teach your child: "You have benefited from systems that were unfair to other people. That is not your fault. Your ancestors did not create those systems, but they benefited from them. You benefit from them now. You did not choose that. But now that you know, you have a choice: will you work to change those systems?"

You teach responsibility, not shame.

\*\*Fourth, we are all called to work for justice.\*\*

The God of the Bible is a God of justice. God cares about systems that exploit the vulnerable. God calls his people to pursue justice.

You can teach your child that justice is not political. Justice is biblical. Justice is pursuing systems that are fair to everyone. Justice is using whatever power or privilege you have to help people who have less.

You can teach your child to pay attention to what is fair and unfair. You can teach your child to speak up when something is unfair. You can teach your child that sometimes doing the right thing costs something.

\*\*Fifth, reconciliation is possible through the gospel.\*\*

This is where you move beyond what any secular structure can offer. You teach your child that the gospel makes possible what politics cannot make possible—genuine reconciliation.

You teach your child that people who have been divided by injustice can actually become family again. Not by ignoring the injustice. But by moving through confession, restitution, and forgiveness.

You teach your child that there is hope. Not the cheap hope that says everything is fine now. But the costly hope that says the work is hard but possible, and that Christ has already paid the ultimate price for reconciliation.

\## Practical Ways to Teach This

How do you actually do this?

\*\*Read diverse stories.\*\* Make sure the books in your home include stories with Black protagonists, Asian protagonists, Latino protagonists. Make sure your child sees people of color as the main characters, not just supporting characters.

\*\*Celebrate diverse holidays.\*\* If you are white, celebrate your own heritage. But also learn about and celebrate the heritage of your neighbors and friends. Not as exotic curiosities, but as legitimate parts of your community's life.

\*\*Build diverse friendships.\*\* If your child only has white friends, that is a problem. Not because white friends are bad. But because your child is not learning to see the full humanity of people different from themselves.

Intentionally build friendships across racial lines. Go to churches that are integrated. Go to schools that are diverse. Live in neighborhoods that are not segregated.

\*\*Have honest conversations.\*\* When your child asks questions about race, answer them honestly. Do not deflect. Do not say, "We do not talk about those things." Do not pretend the questions do not exist.

\*\*Name injustice.\*\* When you see injustice, name it. When a child of color is treated unfairly, name it. When a system is working against a particular group, name it. Model for your child that noticing and naming injustice is normal and healthy.

\*\*Teach them about their own history.\*\* If your child is of color, teach them about the contributions their people have made. Teach them about the struggles their people have faced. Teach them about figures from their tradition who worked for justice.

Teach them their real history, not a sanitized version. Teach them about slavery and segregation. Teach them about people who resisted and fought for freedom.

If your child is white, teach them your real history too. Teach them about where your family came from. Teach them about your ancestors' relationship to issues of race. Be honest about complicity when it is there. Be honest about resistance when it is there.

\*\*Do justice, not just teach it.\*\* Your actions teach more than your words. If you teach your child to work for justice while working to maintain segregation, your child will notice the contradiction.

If you teach your child that race matters while your church is all white, your child will notice.

If you teach your child that all people are equal while voting for policies that harm people of color, your child will notice.

Live what you teach. That is where the real teaching happens.

\## The Risk and the Hope

Teaching your child to see race clearly and to work for justice is risky. Your child might get angry. Your child might become disillusioned with injustice. Your child might demand that you do more to work for change.

That is the risk. But it is a good risk.

The alternative is to raise a child who is blind to injustice, who does not see other people fully, who is comfortable with the way things are.

The hope is to raise a child who sees clearly, who loves fully, who works for justice, who believes that reconciliation is possible, and who trusts that God is in control even when the work is hard.

That is what biblical parenting about race looks like.

\# CHAPTER 12: Building a Church That Does Justice and Loves Mercy

\## Beyond the Culture War on Race

For years, the American church has been reactive on race. The culture shifts, and the church scrambles to respond. Someone launches an initiative, and the church opposes it or adopts it without thinking. CRT shows up, and the church panics.

This is not leadership. This is reaction. This is fear.

What if the American church led on racial justice instead of reacting to it? What if the church, grounded in Scripture and in the gospel, offered a vision of justice and reconciliation that was more searching than anything the culture was offering?

That is possible. But it requires a fundamental shift in how churches operate.

\## What Justice Actually Looks Like in a Local Church

Justice is not abstract. Justice is not a social policy you adopt. Justice is how a church actually operates. Justice is whom you hire. Justice is how you distribute resources. Justice is whom you listen to. Justice is whom you make decisions with.

A church that does justice does these things:

\*\*First, it has diverse leadership.\*\*

Not diversity for diversity's sake. But leadership that reflects the diversity of the community. If your church is in a diverse neighborhood and your leadership is all white, something is wrong.

This requires actually recruiting and hiring and ordaining people of color. This requires paying them equally. This requires trusting them with real power, not just giving them token positions.

This is hard work. It requires admitting that you have been wrong about who is qualified to lead. It requires white leaders stepping aside to make space. It requires resources to support the transition.

But it is non-negotiable for justice.

\*\*Second, it listens to voices it has ignored.\*\*

For years, white churches have been preaching about what Black people need. What Black people need from church. What Black people should do. What Black people should accept.

Justice requires actually asking Black people. Not in token listening sessions where you have already decided what you believe. But genuine listening. Listening where you are open to being changed.

What do Black members say when they are asked what would make them feel valued in the church? What do they say when asked what barriers they face? What do they say about the church's response to racial injustice?

Listen. Believe what they say. Change in response.

\*\*Third, it uses resources to support justice.\*\*

Money talks. Where a church spends money reveals what a church values. If a church spends money on building new facilities but not on supporting racial justice, the church is saying something.

Justice requires redistributing resources. It requires funding Black churches. It requires supporting Black pastors through education and mentorship. It requires investing in Black communities. It requires paying Black staff equally and generously.

This costs something. But real justice costs.

\*\*Fourth, it changes systems.\*\*

It is not enough to change individual attitudes. You have to change systems.

If your church has been segregated for a hundred years, you cannot just say, "Everyone is welcome now." You have to actively dismantle the systems that produce segregation. You have to ask: why is our church segregated? What systems maintain segregation? What would integration actually require?

Sometimes it requires changing where you meet. Sometimes it requires changing the style of worship. Sometimes it requires changing the theological grid you have been teaching.

All of these changes cost. All of them create discomfort. But all of them are necessary for justice.

\*\*Fifth, it teaches the full history.\*\*

Sunday school and Bible studies cannot teach history that is only comfortable. They must teach history that is true.

What did your denomination do during slavery? Did they defend slavery or oppose it? What did your denomination do during segregation? Did they maintain segregation or oppose it? What did your denomination do during civil rights? Were they silent, opposed, or supportive?

Teach the full truth. If the truth is uncomfortable, that is the point. The truth should make you uncomfortable. The truth should call you to repentance.

\## The Difference Between Diversity and Justice

Here is a crucial distinction that many churches miss: diversity is not the same as justice.

Diversity means you have people of different races in your church. That is good. But diversity can be purely cosmetic. You can have Black people in your congregation while maintaining all-white leadership. You can have Black people in the pews while treating them as less-than.

Justice means that power, resources, and voice are genuinely shared. Justice means that people of color are not just represented but are actually listened to and trusted. Justice means that resources are actually distributed in ways that address historical inequities.

You can have diversity without justice. Many churches do. You can have Black people in your congregation and in your marketing materials while maintaining structures of injustice.

But you cannot have justice without diversity. If only white people are in positions of power, if only white perspectives are represented in decision-making, if only white people control resources, then you do not have justice.

\## The Gospel Vision for Community

What would a local church look like if it actually lived the gospel's vision of community?

It would be a place where people of different backgrounds could gather, where no one was trying to assimilate anyone else into their cultural form, where difference was celebrated as a gift from God.

It would be a place where the wealthy served alongside the poor, where power was not hoarded but shared, where decisions were made together, not imposed.

It would be a place where the history of injustice was acknowledged, where repentance was real, where restitution was attempted.

It would be a place where the prophetic tradition was alive, where injustice was named, where comfort was not the goal.

It would be a place where reconciliation was possible—not easy, but possible. Where people who had been divided by injustice could actually become family again.

This is the vision of the book of Acts. Where Jews and Gentiles gathered together. Where people from different nations understood each other as family. Where resources were shared. Where the barriers that had divided people were broken down.

This is not a vision that is outdated. This is a vision that is more relevant now than ever.

\## The Prophetic Role of the Church

The church's role in the culture should be prophetic. That means the church speaks truth that the culture does not want to hear. The church names injustice when the culture is comfortable. The church calls for justice when power does not want to change.

For too long, the American church has been captured by culture. The church has baptized American values. The church has wrapped the flag and the cross together. The church has made it possible to be Christian while maintaining injustice.

A prophetic church cannot do this. A prophetic church must say: this injustice is wrong. This system is broken. This must change.

But the prophetic church does not just name the problem. The prophetic church offers an alternative vision. The prophetic church says: here is what justice looks like. Here is what reconciliation looks like. Here is what community could be.

That is the church's unique contribution. Not just political activism. Not just social commentary. But an alternative community that models what is possible when people actually believe the gospel.

\## Starting Where You Are

You do not have to be a mega-church or a major denomination to do this work. You can start where you are.

If you are a pastor, you can begin by telling the truth about your denomination's racial history. You can begin by inviting voices you have not listened to before. You can begin by asking hard questions about your own church's systems.

If you are a member, you can ask your leadership hard questions. Why is our leadership team all white? Why are most of our giving going to building maintenance instead of racial justice? Why are we not listening to people of color in our congregation?

If you are a church planter, you can plant a church with integration as a core value from the beginning. You can build structures of shared power from the start. You can make clear that this is a congregation where people of all races are not just welcome but are fully valued.

If you are a seminary student, you can demand that your seminary teach the full history of Christianity and race. You can demand that your faculty include voices from the Black church tradition. You can demand that racial justice be central to theological education, not peripheral.

You can start where you are. The work does not require permission from denominational leadership or approval from powerful institutions. The work requires commitment.

\## The Cost

Let me be honest: doing justice in a local church will cost you something.

It will cost you comfort. You will have conversations that are difficult. You will hear truths about your own complicity that are painful.

It will cost you members. Some people will leave your church because the church is too political, too focused on racial issues, too willing to challenge the status quo.

It will cost you resources. Money that was going to building maintenance or staff salaries will now go toward racial justice initiatives, toward hiring diverse staff, toward supporting Black communities.

It will cost you power. If you have been accustomed to having power in your church, sharing power means you have less. That is uncomfortable.

Real justice costs. If your commitment to justice costs you nothing, it is not real.

But here is what justice also offers: it offers integrity. It offers the possibility of actually living what you say you believe. It offers the possibility that your church might become a glimpse of the kingdom of God—a place where people of all races and backgrounds gather as family.

\## The Hope

The hope is not that the American church will perfectly do racial justice. We will make mistakes. We will move imperfectly. We will struggle.

But the hope is that the American church could be prophetic. That the American church could lead on racial justice instead of lagging behind. That the American church could offer the culture a vision of reconciliation that is deeper than the culture can offer.

The gospel has power to reconcile. The gospel has power to transform. The gospel has power to make enemies into family.

The church that actually believes the gospel, that actually practices it, could be a sign of that power. The church could show the world what is possible when people actually take the cross seriously—when they actually believe that Christ has broken down the wall between us.

That is the hope. That is what is possible if the American church will do the hard work of justice and the deeper work of reconciliation.

\# CONCLUSION: The Cross and the Color Line

\## Why the Gospel Demands More of Us Than Any Secular Theory

Critical Race Theory is not the gospel. CRT cannot save you. CRT cannot heal you. CRT cannot reconcile enemies. CRT cannot transform the human heart.

But CRT can name injustice. CRT can identify systems. CRT can call attention to patterns that matter. For that, we can be grateful.

The gospel does something more. The gospel offers what CRT cannot offer: the power to actually transform everything—systems and hearts, the way we treat each other, the way we understand ourselves.

But the gospel demands more. The gospel does not just ask us to see injustice. The gospel asks us to dismantle it. The gospel does not just ask us to acknowledge racism. The gospel asks us to lay down our lives for it.

The cross is the ultimate statement about racial justice.

\## What the Cross Says

At the cross, a Roman instrument of execution becomes an instrument of redemption. At the cross, a condemned criminal is executed in the most shameful way possible. At the cross, all the hierarchies of Rome—power, status, culture—are exposed as illusion.

Who is crucified? The God-man, Jesus. The one who has all authority. The one who is equal with God. The one deserving of worship and power. He is crucified.

Who is saved? Not the powerful. The Gospels record that the powerful mocked Jesus on the cross. The Gospels record that the religious leaders—the people in authority—rejected Jesus.

Who is saved? The thieves. The women. The gentiles. The people the system had already condemned. The people the law had already thrown away. The people who had no status, no power, no claim on the kingdom.

That is the cross. The ultimate reversal. The most powerful becomes the most powerless. The most righteous becomes the most condemned. The judge becomes the judged.

And through that, salvation comes. Through that shame, redemption comes. Through that death, life comes.

\## What the Cross Breaks

The cross breaks every hierarchy. The cross breaks the hierarchy of race. The cross breaks the hierarchy of power. The cross breaks the hierarchy of status.

Paul says: "As many of you as were baptized into Christ have clothed yourselves with Christ. There is neither Jew nor Greek, slave nor free, male nor female; for all of you are one in Christ Jesus." (Galatians 3:27-28)

The barriers are broken. Not in theory. Not in some spiritual realm. In practice. In the church. In the body of Christ.

If the church is the body of Christ, then the church should be the place where racial hierarchies are first broken. The church should be the place where the most marginalized person has as much voice, as much value, as much power as the most privileged person.

This is not a political statement. This is a theological statement. This is what the gospel means.

\## What the American Church Has Done Instead

Instead of embodying what the cross breaks, the American church has reinforced what the cross breaks.

The American church has maintained racial hierarchy. The American church has kept the most marginalized in the back. The American church has given power and voice and resources to the privileged.

The American church has done this theologically. The American church has created theology that justifies injustice. The American church has created theology that tells the oppressed to be patient. The American church has created theology that tells the privileged that their privilege is not their responsibility.

This is not the gospel. This is a denial of the gospel.

\## The Gospel's Radical Demand

But here is what makes the gospel more demanding than any secular theory: the gospel does not just demand that systems change. The gospel demands that we change.

CRT can call for systemic change. CRT can demand that structures be dismantled. But CRT cannot demand that we love our enemies. CRT cannot demand that we forgive those who have harmed us. CRT cannot demand that we lay down our lives for those we have hurt.

The gospel can. The gospel does.

The gospel says: "Love your enemies, bless those who curse you, do good to those who hate you, and pray for those who spitefully use you and persecute you." (Matthew 5:44)

This is not political wisdom. This is not strategic. This is subversive. This is the demand that you would actually prefer the good of the person you have hurt over your own comfort. That you would actually work for their liberation, not because it benefits you, but because they are God's beloved.

The gospel says: "Greater love has no one than this: to lay down one's life for one's friends." (John 15:13)

And Jesus is saying this to people he is about to die for. Jesus is saying this to people who are about to betray him. Jesus is saying this to people who will benefit from his death—yes, but not just the faithful. The betrayer too. The denier too. The mockers too.

The gospel demands everything. The gospel demands that you actually become a new creation. That your self-interest dies. That you are willing to sacrifice your own advantage for the sake of the other.

That is what reconciliation requires. Not just justice. Justice plus willingness to die for the enemy.

\## Why the American Church Cannot Offer This

The American church has become too comfortable to offer this. The American church has become too invested in maintaining power. The American church has become too willing to use the gospel to justify the status quo.

To offer true reconciliation, the American church would have to:

\- Give up power

\- Redistribute resources

\- Listen to people who have been harmed

\- Change structures that have benefited us

\- Repent in ways that cost something

\- Actually become the body of Christ

Instead, the American church offers diversification. The American church offers programs. The American church offers statements of support. The American church offers everything except what would actually require change.

But change is what reconciliation requires. Real change. Costly change.

\## The Vision That Is Possible

But here is the thing: the gospel's vision is possible. Not easy. Not quick. But possible.

There are churches doing this work. There are congregations where people of different races actually gather as family. There are leadership teams where power is genuinely shared. There are communities where resources are actually distributed. There are places where the cross is breaking down the walls.

These are not mega-churches. These are not famous churches. But they exist. And they point to what is possible.

What is possible is a church that actually believes the gospel. What is possible is a church that actually practices reconciliation, not just preaches it. What is possible is a church where the cross has its real power—breaking every hierarchy and making new what was divided.

\## The Prophetic Task

The prophetic task for the American church right now is not to defend itself against CRT. The prophetic task is to offer something better.

To offer a vision of justice that is rooted in God's character, not in human theory. To offer a vision of reconciliation that is rooted in the blood of Christ, not in human ability to forgive.

To say to the culture: you are right that something is wrong. You are right that systems are broken. You are right that injustice is embedded in our institutions.

But we offer you something more than a critique of the systems. We offer you transformation. We offer you the power to actually change. We offer you reconciliation that is real—not just surface-level agreement to disagree, but actual healing.

This is what the gospel offers. This is what the church could offer, if the church believed it.

\## What This Requires

What this requires is a fundamental shift in how white Christianity understands itself.

White Christianity must admit: we have gotten this wrong. We have defended slavery. We have maintained segregation. We have created theology to justify injustice. We have remained comfortable while the marginalized suffered. We have refused to repent in ways that cost.

That admission is hard. That admission requires humility. That admission requires being willing to be wrong.

But until white Christianity makes that admission, white Christianity cannot move forward. Until white Christianity admits what it has done, white Christianity cannot offer the gospel's vision of reconciliation.

\## The Costly Hope

The hope this book offers is not easy hope. It is not the hope that says everything will be fine now that we are paying attention to race. It is not the hope that systemic change will be quick or that reconciliation will be comfortable.

The hope is costly hope. The hope that says the work of justice is hard and will take generations. The hope that says reconciliation requires sacrifice and will cost us something. The hope that says we will make mistakes and will have to keep repenting.

But the hope is real hope. Because the gospel is real. Because the cross is real. Because Christ has already paid the ultimate price.

The gospel does not promise that justice will be easy. The gospel promises that justice is possible. The gospel promises that enemies can actually become family. The gospel promises that what is divided can be reconciled.

That is the hope. Not cheap hope. Costly hope.

That is the only hope worth believing in.

\## The Call

So the call to the American church is this: will you actually believe the gospel?

Will you actually dismantle systems of injustice, or will you maintain them?

Will you actually listen to voices you have ignored, or will you continue to speak for others?

Will you actually share power, or will you perform diversity while keeping control?

Will you actually repent—change your mind and change your behavior—or will you pretend you did not know what you did?

Will you actually lay down your life for the other—for the person of color you have harmed, for the people you have excluded, for the communities you have marginalized—or will you ask them to accept your comfort as reconciliation?

Will you actually become the body of Christ, or will you remain a white institution with a cross on top?

The gospel's demand is clear. The gospel's vision is clear. The gospel's power is real.

The question is: what will the American church do with it?

That question will determine whether Christianity in America is prophetic or complicit. Whether the church is a sign of the gospel or a contradiction of it. Whether we point the world toward the cross or away from it.

The answer is not yet written. The work is still to be done. The time is now.

Will you do it?

\# EXPANSIONS 1: Introduction, Chapters 1-3

\## INTRO EXPANSION: The SBC and CRT — the resolution, the backlash, the departure of Black pastors, how a resolution about analytical tools became a civil war

The Southern Baptist Convention did something in June 2019 that most people did not actually read. The denomination adopted "Resolution 9," a statement on Critical Race Theory and Intersectionality. The resolution was careful. It affirmed that "critical race theory is not to be employed as a tool to interpret Scripture," while simultaneously acknowledging "that some insights from critical race theory can prove helpful to understanding the history of racial injustice."

That was the sentence that started everything. Not the first part about rejection. The second part about acknowledgment. A thousand words of careful theological and institutional language that said: we recognize systemic racism is real, and some of the analytical tools CRT offers can help us understand it, but we do not accept CRT's anthropological structure.

This was an attempt at the impossible thing: to name systemic injustice while maintaining institutional distance from the system naming it. The resolution tried to have it both ways.

Within three years, that resolution had fractured the denomination.

What happened in those three years was not that anyone read CRT. What happened is that the term "Critical Race Theory" became a sign for something else entirely. It became a sign for the possibility that white supremacy was embedded in American institutions. It became a sign that white Christianity might have to change. It became what people who felt their power threatened used to describe the force threatening them.

By 2021, the backlash was visceral. Pastors were writing resolutions against CRT. Megachurch pastors were preaching about it. Conservative Christian activists were mobilizing. The SBC's 2021 annual meeting saw an explicit reversal, with the denomination trying to distance itself from the 2019 resolution, claiming that it had been misinterpreted or taken too far.

But something else was happening underneath the institutional posturing. Black pastors were leaving.

The Southern Baptist Convention's Black pastors had not been demanding CRT. They had been asking for something much more basic: for the denomination to honestly reckon with slavery, with segregation, with the fact that the denomination was founded to defend slavery. They had been asking for integration of leadership. They had been asking for resources to flow toward Black churches instead of away from them.

When the SBC adopted Resolution 9, some Black pastors thought: finally, institutional acknowledgment. When the SBC reversed course, some Black pastors left. They thought: the denomination is not ready to actually change. The denomination is willing to acknowledge the problem only as long as the acknowledgment costs nothing.

This was the real crisis. Not that CRT existed or did not exist. The crisis was that the denomination's attempt to manage the conversation had failed to address what Black pastors had actually been asking for. The SBC wanted to have the intellectual debate about CRT while avoiding the structural changes that actual justice would require.

By 2023, the exodus of Black SBC pastors had become noticeable enough that denomination leaders were alarmed. Preachers who had served the denomination for decades were walking away. Young Black pastors were not affiliating in the first place. The denomination that had built its strength on Black churches—and on the resources those churches generated for denomination structures—was losing the very people who had sustained it.

The resolution had meant to settle something. Instead, it had clarified what had always been true: the denomination wanted racial justice as long as racial justice did not require the denomination to give up power, resources, or theology. As long as justice could be an intellectual exercise instead of a structural transformation.

The real tragedy is not that CRT exists. The tragedy is that a denomination had to be forced by secular theory to even ask the question of whether its own systems perpetuated injustice. The real tragedy is that when Black pastors asked for change, it took an academic structure gaining cultural traction to make white denominational leadership even listen.

And when that system threatened white comfort, the leadership showed what they really believed: that their comfort mattered more than the voices of Black pastors who had been asking for these changes all along.

\## CH01 EXPANSION: More on CRT scholars — intersectionality explained, counter-narratives, interest convergence theory, how CRT differs from Marxism (class vs. race)

When Kimberlé Crenshaw published her essay "Demarginalizing the Intersection of Race and Sex" in 1989, she was trying to solve a specific legal problem. Black women were falling through the cracks of civil rights law. They could sue for discrimination, but the law required them to prove either race discrimination or sex discrimination. It could not handle a claim that was both simultaneously, that was neither reducible to race nor to sex alone.

A Black woman fired from a job did not experience racism in the way a Black man did. She did not experience sexism in the way a white woman did. She experienced a specific, distinct, intersecting harm that was the product of both her race and her gender operating together. The legal system had no category for this.

Crenshaw's intellectual move was to name that intersection. She was not inventing something new. She was naming something that had always existed. But naming it changed everything.

Intersectionality does not mean that every identity dimension always matters equally. It means that identity is never one-dimensional, and the systems of power that operate in a society operate along multiple axes simultaneously. A person's lived experience is shaped by where they sit in relation to all of those axes at once.

The practical implication: if you want to understand how injustice operates, you cannot study race in isolation from gender, class, sexuality, disability, and age. These operate together. They reinforce each other. A policy might seem race-neutral until you look at how it affects Black women versus Black men versus white women versus white men. Then the pattern changes.

This is where intersectionality becomes threatening to people invested in simpler narratives. Intersectionality refuses to let anyone off the hook by claiming that only one form of injustice matters or that they are not affected by any axis of oppression. A white woman can oppose racism while benefiting from gender solidarity with white men who also benefit from racial hierarchy. A Black man can be oppressed by racism while having patriarchal power over Black women. Systems do not sort neatly. People benefit from some hierarchies while being marginalized by others.

Crenshaw's structure is not subversive in the sense of being untruthful. It is subversive in the sense of refusing to allow anyone to claim innocence or to claim that the problem is not theirs. It is subversive in the precision of how it names harm.

The other major concept that CRT scholars developed was the idea of "counter-narratives." Richard Delgado and Jean Stefancic observed that legal systems and historical accounts have always been told from the perspective of the people in power. American legal history was written by judges, by lawyers, by the white men who had access to courts and documents. Their narratives became "the" narrative.

Counter-narratives are the stories told by people who were excluded from the official narrative. They are testimony. They are the account of what actually happened from the perspective of the people who experienced it. A counter-narrative to slavery is not just an academic reinterpretation. It is the lived testimony of enslaved people themselves—the narratives they created, the songs they sang, the resistance they organized.

The intellectual claim is that these counter-narratives are not supplemental to history. They are not anecdotes that complicate the official story. They are sources of knowledge about what actually happened. If you want to understand slavery, you have to listen to enslaved people. If you want to understand segregation, you have to listen to the people who were segregated.

This is where CRT becomes explicitly epistemological—a claim about what counts as knowledge and who gets to be a knower. American law had created a system where the knowledge of people in power counted, and the testimony of people outside power did not. CRT scholars were saying: this is not objective. This is a choice. Different choices would yield different understanding.

Derrick Bell's concept of "interest convergence" is perhaps the most cynical and also the most historically accurate observation CRT made. Bell looked at moments of racial progress in America and asked: what happened that made the people in power willing to accept this progress?

Brown v. Board of Education is the classic example. The Supreme Court ruled in 1954 that "separate but equal" was unconstitutional, that segregation in schools was inherently unequal, that segregation had to end. This was framed as a moral and legal victory, and it was. But Bell asked: why 1954? Why not 1944? Why not 1934?

His answer: interest convergence. America, in 1954, was engaged in the Cold War. The Soviet Union had massive propaganda advantage: America preached democracy while enforcing segregation. America's image in the world was suffering because of segregation. For America's international interests to be served, segregation needed to end.

This does not mean that the people who fought for desegregation did not genuinely believe in justice. It means that progress happened at the moment when justice served the interests of the people in power. Once the Cold War pressure eased, once the world stopped watching, once integration became inconvenient, the momentum reversed.

Bell's point is not optimistic. Bell's point is: do not expect the people in power to choose justice unless justice serves their interests. Plan accordingly.

This is where CRT becomes predictive. Once you understand that power shifts based on interest convergence, you can predict where those shifts will happen and where they will not. You can predict that the white majority will integrate schools as long as they can use private schools to re-segregate. You can predict that businesses will adopt diversity initiatives as long as those initiatives do not cost profit or power. You can predict that the church will acknowledge racial injustice as long as that acknowledgment does not require structural change.

Now, the question people ask: is CRT Marxist? The allegation is common. CRT came out of the critical legal studies movement. The critical legal studies movement was influenced by Marxist thought. Therefore, CRT is Marxist.

This is not entirely false and not entirely true. Marxist theory is concerned with class. Marxism sees history as driven by class conflict—between the people who own production and the people who labor. Marxists believe that capitalism inevitably produces oppression and that only revolutionary transformation of the economic system can create justice.

CRT scholars were reading some of the same critical theory as Marxists were reading, but they were asking a different question. Marxists asked: how does class determine what is possible? CRT scholars asked: how does race determine what is possible? And they observed something important: you cannot reduce race to class. You cannot say that racism is just a mask for class exploitation, that if you eliminate class you eliminate racism.

The historical evidence did not support this. In the Soviet Union, under a supposedly classless system, racial minorities were still oppressed. The problem of racism was not solved by eliminating capitalist class structures. Similarly, in the United States, you can have white working-class people with no economic interest in racism still supporting racism. You can have Black elites who benefit economically from systems that oppress Black people.

Race operates on its own logic. It is not reducible to class.

This distinction mattered historically because Marxist movements in the United States had often asked Black people to set aside racial grievances in the name of class solidarity. The logic was: we are all workers, racial divisions distract from class consciousness, focus on class and race will resolve itself.

Black people repeatedly rejected this. Black communists understood class, but they knew that poor white workers would still choose whiteness over working-class solidarity. They knew that the communist movement would still subordinate Black interests to white interests.

CRT scholars were making a similar but different claim: race is a primary organizing principle in America. It is not secondary to class. You cannot address injustice if you ignore race or reduce it to something else.

This is where CRT's actual intellectual contribution becomes clear and where its limits also become clear. CRT is precise and historically grounded about how race works in American systems. CRT has less to say about what happens after you name those systems. CRT is better at diagnosis than prescription.

\## CH02 EXPANSION: More Scripture — the Table of Nations (Genesis 10), Babel as diversity not punishment, Ruth the Moabitess, Rahab in the genealogy, the Samaritan woman

The Table of Nations is one of the most deliberately boring passages in Scripture, and its boredom is the point. Genesis 10 lists the descendants of Noah's three sons. It says: from Japheth came the peoples of the coastlands, from Ham came the peoples of Africa and Mesopotamia, from Shem came the peoples of the Middle East. And for each, it gives the genealogies and the territories.

This is not random. This is God establishing that the world is ordered into peoples. God makes nations. God marks out their lands. The diversity is intentional. The boundaries are intentional. The very next chapter, Genesis 11, explores what happens when that diversity is disrupted.

Babel is usually read as punishment. God confuses the languages. God scatters the people. God prevents them from building a tower to heaven. This is read as God punishing human pride by destroying human unity.

But there is another way to read it. The people of Babel wanted one language. They wanted one civilization. They wanted to eliminate the very diversity that Genesis 10 had established as God's creation. They wanted to consolidate power—power over land, power over each other, power against God.

God's response was not to punish diversity. God's response was to preserve it. God restored the very thing humans were trying to destroy—the different languages, the different peoples, the inability to fully consolidate power into one unified, totalitarian structure.

Babel is not the punishment of diversity. Babel is the preservation of diversity against human attempts to homogenize it for purposes of power. God, in this reading, is defending the Table of Nations.

This reframes the entire biblical narrative about ethnicity. It says: God loves diversity. God ordained diversity. When humans try to destroy diversity to consolidate power, God resists.

This is the context for understanding Ruth. Ruth is a Moabite, a foreigner. The Moabites were Israel's enemies, the people Israel had conflicts with repeatedly. Ruth comes from an enemy nation. She has no claim on the people of Israel. She has no right to be included in their community.

And yet the entire book of Ruth is about her being included. Her name ends up in the genealogy of David. She is not just included—she is honored. Her loyalty to Naomi. Her hard work. Her moral character. These are what the text celebrates.

The message is subversive: the person you thought was outside, the person from the enemy nation, is actually in. The person you thought had no claim turns out to be central. The foreigner is the one who models faithfulness.

Rahab's inclusion is even more demanding. Rahab is a Canaanite. Canaanites are not just foreigners—they are the people God has ordered Israel to drive out of the land. They are the enemy. They are under the curse that Genesis establishes. And yet Rahab is not driven out. Rahab becomes part of Israel. Rahab becomes part of David's genealogy. Rahab becomes a hero in the story.

How? By crossing a boundary. By siding with the people God is bringing in instead of siding with the people God is displacing. By choosing faithfulness to God over loyalty to her own nation.

This is the pattern: people from outside are brought in. People from the enemy nations are included. The boundaries that seemed permanent are crossed.

The Samaritan woman at the well is the New Testament version of this. Samaritans were the half-breeds, the ones Jews looked down on. The woman is a Samaritan. She is also a woman. She is also a woman with a complicated sexual history. She is multiple layers of outsider in the Jewish world.

And Jesus speaks to her. He asks for water from her. He treats her with dignity. He tells her about the living water. He reveals himself as the Messiah to her before he reveals himself to his own disciples.

The message is consistent across Scripture: the outsider, the foreigner, the enemy, the person from the other nation—these are exactly the people God is interested in. These are the people Jesus seeks out. These are the people who become central to the story.

This is not abstract theology. This is a statement about whose voice matters, whose testimony counts, whose dignity is non-negotiable.

When the American church excluded people from other nations, excluded people who looked different, created hierarchies based on ethnicity—the American church was not following Scripture. The American church was opposing Scripture.

Scripture says: from one man God made all the nations. Scripture says: God preserved diversity when humans tried to destroy it. Scripture says: the foreigner, the outsider, the enemy are the ones I am interested in. The ones the law forgot are the ones I care about.

The diversity of Scripture is not accidental. It is not decoration. It is the core of what Scripture is teaching about who God is and who God cares about.

\## CH03 EXPANSION: More history — the 3/5 compromise and the church's silence, the Dred Scott decision and Christian reaction, Reconstruction and its betrayal, the Red Summer of 1919

The Constitution of the United States was written by men who owned enslaved people. The question they had to solve: how do you count enslaved people for purposes of representation without giving enslaved people the right to vote?

The solution was the three-fifths compromise. Enslaved people would be counted as three-fifths of a person for purposes of apportionment—determining how many representatives a state would have in Congress. This meant that Southern states would have more political power than their white populations alone would justify. Enslaved people would increase Southern power even though those enslaved people had no voice and no voting rights.

It was genius mathematics for oppression: maximize the political power of slave-holding states without the inconvenience of slave-holding states having to grant enslaved people political participation.

Where were the churches? The churches that now claim to be the moral conscience of America—where were they when this was being written? Where were they when this compromise was being debated?

The churches were silent. The churches that should have said "this is an abomination" said nothing. The churches that should have mobilized their prophetic witness against slavery said nothing. The churches that owned enslaved people themselves—and many did—said nothing.

This silence had a cost. The silence meant that one of America's founding documents was written with religious sanction. The silence meant that Christian communities could point to the Constitution and say: this is legitimate. This is lawful. This is accepted by society.

One hundred years later, Chief Justice Roger Taney wrote the Dred Scott decision. Dred Scott was an enslaved man who sued for his freedom on the grounds that he had been taken to a free state. Taney's response was brutal: Black people—enslaved or free—had no rights that the white man was bound to respect. Taney went further. Taney said that slavery itself was not wrong. Taney said that the Constitution protected slavery.

The Christian response? Churches in the South hailed the decision. Southern theology doubled down. Southern churches became more aggressive in defending slavery theologically. The decision gave religious language legal authority. Legal authority gave religious language moral authority.

It was a machine of mutual reinforcement: the law endorsed slavery, the church endorsed the law, the law protected the church's right to endorse slavery. The system was self-perpetuating.

After the Civil War, Reconstruction offered a moment. For a brief period—roughly twelve years—it looked like America might actually integrate. Black people were voting. Black men were holding office. Black churches were being built. Black communities were organizing. It was not perfect. It was not without violence. But it was a moment where the arc of history bent toward justice.

Then white resistance mobilized. The church was part of that resistance. Southern churches lost property and influence during Reconstruction. Southern churches became organizations for resistance. Southern churches organized against Black political participation. Southern theology justified violence against Black communities.

By 1877, Reconstruction was over. The federal government withdrew. White Southern power reasserted itself. Jim Crow was coming. Segregation was coming. And the churches that had resisted Reconstruction would be the churches that defended segregation.

The Red Summer of 1919 is barely known by most Americans, white or Black. It should be remembered the way we remember lynch mobs, because it was organized racial violence, burning of Black neighborhoods, murders of Black people—and it happened during the summer when Americans were supposed to be celebrating victory in World War I.

In Chicago, a young Black man drowned after white swimmers threw rocks at him. A white mob rioted. The riot burned. Thirty-eight people died. Over a thousand Black homes were destroyed. The mobs were white. The police protected the mobs. The newspapers covered it as a race riot—as if it had been mutual violence.

The churches said nothing. The white churches of Chicago said nothing about the violence. The white churches did not open their buildings to refugees from the burned neighborhoods. The white churches did not speak prophetically against the mobs.

The silence was compliance. The neutrality was participation.

This is the history that white Christianity does not teach its children. This is the history that Sunday school omits. This is the history that seminary students do not learn unless they actively seek it out.

And because this history is not taught, white Christianity can claim ignorance. White Christianity can say: we did not know. We were not part of that. That was a different generation.

But you cannot claim the benefits of history while refusing to acknowledge the costs. You cannot benefit from the property, the power, the social position that was built through slavery and segregation and then claim that you are not responsible for what built it.

The churches of America built their wealth on slavery. The churches of America defended segregation. The churches of America remained silent during violence against Black communities. The churches of America then moved to all-white suburbs and started new churches in neighborhoods that had been redlined and kept poor.

That is the unfinished history. That is what the American church has not confessed.

Critical Race Theory did not arrive in American churches and institutions by accident. CRT arrived because something was broken that no one was fixing. CRT arrived because injustice was embedded in systems, and the people who benefited from injustice were not willing to see it or name it.

\## INTRO EXPANSION 3: The book's theological architecture — why systemic injustice is a biblical category, the connection between personal sin and corporate sin, why reconciliation is central to Christian eschatology

\## CH01 EXPANSION 4: How systems persist and adapt — the mechanism of institutional change resistance, the role of cognitive dissonance in defending unjust systems, what it takes to actually dismantle an unjust system

Unjust systems do not persist because people are obviously evil. Unjust systems persist because they are normal. They are the water we swim in. They are the way things are done.

When you are born into an unjust system, you learn the system's logic. You learn what is acceptable. You learn what is not asked. You learn the stories that justify the system.

A white child in 1950s America learned that segregation was necessary, that Black people preferred separation, that integration would cause social chaos. The child learned this from parents, from school, from church. The child did not choose the belief. The child absorbed it.

By the time the child is an adult, the belief feels like truth. It feels like it is rooted in nature, not in the system. The adult has a stake in the system—they have built a home in a segregated neighborhood, they have gotten a job in a segregated workplace, they have joined a segregated church. The system has made them comfortable.

When someone challenges the system, the adult experiences cognitive dissonance. The challenge threatens the worldview the adult was raised in. It threatens the system that has benefited the adult. The adult has a psychological and material stake in defending the system.

What does the adult do? The adult does what people do when facing cognitive dissonance. The adult searches for reasons to dismiss the challenge. The adult looks for evidence that the system is actually just. The adult reinterprets facts to fit the worldview.

This is not conscious evil. This is human psychology. This is how all of us respond to threats to our worldview and our security.

But the effect is that unjust systems become durable. They persist because the people who benefit from them are psychologically invested in defending them.

How do unjust systems actually change? Change happens when the system becomes unsustainable. When the cost of maintaining the system exceeds the benefit. When enough people decide that maintaining the system is not worth the cost.

Change also happens when the people in power face people who refuse to accept the system. When the oppressed organize. When they refuse to cooperate with their own oppression. When they build alternative systems that demonstrate that another way is possible.

In the American South, segregation persisted as long as Black people seemed to accept it. Segregation began to crack when Black people refused to accept it. When Black people organized. When Black people demanded change and were willing to pay the cost for it.

White people did not decide to desegregate because they suddenly became moral. White people desegregated because Black people made segregation too costly. The bus boycott cost the city money. The sit-ins disrupted commerce. The march on Washington demanded change.

What does it take to dismantle an unjust system? It takes pressure from the people who are oppressed by the system. It takes organized, persistent, costly resistance. It takes people who are willing to sacrifice.

It also takes people who benefit from the system deciding that maintaining the system is not worth the cost. It takes some white people deciding to join the movement. It takes the moral courage to say: I benefited from this system, and I am going to stop defending it.

This is what justice requires. Not just the oppressed organizing. But the oppressor choosing to stop participating in their own oppression and in the oppression of others.

This does not happen automatically. It happens when people of conscience are confronted with the reality of what the system does, and they decide that they cannot participate anymore.

The gospel is supposed to create people of conscience. The gospel is supposed to create people who see the oppressed as fully human. The gospel is supposed to create people who are willing to sacrifice their own comfort for the sake of justice.

If the American church offered this, the church would be powerful. The church would be an agent of actual change, not just talk about change.

\## CH02 EXPANSION 2: Diversity as God's design — why God established diversity at Babel, why homogeneity is not godly, what a multiethnic church means theologically

The account of Babel is often told as a story of punishment. God scattered the people because they tried to build a tower. God confused their languages. God dispersed them across the earth.

But this reading misses something crucial. God did not scatter the people because diversity is bad. God scattered the people because attempting to create a unified human empire is rebellion against God.

The humans at Babel wanted to make a name for themselves. They wanted to consolidate power. They wanted to create a centralized human authority that would stand apart from God.

God's response was to establish diversity. God gave them different languages so they could not coordinate the oppressive project. God scattered them so they would have to live in different communities.

This is God protecting diversity. This is God defending against the homogenization that serves power.

Throughout Scripture, diversity is presented as God's design. The Table of Nations in Genesis 10 lists the diversity of peoples and languages that God established. Revelation describes the new creation as including "every nation, tribe, people and language."

This is not a secondary feature. This is central to God's design for creation.

What does this mean for the church? It means that the church's vocation is to embody diversity, not to erase it. It means that a genuinely Christian community is one where people from different nations, tribes, peoples, and languages actually gather together.

But gathering together is not enough. Gathering together while maintaining hierarchy, while maintaining white leadership, while maintaining patterns of dominance—that is not what God intends.

What God intends is genuine multiethnic community where power is actually shared, where different traditions are genuinely valued, where the perspectives of people from different backgrounds shape the community.

This is what Revelation envisions. Not people from every nation assimilated into a single dominant culture. But people from every nation standing together, maintaining their distinct traditions, united in worship before God.

This is the vision the church is called to embody. This is why diversity is not a program or an initiative. This is why diversity is central to the church's mission.

\## CH04 EXPANSION: More King — the Montgomery bus boycott as church-led, the Selma march as liturgical act, King's democratic socialism, his critique of capitalism, why the real King would be too demanding for most white evangelicals today

People think the Montgomery bus boycott was spontaneous. A woman named Rosa Parks was tired, and she would not give up her seat, and that sparked the movement. This is the mythology.

The real history is that the Montgomery bus boycott was organized. Rosa Parks was a member of the NAACP. She was trained in nonviolence. The boycott was planned. The organizers had been waiting for exactly this kind of incident—an incident that was clear, that was easy for people to understand, that did not involve any criminal activity except the crime of being Black in a segregated system.

And who organized it? The churches. The Black churches of Montgomery gathered. The Black pastors organized. Martin Luther King, a young pastor at Dexter Avenue Baptist Church, became the public face. But the Montgomery bus boycott was a church movement. The churches provided the money. The churches provided the meeting spaces. The churches provided the moral authority.

White Christianity has largely forgotten this. When we talk about the civil rights movement, we talk about a secular social movement that King happened to be part of. We do not talk about the fact that the movement was rooted in Black churches, organized by Black pastors, sustained by church people gathering night after night to pray and sing and strategize.

The Selma march is another example. In 1965, Black people were being prevented from registering to vote. The churches organized a march from Selma to Montgomery—fifty-four miles—to demand voting rights. They did not march as a political party. They marched as a religious movement. They sang hymns. They prayed. They carried the spiritual tradition of the Black church into the streets.

The march on the Edmund Pettus Bridge—the moment when state troopers on horseback beat marchers with whips—that was not a political confrontation. That was a liturgical act. People were sacrificing their bodies. People were submitting to violence without retaliating. People were bearing witness.

This is what the Black church tradition understood about resistance: resistance is an act of faith. Resistance is corporate prayer made visible. Resistance is the body of Christ offering itself as testimony.

White Christianity largely missed this. White Christianity saw the march as a political act. White Christianity counted the cost politically. White Christianity asked whether it was wise. White Christianity did not understand that the march was an act of worship.

Now, King's economic thinking. Most white evangelical churches have domesticated King into a safe figure. King the dreamer. King who wanted integration. King who was nonviolent. King who asked nicely for equality.

The real King was asking for something much more demanding. In "Where Do We Go from Here: Chaos or Community?" King explicitly attacked capitalism. He said: "The profit motive alone is not adequate to meet the needs of every member of the society."

This is the King white Christianity does not teach. This is the King that threatens the status quo.

King was not asking for minor reforms to help poor people. King was asking for a fundamental restructuring of the economy. King was arguing that capitalism itself—not just racist capitalism, but capitalism itself—produced poverty and exploitation.

King said in 1967: "In a sense, you could say we're not even using socialism. We used capitalism in the extreme—the most wasteful nation in the world."

And then: "Something is wrong with capitalism... the profit motive alone is not adequate to meet the needs of every member of the society."

This is subversive. This is not the King quoted in evangelical churches. This is the King that evangelicals have had to erase.

King was also deeply critical of American militarism. His sermon "Beyond Vietnam" came in 1967, a year before his assassination. In this sermon, King said that America's military adventures in Vietnam were morally wrong. He said that America was spending money on bombs that should be spent on poverty. He said that America had to undergo "a subversive reordering of national priorities."

This was before he was killed. This is the trajectory he was on. King was moving toward an increasingly subversive critique of American society itself. King was not asking for integration into the American system. King was asking for America to at its core change.

If the real King had lived another decade, what would he have been saying? What would he have been demanding? Would he have been marching against American military presence around the world? Would he have been mobilizing for reparations? Would he have been building international solidarity with liberation movements?

We will never know because King was assassinated. But we can infer from the direction he was moving.

The tragedy is that King's radicalism was necessary in 1967, and it remains necessary in 2026. The American system still produces poverty. The American military is still involved in endless wars. American capitalism still produces inequality that is obscene.

If King were alive today, he would not be celebrating the progress we have made. He would be asking: why are we still in this? Why do we still have poverty in the richest nation in the world? Why are we still spending a trillion dollars a year on military when children are going hungry?

The real King would be calling American evangelicalism to account. The real King would be asking: how can you claim to follow Jesus while supporting systems that kill and exploit? How can you claim to believe in the gospel while defending capitalism that produces poverty?

This is why white evangelicals have had to domesticate King. The real King is too threatening. The real King demands too much. The real King would not let evangelical churches off the hook.

\## CH05 EXPANSION: More on corporate sin — Achan's sin affecting all Israel (Joshua 7), the generational consequences in Exodus 34:7, the prophets' corporate indictments (Amos 1-2, Isaiah 1, Micah 6), why American individualism cannot handle this biblical category

In Joshua 7, Israel is supposed to be conquering Canaan. The city of Jericho falls. Israel is supposed to take the next city, Ai. But something goes wrong. The soldiers of Israel are defeated. Fewer than forty men die, but the loss is shocking because it is unexpected.

Joshua is confused and angry. He asks God what went wrong. God's answer is specific: someone has violated the covenant. Someone has taken something they were not supposed to take. Achan. Achan took a cloak from Jericho. Achan took silver and gold. Achan violated the command that said everything in Jericho is devoted to God.

What happens next is what makes American readers uncomfortable. Achan is found out. Not just Achan is executed—his entire family is executed. His sons, his daughters, his animals, everything he has. The whole family is killed for Achan's sin.

This is deeply offensive to modern American sensibilities. We have a concept called individual responsibility. Achan's sin is Achan's sin. Achan's family should not be punished for it. It is unjust.

But Joshua understands something we have lost: Achan is not an individual. Achan is a member of a family. Achan's sin is a corporate sin. The entire household is implicated.

This is not unique to Joshua. The book of Exodus speaks about the consequences of sin extending to the third and fourth generations. God says: "I, the Lord your God, am a jealous God, punishing the children for the sin of the parents to the third and fourth generation."

This is not about individual guilt. This is about how sin works structurally. When a father steals and builds wealth on that theft, the children inherit that wealth. The children did not steal. But the children benefit. The children are implicated.

When a nation enslaves people and builds its economy on slavery, the next generation does not enslave people. But the next generation inherits the wealth built through slavery. The next generation is implicated.

The prophets understood this. When Amos says, "For three sins of Israel, even for four, I will not relent," Amos is not naming individual sins. Amos is naming the sins of a nation. The sins of Israel as a people.

Isaiah says in chapter 1: "Your hands are full of blood." Isaiah is speaking to the nation. Isaiah is saying that the nation as a whole is guilty. Not just the individuals who committed violence, but the nation that allowed and benefited from violence.

Micah says to the leaders of Jacob: "You eat the flesh of my people and tear away their skin." This is corporate indictment. This is naming that the system as a whole consumes people.

The prophetic tradition in Scripture understands corporate sin in ways that American individualism cannot handle. American individualism says: each person is responsible for their own actions. If you personally did not commit the sin, you are not guilty.

Scripture says something different. Scripture says: you benefit from systems that are sinful. You are part of a people that perpetuates injustice. You inherit the consequences of what your ancestors did. This is not just true spiritually—it is true materially.

White America inherited wealth built on slavery. White America lives in neighborhoods that exist because of redlining. White America benefits from an educational system where schools in white neighborhoods receive more resources than schools in Black neighborhoods. This is not individual virtue or vice. This is structural.

American individualism cannot process this. American Christianity has adopted American individualism. So American Christianity cannot process the prophetic tradition's understanding of corporate sin.

When a Christian says, "I did not personally own slaves, so I am not guilty," that person is operating from American individualism, not from biblical logic. The biblical logic would be: "I benefit from systems built through slavery. I am part of a nation that perpetuates segregation. I inherit the consequences. What do I do about that?"

This is the category the American church has not developed. The American church has no theology of corporate repentance. The American church has no structure for how an individual can identify with corporate sin and seek corporate transformation.

Until we develop this, we cannot address systemic injustice. We can only address individual sin. And individual sin can exist within the most unjust systems.

\## CH06 EXPANSION: More on what CRT gets right — redlining and its church connections, the GI Bill's racial exclusions, how wealth gaps were created by policy not just individual choices, what this means for Christian ethics

Redlining is not ancient history. Redlining is the explicit policy of the Federal Housing Administration to refuse to insure mortgages in neighborhoods with Black residents. The FHA had maps. The maps had red lines around neighborhoods with Black people. Those red lines meant "hazardous." Hazardous meant the government would not insure mortgages there.

This was not accident. This was not market force. This was government policy. This was systematic exclusion of Black people from the primary wealth-building mechanism available to Americans in the twentieth century.

White families in other neighborhoods could get FHA-insured mortgages. They could buy homes. They could build equity. They could pass the homes to their children. Thirty years of mortgage payments created tremendous wealth. The home that cost \$5,000 in 1945 was worth \$150,000 by 1975. That wealth transferred to the next generation.

Black families could not participate. Black families were blocked from the same neighborhoods, blocked from the same mortgages, blocked from the same wealth-building. So Black families remained renters. Black families could not build home equity. Black families remained poor.

The wealth gap between white and Black families is not because white people are more hardworking or more responsible. The wealth gap exists because government policy explicitly excluded Black people from wealth-building mechanisms.

The church was involved. White churches sat in those all-white neighborhoods that were preserved through redlining. White churches benefited from living in neighborhoods where property values were stable. White churches built buildings in redlined neighborhoods, knowing those neighborhoods would remain all-white and prosperous.

Black churches sat in redlined neighborhoods, the ones the government had marked as hazardous, the ones where no one would invest, the ones where property deteriorated and people remained poor.

The GI Bill offers another example. After World War II, the federal government offered education and mortgage benefits to veterans. This was presented as an opportunity for all Americans. But in practice, the GI Bill was distributed unequally.

Universities in the South refused to admit Black students. So Black veterans could not use their education benefits at Southern universities. Northern universities admitted Black students, but segregation in housing meant Black students could not live in the neighborhoods where white students lived. Housing discrimination limited where they could actually settle after completing their education.

Mortgage benefits were available to Black veterans, but mortgage discrimination meant that banks and sellers would not sell to them. So the benefit was theoretical.

The GI Bill created a generation of home-owning, educated white Americans. The same benefits were offered to Black veterans on paper, but implemented in ways that excluded them. The result: white wealth increased dramatically. Black wealth did not increase proportionally.

Again, this is not individual choice. This is policy. This is systems that look neutral—a benefit available to all veterans—that function to exclude based on race.

These are the facts that CRT names accurately. These are the policies that created the wealth gap. These are the structures that remain in place because no one has dismantled them.

What does this mean for Christian ethics?

If you benefit from policies that excluded others, what is your responsibility? If you live in a neighborhood that exists because others were excluded, what do you owe?

American Christianity has avoided these questions. American Christianity has taught individual responsibility—if you personally did not discriminate, you have done nothing wrong. American Christianity has not developed a structure for structural or systemic responsibility.

Biblical ethics would ask different questions. Biblical ethics would ask: am I benefiting from injustice? Am I complicit in systems that harm others? What do I owe the people harmed by these systems?

These are not comfortable questions. These are also the questions that the prophets ask in Scripture.

\## CH04 EXPANSION 2: King's theological method — reading Scripture against culture, hermeneutics of resistance, why white evangelical hermeneutics missed what King saw

Martin Luther King's theological method was different from white evangelical theology. King read Scripture against culture. King asked: what is Scripture saying to the situation we face right now?

This is not the white evangelical method. White evangelical theology tends to read Scripture as timeless truth that applies universally. The method is: what does this text teach about doctrine? What does this text teach about individual spirituality?

King's method was different. King asked: what is Scripture saying to people who are oppressed? What does Exodus teach to enslaved people? What does the prophetic tradition teach to people being crushed by injustice?

This is a hermeneutics of resistance. This is reading Scripture from below, from the perspective of the oppressed, asking what Scripture has to say to the oppressed.

White evangelical theology could not do this. White evangelical theology was reading from above, from the perspective of power, asking what Scripture teaches about the social order.

When King read "Let my people go," King heard God speaking to enslaved people in America. When King read the prophets condemning injustice, King heard God speaking to Black Americans facing segregation.

White evangelical theology read the same texts and heard something different. White evangelical theology heard that God wanted order. White evangelical theology heard that God wanted obedience to authorities. White evangelical theology heard that God wanted people to be satisfied with their spiritual condition even if their material condition was oppressive.

This is not accidental. This is the difference between reading Scripture from the position of power and reading it from the position of powerlessness.

If you have power, you naturally read Scripture as teaching that power is legitimate, that order should be maintained, that your position should be preserved. If you are powerless, you naturally read Scripture as teaching liberation, as calling for justice, as promising that God will overthrow oppressive systems.

This is why white evangelical theology missed what King saw. White evangelical theology could not read Scripture as liberation because white evangelical theologians were not living in oppression. White evangelical theologians were defending the system, not resisting it.

The Black church maintained a different hermeneutic. The Black church read Scripture as liberation literature because the Black church was living the reality of oppression.

This hermeneutical difference is not a side issue. This is fundamental to what it means to read Scripture faithfully.

\## CH05 EXPANSION 3: The personhood of the oppressed — why corporate sin requires naming the oppressed as fully human, dignity and corporate transformation, why individual solutions fail to address corporate harm

When we talk about systemic injustice, we are necessarily talking about persons who are harmed by those systems. You cannot name systemic racism without naming the people who experience it.

This is where the personhood of the oppressed becomes crucial. If you see the oppressed as fully human, as bearing the image of God, then you have to ask: what is the system doing to these people?

But if you have absorbed the logic of the system, you might not see the oppressed as fully human. You might see them as less intelligent, less capable, less worthy. You might see their suffering as deserved, as a result of their own failure.

This is the power of racism. Racism does not just create systems. Racism creates a way of seeing people that justifies those systems. Racism says: the reason Black people are poor is because they are lazy, not intelligent, not disciplined.

Once you have accepted this way of seeing, you have justified the system. You have made the system seem natural, inevitable, deserved.

Addressing systemic racism requires rejecting this way of seeing. It requires seeing the oppressed as fully human, as fully capable, as fully worthy of dignity and respect.

It requires asking: if this person is fully human, fully capable, why are they poor? And the answer cannot be: because they are lazy. The answer must be: because the systems deliberately exclude them. The answer must be: because the system is rigged.

This is where individual solutions fail. Individual solutions say: work harder, educate yourself, pull yourself up by your bootstraps. But if the system is designed to exclude you no matter what you do, individual solutions will not work.

A Black person can be as educated as a white person and still face discrimination in hiring. A Black person can work as hard as a white person and still remain poor. A Black person can do everything right and still be denied the same opportunities as a white person who does less.

Individual solutions cannot address this. Only systemic solutions can. Only changing the system itself can address what the system has created.

This is what corporate repentance requires. It requires that the people who have benefited from the system acknowledge what the system has done and work to dismantle it. It requires that the people who have been harmed by the system be recognized as fully human and have the system changed for their benefit.

\## CH05 EXPANSION 4: The jubilee as systemic reset — why God mandated regular economic reset, what modern jubilee would look like, why American Christianity has erased jubilee from the theological imagination

In the law of Moses, jubilee was mandated. Every fiftieth year, land returned to original owners. Debts were forgiven. Enslaved people were freed. The economic system reset.

This was not optional. This was not something to consider. This was law. This was what kept Israel's society from developing permanent inequality.

Why did God mandate jubilee? Because without jubilee, the rich get richer, the poor get poorer, and economic inequality becomes hereditary. The child born to a rich family has wealth. The child born to a poor family has nothing and has to sell themselves into servitude. Within a few generations, you have a permanent class system.

Jubilee interrupted this. Jubilee said: no, you do not get to inherit permanent power over others. The system resets. Equity is restored.

This is a subversive economic theory. This is a theory that assumes that God cares about economic justice. This is a theory that assumes that permanent inequality is unjust.

Modern American Christianity has largely ignored jubilee. We do not preach about jubilee. We do not think about jubilee. We have erased jubilee from the theological imagination.

Why? Because jubilee is incompatible with capitalism. Jubilee requires regular redistribution of wealth. Jubilee requires that some people give up wealth. Jubilee requires limiting the ability of the rich to accumulate.

American Christianity has aligned with capitalism. American Christianity teaches that the accumulation of wealth is virtuous. American Christianity teaches that the rich deserve their wealth because they worked hard. American Christianity teaches that redistribution is theft.

This is at its core anti-jubilee. This is at its core anti-biblical.

What would modern jubilee look like? It would look like reparations. It would look like education funding for communities that have been disinvested. It would look like healthcare access for communities that have been neglected. It would look like wealth transfer from the wealthy to those who have been robbed.

It would cost something. It would mean that the wealthy lose some wealth. It would mean that the comfortable become less comfortable.

It would also mean that inequality decreases. It would mean that a child born into poverty has a real chance to build a life. It would mean that the system resets.

Is this possible in modern America? Is it realistic to expect jubilee in a capitalist society?

It is not realistic if you think politically. Politically, the wealthy will not vote for policies that redistribute their wealth. Politically, jubilee is impossible.

But theologically, jubilee is non-negotiable. Theologically, regular economic reset is what God requires. Theologically, permanent inequality is idolatry.

The American church has chosen the political calculation over the theological conviction. The American church has decided that capitalism matters more than biblical justice.

What would it look like for the American church to recover jubilee? It would look like churches giving away resources. It would look like churches funding Black institutions. It would look like churches redistributing wealth to repair what was stolen.

It would look like churches resisting capitalism. It would look like churches saying: we will not accumulate wealth. We will distribute what we have. We will practice jubilee.

This is what would make the American church prophetic. Not just talking about justice. But practicing jubilee. But actually redistributing wealth.

Most churches are not willing to do this. Most churches like their wealth. Most churches have no interest in jubilee.

But this is what Scripture requires. This is what God mandates. This is the test of whether the American church actually believes the Bible or just likes the parts that do not cost anything.

\## CH07 EXPANSION: More on CRT weaknesses — standpoint epistemology problems, the risk of essentializing racial identity, the absence of grace and redemption in some CRT-informed activism, how cancellation replaces repentance

Standpoint epistemology is the claim that people who have experienced oppression have a more accurate understanding of how oppression works than people who have not experienced it. This is partly true. It is true that lived experience is valuable. A person who has been refused a mortgage because of race knows something about housing discrimination that statistics alone do not convey.

But standpoint epistemology, taken to its extreme, has a problem: it claims that the oppressed have automatic authority and the oppressor has automatic disqualification. A white person cannot understand racism because they have not experienced racism. An oppressor cannot speak about oppression because they have benefited from it.

This becomes epistemologically bankrupt quickly. If only oppressed people can speak about oppression, then:

\- Only women can speak about sexism (are gender non-binary people excluded?)

\- Only poor people can speak about poverty (but poverty changes, so can someone speak who is no longer poor?)

\- Only people with disabilities can speak about ableism (but there are many disabilities, do all perspectives count equally?)

More troubling: if only the oppressed can speak, then we have conceded that knowledge is not about truth, it is about identity. It is about who you are, not what you know. This is not a structure for finding truth. This is a structure for managing power.

CRT in its academic form is more careful about this. Derrick Bell and Kimberlé Crenshaw both acknowledged that people outside a group can understand systems that oppress that group. They were making an epistemological claim about the value of listening to people who have experienced oppression, not a claim that others cannot understand.

But in popular application, standpoint epistemology becomes weaponized. It becomes: if you are white, your voice does not matter on race. If you are a man, your voice does not matter on gender. This prevents genuine dialogue. This prevents people who have benefited from unjust systems from engaging in the work of changing those systems.

The second weakness is the risk of essentializing racial identity. CRT scholars are careful to say that race is a social construct. But in popular application, race gets treated as an essential category. It gets treated as if all white people are the same, all Black people are the same, all Asian people are the same.

This is not true. There is tremendous diversity within racial categories. A Black person raised in an upper-middle-class home and a Black person raised in poverty have radically different experiences. Both are Black. Both have experienced anti-Blackness. But their material circumstances are different.

When CRT-influenced frameworks treat race as essential—when they treat all white people as oppressors or all Black people as victims—they lose the nuance that CRT itself promised to provide. They become what CRT accused other frameworks of being: reductive.

The third weakness is the absence of grace and redemption. CRT is good at naming what went wrong. CRT is good at analyzing how systems perpetuate injustice. CRT has much less to say about what happens after the analysis.

In some applications, particularly in activist spaces influenced by CRT, the response to racism has become cancellation. A person says the wrong thing, a person's past statements are scrutinized, a person's career ends. The assumption is that the person is irredeemable, that they cannot change, that the only response is removal.

This is where CRT-influenced activism parts ways with the gospel. The gospel assumes that people can change. The gospel assumes that confession and repentance are possible. The gospel assumes that a person who has perpetuated injustice can actually repent and become an agent of justice.

Some CRT-influenced activism does not have room for this. Some CRT-influenced activism has room only for condemnation. This means that people who might otherwise engage in the work of change are instead pushed away. They become defensive. They become convinced that they can never be redeemed.

The prophetic tradition in Scripture is different. The prophets judge. The prophets condemn systems and the people who maintain those systems. But the prophets also offer repentance. The prophets say: you can change. You can turn. You can participate in a different future.

Jonah offers the extreme version. Jonah does not want Nineveh to repent. Jonah wants Nineveh to be destroyed. But God offers Nineveh repentance. And Nineveh repents. The city is saved. Jonah is angry that God is merciful.

This is the tension: God judges, and God offers redemption. God condemns systems, and God calls people to abandon those systems and join the new thing God is doing.

Some CRT-influenced activism loses this. Some CRT-influenced activism becomes purely judgmental. It becomes about purity tests and calling out. It becomes about making sure you are on the right side of the analysis. It stops being about actually changing systems.

This is a significant limit to CRT as a structure for justice. CRT can tell you what is wrong. The gospel can tell you how to actually fix it and how to actually change as a person in the process.

\## CH08 EXPANSION: More on white evangelical history — Jerry Falwell Sr. and Liberty University's segregation origins, Bob Jones University's interracial dating ban, the Southern Strategy and evangelical politics

Jerry Falwell Sr. founded Liberty University in 1971. This is important. This is after desegregation. This is after the Civil Rights Act of 1964. This is in the moment when integration was being forced on American institutions.

Liberty University was founded as a segregation academy. It was founded so that white Christian families could educate their children without them attending integrated schools. Falwell was explicit about this. Falwell said that he was concerned about what was being taught in secular universities, about the moral decline of America. But the timing is crucial. Liberty was founded in response to desegregation.

Falwell said: I am building a Christian university where children will be educated in a Christian environment. But that Christian environment was a segregated environment. Falwell did not accept Black students at Liberty until 1971, when federal pressure forced him to. And even then, Falwell discouraged Black enrollment.

Bob Jones University offers an even clearer example. BJU was founded in 1927 as a Bible college. But by the 1950s, BJU was explicit: the university forbade interracial dating. This was not implicit. This was not suggested. This was an explicit, stated rule: no dating between races.

Why? BJU claimed it was biblical. BJU claimed that God made races distinct and intended for them to remain separate. BJU claimed that interracial dating violated God's design.

This rule remained in place until 2000. Two thousand. Not 1960. Not 1970. 2000. When the rule was finally removed, it was removed quietly, with minimal acknowledgment.

Think about what this means: a Christian university explicitly forbade young people from different races from dating based on biblical grounds. A Christian university enforced segregation on personal relationships. A Christian university kept this rule in place for forty years after the Civil Rights Act.

And evangelicals acted shocked when people outside the church criticized evangelicalism for racism. Evangelicals pointed to figures like Billy Graham, who had taken small steps toward integration. Evangelicals claimed that they were actually progressive on race.

But Liberty University and Bob Jones University reveal the truth: the evangelical response to desegregation was to build alternative institutions that maintained segregation. The evangelical response was not to lead on integration. The evangelical response was to resist and to find ways around integration.

The Southern Strategy is part of this history. The Southern Strategy is the political strategy Republicans adopted in the 1960s and 1970s. The strategy was: if you want Southern votes, you do not have to say you support segregation explicitly. You use coded language. You talk about "law and order." You talk about "states' rights." You talk about "traditional values." You do not mention race. But the message to Southern white voters is clear: we will defend your way of life against the changes coming.

Evangelicals embraced the Southern Strategy. Evangelical leaders like Jerry Falwell shifted from being apolitical or Democratic to being Republican specifically because the Republican Party was using language that signaled opposition to integration without saying it explicitly.

This is the history of the evangelical-Republican alliance. This is where it came from. It came not from abortion. It came from resistance to integration.

The Moral Majority, which Falwell founded in 1979, was presented as a response to moral decline and secular humanism. But the timing is important. The Moral Majority was founded after desegregation had been forced on the country. The Moral Majority provided a way for evangelicals to politically organize against the changes they opposed.

Abortion was part of the coalition. But the core energy, the organizing principle, was resistance to the loss of white evangelical cultural dominance.

Once you understand this history, you understand why white evangelicalism has been so resistant to racial justice. You understand why white evangelicals panic when questions of race come up. You understand why white evangelicals attack CRT so viciously. CRT is naming exactly what white evangelicalism has been defending for fifty years: systems that maintain white power and white cultural dominance.

\## CH09 EXPANSION: More Black theology — James Cone's "The Cross and the Lynching Tree", Frederick Douglass's "Slaveholder's Sermon", the difference between slave religion and slaveholder religion

James Cone wrote a book called "The Cross and the Lynching Tree." This is a description of what it means: the cross, which is the central symbol of Christianity, is the execution of an innocent person by an unjust state. The lynching tree, which is the central image of American racial terrorism, is also the execution of an innocent person by a racist mob.

Cone is saying: these are the same thing. Both are executions. Both are the execution of the powerless by the powerful. Both are acts of violence meant to maintain hierarchy. Both are supposedly justified as necessary. Both claim some moral legitimacy.

Christianity has traditionally read the cross as redemption. The death of Jesus purchases salvation. But Cone is asking: what would it mean to read the cross as lynching? What would it mean to say that Jesus was lynched? That Jesus was executed by a mob that had the law's approval? That Jesus was an innocent person murdered to maintain a power structure?

If Jesus was lynched, then the cross is not primarily about individual salvation. The cross is about God standing with the executed. The cross is about God identifying with the victims of state and mob violence. The cross is God refusing to accept that the execution of the innocent is justified.

This reframes the entire gospel. It says: the gospel is not good news to the powerful. The gospel is good news to the lynched. The gospel says that your execution was wrong. The gospel says that the system that executed you will be judged. The gospel says that God is on your side, not on the side of the mob or the state.

Frederick Douglass wrote something similar. Douglass was an enslaved person who escaped and became an abolitionist. He wrote an essay called "The Slaveholder's Sermon." In this essay, Douglass contrasts the religion of the slave with the religion of the slaveholder.

The slave's religion was about God's liberation. The slave's religion was about Exodus. The slave's religion was about freedom. The slave gathered in secret and sang about getting to the promised land, about freedom by and by, about a God who sees the enslaved and will act.

The slaveholder's religion was about obedience. The slaveholder preached to the enslaved: obey your masters, honor your masters, accept your condition. The slaveholder took Scripture and used it to justify slavery. The slaveholder created theology to defend the indefensible.

These are not two interpretations of the same Christianity. These are two different religions. They share the same book. But they read it completely differently. The slave reads it as a book about liberation. The slaveholder reads it as a book about submission.

Douglass's insight: the religion taught by the slaveholder would destroy the soul of anyone who believed it. If you believed the slaveholder's sermon, you would accept slavery as legitimate. You would accept your own oppression. You would accept the oppression of others.

But the religion practiced by the enslaved creates freedom. It creates the possibility of resistance. It creates the conviction that God is on your side, that your freedom is coming, that the system oppressing you will fall.

This is the difference between slave religion and slaveholder religion. This is the difference between a gospel that liberates and a gospel that domesticates.

White Christianity has largely inherited the slaveholder's religion. White Christianity has read Scripture as a book about obedience, submission, and acceptance of the status quo. White Christianity has created theology that justifies the system.

Black Christianity has maintained the slave's religion. Black Christianity has read Scripture as a book about liberation. Black Christianity has created theology that empowers resistance and hope.

This is why James Cone insists that white Christians have to learn from Black theology. This is why white Christianity needs to learn from the Black church. White Christianity cannot see the liberating Jesus because white Christianity has been trained by the slaveholder's sermon to read Scripture in a way that domesticates it.

The real Jesus is the Jesus of the enslaved. The real Jesus is the Jesus who identifies with the lynched. The real Jesus is the Jesus who says: your oppression is not your fault, your dignity cannot be taken from you, God is working for your liberation.

This is the Jesus that white Christianity has largely lost. This is the Jesus that Black Christianity has never lost.

\## CH07 EXPANSION 2: The prophetic tradition's accountability structure — how the prophets named systems while offering redemption, why Jeremiah called for surrender to Babylon, Ezekiel's vision of restoration, the pattern of judgment followed by hope

The prophets in Scripture offer a model that goes beyond both pure condemnation and cheap reconciliation. The prophets named injustice ferociously. The prophets condemned the leaders of Israel without hesitation. But the prophets also offered a path forward that included redemption.

Jeremiah is the difficult case. Jeremiah preached that Jerusalem should surrender to Babylon. This was not a popular message. This was not a message that made Jeremiah beloved. The people wanted Jeremiah to prophesy victory. Jeremiah prophesied defeat.

But why did Jeremiah preach surrender? Because Jeremiah understood that the system Jerusalem had built was unjust and unsustainable. Jerusalem had become a city of oppression, of corruption, of violence against the vulnerable. God was judging that system. The judgment was coming.

But Jeremiah also offered something: if you surrender, if you acknowledge what you have done, there is a future. If you resist, if you cling to power, the future is destruction. But repentance leads to restoration.

This is the prophetic pattern: judgment is announced. The people can choose submission or destruction. If they submit—if they acknowledge their injustice and change—redemption is possible.

Ezekiel offers another version. Ezekiel sees Jerusalem destroyed. He sees the Temple burned, the people scattered, the city emptied. It is devastating. But then Ezekiel sees a vision of restoration. The dry bones come alive. The Temple is rebuilt. The people return. The city is renewed.

The message is: judgment is real. The consequences are real. But it is not the end of the story. There is a future beyond judgment. There is restoration for those who will accept it.

This is different from CRT-informed activism that sees judgment and has no structure for restoration. But it is also different from white evangelicalism that sees judgment and immediately forgives without requiring change.

The prophetic structure says: judgment is necessary. The system has to be exposed as unjust. The people maintaining the system have to face what they have done. But judgment is not the goal. Restoration is the goal. Judgment is the means to make restoration possible.

What does this mean practically? It means that when we expose systemic racism, we are not primarily interested in condemning white people. We are interested in transforming the system so that it no longer perpetuates injustice. We are interested in white people joining the work of transformation. But transformation requires first acknowledging what the system has done and the benefit you have received from it.

This is costly. This is not comfortable. But this is the prophetic structure. Not cancellation. Not absolution. But accountability that leads to change.

\## CH08 EXPANSION 2: The role of religious education — how Christian schools, seminaries, and Bible colleges shaped white evangelical response to race, what would it look like to teach racial justice history in evangelical institutions

The resistance of white evangelicalism to racial justice is not accidental. It is the result of conscious choice in Christian educational institutions. What evangelicals were taught shaped how they think about race.

Evangelical seminaries in the twentieth century trained pastors with curriculum that had little to nothing about systemic racism. Seminaries focused on biblical exegesis, theology, and church practice. Seminaries did not focus on the history of the church's complicity in slavery, segregation, and racial terrorism.

Bible colleges taught Bible. But they taught Bible in a way that separated Scripture from its sociopolitical context. A pastor could graduate from seminary, know the Greek and Hebrew, understand theology deeply, and still have no structure for understanding how Scripture speaks to systemic injustice.

This was not accident. This was choice. Seminaries could have required courses on church history and racism. Seminaries could have required courses on biblical justice. Seminaries chose not to.

What happened instead was that evangelical institutions taught in a way that reinforced the status quo. Seminaries taught submission to authorities. Seminaries taught individual piety. Seminaries taught that the gospel is about personal salvation, not social transformation.

This meant that when civil rights activists—many of them Christian—began to organize against segregation, white evangelical pastors had no theological grid to engage. Their seminary training had not prepared them to think about systemic injustice.

Some white evangelical pastors aligned with civil rights. But many did not. Many remained silent. Many actively opposed. This was not because they were uniquely evil. It was because their theological education had not equipped them to see systemic racism as a gospel issue.

What would it look like to actually teach racial justice history in evangelical institutions?

It would mean that every seminarian learns the history of the church's complicity in slavery and segregation. It would mean that seminarians study the complicity of their own denominations. Southern Baptist Seminary students would learn about the SBC's defense of slavery. Bob Jones Seminary students would learn about the interracial dating ban.

It would mean that seminarians study the prophetic tradition and understand that prophetic ministry includes calling out systemic injustice. It would mean that seminarians read James Cone and Frederick Douglass alongside traditional theology.

It would mean that every Bible college course on a biblical book asks: what does this book say about justice? What does this book say about the vulnerable? How does this book speak to the systems we live in?

It would mean that when seminarians graduate, they have a structure for understanding that a church building in a segregated neighborhood, built with the proceeds of slavery, maintained through the exclusion of Black people, is not just a building. It is evidence of systemic racism.

It would mean that pastors understand that their silence on racial injustice is not neutrality. Their silence is participation.

This is what real change looks like in evangelical institutions. It is not about political ideology. It is about having the theological grid to see what the prophets see, to name what the prophets name, to be moved to action because the gospel demands it.

\## CH09 EXPANSION 2: The difference between personal conscience and systemic change — why individual conversion stories are insufficient, what Wilberforce got right and what he got wrong, the limits of redemption narratives in addressing systemic injustice

William Wilberforce is a hero in evangelical history. Wilberforce was a British politician who came to faith and became convinced that slavery was wrong. Wilberforce spent decades working to abolish slavery throughout the British Empire. Eventually, he succeeded. Slavery was abolished.

Evangelicals point to Wilberforce as an example of the gospel producing justice. See, we say, a man converted to faith, convicted by his conscience, and changed a system.

But the Wilberforce story is much more complicated. Yes, Wilberforce opposed slavery. But Wilberforce also supported colonialism. Wilberforce believed that Britain should colonize Africa and India and other places. Wilberforce believed that this colonization was a civilizing mission, a Christian mission.

So Wilberforce freed enslaved people from the direct institution of slavery. But he supported systems that maintained colonized people in dependent, exploited relationships with the British Empire.

This is the problem with redemption narratives based on individual conscience. An individual can be converted, can have their conscience transformed, can work for change in one area—and still be committed to systems of injustice in other areas.

This is also the problem with separating personal morality from systemic analysis. Wilberforce was a moral person. Wilberforce cared about justice for enslaved people. But Wilberforce lived within a system of colonialism and did not see it as a system of injustice. Wilberforce saw colonialism as Christian civilization.

This is how systems maintain themselves. Individual people within the system can work for change in one area while remaining blind to injustice in other areas. The system adapts. It gives up slavery, but maintains colonialism. It gives up segregation, but maintains redlining.

This is why personal conversion is necessary but insufficient. A white pastor who has their conscience awakened about racism and decides to pastor differently—that is good. But if the church still benefits from segregated neighborhoods, from property purchased through redlining, from wealth accumulated through systems that excluded Black people, then the pastor's individual conversion does not solve the systemic problem.

Real change requires more than personal conversion. Real change requires structural transformation. Real change requires that systems that benefited from injustice are actually changed.

What would that look like? It would look like churches that built wealth through segregation actually redistributing that wealth to repair what they damaged. It would look like seminaries that trained pastors to defend segregation actually teaching a different history. It would look like denominations that maintained segregation actually taking concrete steps to become genuinely multiethnic.

Personal conversion is the beginning. But it cannot be the end. The system has to change. And the people within the system have to be willing to pay a cost to change it.

\## CH07 EXPANSION 3: The grace and justice tension — why grace without justice is sentimentalism, why justice without grace is despair, how the cross holds both simultaneously

American evangelicalism has emphasized grace. American evangelicalism preaches that all are sinners, all have fallen short, all are justified by faith in Christ alone. This is true. But American evangelicalism has often used grace to avoid justice.

If everyone is a sinner, then no one is uniquely culpable. If everyone needs grace, then no one specifically needs to repent of perpetuating injustice. If God forgives all, then we can move on without addressing what was done.

This is grace distorted into sentimentalism. This is grace that costs nothing and changes nothing.

The danger of this grace-without-justice approach is that it allows injustice to continue unchecked. It allows people to apologize without repenting. It allows systems to be defended because everyone is sinful anyway.

But there is also the opposite error: justice without grace. If the focus is entirely on naming systems and condemning the people who maintain them, if there is no possibility of redemption or transformation, then justice becomes despair.

Justice without grace says: you have benefited from injustice, and therefore you are irredeemable. Justice without grace says: the system is too broken, and there is no point in trying to change it.

This is despair. This is a justice that has lost hope.

What does the cross offer? The cross offers both justice and grace held simultaneously. The cross is God's judgment on injustice—the execution of the innocent is inexcusable, and God refuses to accept it. The cross is God's grief over what humans have done to each other.

But the cross is also God's offer of redemption. The cross is God saying: you have done terrible things, and they matter. The consequences are real. But there is a way forward. There is forgiveness. There is transformation.

This is different from both grace-without-justice and justice-without-grace.

Grace-without-justice says: it does not matter what you did. You are forgiven. Move on.

Justice-without-grace says: what you did matters so much that you can never be forgiven. You are condemned forever.

The cross says: what you did matters immensely. The consequences are real. The people you harmed deserve restitution. But you can change. You can repent. You can join the work of transformation. Your guilt does not have to define you forever.

This is the message the American church needs to recover. Not grace that avoids justice. Not justice that avoids grace. But both simultaneously.

What would this look like practically in a church pursuing racial justice? It would mean that people who have benefited from systems of injustice are not absolved without repentance. They cannot say: I had racist thoughts, but I am sorry now, so we can move on. Repentance means addressing what the system did and working to change it.

But it also means that people who repent are not permanently condemned. It means that transformation is possible. It means that a white person who has spent decades defending racism can actually change and can actually participate in the work of justice.

This is the tension the cross holds. This is what the American church needs.

\## CH09 EXPANSION 3: The scandal of evangelical biblical literacy — knowing the text but missing the point, how white evangelical biblical scholarship has domesticated the text, what it would look like to read Scripture like the oppressed read it

White evangelicalism prides itself on biblical literacy. White evangelical pastors study Greek and Hebrew. White evangelical churches emphasize reading the Bible carefully. White evangelicals claim to take Scripture seriously.

But there is a paradox: white evangelicals are biblically literate while being biblically blind.

White evangelicals can parse the Greek. White evangelicals can explain the literary structure of the psalms. White evangelicals can defend the reliability of the Gospel accounts. And yet, white evangelicals have missed what the Bible is at its core about: God's commitment to justice for the vulnerable.

This is not accidental. This is the result of how white evangelical biblical scholarship has been structured.

White evangelical biblical scholarship has been heavily influenced by the German historical-critical school. This school of biblical scholarship treated Scripture as a historical document to be analyzed objectively. The scholar's job is to stand back and analyze what the text says, not to listen to what the text is saying to the scholar's condition.

But Scripture was not written for detached scholars. Scripture was written for the people of God living in oppression. Scripture was written to be heard, not just analyzed. Scripture was written to transform, not just to inform.

The Black church has always understood this. The Black church has read Scripture as a living word. The Black church has read Scripture as God speaking to their condition. The Black church has asked: what is God saying to us in our situation?

White evangelicalism has read Scripture as a collection of doctrines and principles. The white evangelical question is: what does this text teach about theology? What does this text teach about individual spirituality?

These are not bad questions. But they are not the primary question. The primary question is: what is God saying to us about justice? What is God saying to us about the vulnerable? What is God saying to us about the systems we are participating in?

White evangelical biblical literacy has produced pastors who can explain the theology of atonement without asking: am I complicit in systems that require atonement? White evangelical biblical literacy has produced scholars who can defend the authority of Scripture while resisting what Scripture actually says about justice.

What would it look like to read Scripture like the oppressed read it?

It would mean starting with the recognition that I do not stand above Scripture as an objective analyzer. I am inside Scripture. Scripture is speaking to my situation. Scripture is asking me: are you part of an oppressed people? Are you part of a system that oppresses?

It would mean recognizing that the biblical narrative is the story of God standing with the vulnerable. That is not one theme among many. That is the fundamental narrative arc. From Exodus to the prophets to Jesus, the pattern is consistent: God is on the side of the vulnerable.

It would mean reading every biblical text with the question: does this challenge the systems of power I am invested in? Does this expand my understanding of who God is concerned about? Does this call me to change?

It would mean being willing to be transformed by Scripture rather than using Scripture to confirm what I already believe.

This is what white evangelicalism has lost. This is what the Black church has never lost.

If white evangelicalism recovered this—if white evangelical biblical scholars and pastors started reading Scripture like the oppressed read it, asking what God is saying about justice—the American church would be transformed. The American church would have prophetic power.

But this would require white evangelical biblical scholars to question the system they were trained in. This would require acknowledging that objective detachment is not the most faithful way to read Scripture. This would require submitting to Scripture as a word that judges, not just a text that teaches.

This is too costly for most white evangelical scholars. So white evangelical biblical literacy continues to be biblically blind.

\## CH10 EXPANSION: More on what genuine reconciliation looks like — the Truth and Reconciliation Commission model (South Africa), Briarwood Presbyterian's apology for supporting segregation, what reparations means theologically

After apartheid ended in South Africa, the country faced a choice: retribution or reconciliation. They could have hunted down every person involved in apartheid violence and executed them. The victims would have had that justice.

Instead, South Africa chose a different path: the Truth and Reconciliation Commission. The idea was subversive. Perpetrators of apartheid violence would confess what they had done. They would tell the truth in public. Victims would hear them. Victims could ask questions. And then, in some cases, perpetrators would be granted amnesty if they had confessed fully.

This was not cheap grace. This required the perpetrator to face the person they had harmed. This required the perpetrator to speak the truth about what they had done. This required vulnerability and exposure.

For the victim, this meant hearing the confession. It meant getting a chance to speak. It meant a public acknowledgment that what happened was real and was wrong.

Some perpetrators refused to confess. Those people faced prosecution. Some victims could not accept the confession even when it was offered. They sought prosecution instead.

But the structure was clear: truth first, then reconciliation. Justice through acknowledgment and change, not through retribution.

What would an American Truth and Reconciliation Commission look like? What if evangelical leaders went to Black churches and said: here is what we did. We defended slavery. We created segregation academies. We remained silent during violence. We built wealth from systems of injustice. We taught theology that justified oppression.

We did this. We benefited. We have not changed.

Now what? Now what would reconciliation require?

Briarwood Presbyterian Church in Birmingham, Alabama offers a small example. Briarwood was founded in 1971 in explicit response to desegregation. Parents pulled their children out of integrated public schools and founded Briarwood to maintain segregation.

In 2021, the church's pastor stood up and apologized. The church officially acknowledged and repented of its founding as a segregation academy. The church acknowledged that it had been created to support white flight and to maintain segregation.

This is what genuine acknowledgment looks like. Not: we had some members who were racist. But: the institution itself was created for racist purposes.

But acknowledgment is only the beginning of reconciliation. The question then becomes: what do we do about it?

This is where reparations enter the picture. Reparations is not about money. Reparations is about structural change. Reparations is about the perpetrator attempting to repair what was broken.

If Briarwood was founded to benefit white families by allowing them to escape integration, then reparations from Briarwood might look like: Briarwood financially supports integrated public schools. Briarwood commits a percentage of its budget to funding schools in historically Black neighborhoods. Briarwood works to actually become integrated, not just in membership, but in decision-making power.

If churches benefited from slavery and segregation, then reparations from those churches might look like: churches fund Black churches. Churches close the wealth gap between white and Black congregations. Churches redistribute power and resources.

Reparations is not about paying money to people and feeling better about yourself. Reparations is about structural change. Reparations is about looking at what was taken, what was built, what was benefited from, and systematically dismantling those benefits and rebuilding in a different way.

This is what genuine reconciliation looks like. Not: let's move forward and not talk about the past. But: let's acknowledge what happened, let's understand what it cost, let's change the systems that benefited from it.

This is costly. This is why cheap reconciliation is so appealing. Cheap reconciliation costs nothing. Genuine reconciliation costs everything.

\## CH11 EXPANSION: More practical church steps — sermon series on justice, cross-racial pastoral partnerships, budget allocation for justice, hiring practices, how to build multi-ethnic churches without tokenizing

A white pastor can preach a sermon series called "Justice and Reconciliation" without actually changing anything about the church. The pastor can preach eloquently about biblical justice, can condemn systemic racism, can call people to action—and then the church budget remains the same. The leadership remains the same. The congregation remains segregated.

Real change starts with the pastor making it clear that this is not an intellectual exercise. Justice is central to what the church is about. Not as a special series. As the ongoing work of the church.

What does that look like practically?

A cross-racial pastoral partnership is one structure. A white pastor and a Black pastor share leadership. Not a Black assistant to a white senior pastor. But actual shared leadership. Shared preaching. Shared decision-making power.

This is uncomfortable. It requires the white pastor to be willing to not be in control. It requires the white pastor to accept that their perspective is not the only perspective that matters. It requires the white pastor to be challenged.

It also requires the denomination or church board to value this partnership enough to hire and pay equally. If the Black pastor is always the assistant, if the Black pastor is paid less, if the Black pastor has less authority—then it is not a partnership.

Budget tells the truth about priorities. If a church says it values justice but allocates 2% of the budget to justice work and 50% to building maintenance, the budget reveals the real priorities.

What would it look like for a church to allocate resources to justice? It might look like:

\- A percentage of the annual budget (15-20%) going to racial justice work

\- Funding Black churches in the community

\- Supporting Black pastors through education and mentorship programs

\- Investing in neighborhoods that have been historically disinvested

\- Paying diverse staff equally and generously

This is not charity. This is repair. This is attempting to restore what was taken.

Hiring practices reveal who a church actually values. If a church says it values diversity, but the hiring process remains all-white gatekeeping—if only people who look, sound, and think like the current leadership are hired—then the church is not actually committed to diversity.

Real commitment to diversity in hiring looks like:

\- Recruiting from Black seminaries and Bible colleges

\- Valuing different theological perspectives and different ways of articulating faith

\- Hiring people who will challenge the status quo

\- Paying fairly—if a church is serious about hiring Black staff, they will pay to attract talented people

\- Committing to retention—not hiring someone and then creating an unwelcoming environment so they leave

Building a multi-ethnic church without tokenizing is the hardest work. Tokenizing is when a church brings in a few people of color, features them prominently in the marketing materials, and then keeps them excluded from real power. The church gets to say it is diverse while maintaining white control.

Real multi-ethnic community means:

\- People of color in leadership, including senior leadership

\- Worship style that reflects diverse traditions, not just white evangelical style with a few gospel songs added

\- Music and preaching that comes from diverse perspectives

\- Children's ministry and youth ministry that reflects diverse representation

\- Small groups and fellowship that intentionally cross racial lines

\- Making it clear that this is not a program. This is who we are.

\## CH11 EXPANSION 2: From theory to systems — how to measure whether your church is actually changing or just performing change, the gap between stated values and budget allocation, what it means to institutionalize justice

Most churches that want to pursue racial justice begin with a sermon series or a study or a statement. This is visible. This is something that can be pointed to as evidence of commitment.

But a sermon series is not a system. A sermon series is words. Systems are what remains after the sermon series ends.

How do you know whether your church is actually committed to justice or just performing it?

You look at the budget. The budget never lies. The budget tells you what a church actually values.

If a church says it is committed to racial justice but allocates 2% of the budget to justice work and 70% to building maintenance, the budget reveals the truth.

If a church says it is committed to diversity but pays its white staff better than its Black staff, the payroll reveals the truth.

If a church says it is committed to community but does nothing to repair the neighborhoods where Black members live, the absence of action reveals the truth.

Institutionalizing justice means building systems that survive the sermon series. It means that when the pastor who preached about justice leaves, the systems remain. It means that when the cultural moment shifts, the systems continue.

What does this look like practically?

First: a racial equity committee with actual power. Not a committee that meets once a month to discuss racial equity while the real power remains in the hands of white leadership. But a committee with decision-making authority over hiring, budget allocation, and strategic direction.

Second: measurable goals with accountability. Not vague commitments to "become more diverse" but specific goals: by 2028, 25% of leadership positions are held by people of color. By 2030, the church budget reflects a 20% allocation to justice work. By 2027, the church has established a reparations fund and allocated \$X to it.

Third: external accountability. Not just internal evaluation. A church should invite external evaluators to assess whether it is actually pursuing justice or just talking about it.

Fourth: staffing that ensures continuity. A church should hire a director of racial justice whose entire job is to oversee the systems and ensure that justice work does not become a special project but becomes embedded in how the church operates.

Fifth: financial investment in communities that have been harmed. Not just individual charity. Systemic investment in schools, housing, small businesses in historically disinvested neighborhoods.

This is what institutionalization looks like. It is not comfortable. It requires resources. It requires power to actually shift. But it is what it means for a church to not just talk about justice but to actually pursue it.

The hard truth: most churches are not willing to do this. Most churches want the prophetic witness without the structural cost. Most churches want to say they care about justice without actually changing.

Until churches are willing to institutionalize justice, the work will not happen.

\## CONCLUSION EXPANSION 3: The cost of discipleship — what it means to follow Jesus in a context of systemic injustice, why the cross is not a metaphor for personal transformation but a call to systemic challenge, what it means to deny yourself and take up your cross

\## CH10 EXPANSION 2: The practical theology of reparations — moving from guilt to repair, why reparations is not charity but restitution, how churches can begin the work of systemic repair

Reparations is a confusing concept for white evangelicals because evangelicals have been taught that justice is about individual guilt and forgiveness. From this perspective, reparations do not make sense. If I personally did not enslave anyone, why should I pay reparations? If an individual Black person who comes to my church was not enslaved, why would they receive reparations?

This perspective misses what reparations actually are. Reparations are not about individual guilt. Reparations are about systemic harm and systemic repair.

Here is the practical theology of reparations:

First: acknowledge what was taken. Slavery extracted trillions of dollars of labor from African Americans. Segregation extracted opportunity, resources, dignity. Redlining extracted the ability to build wealth. These are not small harms. These are massive systemic extractions.

Second: acknowledge the ongoing benefit. White Americans have benefited from this extraction, whether they intended to or not. White neighborhoods exist because of redlining that excluded Black people. White schools received resources because segregated funding meant white schools got better funding. White wealth accumulated because slavery was free labor and segregation prevented wealth accumulation by Black people.

Third: acknowledge that the harm continues. The effects of slavery, segregation, and redlining are not historical. The effects are current. A person born into a segregated neighborhood, into a family with less wealth because of segregation, into a community with fewer resources because of disinvestment, experiences the ongoing effect of historical injustice.

Fourth: recognize that repair is necessary. Repair is not charity. Repair is not feeling sorry. Repair is systematic restitution for what was taken.

What might reparations look like at a church level?

A church that is located in a neighborhood that was preserved through redlining, that benefited from property values that were stable because Black people were excluded, could:

First, acknowledge this history explicitly. A statement: "Our church building is located on a street that was preserved as all-white through FHA redlining. This exclusion created stable property values for white homeowners and prevented wealth accumulation by Black families. We acknowledge this history and our church's role in it."

Second, commit a percentage of the church's annual budget to repair. Perhaps 15-20% of the budget goes to repair work—funding Black churches in disinvested neighborhoods, funding schools in those neighborhoods, funding economic development initiatives led by people of color.

Third, work with the community on what repair looks like. Do not assume you know what repair should be. Ask: what do you need? What would actually repair the harm we participated in?

Fourth, change the structure of the church so that power is actually shared with people from the communities being repaired. Do not make this a white-led charity initiative. Make this a partnership where the people being harmed by the system have decision-making authority.

This is what reparations looks like in practice. It is not comfortable. It requires resources and humility and a willingness to have your decisions questioned by people you have harmed.

But this is what justice requires. This is what the gospel requires.

Most churches will not do this. Most churches prefer charity. Charity allows the giver to feel good about giving. Charity does not require structural change. Charity does not require power to actually shift.

But reparations requires all of this. Reparations requires that you acknowledge what you did, that you pay what you owe, that you change the systems that perpetuated the harm.

This is the work that American churches need to do. This is the work that will make the American church prophetic and credible.

\## CH01 EXPANSION 2: What CRT is NOT — it is not calling all white people racist, it is not rejecting individual agency, it is not Marxism — the straw men that prevent honest engagement

The most common misrepresentation of CRT is that it says "all white people are racist." This is not what CRT says. No major CRT scholar has made this claim.

What CRT scholars do say is that systems operate on racial lines, that people in dominant groups benefit from those systems, and that this happens whether or not the individuals in those dominant groups consciously intend it.

An analogy: if you grow up in a house built on stolen land, the house is real, you benefit from living in it, but you did not personally steal the land. You are not personally guilty of theft. But you are benefiting from theft. And you have a choice about what you do with that knowledge.

This is not the same as saying "you are a racist." It is saying "you are benefiting from racism." These are different claims. But the distortion of CRT says "you are racist" when CRT actually says "systems around you are structured in ways that benefit you because of your race."

The second misrepresentation is that CRT rejects individual agency. The claim is that CRT sees people as purely determined by systems, as having no choice or responsibility.

Again, this is a distortion. CRT scholars like Derrick Bell and Kimberlé Crenshaw are clear that individuals have agency. People make choices. People can resist systems. People can work to change systems.

But CRT is also saying that your individual choices do not happen in a vacuum. Your choices are shaped by the systems you are in. You can choose to be generous or selfish, but your generosity or selfishness happens within a system that privileges some people and disadvantages others.

The claim is not that you have no agency. The claim is that your agency is exercised within structured constraints. Some people can choose to be poor and it is seen as noble sacrifice. Some people are poor and it is seen as personal failure. The individual choice is the same. The system interprets it differently.

The third misrepresentation is that CRT is Marxism. We have already addressed this, but it is worth noting how this misrepresentation prevents honest engagement.

If you decide CRT is Marxism, then you decide that CRT is un-American, that it comes from a foreign ideology, that it is a threat to democracy. This frame prevents you from actually engaging with what CRT is saying about American systems.

But CRT is not Marxism. CRT came out of American law schools. CRT was developed by American scholars trying to understand American legal history. CRT is as American as Brown v. Board of Education.

The misrepresentation as Marxism is useful if your goal is to dismiss CRT without engaging with its arguments. But if your goal is to understand CRT, the misrepresentation is an obstacle.

The real tragedy is that these misrepresentations have become so dominant that most people who attack CRT are not attacking what CRT actually says. They are attacking caricatures of CRT. This is intellectually dishonest.

If you are going to disagree with CRT, disagree with what CRT actually says. Read Derrick Bell. Read Kimberlé Crenshaw. Read Richard Delgado. Then make your argument against what they actually wrote.

Anything less is not thinking. It is just reaction.

What actually happened: evangelical leaders aligned themselves with the panic about CRT. Evangelical leaders became defenders of the status quo.

The departures tell the story. Black evangelical pastors who had hoped the 2020 conversations would lead to change found themselves watching evangelical leadership reverse course. Young evangelicals who thought the movement was moving toward justice found themselves disappointed.

This is the real scandal. Not that CRT exists. The scandal is that when it became politically inconvenient to pursue racial justice, evangelical leadership abandoned it.

\## CH12 EXPANSION 2: International examples — the church and race in South Africa, Brazil, the UK — what American Christians can learn from how other churches have handled racial injustice

South Africa's churches faced a choice during apartheid: continue participating in apartheid, or resist. Some churches resisted. Some churches collaborated. After apartheid, the churches that had resisted had credibility. The churches that had collaborated had to account for what they had done.

The South African churches that had resisted apartheid—churches like the Dutch Reformed Church (Mission Church) and the Anglican Church—became leaders in reconciliation work after apartheid. These churches had credibility because they had already paid a cost for their convictions.

The lesson: the church that maintains power while injustice continues loses its moral authority. The church that is willing to sacrifice for justice gains it.

Brazil's churches have engaged with race in different ways. Brazil has large evangelical populations. Brazilian evangelicalism has, in many cases, avoided conversations about race by emphasizing spiritual equality. The message is: in Christ, race does not matter.

But this avoidance of race allows racial hierarchies to continue. Brazil has significant racial inequality. Brazil's churches, by not addressing racial injustice, have become complicit in it.

The lesson: spiritual equality without structural justice is not the gospel. The gospel requires both.

The UK's churches have had to reckon with colonialism and with immigration. British churches have more experience with non-white membership than many American churches. British churches have, in some cases, done better work on racism than American churches.

But British churches also contain resistance. British nationalism. The desire to protect British identity. These can create barriers to genuine racial justice.

The lesson: nationalism and racial justice are in tension. A church that is identified with national identity will struggle to pursue racial justice when justice threatens national identity.

What American churches can learn from these international examples:

\- The church that opposes injustice in real time has credibility

\- Spiritual equality without structural change is not the gospel

\- Nationalism and racial justice are in tension

\- Reconciliation requires that the perpetrator pay a cost, not that the victim forgive without the perpetrator changing

American churches should look at what churches around the world have learned. American churches should learn from both the successes and the failures of churches in other contexts.

The most important lesson: what the church does now matters for what the church becomes. If the American church continues to choose comfort over justice, the American church will become a symbol of comfort rather than a symbol of the cross. If the American church is willing to sacrifice for justice, the American church can become a sign of what is possible when people take the gospel seriously.

The choice is still in front of us.

\## CH01 EXPANSION 3: What it means to read the Bible seriously on race — hermeneutics that listen to the text rather than imposing frameworks, the importance of historical context, what it means for the whole biblical narrative to be a story of God standing with the vulnerable

When we read the Bible seriously on race, we cannot read it the way white evangelical Christianity has read it. White evangelical Christianity has read the Bible as a text that confirms what white evangelical Christianity already believes.

But to read the Bible seriously means to let the Bible challenge what we believe. It means to ask: what is the text actually saying, not what do I want it to say?

The Bible is a story of God standing with the vulnerable. This is not a theme that appears in a few verses. This is the consistent pattern throughout Scripture.

God hears the cry of enslaved people in Egypt. God does not hear the cry of Pharaoh. God stands with Moses, who is a fugitive from justice, who has killed an Egyptian. God does not stand with the state apparatus that maintains slavery.

When Israel enters the promised land, God commands jubilee. Every fiftieth year, debts are forgiven, land is returned to original owners, enslaved people go free. This is not optional. This is central to the law.

The prophets do not spend their time comforting the comfortable. The prophets spend their time confronting the powerful. The prophets say: you have taken the land of the poor. You have made slaves of the vulnerable. The Lord will judge you.

In the gospels, Jesus's first sermon announces good news to the poor, release to the prisoners, sight to the blind, freedom for the oppressed. Jesus spends his time with the vulnerable. Jesus does not spend his time with the powerful, except to confront them.

When you read the entire biblical narrative with this lens, you see something different from what white evangelical Christianity teaches. You see a God who is not interested in maintaining order. You see a God who is interested in establishing justice.

This requires reading the Bible in its own logic, not importing modern categories. It requires understanding the social structure of ancient Israel, understanding what jubilee would have meant, understanding what it meant for women and slaves and foreigners to be included in the covenant.

It requires being willing to let the Bible say things that challenge your power and your comfort.

This is what it means to read the Bible seriously on race. Not to find verses that support your position. But to ask: what is this whole narrative saying? What is God doing throughout Scripture? And how does that call me to act?

If you read the Bible seriously on race, you cannot support systems that oppress. You cannot be comfortable with injustice. You cannot use Scripture to defend the status quo.

This is why white evangelical Christianity has avoided reading the Bible seriously on race. Because the Bible, read seriously, demands everything.

\## CH08 EXPANSION 3: The failure of white evangelical leadership to prophesy — the cost of safety, why platform matters, what it would look like for white evangelical leaders to actually speak

There are white evangelical leaders with platforms. These leaders have books, podcasts, speaking engagements, influence. These leaders could speak prophetically about race. These leaders could speak prophetically about the evangelical alignment with white nationalism. These leaders could speak prophetically about the failure of white evangelicalism.

Most of them do not.

Why? Because prophetic speech costs something. Prophetic speech makes people uncomfortable. Prophetic speech can result in loss of audience, loss of platform, loss of income.

White evangelical leadership has chosen safety over prophecy. White evangelical leadership has chosen comfort over truth-telling. White evangelical leadership has chosen alignment with power over standing with the vulnerable.

Some white evangelical leaders have tried to thread the needle. They have said things like: racism is bad, but we should not talk about systemic racism. Injustice is wrong, but we should not challenge the systems that perpetuate injustice. The gospel calls us to justice, but we should not be political.

This is what compromise sounds like. This is what it looks like when someone wants the credibility of prophetic voice without the cost of prophetic speech.

Real prophecy does not thread the needle. Real prophecy names the thing. Real prophecy makes it clear what is at stake. Real prophecy is willing to lose the platform.

There are some white evangelical leaders who have done this. These leaders have spoken clearly about the failure of white evangelicalism. These leaders have lost audience. Some have lost speaking engagements. Some have been attacked by other evangelical leaders.

But they have maintained integrity. They have maintained the right to call themselves prophetic.

What would it look like for more white evangelical leaders to speak prophetically?

It would mean that leaders with platforms would use those platforms to say: white evangelicalism has failed. White evangelicalism has aligned with forces of oppression. White evangelicalism has lost credibility because we have valued safety and status over justice.

It would mean that leaders would publicly repent for their own participation in systems of injustice. It would mean that leaders would acknowledge that their success, their books, their platforms are built on systems that oppress.

It would mean that leaders would call out other evangelical leaders who have not repented. It would mean that the evangelical tribe would no longer protect its own at the expense of justice.

This is costly. This is what prophecy costs. But this is what the moment demands.

Without white evangelical leadership speaking prophetically, without white evangelical leaders being willing to lose their platforms to speak truth, the evangelical institution will continue as it is: comfortable, defensive, and complicit.

\## CH12 EXPANSION 3: The vision beyond reconciliation — toward beloved community, what Cornel West and the Black church understand about beloved community that American Christianity has lost, the possibility of genuine multiethnic communion

Reconciliation is often the goal that white evangelicals hold out. Reconciliation sounds like healing. Reconciliation sounds like moving forward together.

But reconciliation, as often understood, is too small. Reconciliation can mean that the oppressor and the oppressed agree to stop fighting and move on. But that does not heal what was broken. That does not restore what was taken.

Cornel West has a different vision. West talks about beloved community. Beloved community is not just reconciliation. Beloved community is the possibility of genuine love across difference. It is the possibility of people who have been enemies becoming family.

This is more than white people and Black people deciding to be civil to each other. This is the possibility of white people and Black people actually loving each other, actually caring for each other's flourishing, actually being willing to sacrifice for each other.

The Black church has maintained the vision of beloved community even when white Christianity abandoned it. The Black church believed that genuine community was possible. The Black church believed that enemies could become family in Christ.

But here is what beloved community requires: it requires that the people who have benefited from injustice are willing to give something up. It requires that the comfortable are willing to become uncomfortable. It requires that the powerful are willing to share power.

Most churches are not willing to do this. Most churches want reconciliation without cost. Most churches want to reconcile with Black people without actually changing anything about how power operates in the church.

But beloved community is not possible without genuine change. Beloved community is not possible when white leadership maintains control. Beloved community is not possible when resources remain concentrated in white hands.

What would beloved community look like in an American church?

It would look like white Christians sitting with Black Christians and hearing the stories of what was done. It would look like genuine grief over what the church has done. It would look like a willingness to make things right that goes far beyond a sermon or a statement.

It would look like white Christians becoming the minority in their own churches. It would look like power actually shifting. It would look like a church where the perspective of Black Christians shapes the life of the church, not just the Sunday they highlight diversity.

It would look like a church that is willing to become something radically different from what it was. Because beloved community is not reconciliation to the old thing. Beloved community is the creation of something new.

This is the vision. This is what is possible. But it requires churches willing to die to themselves and be reborn as something else.

\## CH01 EXPANSION 4: The question beneath the question — why the real argument is not about CRT but about power, why the panic about CRT reveals what evangelicals are actually afraid of losing, the argument evangelicals cannot articulate

The arguments about CRT are proxy arguments. They are not really about whether CRT is academically coherent or whether intersectionality is a helpful analytical tool. They are about power.

The real argument is: if we acknowledge systemic racism, if we acknowledge that systems were designed to benefit white people and harm Black people, if we acknowledge that white Christians have been complicit in those systems, then what does that mean for our authority? What does that mean for our institutions? What does that mean for the way we have understood ourselves?

This argument cannot be stated directly. So it is stated through arguments about CRT. It is stated through claims that CRT is Marxist, that CRT is un-American, that CRT is dangerous.

But the panic about CRT reveals what evangelicals are actually afraid of losing: the ability to see themselves as inherently good. The ability to see the institutions they built as inherently good. The ability to see America as inherently good.

If systemic racism is real, if systems were designed to exclude Black people and benefit white people, then white people cannot claim innocence. White institutions cannot claim to be neutral. America cannot claim to be a beacon of freedom.

This is the argument evangelicals cannot articulate because admitting what they fear would require admitting what they have been unwilling to acknowledge.

What evangelicals fear is not CRT. What evangelicals fear is the possibility that they have been on the wrong side of history. What evangelicals fear is the possibility that their institutions have perpetuated injustice. What evangelicals fear is the possibility that they have benefited from systems they have claimed to oppose.

This is the real conversation that needs to happen. Not a debate about whether CRT is academically sound. But a conversation about: have we been complicit? Have we benefited from injustice? Are we willing to change?

These are harder questions. These are more costly questions. So instead, evangelicals debate the merits of CRT.

But anyone paying attention can see through this. The argument about CRT is a distraction from the real argument about power and complicity and change.

A church that wants to pursue justice has to be willing to have the real argument. A church that wants to pursue justice has to be willing to ask: have we benefited from injustice? The answer will almost certainly be yes. And then the church has to be willing to do something about it.

This is what the evangelical resistance to CRT is really about. Not the academic structure. But the fear of what acknowledging the system would require.

\## CH08 EXPANSION 4: The future of evangelicalism — whether the movement can survive without transformation, what it would take to rebuild evangelical credibility, the necessity of prophetic leadership

American evangelicalism faces a crisis. Young people are leaving. Credibility is eroding. The institution is declining.

There are many diagnoses of why evangelicalism is in crisis. Some blame secularization. Some blame the rise of the "nones"—people with no religious affiliation. Some blame cultural trends.

But the core crisis is credibility. Evangelicalism has lost moral credibility because it has defended systems of injustice. Evangelicalism has lost the ability to speak prophetically because evangelicalism has aligned itself with power.

Young people see this. Young people see that evangelicals defended segregation academies while claiming to follow Jesus who broke every boundary of separation. Young people see that evangelicals defend capitalism while claiming to follow Jesus who cared for the poor. Young people see that evangelicals remain silent on racism while claiming to preach the whole gospel.

The young people are not wrong. The disconnect is real.

Can evangelicalism survive without transformation? Possibly. Evangelicalism can survive as a subculture for people committed to white evangelical Christianity. It can survive as a political movement. It can survive as an institution.

But it will not survive as a prophetic movement. It will not survive as a credible voice on morality. It will not survive as a witness to the gospel.

What would it take to rebuild evangelical credibility?

First: honest reckoning with history. Evangelicalism would have to acknowledge its complicity in slavery, segregation, and systemic injustice. Evangelicalism would have to acknowledge that evangelical leaders defended these systems. Evangelicalism would have to acknowledge that evangelicals benefited from these systems.

This requires more than a statement. This requires education. Evangelical denominations would have to require that every pastor, every educator, every person in leadership learn the real history of evangelicalism's complicity in racism.

Second: structural change. Evangelicalism would have to actually change the power structures that perpetuate injustice. Evangelicalism would have to put people of color in positions of real authority, not token positions. Evangelicalism would have to redistribute resources from white evangelical institutions to Black evangelical institutions.

Third: prophetic leadership. Evangelical leaders would have to be willing to speak prophetically. They would have to be willing to challenge their own movement. They would have to be willing to lose white members who reject the prophetic message.

This is costly. This is why most evangelical leaders are not willing to do it.

But without this, evangelicalism will continue its decline. Without this, evangelicalism will become increasingly identified with white nationalism and political power-seeking. Without this, evangelicalism will lose the possibility of being a prophetic voice.

The choice is before evangelicalism now. The movement can either transform or it can decline. But transformation requires genuine repentance. And genuine repentance requires change.
