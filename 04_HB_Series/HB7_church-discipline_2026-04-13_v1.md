**Introduction: The Word Nobody Wants to Say**

The last time I watched a church practice discipline well, I was twenty-six years old and sitting in the third row of a congregation I had been part of for less than a year.

A man stood at the front. Mid-forties. Hands trembling. He had been a deacon for over a decade. He had also been carrying on an affair for almost two of those years. The elders had confronted him privately. Then with witnesses. Then before the congregation. This was the final step. Not because they wanted to humiliate him. Because he had refused every prior intervention and continued lying to the people who trusted him most.

What happened next is the part nobody talks about when they talk about church discipline.

The pastor read a prepared statement. Short. Specific. No embellishment. Then he said something I have never forgotten: “We are not removing David from fellowship because we are better than him. We are doing this because we love him too much to pretend this is not destroying him. And we love this church too much to pretend it is not destroying us.“

The room was silent. A woman in the second row was weeping. Someone near the back got up and left. The pastor asked the congregation to commit to praying for David’s repentance and to welcoming him back when restoration was real. Then he dismissed the service.

I walked to my car and sat there for twenty minutes.

That was the most Christ-honoring thing I had ever seen a church do. And it looked nothing like what I expected church discipline to look like.

Here is the problem. That story is rare. Not because the Bible is unclear about discipline. The Bible is strikingly clear. But because the American church has lost the ability to hold two truths at the same time: that grace is free and that sin has consequences. That love is unconditional and that love sometimes says no. That the church is a hospital for the broken and that hospitals do not let infections spread unchecked through the ward.

We have swung between two catastrophic failures, and most churches have landed in one ditch or the other.

The first ditch is abandonment. The church that never confronts anyone about anything because confrontation feels judgmental, and judgment is the one sin the modern church recognizes. These congregations talk endlessly about love and grace and acceptance. They mean it. But they have confused kindness with silence, and silence with safety. They watch leaders destroy themselves and say nothing. They watch marriages unravel in slow motion and call it privacy. They watch doctrine erode and call it growth. They have replaced the surgeon’s scalpel with a shrug.

The second ditch is weaponization. The church that uses discipline as a tool of control, exclusion, and power maintenance. These congregations practice something they call accountability, but what they mean is compliance. Discipline in these churches protects the institution, not the person. It silences dissent. It punishes the inconvenient. It cloaks authoritarianism in the language of holiness. The shunning. The excommunication without process. The pastor who cannot be questioned because questioning leadership is rebellion against God.

Both ditches are full. Both are producing casualties. And both have the same root cause.

The church no longer understands what it is.

If the church is a social club, then discipline is rude. You do not confront people at the country club. You smile, you mingle, you avoid the uncomfortable, and you let people do what they want as long as they keep paying dues. If the church is a social club, the worst thing you can do is make someone uncomfortable enough to leave.

If the church is an authoritarian hierarchy, then discipline is a weapon. The leader speaks for God. Questioning the leader is questioning God. Discipline exists to enforce obedience, protect the brand, and maintain the power structure. In this model, the institution is always right and the individual is always suspect.

But the church is neither of those things. The church is a body. Paul said it in Romans 12 and 1 Corinthians 12 and Ephesians 4. Not a metaphor he reached for because it sounded nice. A body. An organism. Living tissue connected to a living head, each part dependent on every other part, each part affected by every other part’s health or sickness.

And bodies have immune systems.

That is what church discipline is. Not punishment. Not exclusion. Not a weapon. An immune system. The body’s God-designed capacity to identify what is destroying it and respond in a way that heals rather than kills.

When the immune system shuts down, the body dies of infection. That is the first ditch. When the immune system turns on the body’s own tissue, the body dies of autoimmune disease. That is the second ditch. Both are fatal. Both are preventable. Both require the same thing: an immune system that actually works the way it was designed to work.

This book is about what that looks like.

I am writing it because I have been a pastor for over two decades and I have watched both failures up close. I have watched churches that never confronted anyone slowly rot from the inside until the people who needed the church most — the broken, the grieving, the genuinely repentant — could not find it because there was nothing left to find. I have also watched churches that wielded discipline like a cudgel destroy people whose only sin was asking a question the leadership did not want to answer.

I have also gotten this wrong myself. I have been the pastor who avoided a hard conversation because I was afraid of losing someone. I have been the pastor who confronted someone too late, after the damage had metastasized into something much harder to heal. I have been the pastor who knew something was wrong and talked myself into believing it would resolve on its own. It never resolved on its own.

This book will walk through what the Bible actually says about discipline — not what your tradition assumes it says, not what your bad experience made you believe it says, but what the text says when you read it carefully, in context, with the whole counsel of Scripture in view.

We will look at Matthew 18, where Jesus laid out the process more clearly than most churches admit. We will look at 1 Corinthians 5, where Paul demanded action the Corinthian church was too cowardly to take. We will look at 2 Corinthians 2, where that same Paul demanded restoration the Corinthian church was too self-righteous to offer. We will look at Galatians 6, where the whole community is called to carry the weight. We will look at the history of how the church has practiced discipline across two thousand years — sometimes with breathtaking grace, sometimes with horrifying cruelty.

And we will talk about what it looks like to build a church that can actually do this. A church that can bear the truth about itself. A church where sin can be named without the person being destroyed. A church where restoration is the goal, not the afterthought. A church where the leaders are under the same standard as the congregation. A church that understands discipline as an act of love and practices it like one.

That is a high bar. Most churches will not clear it. Not because they lack theology but because they lack courage. Discipline requires the willingness to lose someone in order to love them. It requires the willingness to be misunderstood, to be accused of legalism by people who confuse grace with permissiveness, and to be accused of harshness by people who confuse love with silence.

It requires something the American church is desperately short on: the willingness to be honest about what is happening inside our own walls.

This is not a comfortable book. It is not meant to be. But it is meant to be hopeful. Because the God who designed the body also designed its immune system. And when that system works — when discipline is practiced the way Scripture describes it — the result is not death. The result is healing. The result is a community where people can be honest about their sin because they know the response will be truth and grace held together, not one at the expense of the other.

The man I watched stand at the front of that church when I was twenty-six came back. It took fourteen months. He repented. His marriage survived — barely, and with scars. The church welcomed him back with the same seriousness they had used to confront him. No fanfare. No pretending it did not happen. Just the quiet, costly work of restoration.

That is what this book is about. Not the easy version. The real one.

**Chapter 1: What We Lost When We Lost Discipline**

In 1742, Jonathan Edwards excommunicated a man named Joseph Hawley from the Northampton congregation for public drunkenness and blasphemy. The process had taken months. Edwards had spoken with Hawley privately, then with witnesses, then before the church. At each step, Hawley had been offered repentance and refused it. The excommunication was not sudden. It was the final act of a long pursuit.

What is striking about the Northampton records is not the severity of the act. It is the ordinariness of it. Edwards was not doing something unusual. He was doing something every colonial congregation did as a matter of course. Church discipline was as routine as the Lord’s Supper. The membership rolls were living documents — people were added through profession of faith and removed through disciplinary action when their lives contradicted that profession. The congregation understood itself as a covenanted body with mutual obligations. When someone violated those obligations persistently and without repentance, the body responded. Not with glee. Not casually. But with the seriousness of a community that believed its integrity before God depended on its willingness to tell the truth about itself.

That world is gone.

The average American church in 2026 has no functioning process for addressing unrepentant sin among its members. Not because pastors do not see it. They see it constantly. The elder sleeping with someone who is not his wife. The deacon who has been defrauding his business partner for years. The worship leader whose public faith is a performance disconnected from a private life in freefall. The church member spreading malicious gossip that has destroyed three families and shows no sign of stopping.

Pastors see all of it. And most of them do nothing.

I know this because I am a pastor who has talked to thousands of other pastors, and the confession is always the same: I know I should say something. I know the Bible tells me to. But I am terrified of what will happen if I do.

The fear is not irrational. It is born from experience. The pastor who confronts a wealthy donor risks losing the giving that keeps the lights on. The pastor who confronts a popular leader risks a church split. The pastor who confronts anyone in an era of Google reviews and social media risks public shaming, defamation, and the kind of coordinated campaign that can destroy a ministry overnight. The cost of confrontation has never been higher, and the cultural support for it has never been lower.

But the cost of silence is higher still. We just do not see it because silence kills slowly.

Here is what the church lost when it lost discipline.

**We lost the meaning of membership.**

If membership means nothing more than having your name on a list, then the church is a club and the list is a mailing roster. But the New Testament describes membership as covenant. When you join the body, you are entering a relationship with mutual obligations. You are committing to live in a way that honors Christ and the community. The community is committing to walk with you, encourage you, correct you, and bear your burdens. Church discipline is what gives that covenant teeth. Without it, membership is a formality. With it, membership means someone is watching your life with love and will not let you destroy yourself in silence.

The average church member in America can live in open, visible, destructive sin for years and never have a single person from their church say a word to them about it. Not because nobody cares. Because nobody believes they have the standing to speak. We have so thoroughly privatized faith that confronting someone about their behavior feels like a violation of their autonomy rather than an expression of love.

Dietrich Bonhoeffer saw this coming. In Life Together, written in 1939 while training pastors for the underground Confessing Church in Germany, he wrote that the person who sins without being confronted is more alone than the person who is rebuked. Because the confronted person at least knows they are in a community that takes their soul seriously. The unchallenged person is in a community that has already given up on them. They just do not know it yet.

**We lost the ability to protect the vulnerable.**

This is the part that should keep every pastor awake at night. When the church has no process for addressing predatory behavior, abusive leadership, or destructive patterns, the people who pay the price are always the weakest and most vulnerable members of the community. The abused spouse whose husband is a deacon. The child whose Sunday school teacher has boundary issues nobody addresses. The new believer being manipulated by someone in a position of spiritual authority.

Every headline about a church abuse scandal follows the same arc. Someone in power sinned. People knew. Nobody acted. The institution protected itself. The vulnerable were sacrificed. And when the truth finally broke into the open — through a lawsuit, a news report, or a victim brave enough to speak — the church acted shocked. As if they could not have known. As if there were no mechanism to intervene.

There was a mechanism. Jesus designed it. It is recorded in Matthew 18. It has been in the Bible the entire time. The church chose not to use it because using it would have been costly, uncomfortable, and disruptive. So the vulnerable paid the cost instead.

I have sat across from abuse survivors who told me the same story. The church knew. The church did nothing. The church chose peace over protection. And when the survivor finally left, the church treated their departure as the problem rather than the abuse that caused it.

That is what happens when discipline dies. The predator stays. The victim leaves. And the church congratulates itself on maintaining unity.

**We lost the credibility of the gospel.**

The watching world is not stupid. When a church claims to follow a God of holiness and justice but tolerates open corruption among its leaders, the world draws the obvious conclusion: either the God is not real, or the church does not actually believe in the God it claims to serve.

Every survey of young adults who have left the faith tells the same story. The number one reason is not intellectual doubt. It is hypocrisy. Not the ordinary, garden-variety hypocrisy of people who fail to live up to their ideals — everyone does that. The structural hypocrisy of institutions that demand standards they refuse to enforce, that preach accountability for everyone except the people in power, that use grace as a cover for institutional self-protection.

When a megachurch pastor is exposed for sexual misconduct and the board covers it up, that is not a failure of one man. That is a failure of discipline. When a denominational leader embezzles funds and the denomination quietly reassigns him rather than removing him, that is not mercy. That is cowardice masquerading as grace. When a church tolerates racism in its ranks and calls it a difference of opinion, that is not unity. That is complicity.

The world sees all of it. And the world is right to be repulsed.

Church discipline, practiced honestly and consistently, is one of the most compelling apologetic arguments the church could offer. A community that holds itself accountable — that takes sin seriously in its own house before pointing at the world’s sin — has a credibility that no sermon series or marketing campaign can manufacture. The early church had that credibility. The second-century apologist Aristides wrote to Emperor Hadrian that Christians “do not commit adultery or fornication, they do not bear false witness, they do not deny a deposit, they do not covet what is not theirs.“ He was not describing perfection. He was describing a community that took its own standards seriously enough to enforce them.

That description would make most modern congregations blush. Not because we are worse than the early church — we may not be — but because we have stopped even attempting to maintain the standard. We have decided that grace means never having to be confronted and love means never having to be told no. And the watching world has noticed that the church’s moral claims have no institutional backbone.

**We lost the practice of repentance.**

Repentance is not a feeling. It is a turn. It requires a destination — something to turn toward — and an acknowledgment of what you are turning from. In a church without discipline, there is no structure to support that turn. The person caught in sin has nowhere to go with it. They cannot confess to a community that has no structure for receiving confession. They cannot repent to a body that has no expectation of holiness. They cannot be restored to something that never held them accountable in the first place.

The result is what Bonhoeffer called cheap grace. Grace without cost. Forgiveness without repentance. Communion without confession. Absolution without discipline. Cheap grace is the preaching of forgiveness without requiring repentance, baptism without church discipline, communion without confession, absolution without personal confession. Cheap grace is grace without discipleship, grace without the cross, grace without Jesus Christ, living and incarnate.

That passage from The Cost of Discipleship was written in 1937. It has only gotten more relevant. The American church is drowning in cheap grace. We hand out forgiveness like candy and wonder why nobody values it. We proclaim freedom in Christ and watch people use that freedom to destroy themselves and others without anyone in the church saying a word.

Discipline is the structure that makes repentance possible. When the church says to someone, “What you are doing is destroying you and grieving the God who loves you, and we love you too much to watch it continue,“ that confrontation creates a crisis. And crisis is where repentance lives. Not in comfort. Not in silence. In the honest, painful, grace-saturated confrontation with reality.

**We lost the health of the body.**

Paul’s body metaphor is not decorative. He means it. If the church is a body, then sin in one member affects every other member. The hand that is infected threatens the arm. The eye that is diseased threatens the brain. This is not a moral abstraction. It is an observable reality in every local church.

When a leader is living in unrepentant sin and nobody addresses it, the whole leadership culture shifts. Other leaders begin to rationalize their own compromises. Standards become negotiable. The difference between what the church says and what the church tolerates widens until nobody takes the stated standards seriously anymore.

When a member is engaged in gossip that is tearing relationships apart and nobody confronts it, the gossip does not stay contained. It spreads. Trust erodes. Small groups fracture. Families leave. The church wonders why attendance is declining and never connects it to the relational poison it refused to address.

When domestic abuse is happening in a church family and the church treats it as a private matter, the message to every other abuse victim in the congregation is unmistakable: this church will not protect you. Stay quiet. Endure it. The abuser’s comfort matters more than your safety.

All of this is the body without an immune system. An organism that cannot identify threats and cannot respond to them. An organism that is slowly dying of preventable disease while assuring itself that everything is fine because confrontation would be unloving.

The irony is staggering. We call ourselves the body of Christ. We sing about being one body, one Spirit, one Lord. And then we refuse to do the one thing that bodies must do to stay alive: fight the infection before it kills the host.

This is what we lost. Not a rule. Not a procedure. Not a mechanism of control. We lost the immune system of the body of Christ. And we are dying because of it.

The question is whether we are willing to recover it. Not the distorted version — the shunning, the power plays, the authoritarian control dressed in spiritual language. The real thing. The costly, grace-filled, truth-telling, restoration-aimed practice that Jesus described and the early church embodied.

That recovery begins with understanding what Jesus actually said. Which is what the next chapter is about.

**Chapter 2: What Jesus Actually Said — Matthew 18 and the Process Nobody Follows**

The most misquoted verse in the entire discipline conversation is Matthew 18:20. “For where two or three gather in my name, there am I with them.“

Every small group leader in America has used that verse to encourage a poorly attended Bible study. Every prayer meeting has claimed it. It shows up on church websites and bulletin covers and inspirational graphics shared by people who have never read the paragraph it sits inside.

But Matthew 18:20 is not about small groups. It is not about prayer meetings. It is not about God showing up wherever a handful of believers get together. It is the conclusion of the most detailed instruction Jesus ever gave about church discipline. And ripping it from that context has allowed the church to ignore the most important passage on the subject in the entire New Testament.

Here is the full text. Matthew 18:15-20.

“If your brother or sister sins, go and point out their fault, just between the two of you. If they listen to you, you have won them over. But if they will not listen, take one or two others along, so that every matter may be established by the testimony of two or three witnesses. If they still refuse to listen, tell it to the church; and if they refuse to listen even to the church, treat them as you would a pagan or a tax collector. Truly I tell you, whatever you bind on earth will be bound in heaven, and whatever you loose on earth will be loosed in heaven. Again, truly I tell you that if two of you on earth agree about anything they ask for, it will be done for them by my Father in heaven. For where two or three gather in my name, there am I with them.“

Read that passage carefully. Read it without the baggage of every bad church discipline experience you have had or heard about. Read it as if Jesus meant what he said. Because he did.

**The first thing to notice is who initiates.**

“If your brother or sister sins, go.“ Not “wait for the pastor to handle it.“ Not “report it to the elders and hope they deal with it.“ Not “post about it online and let the court of public opinion sort it out.“ Go. You. The person who sees the sin. The person who is affected by it. The person who cares about the other person’s soul.

This is where most churches fail before they even begin. We have created a consumer culture in our congregations where the expectation is that the paid staff handles all difficult conversations. If someone is sinning, tell the pastor. If there is conflict, tell the pastor. If someone is hurting, tell the pastor. The congregation has outsourced confrontation to professionals, and the professionals are drowning.

Jesus did not design a system where one person handles all the hard conversations. He designed a community where every member takes responsibility for every other member. That is what “go“ means. It means the burden is distributed. It means the body takes care of itself. It means you do not get to watch your brother destroy himself and call it not your problem.

The Greek word behind “sins“ here is hamartano — to miss the mark, to wander from the path. The sin Jesus describes is not ambiguous. It is observable. It is the kind of behavior that a fellow believer can see, identify, and name. This is not about thought crimes or doctrinal disagreements or personality conflicts. This is about visible, identifiable sin that is damaging the person, the community, or both.

**The second thing to notice is the goal.**

“If they listen to you, you have won them over.“ The Greek is kerdaino — to gain, to win. The same word Paul uses in 1 Corinthians 9:19-22 when he describes becoming all things to all people “so that by all possible means I might save some.“ This is rescue language. The goal of the first conversation is not punishment. It is not humiliation. It is not “getting it on the record.“ The goal is winning the person back. Restoration. Healing. A return to health.

This means the first conversation is not a prosecution. It is a plea. You go to your brother or sister not as a judge but as a friend. Not with a verdict but with a concern. Not to condemn but to call them back from a path that is destroying them.

I have had these conversations. They are the hardest thing in pastoral ministry. Sitting across from someone you love and telling them that what they are doing is sin. Watching their face shift from friendliness to defensiveness to anger. Feeling the relationship strain under the weight of truth. Every instinct screams at you to soften the message, to add qualifiers, to protect the relationship at the expense of honesty.

But Jesus does not give you that option. He says go. He says tell them. He says the goal is their soul, and their soul is worth the discomfort.

**The third thing to notice is the escalation.**

“But if they will not listen, take one or two others along, so that every matter may be established by the testimony of two or three witnesses.“

Jesus is quoting Deuteronomy 19:15, the Old Testament principle that no accusation can stand on the testimony of a single witness. This is not just procedural. It is protective. Protective of the accused, who deserves more than one person’s interpretation of events. And protective of the accuser, who needs the wisdom and accountability of others in the room.

The witnesses serve multiple functions. They verify the facts. They provide objectivity. They demonstrate to the offending person that this is not a personal grudge — multiple people see the same problem. And they share the burden of confrontation so that one person is not carrying it alone.

This step is where most church discipline attempts die. Not because the church refuses to escalate, but because the church never took the first step. If nobody had the private conversation, there is nothing to escalate. The person was never confronted at all. They were just talked about. And gossip — even prayerful gossip, even concerned gossip — is not the same as the confrontation Jesus commanded.

**The fourth thing to notice is the community's role.**

“If they still refuse to listen, tell it to the church.“ Not the board. Not the elders only. Not a closed-door meeting where the congregation is kept in the dark. The church. The assembly. The gathered body.

This is where the passage gets uncomfortable for every governance model. Congregational churches, elder-led churches, bishop-governed churches — none of them practice this step the way Jesus described it. Because telling the church means the church has to know. And knowledge means responsibility. And responsibility means the congregation can no longer claim ignorance when things go wrong.

The early church took this literally. The Didache, written in the late first or early second century, describes a community where disputes were brought before the assembly and resolved publicly. Clement of Rome, writing around 96 AD, urged the Corinthian church to deal with its conflicts openly rather than letting them fester in private. The pattern is consistent: the early church believed that sin affecting the body should be addressed by the body.

The modern church has largely abandoned this practice for understandable reasons. Privacy concerns. Legal liability. The fear of public shaming. The desire to protect the accused from mob justice. These are real concerns. They deserve real answers. But the solution cannot be to eliminate the step entirely, because when we do, we eliminate the congregation’s participation in the process that Christ designed for them.

**The fifth thing to notice is the consequence.**

“And if they refuse to listen even to the church, treat them as you would a pagan or a tax collector.“

This is the sentence that terrifies gentle pastors and delights harsh ones. And both groups tend to misread it.

The harsh reading: cast them out. Cut them off. Shun them. They are dead to us. This reading ignores how Jesus actually treated pagans and tax collectors. He ate with them. He sought them out. He told stories about them that made the religious establishment furious. He called Matthew from a tax booth and made him an apostle. He told a Samaritan woman about living water while the disciples stood around scandalized.

To treat someone as a pagan or tax collector in the way of Jesus is not to abandon them. It is to recognize that they are no longer functioning as a member of the covenant community. They have placed themselves outside the body by their persistent refusal to repent. The relationship changes — not from love to hatred, but from mutual accountability to missionary concern. You treat them the way you would treat anyone who does not yet know Christ: with persistent love, honest witness, and an open door.

The gentle misreading is equally dangerous. Some pastors read this verse and conclude that Jesus cannot really mean what he is saying. Surely we should just keep forgiving. Surely we should just keep trying. Surely we should never reach the point where someone’s status in the community changes.

But Jesus is clear. There is a point. When the private conversation, the witnessed conversation, and the congregational appeal have all failed — when the person has been given every opportunity to repent and has refused every one — the community must acknowledge reality. This person is not living as a member of the body. Pretending otherwise helps no one. It does not help the unrepentant person, who needs the crisis of exclusion to wake them up. And it does not help the community, which needs to know that its standards are more than aspirational.

**The sixth thing to notice is the authority.**

“Truly I tell you, whatever you bind on earth will be bound in heaven, and whatever you loose on earth will be loosed in heaven.“

This is staggering language. Jesus is giving the church — not the pope, not the bishop, not the senior pastor, but the church — the authority to make decisions about membership and fellowship that carry heavenly weight. The binding and loosing language comes from rabbinic practice, where authoritative teachers would “bind“ (forbid) and “loose“ (permit) certain practices. Jesus is saying that when the church follows this process faithfully, the decision it reaches is ratified by heaven.

This does not mean the church is infallible. History is littered with churches that excommunicated the wrong people for the wrong reasons. But it does mean that when the process is followed — when private confrontation, witnessed confrontation, congregational appeal, and persistent refusal have all occurred — the church is not overstepping its authority by acting. It is exercising the authority Christ gave it.

And then comes the verse we have been using wrong for two thousand years. “For where two or three gather in my name, there am I with them.“ In context, this is not a promise about God’s presence at small gatherings. It is a promise about God’s presence in discipline. When two or three believers gather to confront sin and pursue restoration in the name of Christ, Christ himself is in the room. The discipline process is not a human invention. It is a divine activity. Christ is present in the confrontation. Christ is present in the witness. Christ is present in the decision.

That changes everything.

If Christ is present, then the process cannot be casual. It must be prayerful, deliberate, and saturated with the character of Christ — truthful and gracious, firm and compassionate, just and merciful.

If Christ is present, then the process cannot be weaponized. Using discipline to settle personal scores, protect institutional power, or punish people you do not like is using the name of Christ for purposes Christ would condemn.

If Christ is present, then the process cannot be avoided. The church that refuses to practice discipline is not being more loving than Jesus. It is being less obedient.

I have talked to hundreds of pastors who have read Matthew 18 and concluded it is impractical for the modern church. Too confrontational. Too likely to result in lawsuits. Too disruptive. Too old-fashioned.

And I understand the hesitation. This passage demands something the modern church is not built for. It demands honesty. It demands courage. It demands a community where people know each other well enough to see the sin and care enough to address it. It demands leaders who are willing to lose members in order to love them.

But impractical is not the same as wrong. And the consequences of ignoring this passage — the abuse that goes unchecked, the leaders who fall with no one to catch them, the congregations that rot from the inside while the outside looks polished — those consequences are worse than the discomfort of obedience.

Jesus told his church exactly what to do. The church decided it was too hard. That decision has cost us more than we know.

**Chapter 3: What Paul Actually Did — Corinth, Galatia, and Discipline in the Early Church**

Paul’s first letter to the Corinthians was not polite. It was an emergency intervention.

The church at Corinth was a mess. Division. Lawsuits between members. Sexual immorality tolerated and even celebrated. Drunkenness at the Lord’s Table. Spiritual gifts wielded as status symbols. A community that had confused freedom in Christ with license to do whatever it wanted.

And in the middle of all of it, one situation so egregious that even the surrounding pagan culture would have been horrified.

A man was sleeping with his father’s wife.

Paul does not specify whether the woman was the man’s stepmother or his biological mother. The phrase “his father’s wife“ echoes Leviticus 18:8 and Deuteronomy 22:30, both of which prohibit sexual relations with a father’s wife as a violation of the most basic family boundaries. The relationship was ongoing. The church knew about it. And the church’s response was nothing.

Worse than nothing. The church was “proud“ of it. First Corinthians 5:2 says they were “puffed up“ — the Greek word phusioo, meaning inflated, swollen with self-importance. They had convinced themselves that their tolerance of this man’s sin was evidence of their spiritual maturity. They were so enlightened, so liberated from legalism, so full of grace that they could absorb even incest without blinking.

Paul’s response is the most severe rebuke in any of his letters. “Hand this man over to Satan for the destruction of the flesh, so that his spirit may be saved on the day of the Lord“ (1 Corinthians 5:5).

That sentence has been debated for two millennia. What does “hand over to Satan“ mean? What is “the destruction of the flesh“? Is this damnation? Physical punishment? Something else entirely?

The most responsible reading, consistent with Paul’s usage elsewhere and with the broader teaching of Scripture, is that Paul is calling for excommunication — removal from the protective fellowship of the church and the exposure to the consequences of living outside covenant community. “The destruction of the flesh“ is the ruination of the sinful pattern — the behavior itself — through the crisis of exclusion. And the purpose is explicitly redemptive: “so that his spirit may be saved.“ This is not a death sentence. It is a severe mercy. A last resort aimed at jolting the man awake when every gentler approach has failed.

But Paul’s rebuke is not aimed primarily at the man. It is aimed at the church.

“Don’t you know that a little yeast leavens the whole batch of dough? Get rid of the old yeast, so that you may be a new unleavened batch — as you really are. For Christ, our Passover lamb, has been sacrificed“ (1 Corinthians 5:6-7).

The yeast metaphor is devastating. Yeast does not stay in one corner of the dough. It spreads until the entire batch is affected. Paul is telling the Corinthian church that their tolerance of unrepentant sin is not an act of grace toward one person. It is a threat to the entire community. The sin will spread. The tolerance will become the norm. What was once shocking will become merely uncomfortable. What was uncomfortable will become accepted. What was accepted will become celebrated. That is how moral decay works in every human institution, and the church is not immune.

The Passover language intensifies the point. Israel ate unleavened bread at Passover because leaven represented the old life in Egypt — the corruption, the compromise, the slavery. Removing the leaven was an act of identity: we are not who we were. We are a delivered people. Paul is saying the same thing to Corinth: you are a redeemed community. Act like it. The cross of Christ did not purchase your freedom so that you could use it to tolerate the very things Christ died to rescue you from.

Then Paul draws a line that the modern church consistently erases.

“I wrote to you in my letter not to associate with sexually immoral people — not at all meaning the people of this world who are immoral, or the greedy and swindlers, or idolaters. In that case you would have to leave this world. But now I am writing to you that you must not associate with anyone who claims to be a brother or sister but is sexually immoral or greedy, an idolater or slanderer, a drunkard or swindler. Do not even eat with such people. What business is it of mine to judge those outside the church? Are you not to judge those inside? God will judge those outside. Expel the wicked person from among you“ (1 Corinthians 5:9-13).

This passage demolishes two opposing errors simultaneously.

It demolishes the church that judges the world. Paul says explicitly: it is not your business to judge outsiders. The church has no authority to impose its standards on people who have not entered the covenant community. The culture wars, the moral crusades aimed at non-Christians, the demand that secular society conform to biblical ethics — Paul would consider all of it misplaced energy. God will judge those outside. That is his domain, not ours.

And it demolishes the church that refuses to judge its own. Paul says with equal clarity: you are responsible for judging those inside. When someone who “claims to be a brother or sister“ is living in open, unrepentant sin, the community has not just the right but the obligation to act. The church that refuses to judge its own members in the name of love is not following Jesus. It is following a god of its own making — a god who demands nothing, confronts nothing, and in the end saves nothing, because a god who does not care about holiness is a god who does not care.

“Expel the wicked person from among you.“ Paul quotes Deuteronomy 17:7, a phrase that appears repeatedly in the Old Testament’s instructions for maintaining covenant community purity. The echo is deliberate. Paul is connecting the church’s practice of discipline to the entire biblical arc of God’s insistence that his people take holiness seriously. This is not a new idea Paul invented. This is the consistent demand of a holy God across both testaments.

Now here is where the story turns. Because Paul does not stop at 1 Corinthians.

In 2 Corinthians 2:5-11, written sometime later after the man apparently repented, Paul writes something that the churches who love discipline often conveniently ignore:

“If anyone has caused grief, he has not so much grieved me as he has grieved all of you to some extent — not to put it too severely. The punishment inflicted on him by the majority is sufficient. Now instead, you ought to forgive and comfort him, so that he will not be overwhelmed by excessive sorrow. I urge you, therefore, to reaffirm your love for him.“

Read that sentence again. “Reaffirm your love for him.“

The man who was sleeping with his father’s wife. The man Paul commanded them to expel. The man they were told to hand over to Satan. Paul now commands the church to forgive him, comfort him, and publicly reaffirm their love for him.

Not because the sin was not serious. The sin was devastating. Not because accountability does not matter. Paul was the one who demanded accountability. But because the purpose of discipline was never destruction. It was restoration. And when restoration happens — when repentance is real, when the turn is genuine — the community’s job changes from confrontation to welcome.

Paul even warns them about the danger of getting discipline wrong in the opposite direction: “so that he will not be overwhelmed by excessive sorrow.“ The word is katapino — to be swallowed up, devoured. Paul is concerned that the church, having finally found the courage to discipline, will become addicted to it. That they will enjoy the power of exclusion and be reluctant to extend the grace of restoration. That the man will drown in shame because the community that confronted his sin refuses to celebrate his repentance.

This is the second ditch. And Paul sees it coming.

The church that cannot discipline destroys people through permissiveness. The church that cannot restore destroys people through severity. Both failures come from the same root: a community that does not understand grace. Grace confronts. Grace also embraces. And the measure of a church’s maturity is its ability to do both with equal seriousness.

Galatians 6:1 adds one more critical dimension. “Brothers and sisters, if someone is caught in a sin, you who live by the Spirit should restore that person gently. But watch yourselves, or you also may be tempted.“

Three things in that verse deserve attention.

First: “caught in a sin.“ The Greek word is prolambano — to be overtaken, surprised, caught off guard. This is not the calculated, defiant sinner of 1 Corinthians 5. This is someone who has been ambushed by temptation. Someone who fell. The distinction matters because the approach is different. The defiant sinner who refuses repeated confrontation needs the severity of excommunication. The person who has been overtaken by sin needs gentleness.

Second: “restore.“ The Greek is katartizo — the same word used in Mark 1:19 for mending fishing nets. The image is repair. You take the torn net and put it back together. You take the broken person and help them heal. The goal is not punishment. It is not exposure. It is not a record of wrongs. It is mending.

Third: “watch yourselves, or you also may be tempted.“ This is the self-implication clause. The person doing the restoring is not above the sin they are addressing. They are susceptible to the same failures. Discipline is never done from a position of moral superiority. It is done from a position of shared vulnerability. The moment the confronter believes they are immune to the sin they are confronting, the confrontation becomes dangerous. Pride turns the healer into a judge and the restoration into a prosecution.

Paul knew this because Paul had been both. He had been the self-righteous enforcer — holding coats at the stoning of Stephen, hunting Christians with the zeal of a man convinced of his own holiness. And he had been the broken man — blinded on the Damascus road, stripped of every credential he had accumulated, dependent entirely on the grace of the God he had been persecuting.

That biography matters. The person who disciplines must be a person who knows their own need for grace. Not as a theological abstraction. As a lived reality.

The early church took all of this seriously. The Shepherd of Hermas, written in the early to mid-second century, describes a rigorous system of penance and restoration. Those who sinned after baptism could undergo a period of public repentance and be restored to full fellowship. The process was costly — it involved public confession, a period of exclusion from the Eucharist, and demonstrable change in behavior — but it was always aimed at return.

Tertullian, writing around 200 AD, described the practice of exomologesis — a public confession that included weeping, fasting, and prostration before the congregation. It sounds extreme to modern ears. But Tertullian’s point was that the severity of the process reflected the seriousness of the sin and the value of the restoration. You do not undergo that kind of humility for something trivial. You undergo it because your place in the body matters that much, and your return is worth that much.

The early church was neither soft on sin nor cruel to sinners. It held both with a seriousness the modern church can barely imagine. And the result was communities that the pagan world found strange, disruptive, and irresistible.

We could have that again. If we were willing to take Paul as seriously as he took the churches he planted.

**Chapter 4: The Difference Between Discipline and Punishment**

When my oldest son was four years old, he hit his brother. Not the accidental, playing-too-rough kind of hit. The deliberate, I-am-angry-and-I-want-to-hurt-you kind. My wife Susanna and I had a decision to make. And the decision we made in that moment — the why behind our response, not just the what — is the same decision every church faces when sin surfaces in the body.

We could punish him. Make him suffer a consequence severe enough that he would associate hitting with pain and stop doing it out of fear. This approach works, to a degree. Fear modifies behavior. Every authoritarian regime in history has proven that. The problem is that fear only modifies behavior as long as the threat is present. Remove the enforcer and the behavior returns. Because the heart has not changed. Only the calculation has.

Or we could discipline him. Sit with him. Name what he did. Explain why it was wrong — not just that it was against the rules, but that it hurt someone he loved. Help him understand what was happening inside him that made him want to hurt his brother. Walk him through what repentance looks like — not just saying sorry but meaning it, and then doing something different. And yes, there would be a consequence. But the consequence was not the point. The consequence was the container for a conversation about the kind of person he was becoming.

Punishment asks: How do I stop this behavior? Discipline asks: How do I form this person?

The church has confused these two things for most of its history, and the confusion has been catastrophic.

The Latin word disciplina comes from discipulus — student, learner, disciple. Discipline, in its original meaning, is formation. It is the process by which a student becomes like the teacher. When Jesus called his followers “disciples,“ he was calling them into a lifelong process of being shaped, corrected, formed, and transformed into his likeness. Discipline is how disciples are made.

Punishment comes from a different root and serves a different function. Punishment is the imposition of a penalty for an offense. Its primary orientation is backward — toward the offense. Its primary currency is suffering. Its primary logic is retribution: you did this, so you deserve that.

There is a place for punishment in a just society. The criminal justice system, for all its failures, is built on the principle that certain offenses warrant penalties imposed by legitimate authority. Romans 13 acknowledges this — governing authorities bear the sword as agents of God’s justice in the civil sphere.

But the church is not a government. The church does not bear the sword. The church has no authority to impose civil penalties, extract fines, or inflict physical suffering. And when the church confuses its role with the state’s role — when it begins treating discipline as punishment — it produces something that looks less like the kingdom of God and more like the Inquisition.

The history is damning.

In 385 AD, a Spanish bishop named Priscillian was executed for heresy. He was the first Christian to be killed by other Christians for theological error. The emperor ordered the execution, but the institutional church was complicit. Bishop Ithacius of Ossonuba pressed the charges. The church had discovered that it could use the power of the state to enforce theological conformity, and some of its leaders found the arrangement intoxicating.

Martin of Tours and Ambrose of Milan protested. They understood that something essential had been violated. The church’s authority to discipline was spiritual, not physical. The moment discipline became punishment — the moment exclusion from fellowship became execution — the church had crossed a line that Christ never authorized it to cross.

But the line kept moving. The medieval church developed an elaborate penitential system that tied forgiveness to suffering. Penance was not just confession and repentance — it was calculated suffering. So many prayers, so many fasts, so many pilgrimages, calibrated to the severity of the offense. The system was codified in penitential manuals that read like sentencing guidelines: adultery equals seven years of penance. Murder equals fifteen. Missing Mass without excuse equals forty days of bread and water.

The logic was punitive. You sinned. Now you pay. And the church was the collection agency.

This system reached its grotesque zenith in the sale of indulgences — the practice that provoked Martin Luther’s Ninety-Five Theses in 1517. Johann Tetzel, the Dominican friar who made the practice famous, had a slogan: “As soon as the coin in the coffer rings, the soul from purgatory springs.“ It was the logical endpoint of a church that had confused discipline with punishment. If forgiveness requires suffering, and suffering can be quantified, then suffering can be purchased. The gospel had been turned into a transaction, and the church was running the cash register.

Luther’s protest was not primarily about money. It was about the nature of grace. Grace is free. Forgiveness is not earned through suffering. The cross is not a down payment — it is full payment. When the church turns discipline into punishment, it implicitly denies the sufficiency of the cross. It says that Christ’s suffering was not enough. That the sinner must add their own suffering to complete the transaction. That is not Christianity. That is paganism wearing a cross.

The Reformation corrected some of this, but not all of it. Calvin’s Geneva practiced a form of church discipline that was theologically motivated but often functionally punitive. The Consistory — the body of pastors and elders that oversaw discipline — dealt with everything from adultery to naming your child after a Catholic saint. The penalties ranged from public reprimand to banishment. And while Calvin insisted that the goal was always repentance, the system in practice often felt more like social control than spiritual formation.

The Puritans carried the same tension across the Atlantic. Their congregational discipline was more democratic than Calvin’s model — the whole church participated in discipline decisions — but the intensity could be crushing. Nathaniel Hawthorne’s The Scarlet Letter, published in 1850, is a fictional account, but its portrait of Hester Prynne forced to wear the mark of her adultery resonated because it captured something real about how discipline had been practiced. The public shame. The permanent mark. The community that could condemn but not restore.

I raise this history not to condemn every instance of church discipline before the modern era. Much of it was practiced with genuine care and theological integrity. But the pattern of confusion — discipline sliding into punishment, formation sliding into retribution — is consistent enough to deserve naming.

So how do we tell the difference? How does a church know whether what it is practicing is discipline or punishment?

Here are the markers.

**Discipline is oriented toward the future. Punishment is oriented toward the past.**

When the church confronts a member’s sin, the central question reveals everything. If the question is “What do you deserve for what you did?“ — that is punishment. If the question is “What does your restoration require?“ — that is discipline. The first question looks backward. The second looks forward. The first is about justice in the retributive sense. The second is about justice in the restorative sense.

This does not mean the past does not matter. Understanding what happened, why it happened, and what damage it caused is essential to the restoration process. But the past is the diagnosis, not the sentence. The church examines the wound to heal it, not to deepen it.

**Discipline is proportional. Punishment escalates.**

The Matthew 18 process is deliberately graduated. Private conversation first. Witnesses second. The church third. Exclusion last. Each step is proportional to the person’s response. If they repent at step one, there is no step two. The process does not continue past the point where restoration occurs.

Punishment, by contrast, tends to escalate. The offender must suffer enough to satisfy the community’s sense of justice. The penalty must match the severity of the offense as the community perceives it. And communities in the grip of self-righteousness tend to perceive offenses as very severe indeed. The punishment grows until it satisfies not the requirements of restoration but the emotional needs of the punishers.

**Discipline is honest about its purpose. Punishment hides behind spiritual language.**

A church practicing discipline can articulate what it is trying to accomplish: we are confronting this sin because we love this person and we want to see them restored. The process, the timeline, and the path to restoration are clear. The person being disciplined knows what is expected of them and what will happen when they meet those expectations.

A church practicing punishment wraps retribution in spiritual vocabulary. “We are doing this for your own good.“ “God is teaching you through suffering.“ “You need to learn your lesson.“ The language sounds spiritual, but the function is punitive. And the path to restoration is often unclear, undefined, or nonexistent — because the goal was never restoration. The goal was making the person pay.

**Discipline involves the discipliner's own vulnerability. Punishment maintains the discipliner's moral distance.**

Galatians 6:1 is the test here. “Watch yourselves, or you also may be tempted.“ The person who practices discipline genuinely does so with an awareness of their own frailty. They approach the confrontation as a fellow sinner who understands the gravitational pull of temptation. They can say, with honesty, “I am not above what you are going through. I confront you not from a place of superiority but from a place of shared need.“

The person who punishes maintains distance. They position themselves above the offender — morally, spiritually, and relationally. The subtext is always: I would never do what you did. And that superiority poisons the entire process. It makes the offender feel not like a patient in a hospital but like a defendant in a courtroom. And defendants do not repent. They defend.

**Discipline has a defined end point. Punishment is indefinite.**

When restoration occurs — when genuine repentance is demonstrated, when behavior changes, when trust begins to rebuild — discipline is finished. Paul told the Corinthians to “reaffirm your love“ for the restored brother. The process has a goal, and when the goal is reached, the process ends.

Punishment has no clear end point. The person remains marked. Their sin remains the primary thing the community associates with them. Years after the offense, they are still “the person who...“ Their restoration is never quite complete. The community may say it forgives, but its memory is long and its welcome is conditional. The scarlet letter fades but never disappears.

I have seen both in local churches, sometimes in the same church.

I watched a church discipline a man for an affair with the kind of theological precision and pastoral care that left me hopeful for the future of the church. The confrontation was private. The witnesses were wise. The congregation was informed with appropriate discretion. The man was removed from leadership and asked to enter a restoration process — regular meetings with an elder, counseling with his wife, accountability for his schedule and relationships.

And I watched another church, three counties over, discipline a woman for questioning the pastor’s financial decisions. They called it “rebellion against spiritual authority.“ They removed her from her small group. They told other members not to associate with her. They spread rumors about her mental health. When she tried to meet with the elders to discuss the situation, they refused. When she left the church, they sent a letter to her new church warning them about her “divisive spirit.“

Same word — discipline. Two entirely different realities. One was the immune system functioning as designed. The other was the immune system attacking the body’s own tissue.

The difference was not theological competence. Both churches could cite Matthew 18 and 1 Corinthians 5. The difference was the purpose driving the process. The first church asked: how do we help this person return to health? The second church asked: how do we protect the institution from someone who threatens the leader’s control?

Purpose determines practice. Every time.

The church that wants to practice discipline faithfully must interrogate its own motives before it confronts anyone else’s sin. Am I doing this because I love this person? Or because I am angry? Am I doing this because their soul is at stake? Or because my authority is threatened? Am I doing this because the body needs healing? Or because the institution needs protecting?

Those questions are not comfortable. They are not meant to be. But they are the difference between the church that heals and the church that harms. And the watching world — the world that has seen the church do both — can tell the difference even when we cannot.

**Chapter 5: When Discipline Becomes Abuse — The Dark Side Nobody Wants to Admit**

In 2013, a woman I will call Rachel walked into my office carrying a manila folder. She was forty-two years old. She had been a Christian since she was seven. She had been a member of her previous church for eighteen years. And she had been under “church discipline“ for the last three of those years for the sin of filing for divorce from a man who had been hitting her.

The folder contained letters. Seven of them. All from church leadership. All addressed to Rachel. The tone was consistent across every letter: her decision to leave her marriage was rebellion against God. The fact that her husband hit her was regrettable but did not constitute biblical grounds for divorce. She was instructed to return to the marital home, submit to her husband’s authority, and trust God to change him. Each letter ended with a warning that her continued refusal to comply would result in further discipline, up to and including removal from membership.

The seventh letter informed her that she had been excommunicated.

Rachel was not excommunicated for adultery. She was not excommunicated for fraud or slander or any of the sins Paul lists in 1 Corinthians 5. She was excommunicated for protecting herself and her children from a man who broke her ribs twice and fractured her orbital bone once.

That church believed it was practicing discipline. It was practicing abuse.

I tell Rachel’s story — with her permission, details changed to protect her identity — because it represents a pattern so consistent across American churches that ignoring it would make this book dishonest. For every church that has failed by refusing to practice discipline, there is another church that has failed by practicing it in ways that Jesus would not recognize and Paul would condemn.

The abuse of church discipline is not a fringe phenomenon. It is not limited to cults or extreme fundamentalist groups. It happens in mainstream evangelical churches, in Reformed congregations, in charismatic communities, in denominational and nondenominational contexts alike. It happens wherever authority is concentrated without accountability, wherever theology is wielded as a tool of control, and wherever the institution’s reputation matters more than the individual’s welfare.

And it is time to name it clearly.

**Spiritual abuse through discipline looks like control masquerading as care.**

The defining characteristic of abusive discipline is not its existence but its direction. Healthy discipline flows toward the person being disciplined — toward their restoration, their healing, their return to wholeness. Abusive discipline flows toward the institution — toward its protection, its reputation, its leaders’ authority.

In Rachel’s case, the concern was never genuinely about her spiritual welfare. If it had been, the church would have asked different questions. What is happening in this marriage? Is this woman safe? Are her children safe? What does God’s protection look like for a person in this situation? Instead, the questions were all institutional. What does our theology say about divorce? What precedent does this set? What will people think if we allow a woman to leave her husband?

The institution was protected. Rachel was not.

This inversion of priority is the diagnostic marker. When a church’s discipline process protects the institution at the expense of the individual — when the reputation of the church matters more than the safety of the person — the process has been corrupted. It does not matter how many Bible verses are cited. It does not matter how sincere the leaders are. Sin dressed in theological language is still sin.

**Spiritual abuse through discipline looks like secrecy enforced by shame.**

Healthy discipline, as Jesus described it, involves increasing levels of transparency. Private conversation. Witnesses. The congregation. Each step brings more people into the process, providing accountability not just for the person being disciplined but for the people doing the disciplining.

Abusive discipline inverts this. It demands secrecy. The person being disciplined is told not to discuss the situation with anyone outside the leadership. They are told that talking about it would be gossip. They are told that questioning the process is evidence of an unrepentant heart. They are isolated from the very people who might offer perspective, support, or challenge to the leaders’ decisions.

This isolation is not an accident. It is a strategy. Abusers — whether domestic abusers, workplace abusers, or spiritual abusers — all use the same playbook: isolate the victim from their support network and control the narrative. When a church tells a member under discipline that they cannot talk to anyone about what is happening, that church is using the abuser’s playbook while quoting the Bible.

I have talked to people who were under church discipline and were told that seeking counsel from a Christian counselor outside the church was “a sign of distrust in the elders’ wisdom.“ I have talked to people who were told that hiring a lawyer was “evidence of a worldly mindset.“ I have talked to people who were told that contacting the police about a crime committed by a church member was “taking matters into your own hands instead of trusting God.“

Every one of those instructions was given in the name of biblical discipline. Every one of them was abuse.

**Spiritual abuse through discipline looks like disproportionate response to minor offenses.**

Matthew 18 describes a process for addressing sin — observable, identifiable behavior that damages the person, the community, or both. Paul’s instructions in 1 Corinthians 5 address severe, unrepentant sin. Neither passage was designed to address personality conflicts, differences of opinion, questions about leadership decisions, or the ordinary friction of human community.

But in churches where authority is concentrated and unchecked, the definition of “sin“ expands to include anything that threatens the leader or the institution. Asking questions becomes rebellion. Disagreeing with a decision becomes divisiveness. Leaving the church becomes abandonment of the body. Having an opinion the pastor does not share becomes a spirit of independence.

I know of a church that disciplined a family for attending a conference at another church without permission. I know of a church that disciplined a woman for wearing pants to a Wednesday evening service. I know of a church that disciplined a man for voting for a political candidate the pastor opposed. I know of a church that disciplined a couple for placing their children in public school.

None of those offenses appear in Scripture. None of them meet any biblical definition of sin. But in each case, the church’s leadership framed the behavior as sin, cited Scripture selectively to support their framing, and used the discipline process to enforce compliance.

This is not discipline. This is authoritarianism wearing a clerical collar.

**Spiritual abuse through discipline looks like selective application.**

The test of any justice system is consistency. Does the rule apply to everyone, or only to the people without power?

In abusive church discipline, the answer is always the second. The senior pastor’s indiscretions are handled quietly, privately, and with maximum compassion. The church member’s indiscretions are handled publicly, harshly, and with maximum shame. The leader who sins is “struggling.“ The member who sins is “in rebellion.“ The leader is given time, grace, and a soft landing. The member is given an ultimatum.

This double standard is so common that many church members do not even recognize it as wrong. They have been trained to believe that leaders deserve different treatment because of the weight of their calling. That confronting a pastor is different from confronting a layperson. That the anointing of leadership provides a kind of immunity from the accountability that applies to everyone else.

That is not the Bible’s view. James 3:1 says teachers will be judged more strictly, not less. The standard for leaders is higher, not lower. When David sinned with Bathsheba, Nathan did not give him a quiet talking-to and a severance package. He walked into the throne room and told the most powerful man in Israel that he had sinned against God. And God’s discipline of David — the death of the child, the turmoil in his family, the sword that would never depart from his house — was proportional to the magnitude of his responsibility.

A church that disciplines members but protects leaders is not practicing biblical discipline. It is running a protection racket.

**Spiritual abuse through discipline looks like shunning without restoration.**

Excommunication — the final step of the Matthew 18 process — is meant to be temporary. Its purpose is to create a crisis severe enough to provoke repentance. When repentance occurs, restoration follows. Paul made this explicit: “Reaffirm your love for him“ (2 Corinthians 2:8).

But in many churches, excommunication is permanent. The shunned person is cut off not just from the institution but from every relationship they had within it. Members are instructed not to associate with the excommunicated person. Friendships that predated the discipline are severed. Family members within the church are pressured to choose between the church and their excommunicated relative.

The Amish practice of Meidung — formal shunning — is the most visible example, but the practice exists in diluted forms across American evangelicalism. The member who is asked to leave is also informally blacklisted. Word spreads to other churches. The person’s reputation is destroyed in ways that make it difficult to find a new church home. The discipline follows them.

This is not what Jesus described. Jesus said to treat the unrepentant person “as you would a pagan or a tax collector“ — which, as we have established, means to continue pursuing them with love. Shunning without ongoing love is abandonment. And a church that abandons its members, even its wayward members, has ceased to function as the body of Christ.

**How do we name it without losing it?**

Here is the danger of this chapter. The person who has been hurt by abusive discipline reads this and concludes: church discipline is always abusive. The concept itself is the problem. Better to never confront anyone about anything than to risk doing what was done to me.

I understand that response. I respect it. But I cannot agree with it.

The fact that medicine can be practiced badly does not mean medicine should be abandoned. The fact that parenting can become abuse does not mean parenting should be avoided. The fact that discipline has been weaponized does not mean discipline is a weapon. It means fallen humans have corrupted something good — which is what fallen humans do with everything, including grace, love, truth, and the gospel itself.

The solution is not to abandon discipline. The solution is to practice it the way Jesus described it. With the safeguards he built in. With the transparency he demanded. With the goal he specified: restoration, not destruction.

That means churches must build structures that prevent abuse. Independent oversight of discipline processes. Clear, written policies that define what constitutes disciplinable offenses and what does not. External accountability — a denominational body, an outside board, a network of peer churches — that can review discipline decisions and challenge them when they go wrong. Legal counsel that ensures the church’s practices comply with civil law. And above all, a culture where questioning leadership is not treated as sin but as the healthy function of a community that takes truth seriously.

Rachel eventually found a church that understood the difference. A church that heard her story and wept. A church that told her she had been sinned against, not by her husband alone, but by the institution that was supposed to protect her. A church that practiced discipline when discipline was needed and refrained when it was not. A church where the leaders held themselves to the same standard they held the congregation.

She told me it took her four years to stop flinching when the pastor opened his Bible.

Four years. Because a church that called itself the body of Christ used the Bible as a weapon against a woman whose only offense was refusing to let her husband break her bones.

That is the cost of getting this wrong. And the church that refuses to reckon with that cost has no business picking up the tools of discipline until it does.

**Chapter 6: The Sin Nobody Confronts — When Leaders Fall and Institutions Cover**

In March 2014, Mark Driscoll was the pastor of Mars Hill Church in Seattle. The church had grown to fifteen thousand members across fifteen locations. Driscoll was one of the most influential pastors in America — a bestselling author, a conference headliner, a media presence who had almost single-handedly reshaped the image of Reformed theology for a younger generation.

By December 2014, Mars Hill Church no longer existed. Every campus had closed or rebranded. Thousands of members had scattered. A church that had taken twenty years to build was dismantled in nine months.

The collapse did not happen because of one event. It happened because of a pattern that stretched back years — bullying leadership behavior, financial irregularities, plagiarism in published works, a culture of fear among staff, and a governing structure that concentrated authority in one man while eliminating any mechanism to hold that man accountable. People inside the organization had been raising concerns for years. Those people were silenced, marginalized, or removed. The concerns were labeled as “attacks“ on the anointed leader. The institution’s immune system had been disabled, and by the time the infection became visible to the outside world, the body was already dead.

Mars Hill is the most visible example. It is not the most severe. The Southern Baptist Convention’s abuse scandal, which broke into public view in 2019, revealed that hundreds of church leaders across the denomination had been credibly accused of sexual abuse over a period of decades. The denomination’s response — or lack of response — followed the same pattern. Accusations were received and buried. Victims were discouraged from reporting. Abusive pastors were quietly moved to new churches rather than removed from ministry. The institution protected itself. The victims were sacrificed on the altar of denominational reputation.

The Catholic Church’s abuse crisis, which began making international headlines in 2002 with the Boston Globe’s investigation, demonstrated the same dynamic on a global scale. Thousands of priests. Tens of thousands of victims. Decades of institutional cover-up. Cardinals and bishops who knew what was happening and made the calculated decision that the institution’s survival was more important than the children’s safety.

These are not isolated failures. They are systemic ones. And they all share the same root cause: the church has no functioning mechanism for holding its leaders accountable.

Why?

The answer is theological, cultural, and structural. All three layers must be addressed.

**The theological problem: the anointing shield.**

A persistent strain of pastoral theology — present across denominational lines, from Pentecostal to Reformed — teaches that the senior pastor occupies a position of unique spiritual authority that should not be challenged by those under that authority. The supporting texts are familiar: “Touch not the Lord’s anointed“ (1 Chronicles 16:22, Psalm 105:15). “Do not bring a charge against an elder except on the evidence of two or three witnesses“ (1 Timothy 5:19). “Obey your leaders and submit to their authority“ (Hebrews 13:17).

Each of these texts, carefully read, says something other than what the anointing shield theology claims.

“Touch not the Lord’s anointed“ was spoken by David about Saul, and it meant “do not assassinate the king.“ It is a prohibition against political violence, not a prohibition against questioning a pastor’s decisions. Using this verse to silence dissent in a local church is exegetical malpractice.

First Timothy 5:19 — “Do not bring a charge against an elder except on the evidence of two or three witnesses“ — is not a protection against accountability. It is a procedural safeguard that makes accountability possible. Paul is saying that accusations against elders must meet a higher evidentiary standard, precisely because false accusations against leaders are destructive. But the very next verse demolishes the anointing shield: “But those elders who are sinning you are to reprove before everyone, so that the others may take warning“ (1 Timothy 5:20). Public rebuke. Before the entire congregation. For sinning elders. Paul is not protecting leaders from accountability. He is demanding that their accountability be more rigorous and more public than anyone else’s.

Hebrews 13:17 tells the congregation to “obey your leaders and submit to their authority.“ But the same verse gives the reason: “because they keep watch over you as those who must give an account.“ The leaders’ authority is not absolute. It is stewardship. They will answer to God for how they exercised it. And a steward who abuses the trust placed in them is not protected by the instruction to follow them — they have invalidated the premise that makes the instruction operative.

The anointing shield theology is not a careful reading of Scripture. It is a selective reading designed to protect power. And it has been used to shield every kind of pastoral failure — from minor arrogance to criminal predation — from the accountability that Scripture demands.

**The cultural problem: the celebrity pastor.**

The American church has created a system that produces celebrity pastors and then has no mechanism for holding them accountable once they arrive.

The system works like this. A gifted communicator builds a large platform. The platform generates revenue. The revenue creates infrastructure — staff, buildings, media departments, publishing deals. The infrastructure becomes dependent on the leader’s personal brand. The leader’s personal brand becomes the church’s identity. And at that point, confronting the leader threatens the entire enterprise. Every staff salary. Every mortgage payment. Every ministry program. The church cannot afford to lose the leader, so the church cannot afford to confront the leader. The economics of scale have created an accountability vacuum.

This is not a new problem. It is as old as medieval Christendom, where bishops accumulated so much temporal power that confronting them meant confronting the political and economic structures of entire regions. The scale is different. The dynamic is identical.

The megachurch board that knows its pastor is having an affair faces the same calculation that every medieval church official faced: if we expose this, we lose everything. If we cover it, we might survive. And the calculation almost always lands on the side of survival.

I am a small-church pastor. I lead a church of three hundred people. I do not have a personal brand that the church’s survival depends on. If my elders confronted me tomorrow about a pattern of sin in my life, the church would survive my departure. It would hurt. It would be disruptive. But the church is not built on James Bell. It is built on Christ.

That is how it should be. And the megachurch pastor who has allowed a structure to develop where the church cannot survive without them has already committed the sin that makes all other accountability impossible. They have made themselves indispensable. And an indispensable leader is an unaccountable leader.

**The structural problem: who watches the watchmen?**

In most evangelical churches, the senior pastor is the most powerful person in the room. The board or elder body that theoretically holds the pastor accountable was often selected by the pastor, trained by the pastor, and is personally loyal to the pastor. This is not inherently corrupt — pastors should have the ability to build teams they trust. But it creates a structural problem that few churches address: the people responsible for holding the pastor accountable are the same people whose positions depend on the pastor’s continued leadership.

The solution is not complicated. It is just unpopular.

Every church needs an accountability structure that the senior pastor does not control. This might take different forms depending on the church’s size and governance model. A denominational relationship that includes genuine oversight. An outside board of advisors with the authority to investigate complaints. A peer group of pastors in other churches with a mutual accountability covenant. An annual independent review of the pastor’s conduct, finances, and use of authority.

Whatever the form, the principle is the same: the person being held accountable cannot control the process by which they are held accountable. When they can, accountability is theater. And theater is what most churches have.

I submit to accountability that I did not design and cannot dismiss. My deacon body has the authority to confront me, investigate complaints against me, and remove me from my position if the evidence warrants it. That authority is written into our church constitution. It does not depend on my cooperation. It does not depend on my approval. It exists independently of my desire, and I would not have it any other way.

Not because I am more virtuous than the pastors who have fallen. I am not. I am made of the same material. I am susceptible to the same temptations. The difference is not my character. The difference is the structure I have voluntarily placed around my ministry. A structure designed to catch me before I fall, or to hold me accountable after I do.

Every pastor needs that structure. The ones who resist it are the ones who need it most.

**What the church owes its people.**

When a pastor falls — when the sin is sexual misconduct, financial fraud, or the kind of persistent leadership abuse that destroys people — the congregation is not a spectator. The congregation is a victim.

The members who trusted this leader with their spiritual formation, who brought their families to this church, who tithed from their income, who volunteered their time, who believed the gospel this leader preached — they have been betrayed. And the church’s response to that betrayal tells the world everything about whether the gospel it preaches is real.

The first obligation is truth. The congregation deserves to know what happened. Not every prurient detail, but enough to understand the nature and severity of the offense, the process that was followed in investigating it, and the action that was taken. Secrecy in the aftermath of leadership failure is not protection. It is a second betrayal.

The second obligation is care. The victims — both direct victims of the leader’s sin and the broader congregation who has been traumatized by the revelation — need pastoral support. Counseling. Grief processing. The space to be angry without being told their anger is ungodly. The freedom to leave if they need to without being labeled as disloyal.

The third obligation is systemic change. If the structure allowed the abuse to happen, the structure must change. New governance. New accountability mechanisms. New policies for financial oversight, pastoral conduct, and the handling of complaints. The church that responds to a leadership failure with “we just need to pray and move on“ will repeat the failure. Systems produce outcomes. If you do not change the system, you will get the same outcome with a different name.

The fourth obligation is honesty about what happened to the gospel’s credibility. Every time a church leader falls and the church covers it up, the watching world adds one more piece of evidence to the case against Christianity. Every time. The church that takes accountability seriously — that confronts its own leaders with the same rigor it applies to its members, that tells the truth about its failures, that grieves publicly and repents publicly — that church does more for the gospel’s reputation than a thousand evangelism campaigns.

The world is not waiting to see perfect churches. The world is waiting to see honest ones. Churches that practice what they preach. Churches where the rules apply to everyone. Churches where power does not exempt you from accountability but subjects you to more of it.

That church is worth building. Even if it means the pastor does not get to be comfortable. Especially then.

**Chapter 7: Who Has the Authority? — Governance, Power, and the Question Nobody Settles**

Every church discipline failure I have ever witnessed — every abuse of power, every refusal to act, every process that went sideways — can be traced back to one unanswered question: who has the authority to do this?

The question sounds simple. It is anything but.

In a congregational church, the authority theoretically belongs to the assembled body. The congregation votes on discipline matters the way it votes on budgets and pastoral calls. The people govern themselves under Christ’s headship. In theory.

In practice, congregational governance often means either that the pastor runs everything and the congregation rubber-stamps it, or that the congregation is so fractured and politically charged that discipline becomes impossible because every decision becomes a proxy war between competing factions. I have watched congregational churches where the discipline vote devolved into a popularity contest — the well-liked member received grace while the unpopular member received the full weight of Matthew 18. That is not discipline. That is politics.

In an elder-led church, the authority belongs to a body of elders — pastors and lay leaders who share governance responsibility. The congregation participates through membership, giving, and voluntary submission, but the elders make the final decisions on discipline and doctrine. In theory.

In practice, elder-led governance often concentrates power in the senior pastor who hand-picks the elders, sets the agenda, and controls the information flow. I have watched elder boards that functioned as accountability structures gradually transform into advisory bodies, and then into approval mechanisms, and finally into decorative features of a one-man show. The elders did not lose their authority overnight. It was eroded by a thousand small concessions, each one reasonable in isolation, until the structure that was supposed to hold the pastor accountable was held together only by the pastor’s goodwill.

In an episcopal church — governed by bishops — the authority belongs to an office that transcends the local congregation. The bishop oversees multiple churches and has the authority to intervene in local discipline matters when necessary. In theory.

In practice, bishops can be as corrupted by power as any other leader. The Catholic Church’s abuse crisis was an episcopal failure on a global scale. Bishops who knew about predatory priests protected the institution instead of the people it was supposed to serve. The office that was designed to provide oversight instead provided cover.

No governance model is immune to corruption. That is the first thing to admit. The problem is not congregational versus elder-led versus episcopal. The problem is human nature. Fallen people with power will eventually abuse it unless structures exist to prevent them. And structures staffed by fallen people will eventually be circumvented unless those structures are reinforced by a culture of transparency and accountability that pervades the entire community.

So who has the authority?

Jesus answered this question. The answer is layered, and most churches pick the layer they prefer and ignore the rest.

**Layer one: the individual believer has the authority to confront.**

Matthew 18:15 does not say “If your brother sins, tell the pastor.“ It says “go.“ The individual believer has the standing, the obligation, and the authority to confront a fellow believer about sin. This is not a privilege reserved for ordained clergy. It is a responsibility given to every member of the body.

The theological basis is the priesthood of all believers. First Peter 2:9 calls the entire community “a royal priesthood.“ Luther recovered this doctrine in the sixteenth century and applied it to the question of authority: every baptized believer has direct access to God and direct responsibility for their brothers and sisters. You do not need a credential to confront sin. You need a relationship and the courage to speak the truth in love.

Most church members do not believe this about themselves. They have been trained to think that spiritual authority flows from ordination or position. They defer hard conversations to professionals because they do not believe they have the standing to initiate them. And the result is a church where 90 percent of the body has abdicated its responsibility and 10 percent is drowning under the weight of conversations that everyone should be having.

**Layer two: the witnesses provide accountability for the process.**

The second step of Matthew 18 introduces witnesses — “one or two others.“ These witnesses are not a jury. They are not there to render a verdict. They are there to ensure that the process is fair, that the facts are established, and that the confrontation is done in a spirit consistent with the gospel.

The witnesses hold both parties accountable. They hold the accused accountable for their response — is there genuine repentance, or defensiveness and denial? And they hold the accuser accountable for their approach — is this a loving confrontation or a personal vendetta? Is the accusation substantiated or speculative?

This dual accountability is what most churches miss when they practice discipline. The focus is entirely on the person being disciplined. The question is always “Did they repent?“ and never “Did we confront them appropriately?“ But Jesus built accountability into both sides of the equation, because he knew that sin corrupts the confronter as readily as the confronted.

**Layer three: the congregation has the authority to act.**

“Tell it to the church.“ This is where governance models diverge, and where the deepest questions about authority arise.

In my reading of the text, the church — the assembled body — has a role that cannot be delegated entirely to a subset of leaders. The congregation must know what is happening because the congregation is affected by what is happening. The sin is in their body. The person is their member. The outcome will shape their community. They have a right to participate in the process, even if the mechanics of that participation differ from one governance model to the next.

This does not mean that every detail of every discipline case must be aired before the congregation. Prudence, compassion, and legal responsibility all require discretion. The congregation does not need to know the explicit details of a sexual sin. They do not need to hear testimony that would re-traumatize victims. They do not need a level of detail that would humiliate the offender beyond what the process requires.

But the congregation does need to know that discipline is happening. They need to know the general nature of the offense. They need to know the process that was followed. They need to know what is being asked of them — prayer, patience, a vote, a commitment to restoration. And they need to know that the leaders who are managing the process are themselves under accountability.

A church that hides discipline from the congregation is not protecting the person being disciplined. It is protecting the leaders from scrutiny. And leaders who cannot bear scrutiny should not be making decisions about other people’s standing in the body of Christ.

**Layer four: external accountability guards against insularity.**

This is the layer that independent churches resist most fiercely and need most desperately.

A local church that answers to no one outside itself has no mechanism for correcting its own errors. If the elders make a wrong discipline decision, who overturns it? If the pastor abuses the discipline process, who intervenes? If the congregation is manipulated into supporting an unjust action, who advocates for the person who was wronged?

Denominations provide one answer. The denominational structure — district superintendents, regional bishops, national councils — can serve as a court of appeal for discipline decisions that go wrong. This is one of the strongest arguments for denominational affiliation, and one that independent churches rarely consider until they need it.

But denominations are not the only option. Churches can form voluntary associations with mutual accountability covenants. They can appoint outside advisors with the authority to review discipline decisions. They can commit to consulting with trained mediators when cases become complex. The form matters less than the principle: no church should be the final arbiter of its own discipline decisions. The temptation to self-justify is too strong. The potential for abuse is too high.

Paul modeled this in his own ministry. He submitted his gospel to the Jerusalem council (Galatians 2:1-2) — not because he doubted its truth, but because he recognized that independent authority without external verification was dangerous. If the apostle Paul submitted to external review, every local church pastor can do the same.

**The question behind the question.**

Every debate about governance and authority in discipline is really a debate about a deeper question: do we trust the body to do what Christ designed it to do?

If we trust the body, we distribute authority. We empower individuals to confront. We include the congregation in the process. We seek external accountability. We build structures that prevent any single person from accumulating unchecked power. We believe that the Holy Spirit works through the community, not just through the leaders, and we act accordingly.

If we do not trust the body, we concentrate authority. We professionalize confrontation. We exclude the congregation from knowledge and decision-making. We resist external accountability. We build structures that protect the leaders’ ability to act unilaterally. We believe — though we would never say it out loud — that the Holy Spirit works primarily through the senior pastor, and the congregation’s role is to follow.

Most churches that talk about trusting the body practice concentrating authority. The gap between their theology and their governance is the space where discipline goes wrong.

I believe the body can be trusted. Not because people are reliable — people are not — but because the Spirit of Christ is reliable. And when the Spirit is genuinely present in a community, that community can do hard things. It can confront sin and pursue restoration. It can hold leaders accountable and protect the vulnerable. It can make difficult decisions and live with the mess that follows.

The church does not need a perfect governance model to practice discipline well. It needs a governance model that distributes authority, encourages transparency, provides accountability at every level, and assumes that everyone in the room — leaders included — is capable of both wisdom and corruption.

That is a humble governance. And humility is the only posture from which discipline can be practiced faithfully.

**Chapter 8: The Conversation Nobody Wants to Have — How to Actually Do This**

I sat in my truck in a church parking lot for forty-five minutes before I went inside for the conversation.

The man I was about to confront had been my friend for six years. We had grilled together on the Fourth of July. Our kids played together. He had been one of the first people to welcome me when I came to this church. He was also embezzling from his employer — a fact I had learned not from gossip but from his wife, who had found the evidence and was terrified.

I had spent three days preparing. Not the theology. I knew the theology. Matthew 18. Galatians 6:1. The goal is restoration. The posture is humility. I had preached on all of it. But knowing the theology and sitting across from your friend with a folder of evidence are two entirely different experiences.

The theology does not prepare you for the silence after you say the words. The theology does not prepare you for the way a grown man’s face changes when he realizes you know. The theology does not prepare you for the twenty seconds — they feel like twenty minutes — when you wait to find out whether this conversation will end in repentance or rage.

This chapter is about what happens in those twenty seconds. And in the weeks and months before and after them.

**Before the conversation: prepare like a surgeon, not a prosecutor.**

A surgeon does not walk into the operating room and improvise. They review the imaging. They study the anatomy. They plan the incision. They anticipate complications. And they know, before they make the first cut, exactly what outcome they are aiming for.

The first question is not “What did they do?“ It is “What is the goal?“ If the goal is punishment, do not have the conversation. You will do more harm than good. If the goal is venting your frustration, do not have the conversation. Call a counselor and vent there. If the goal is collecting ammunition for a future confrontation, do not have the conversation. That is manipulation, not discipline.

The only legitimate goal is the one Jesus specified: winning them over. Restoration. If you cannot honestly say, sitting in your truck with your hands on the steering wheel, that you want this person to be healed more than you want them to be punished — wait. Pray. Process your anger. Talk to a wise counselor. Come back when your motives are clean enough to bear the weight of what you are about to do.

The second preparation is evidence. Not hearsay. Not suspicion. Not a feeling. Evidence. Something you have seen. Something you have been told by a direct witness. Something documented. Confronting someone about sin based on rumor is gossip with a Bible verse attached. It is one of the most destructive things a church member or leader can do.

The third preparation is prayer. Not the perfunctory kind. The agonizing kind. The kind where you ask God to search your own heart before you attempt to search someone else’s. The kind where you confess your own susceptibility to the same sin you are about to name. The kind where you ask the Holy Spirit to be present in the conversation because you know that without divine intervention, two sinners talking about sin will produce nothing but defensiveness and shame.

The fourth preparation is counsel. Before you confront someone about serious sin, talk to a mature believer — your pastor, an elder, a trusted mentor — about what you have seen and what you plan to do. Not to gossip. To get perspective. Because your perception of the situation may be incomplete, and a second pair of eyes can see what you cannot.

**During the conversation: hold truth and grace in the same hand.**

Go in person. Not by text. Not by email. Not by phone unless geographic distance makes it impossible. The body language of love — eye contact, open posture, presence — communicates things that words alone cannot. The person needs to see your face. They need to see that you are not enjoying this. They need to see that you are there because you care about them, not because you want to win.

Open with relationship, not accusation. “I care about you. I am here because I care about you. What I am about to say is hard for me to say, and it will be hard for you to hear. But I would rather have this conversation than watch you destroy yourself in silence.“

Name the sin clearly. This is where most confrontations fail. The confronter, out of a desire to soften the blow, becomes vague. “I am concerned about some choices you have been making.“ “I have noticed some things that worry me.“ “I want to talk about where you are spiritually.“

Vagueness kills the conversation before it starts. The person does not know what you are talking about. They cannot repent of something they do not understand. And the ambiguity gives them room to deflect, rationalize, or change the subject.

Be specific. “I know you have been taking money from your employer’s accounts. Your wife showed me the documentation. I know this has been going on for at least eight months.“

The specificity is an act of respect. It says: I am not here to play games. I am not here to fish for a confession. I know what is happening, and I care about you enough to say it out loud.

Then listen. Close your mouth and listen. The person may deny it. They may explain it. They may confess it. They may weep. They may rage. They may go silent. Whatever happens in the first thirty seconds after you speak, do not fill the silence. Let them process. Let the Holy Spirit work. Your job in that moment is not to convince. It is to be present.

If they deny: do not argue. Present the evidence. Calmly. Without emotion. Then say, “I am not your judge. I am your friend. I have brought this to you because I believe it is true, and I believe it is destroying you. I am asking you to be honest with me.“

If they deflect: do not chase the deflection. Bring the conversation back to the specific behavior. “I understand that you feel justified in what you are doing. But taking money that is not yours is theft. That is what the Bible calls it. And I love you too much to call it something else.“

If they confess: do not fix it in the first conversation. Confessing is not the same as repenting. Repentance involves a change of direction, and that change takes time. Say, “Thank you for being honest. That took courage. Here is what I think needs to happen next.“ Then lay out the next steps — restitution, counseling, accountability, confession to the appropriate parties.

If they rage: do not escalate. Anger is often the first stop on the road to repentance, even when it does not feel like it. Say, “I understand that you are angry. I would be angry too. I am not going anywhere. When you are ready to talk about this, I will be here.“

**After the conversation: stay in the room.**

The confrontation is not the end. It is the beginning. Most church discipline fails not because the initial conversation was bungled but because there was no follow-through. The confrontation happens. The person reacts. And the confronter — relieved that the hard part is over — goes home and hopes for the best.

That is not discipline. That is hit-and-run.

If the person responds with repentance, the follow-through looks like accountability. Regular check-ins. Specific questions about specific behaviors. Not surveillance — partnership. The person who has confessed needs a companion on the road back, not a parole officer. They need someone who will ask “how are you doing?“ and actually want to hear the answer.

The accountability structure should be agreed upon together. “I would like to meet with you every two weeks for the next six months. I am going to ask you direct questions about your finances. I am going to ask you about your relationship with your wife. I am going to ask you about your emotional health. And you can ask me the same questions. This is not a one-way street.“

If the person responds with denial or defiance, the follow-through looks like escalation — the second step of Matthew 18. “I am bringing one or two others into this conversation. Not because I want to gang up on you. Because I love you too much to let this go, and I need the wisdom of others to walk this road.“

The witnesses should be chosen carefully. Not the person’s enemies. Not people with an agenda. People who are known for wisdom, discretion, and genuine love for the person being confronted. People who can speak truth without cruelty and hear pain without flinching.

The witnessed conversation follows the same pattern as the private one — relationship first, specific naming of the sin, invitation to repentance, and a clear articulation of what happens next if repentance does not occur.

**When it reaches the church: handle the congregation like the body it is.**

If the first two steps fail and the matter comes before the congregation, the stakes are higher and the margin for error is narrower.

The pastor or lead elder should address the congregation with the same combination of truth and grace that characterized the private conversations. The tone should be sorrow, not triumph. The information should be sufficient for understanding but not gratuitous. The congregation should know the nature of the offense, the steps that were taken prior to this point, the person’s response, and what is being asked of the congregation now.

What is being asked is significant. The congregation is being asked to participate in something costly. They are being asked to grieve the sin of someone they love. They are being asked to uphold the community’s standards without becoming self-righteous in the process. They are being asked to commit to praying for the person’s repentance and to welcoming them back when restoration is real.

This is not a vote to destroy someone. It is a communal acknowledgment of reality. This person is not living as a member of the covenant community. We have done everything we can to call them back. They have refused. We now entrust them to God and commit to continuing love from outside the fellowship, the way Jesus loved the tax collectors and sinners who had not yet responded to his call.

The congregation should also be told what not to do. Do not gossip about this person or their family. Do not contact them to shame them. Do not use this information as social currency. If you encounter them in the community, treat them with kindness and dignity. They are still made in the image of God. They are still someone Christ died for. Their sin does not erase their humanity.

**What I learned in my truck.**

The man I confronted that day in the parking lot denied it for twelve minutes. Then he cried for forty. Then he asked me what he needed to do.

He repaid the money over the following eighteen months. He told his employer, who chose not to press charges. He entered counseling with his wife. He met with me every other Thursday morning at a diner for a year. He ordered the same thing every time — eggs over easy, wheat toast, black coffee. He told me things about his childhood, his father’s gambling addiction, and his terror of financial failure that helped me understand how a good man ends up stealing from people who trusted him.

He is still in the church. He serves on the finance team now — not because we are naive, but because restoration means the person becomes who God designed them to be. And sometimes the area of your greatest failure becomes the area of your greatest contribution, because you understand the temptation from the inside and you can see it coming for someone else before anyone else can.

That is what discipline looks like when it works. Not clean. Not painless. Not efficient. Costly, slow, messy, relational, and in the end redemptive.

Nobody wants to have that conversation. But the people who are willing to have it are the people who save churches, marriages, careers, and souls.

**Chapter 9: Restoration Is the Goal — What Coming Home Actually Looks Like**

The most overlooked verse in the entire discipline conversation is 2 Corinthians 2:7.

“Now instead, you ought to forgive and comfort him, so that he will not be overwhelmed by excessive sorrow.“

The man Paul had ordered the Corinthian church to expel — the man sleeping with his father’s wife, the man handed over to Satan for the destruction of the flesh — had repented. And Paul’s instruction to the church is not “Good, now make sure he never forgets what he did.“ It is not “Let him back in slowly and keep a close eye on him.“ It is “Forgive him. Comfort him. Reaffirm your love for him. Now.“

The urgency in Paul’s language is striking. He is worried. Not about the man’s sin — that has been dealt with. He is worried about the church’s response to the man’s repentance. He is worried that the community that finally found the courage to confront will become the community that cannot find the grace to restore. That they will get addicted to the power of exclusion and resist the vulnerability of welcome.

Paul knows something about human nature that every church must learn: it is easier to confront than to restore. Confrontation requires courage. Restoration requires humility. Confrontation puts you in a position of moral authority. Restoration puts you in a position of risk — because the person you are welcoming back might fail again, and if they do, you will be the one who vouched for them.

The church that can confront but cannot restore has only done half the work. And the half it did may end up causing more damage than if it had done nothing at all.

**What restoration is not.**

Restoration is not pretending the sin did not happen. The sin happened. It caused damage. People were hurt. Relationships were broken. Trust was violated. Restoration does not erase any of that. What it does is create a path forward through the wreckage rather than around it.

The impulse to “just move on“ — to treat restoration as a return to the status quo — is one of the most destructive tendencies in church culture. It protects everyone from the discomfort of honesty and ensures that the underlying issues are never addressed. The affair happened? Let us not talk about it. The embezzlement was repaid? Let us pretend it never occurred. The abusive behavior stopped? Let us act like the people who were abused should just be grateful it is over.

That is not restoration. That is amnesia. And amnesia is not a fruit of the Spirit.

Restoration is also not a transaction. It is not: you sinned, you repented, here is your old position back. The person who was the worship leader before they fell does not automatically return to the worship team. The person who was the treasurer before they stole does not automatically regain access to the church accounts. Restoration of fellowship — being welcomed back into the community as a member in good standing — is distinct from restoration of position. The first happens when repentance is genuine. The second happens when trust is rebuilt. And trust is rebuilt over time through demonstrated faithfulness, not through a single conversation.

Paul does not say “give him back his old job.“ He says “reaffirm your love for him.“ The love is immediate. The position, if it comes back at all, is earned over time. Churches that confuse these two things either rush people back into leadership too quickly — setting them up for a second and more devastating failure — or use the withholding of position as ongoing punishment, which Paul explicitly warns against.

Restoration is also not cheap. The temptation, especially in a culture that prizes efficiency, is to shortcut the process. The person repents on Sunday. They are restored on Tuesday. Everyone is relieved. And nobody does the slow, costly work of examining what went wrong, what systems failed, what relational patterns contributed to the fall, and what safeguards need to be in place going forward.

That shortcut produces recidivism. The person falls again, because the conditions that produced the first failure were never addressed. The church is shocked — they thought restoration was complete. But restoration that does not address root causes is cosmetic. And cosmetic restoration does not heal anything.

**What restoration looks like.**

I have walked through the restoration process with seven people during my time in pastoral ministry. Each situation was different. Each person was different. But the elements that made restoration real — not performed, not rushed, not superficial, but genuinely transformative — were consistent.

The first element is genuine repentance. Not “I am sorry I got caught.“ Not “I am sorry you were hurt.“ Not the kind of apology that shifts blame from the offender to the offended. Genuine repentance means the person takes full responsibility for their actions. They name what they did without minimizing it. They acknowledge the damage it caused without deflecting to others’ failures. They demonstrate, through their demeanor and their actions, that something has changed inside them — not just their behavior, but their heart.

The Greek word for repentance is metanoia — a change of mind, a reorientation of the whole person. It is not a feeling. It is a direction. The person who has genuinely repented is walking in a different direction than they were walking before. Not perfectly. Not without stumbling. But the trajectory has changed.

How do you know the difference between genuine repentance and performance? Time. Genuine repentance shows up in behavior over weeks and months, not in a single tearful confession. It shows up in the willingness to submit to accountability without resentment. It shows up in the willingness to make restitution without being forced. It shows up in the willingness to do the hard work of counseling, confession, and behavioral change without complaint.

Performance shows up in tears at the altar and unchanged behavior on Tuesday. Performance produces dramatic confessions that are never followed by structural change. Performance says all the right words and resists all the right actions.

The church must be patient enough to tell the difference. And patience, in a culture addicted to quick resolution, is one of the hardest disciplines of all.

The second element is accountability. The restored person needs a small group of people — two or three, not the entire congregation — who are committed to walking with them through the rebuilding process. These accountability partners should be people the restored person trusts, respects, and has given permission to ask hard questions.

The accountability should be specific, not general. “How are you doing spiritually?“ is not accountability. “Have you accessed pornography this week?“ is accountability. “Are you feeling tempted?“ is not specific enough. “Have you been alone with anyone other than your wife in circumstances that could compromise your integrity?“ is specific.

The specificity is not invasive. It is loving. The person who has fallen needs the structure that specificity provides, because the structures they built for themselves failed. They need external scaffolding while they rebuild internal integrity. The accountability partners provide that scaffolding.

The third element is restitution. Where the sin caused material damage, the restored person must make it right. Money stolen must be repaid. Lies told must be corrected. Reputations damaged must be restored insofar as it is possible. Relationships broken must be addressed — which does not mean forced reconciliation, because the offended party is not obligated to reconcile on the offender’s timeline, but it does mean the offender takes responsibility for initiating whatever healing the offended party is willing to accept.

Zacchaeus understood this. When Jesus came to his house, Zacchaeus did not just apologize for years of financial exploitation. He committed to paying back four times what he had stolen (Luke 19:8). The generosity of his restitution reflected the genuineness of his repentance. He was not trying to buy forgiveness. He was demonstrating that his encounter with Jesus had completely reordered his relationship with money, power, and the people he had harmed.

Restitution is the outward evidence of inward change. When the church skips it — when the person is “restored“ without making right what they made wrong — the community’s trust remains unrepaired and the restored person carries unresolved guilt that will eventually surface in new destructive patterns.

The fourth element is time. There is no formula. No twelve-week program that produces certified restoration. The timeline depends on the severity of the offense, the depth of the damage, the genuineness of the repentance, and the health of the community that is receiving the person back.

Some restorations take six months. Some take two years. Some take longer. The church must resist the urge to set an arbitrary deadline and stick to it regardless of the person’s actual progress. Restoration is not a bureaucratic process with mandated timeframes. It is a relational process that follows the rhythm of genuine healing.

The fifth element is community reintegration. This is the step that Paul is most concerned about in 2 Corinthians 2, and it is the step that most churches get wrong.

When the time comes for the restored person to re-enter full fellowship, the community must make a deliberate, public, and unambiguous commitment to welcome them. Not grudgingly. Not conditionally. Not with the subtext of “we are watching you.“ With the same seriousness and care that characterized the original discipline.

This might look like a public statement from the elders affirming that the restoration process is complete and inviting the congregation to embrace this person as a full member of the body. It might look like a small gathering where the restored person shares what they have learned and the community responds with prayer and commitment. It might look like the simple, quiet act of a few key people making a point to sit with the restored person at the next fellowship meal and treating them as if they belong — because they do.

The community’s welcome is not a reward for good behavior. It is the completion of the process that began with confrontation. Discipline that ends in exclusion is failure. Discipline that ends in restoration is the body working the way Christ designed it to work.

**The story Paul tells.**

Second Corinthians 2:5-11 is the only narrative we have in Scripture of a completed discipline-and-restoration cycle. And Paul’s tone is striking. He is not triumphant. He is relieved. He is worried — worried that the church will miss the moment and turn appropriate discipline into crushing punishment.

“I urge you, therefore, to reaffirm your love for him. Another reason I wrote you was to see if you would stand the test and be obedient in everything“ (2 Corinthians 2:8-9).

The test is not the discipline. The test is the restoration. The church had already proven it could confront. Now Paul wants to know if it can welcome. Because any community can exclude people — governments do it, social clubs do it, neighborhoods do it. But only the church, empowered by the Spirit of the God who pursues prodigals, can restore them.

And then Paul adds a warning that the church has consistently ignored: “I have forgiven in the sight of Christ for your sake, in order that Satan might not outwit us. For we are not unaware of his schemes“ (2 Corinthians 2:10-11).

Satan’s scheme is not just the original sin. Satan’s scheme is the church’s response to the sin. If he can get the church to refuse restoration, he wins twice — the person is destroyed by their sin, and the church is destroyed by its self-righteousness. The adversary does not care which direction the corruption runs. He just wants the body damaged. Permissiveness damages it. Mercilessness damages it. Both are his tools.

The church that restores well defeats both schemes. It confronts the sin — defeating permissiveness. And it welcomes the sinner home — defeating mercilessness. That double victory is what discipline looks like when it is practiced in the power of the Spirit rather than the flesh.

**Why this is so hard.**

I will not pretend it is not hard. Welcoming someone back after they have hurt you, lied to you, betrayed the community’s trust — that is among the most difficult things a Christian community is asked to do.

It requires the community to absorb the cost of someone else’s sin. The trust that was broken does not rebuild itself. The relationships that were damaged require effort to repair. The hurt that was caused does not disappear because the person who caused it says sorry. The community pays a price for restoration — in time, in emotional energy, in the risk of being hurt again.

That is what makes it look like the cross.

The cross is God absorbing the cost of human sin. The cross is the innocent party bearing the burden of the guilty party’s failure. The cross is love that pays the price for someone else’s rebellion and then invites them home.

When the church restores a repentant sinner, it participates in the pattern of the cross. Not as co-redeemers — only Christ redeems. But as people who have been so transformed by the grace they received that they are willing to extend it to others at cost to themselves.

That is the church at its best. That is the body functioning as designed. That is what the watching world has never seen from any other institution on earth — a community that takes sin seriously enough to confront it and takes grace seriously enough to restore the person who committed it.

That community is rare. It does not have to be.

**Chapter 10: When They Do Not Come Back**

Not every story ends with the prodigal walking through the door.

I have been a pastor long enough to know this in my bones. You follow the process. You confront with love. You bring witnesses. You bring it before the church. You pray. You wait. You keep the light on. And the person does not come back.

They do not repent. Or they do not repent in a way that involves the community. They disappear. They join another church without ever addressing what happened. They leave the faith entirely. They stay angry for years, convinced that you are the problem, that the church was wrong, that the confrontation was an attack rather than a rescue.

And you sit with the silence and wonder if you did it right.

This chapter is for the pastor sitting in that silence. The elder who followed the process and watched the person walk away. The friend who had the conversation and lost the friendship. The church that did the hard thing and paid the price.

Because the New Testament is honest about this outcome. Jesus told a story about a shepherd who left ninety-nine sheep to find one. But he also told a story about a son who left home and stayed gone for what seemed like forever. The text does not specify how long the father waited. It only says the father watched the road. Every day. For as long as it took.

Some stories end in restoration. Some stories end in waiting. The church must be prepared for both.

**Why they do not come back.**

The reasons are varied, and the church must resist the temptation to reduce them all to one explanation.

Sometimes the person does not come back because they are not ready to repent. The sin still has a grip on them. The cost of repentance — the confession, the restitution, the behavioral change, the humility of submitting to accountability — is more than they are willing to pay. Not because they do not understand the gospel. Because the idol they are serving is still more compelling to them than the freedom Christ offers.

This is the hardest thing to watch. A person you love choosing destruction with their eyes open. You have told them the truth. You have offered a path back. And they have looked at that path and decided the other direction is more attractive. There is nothing you can do to force that decision. You cannot repent for someone else. You cannot want their restoration more than they want it and make it happen through the force of your desire.

Sometimes the person does not come back because the discipline was done badly. The confrontation was harsh rather than loving. The witnesses were judgmental rather than compassionate. The process was punitive rather than restorative. The church communicated rejection rather than broken-hearted concern. And the person, already ashamed and defensive, experienced the discipline as confirmation that the church is everything the world says it is — hypocritical, cruel, and self-righteous.

This is where the church must look in the mirror. If the person left because the process was abusive, the failure is the church’s. If the person left because the confrontation was done in anger rather than love, the failure is the confronter’s. If the person left because the community made them feel that their sin placed them beyond the reach of grace, the community needs to repent before it can expect anyone else to.

I have been part of discipline processes that I look back on with satisfaction. And I have been part of processes that I look back on with grief — not because discipline was wrong, but because the way we did it was wrong. The theology was right and the execution was terrible. The doctrine was sound and the delivery was devastating. And the person who left carries scars that I helped create.

That admission costs something. It should.

Sometimes the person does not come back because they were never genuinely part of the community. Their attachment to the church was social rather than spiritual. They attended because of relationships, habit, or cultural expectation. When the community asked something of them — when it confronted their behavior and demanded a response — they discovered that their commitment to the community had a limit, and that limit was located exactly at the point of personal cost.

Jesus described this in the parable of the sower. The seed that fell on rocky soil sprang up quickly and withered when trouble came. The person looked like a thriving member of the community. But there was no root system. And when the heat of confrontation came, the plant died.

This is not the church’s failure. It is a revelation of something that was already true but invisible. The discipline process did not drive the person away. It revealed that they were never rooted in the first place. That revelation is painful, but it is honest. And honesty is always better than the alternative.

Sometimes the person does not come back because they find a church that will not confront them. This is epidemic in American Christianity. The church shopper who has been disciplined at one church just moves to another, presents a sanitized version of why they left, and begins the cycle again.

The lack of inter-church communication is one of the most significant structural failures in American church life. When a member under discipline leaves and joins another congregation, the receiving church almost never contacts the sending church to ask why the person left. Privacy concerns, legal fears, and a general reluctance to “get involved“ combine to create a system where people can escape accountability indefinitely by switching congregations.

Paul would have been horrified. His letters circulated among churches. His instructions assumed a level of inter-church connection and communication that the modern church has largely abandoned. When he told the Corinthians to expel the immoral brother, he was not imagining a world where that brother could drive to the next town and be welcomed as a member without anyone asking questions.

The solution is not a blacklist. It is not a database of disciplined members shared among churches. It is a culture shift — a recovery of the practice of giving and receiving church references when members transfer. A willingness to make and receive phone calls. A network of pastoral relationships where honest conversations about member transfers can happen. A commitment to the principle that a person leaving one church under discipline should not be received by another church without that church understanding the situation.

This will not happen quickly. It requires a level of pastoral trust and cooperation that the competitive, consumer-driven American church model actively undermines. But it is worth pursuing, because without it, discipline is a revolving door and accountability is fiction.

**What the church owes the person who does not return.**

Here is what the church does not owe them: pursuit to the point of harassment. There is a line between faithful shepherding and stalking, and the church must know where that line is. If the person has made it clear that they do not want contact, the church must respect that boundary. Not because the church stops caring, but because love does not force itself on the unwilling.

Here is what the church does owe them: an open door. The statement the church makes when someone is removed from fellowship should include, explicitly and publicly, the commitment that the door remains open. “If and when repentance occurs, we will welcome you back with the same love that compelled us to confront you.“

That is not a throwaway line. It is a promise. And it must be kept. Five years from now, if that person walks through the door with genuine repentance, the church cannot respond with suspicion or resentment. The church must respond the way the father in Luke 15 responded to the prodigal: running down the road, arms open, ring and robe ready, party being planned before the son finishes his prepared speech.

The church also owes the person prayer. Persistent, specific, ongoing prayer. Not the vague “Lord, be with them“ kind. The agonizing, “Lord, break through, bring them home, do whatever it takes“ kind. The kind of prayer that costs the person praying something — time, energy, sleep, emotional bandwidth.

And the church owes the person an honest reckoning with its own role. Was the discipline done well? Were there ways it could have been done better? Did the process reflect the character of Christ or the frustration of the leaders? Were there moments where grace was absent, where the community became more concerned with its own purity than with the person’s restoration? If so, what needs to change?

That self-examination is not optional. It is the Galatians 6:1 principle applied to the institutional level: “Watch yourselves, or you also may be tempted.“ The church that disciplines without self-examination will eventually become the church that disciplines badly. And the church that disciplines badly does damage that takes decades to repair.

**What the church owes itself.**

There is a grief that comes with unresolved discipline. The church mourns the person who did not come back. The friendships that were severed. The family that left. The empty seat.

The church must be given permission to grieve. Not to wallow. Not to second-guess every decision indefinitely. But to feel the loss and acknowledge it as real. The body has lost a member. That is worth mourning.

And the church must be encouraged to keep going. The temptation, after a discipline process that ends in loss rather than restoration, is to conclude that discipline is not worth the cost. The pain was too great. The outcome was too uncertain. Better to go back to silence and avoidance than to risk this again.

That temptation must be named and resisted. Discipline did not fail because the person did not come back. Discipline succeeded in telling the truth. It succeeded in protecting the body. It succeeded in offering a path to restoration, even if the path was not taken. The outcome belongs to God. The obedience belongs to the church. And obedience is not measured by outcomes. It is measured by faithfulness.

The farmer who plants seed in good soil has done the right thing even if the rain does not come. The doctor who prescribes the right treatment has done the right thing even if the patient refuses to take the medication. The church that practices discipline faithfully has done the right thing even if the person does not repent.

That is cold comfort when you are standing at the window, watching the road, hoping to see a familiar figure on the horizon.

But it is true comfort. And in a world where the church is tempted to measure everything by results, it is the only comfort that holds.

Keep the light on. Watch the road. And trust the God who pursues prodigals with a persistence that puts every human shepherd to shame.

**Chapter 11: The Discipline the Church Owes Itself — Corporate Repentance and Institutional Sin**

In 1995, the Southern Baptist Convention passed a resolution apologizing to African Americans for the denomination’s role in supporting slavery and for the subsequent century of institutional racism. The resolution was forty years late by the most generous calculation. A hundred and fifty years late by an honest one.

The SBC was founded in 1845 specifically to protect the right of slaveholding missionaries to serve under denominational appointment. That is not a disputed historical claim. It is the denomination’s founding charter. The convention existed because a group of Southern Baptists believed that owning human beings was compatible with preaching the gospel, and they were unwilling to be told otherwise by their Northern brethren.

For a hundred and fifty years after its founding, the SBC lived with that original sin woven into its institutional DNA. It did not cause slavery — slavery predated the denomination by centuries. But it baptized slavery. It provided theological justification for it. It read the Bible through a lens that confirmed what the culture already believed and called that reading “faithfulness to Scripture.“ And when slavery ended, the denomination transferred the same lens to Jim Crow, segregation, and the defense of racial hierarchy.

The 1995 resolution was an act of corporate repentance. It was late, incomplete, and insufficiently followed by structural change. But it was something. It was the institution acknowledging, publicly and formally, that it had sinned — not as individuals who happened to be Southern Baptists, but as an institution whose founding and ongoing culture had participated in one of the great moral catastrophes of human history.

That kind of repentance is what this chapter is about.

Church discipline, as most people understand it, is aimed at individuals. A member sins. The church confronts. The member repents or is excluded. The process is personal.

But what happens when the sin is institutional? What happens when the church itself — not a member, but the church as a body — has sinned? When its policies have caused harm? When its culture has enabled abuse? When its theology has been weaponized to justify oppression? When its silence has made it complicit in injustice?

Who disciplines the church?

**Institutional sin is real.**

The concept of corporate or institutional sin has deep biblical roots, even though the individualistic theology dominant in American evangelicalism tends to ignore them.

When the prophets spoke, they rarely addressed individuals. They addressed nations. Amos did not say “There is one merchant in the northern kingdom who cheats the poor.“ He said “You trample on the poor and force him to give you grain“ (Amos 5:11). The sin was systemic. The marketplace was structured to exploit the vulnerable. Individual merchants may have been more or less culpable, but the system itself was sinful, and the system required corporate repentance.

When Daniel prayed in Daniel 9, he confessed sins he had not personally committed. “We have sinned and done wrong. We have been wicked and have rebelled“ (Daniel 9:5). Daniel was not guilty of the idolatry that sent Israel into exile. But he identified with the community’s sin because he understood that he was part of a body, and the body’s sin was his sin in a real and meaningful sense.

When Nehemiah heard about the state of Jerusalem’s walls, he wept and fasted and confessed: “I confess the sins we Israelites, including myself and my father’s family, have committed against you“ (Nehemiah 1:6). Nehemiah had not torn down the walls. He had not been part of the disobedience that led to the exile. But he understood corporate identity — that the sins of the community were his to confess because the community was his.

The American evangelical church, shaped by a culture of extreme individualism, has largely lost this understanding. We treat sin as exclusively personal. My sin is my problem. Your sin is your problem. The church’s sin is... nobody’s problem? Or everybody’s problem in theory but nobody’s responsibility in practice?

This blind spot has produced churches that can discipline an individual member for adultery but cannot confront their own institutional culture of racial exclusion. Churches that can confront a deacon for lying but cannot acknowledge that their entire governance structure is built to protect power rather than serve the vulnerable. Churches that can address personal immorality but cannot address systemic injustice — because they have no category for institutional sin and therefore no mechanism for institutional repentance.

**What institutional sin looks like in local churches.**

It is not always as dramatic as the SBC’s complicity in slavery. More often, it is quieter. Slower. The kind of sin that becomes visible only when you step back far enough to see the pattern.

It looks like a church culture where women’s concerns are systematically dismissed. Not because any individual leader is a misogynist, but because the decision-making structures, the communication patterns, and the unwritten rules of the community have developed over decades in ways that marginalize women’s voices. No single decision is egregiously wrong. But the cumulative effect is a community where half the body has been functionally silenced.

It looks like a church that claims to welcome all races but whose leadership, programming, worship style, and communication are designed exclusively for one demographic. Not because anyone sat in a meeting and said “Let us exclude people of other races.“ But because the church was built by one group, for one group, and the systems it created reflect the assumptions, preferences, and blind spots of that group. The exclusion is structural, not deliberate. But the effect is the same.

It looks like a church that has protected predators for decades — not through a conspiracy but through a culture of deference to authority, a theological system that makes questioning leaders feel like questioning God, and a conflict-avoidance norm that treats all confrontation as divisive. No individual in the church is pro-abuse. But the system the church has built creates the conditions under which abuse thrives.

It looks like a church whose financial practices are opaque, whose decision-making is concentrated in the hands of a few, and whose response to questions is “Trust the leadership.“ Not because the leaders are necessarily corrupt, but because the structures were never designed for transparency, and the culture punishes inquiry.

Each of these is institutional sin. The sin is embedded in the system, not just in the people operating it. Replacing the people without changing the system will produce the same outcomes with different names on the doors.

**How institutions repent.**

Individual repentance follows the metanoia pattern — acknowledgment of sin, change of direction, restitution where possible. Institutional repentance follows the same pattern, but the scale is different and the process is more complex.

The first step is acknowledgment. The institution must name what it did or what it allowed. Not in vague, aspirational language. Not “We recognize that we have not always lived up to our ideals.“ That is a press release, not a confession. In specific language that names the specific harm.

“Our church created a governance structure that concentrated authority in one person and eliminated the checks that would have prevented the abuse that occurred. We are responsible for that structure. The people who were harmed under that structure were harmed by our institutional choices.“

“Our church has practiced racial exclusion — not through explicit policy, but through cultural norms, worship practices, and leadership selection patterns that communicated to people of color that they were welcome as visitors but not as leaders. That communication was sinful, and we repent of it.“

“Our denomination was founded to protect the institution of slavery. We have not adequately reckoned with that founding sin or its ongoing effects on our institutional culture, our theological assumptions, or our relationship with the communities we harmed.“

The specificity matters. Vague confession is cheap grace at the institutional level. It costs nothing and changes nothing. Specific confession is costly because it identifies what must change.

The second step is structural change. Confession without structural reform is theater. If the institution acknowledges that its governance structure enabled abuse, it must change the governance structure. If it acknowledges that its culture marginalized women, it must change the policies, practices, and norms that produced the marginalization. If it acknowledges that its financial practices lacked transparency, it must implement transparency.

The structural change must be concrete, measurable, and accountable. Not “We commit to doing better.“ But “We are implementing these specific changes to our bylaws, our policies, our leadership selection process, and our financial reporting. Here is the timeline. Here is who is responsible. Here is how you will know whether we followed through.“

The third step is restitution. Where the institutional sin caused material harm, the institution must make it right. This is the step that churches resist most fiercely, because institutional restitution is expensive, legally complicated, and publicly embarrassing.

But the principle is the same as individual restitution. Zacchaeus did not just apologize to the people he defrauded. He paid them back four times over. An institution that acknowledges it enabled abuse must provide resources for the survivors’ healing. A denomination that acknowledges it participated in racial oppression must invest in the communities it harmed. A church that acknowledges it misused funds must repay them.

This is not popular. It is not easy. But it is what repentance looks like when the repentance is real.

The fourth step is ongoing accountability. Institutional sin tends to recur because institutions are conservative by nature — they revert to established patterns. The church that repents of its institutional failures must build accountability mechanisms that prevent the same failures from developing again. Independent oversight. Regular audits. External review. A culture that welcomes criticism rather than punishing it.

**Why this matters for individual discipline.**

A church that practices individual discipline without examining its own institutional sins is a hypocrite. The individual member being confronted for their personal failure can look at the institution and see institutional failures that are never confronted, never confessed, never changed. The double standard is obvious, and it destroys the moral authority that discipline requires.

Jesus addressed this directly. “Why do you look at the speck of sawdust in your brother’s eye and pay no attention to the plank in your own eye?“ (Matthew 7:3). The usual application is personal — examine your own sin before confronting someone else’s. But the principle applies to institutions as well. The church that demands individual repentance while refusing institutional repentance has a plank in its eye. And that plank blinds it to the very thing it claims to see clearly in others.

The church that disciplines well begins with self-discipline. It examines its own structures, policies, and culture with the same rigor it applies to individual behavior. It confesses its own failures with the same specificity it demands of its members. It makes restitution for its own sins with the same urgency it expects from others.

That church has the moral authority to confront individual sin — because it has demonstrated that it takes sin seriously everywhere, including in its own house. Especially in its own house.

**Chapter 12: Building a Church That Can Bear the Truth**

The church I serve is not perfect. I need to say that before I say anything else in this chapter, because the temptation when writing about how churches should function is to position yourself as the expert who has figured it out. I have not figured it out. I lead a congregation of three hundred people in Fenton, Michigan, and we are still learning how to do this. What I can offer is not a success story. It is a set of convictions forged in the furnace of actually trying — and failing, and trying again — to build a community where truth can be told and people can survive the telling.

That is what this chapter is about. Not a model. A set of principles. Because every church is different — different size, different culture, different governance, different history — and the church that copies another church’s discipline process without adapting it to its own context will produce the same distortions that copying any template without understanding produces.

But the principles are transferable. They are rooted in Scripture, tested in practice, and applicable across every context I have encountered in two decades of pastoral ministry.

**Principle 1: Build the culture before you need the process.**

Church discipline does not start when someone sins. It starts years earlier, in the culture the church builds long before a crisis arrives.

A church that has never talked about sin, accountability, or mutual responsibility will not suddenly develop the capacity to confront when confrontation is needed. A church that has never practiced honest conversation at the small group level will not be equipped for honest conversation at the congregational level. A church that has taught its members to be consumers — to attend, receive, and leave without ever being known — cannot suddenly ask those members to be a community that knows each other deeply enough to notice when something is wrong.

The culture must come first. And culture is built in the small things. In the sermon series that addresses sin and repentance with specificity and self-implication rather than vague generalities. In the small group curriculum that includes real questions about real struggles rather than sanitized discussions of abstract theology. In the membership process that explains, clearly and unapologably, what the church expects of its members and what its members can expect of the church.

At our church, the membership class includes a section on discipline. We tell every prospective member, before they join, that this is a community that practices biblical discipline. We explain what that means. We explain the Matthew 18 process. We explain the goal — restoration, not punishment. We explain the safeguards. And we tell them that by joining, they are consenting to be part of this process — both as people who might be confronted and as people who are responsible for confronting.

Some people hear that and choose not to join. That is fine. We would rather have a smaller church that understands its commitments than a larger church that has never been told what membership means.

**Principle 2: Define the boundaries before you need to enforce them.**

Every church should have a written discipline policy. Not a novel. A clear, concise document that answers the basic questions: What kinds of behavior can result in discipline? Who initiates the process? What steps are followed? What safeguards exist to prevent abuse? What does restoration look like? Who has the authority to make decisions at each stage?

The document should be developed collaboratively — not by the senior pastor alone, but by the elder body, the deacons, and ideally with input from the congregation. It should be reviewed by legal counsel to ensure compliance with civil law. It should be available to every member. And it should be reviewed and updated regularly.

The purpose of the document is not to create a legalistic rulebook. It is to create clarity. When a discipline situation arises — and it will arise — the church needs to know what to do before emotions take over. The document provides a path. It prevents improvisation. It ensures consistency. And it protects both the person being disciplined and the people doing the disciplining from the chaos that occurs when nobody knows what the process is supposed to look like.

The boundaries should be specific enough to be useful and flexible enough to accommodate the complexity of real human situations. I have seen discipline policies that list every conceivable sin alongside its prescribed consequence, like a medieval penitential manual. That is legalism on paper. And I have seen discipline policies that say nothing more than “The elders will handle discipline on a case-by-case basis.“ That is an invitation to abuse.

The sweet spot is a policy that identifies categories of behavior that may result in discipline — sexual sin, financial dishonesty, divisive behavior, doctrinal deviation that endangers the community, persistent patterns of harm — while acknowledging that the specifics of each situation will determine the specifics of the response.

**Principle 3: Train people for the conversations they will need to have.**

The average church member has never been taught how to confront someone about sin. They have been taught to be nice. They have been taught to avoid conflict. They have been taught that if something bothers them, they should pray about it and trust God to handle it. These are all terrible preparation for the moment when a fellow believer’s life is falling apart and someone needs to say something.

Churches should invest in training their members for confrontation the way they invest in training their members for evangelism, hospitality, or small group leadership. This training should include practical skills — how to initiate a hard conversation, how to listen without becoming defensive, how to speak truth without being cruel — and theological grounding — why confrontation is an act of love, what the Bible says about the community’s responsibility for its members, how to maintain humility while speaking directly.

Role-playing is more valuable than lecture. Give people scenarios and let them practice. “Your close friend in the church confides that she is having an emotional affair. What do you say? How do you say it? What do you do if she becomes angry? What do you do next?“ That kind of practice builds the muscle memory that theory alone cannot provide.

The elders and deacons should receive additional training in the procedural aspects of discipline — how to document conversations, how to involve witnesses, how to communicate with the congregation, how to manage the legal dimensions, how to care for victims when the sin involves abuse or exploitation.

This training should be ongoing, not a one-time event. Because the people who lead discipline processes rotate, and the situations they encounter are always more complex than the training anticipated.

**Principle 4: Protect the vulnerable at every stage.**

Every discipline process should begin by asking: who is vulnerable here? And the answer should shape the process from start to finish.

If the sin involves abuse — domestic violence, sexual abuse, child abuse — the first priority is not the offender’s repentance. It is the victim’s safety. This is where many churches go catastrophically wrong. They apply the Matthew 18 process to abuse situations as if abuse were just a sin like any other, to be addressed through private conversation and gradual escalation. But abuse is not like any other sin. It involves a power dynamic that makes the Matthew 18 process dangerous for the victim.

The private conversation between accuser and accused, which is the first step of Matthew 18, presupposes a relationship of relative equality. Two members of the body, roughly equal in standing, addressing a wrong between them. But when the sin is abuse, the relationship is defined by power imbalance. The abuser has power over the victim — physical, emotional, financial, spiritual. Sending the victim to confront the abuser privately is not biblical faithfulness. It is dangerous incompetence.

When abuse is involved, the church’s first action must be to ensure the victim’s safety. Contact law enforcement if the abuse is criminal. Provide the victim with resources — shelter, counseling, legal assistance. Do not require the victim to participate in the discipline process against their will. Do not mediate between abuser and victim as if the situation were a conflict between equals. It is not a conflict. It is a crime.

The discipline of the abuser can and should proceed. But it proceeds from a posture of victim protection, not offender management. The church’s first loyalty is to the person who was harmed, not the person who caused the harm. When those loyalties conflict — and they will — the church must choose the vulnerable every time.

**Principle 5: Hold leaders to a higher standard, not a lower one.**

First Timothy 5:19-20 establishes the principle: accusations against elders require a higher evidentiary standard (two or three witnesses), but confirmed sin in elders receives a more severe response (public rebuke “so that the others may take warning“).

A church that disciplines members but protects leaders is practicing the opposite of what Paul described. The higher standard is for the evidence, not the consequence. The leader who sins has more to answer for, not less. Because their position amplifies both the impact of their sin and the message their sin sends to the community.

When a leader falls, the discipline must be proportional to their responsibility. This does not mean cruel. It means serious. It means the leader steps down from their position immediately. It means the congregation is informed. It means the restoration process, if restoration occurs, is longer and more rigorous than it would be for a member without leadership responsibility. It means the leader does not return to the same level of authority without an extended period of demonstrated trustworthiness.

And it means the systems that allowed the failure to occur are examined and changed. A leader does not fall in a vacuum. They fall within a structure that either held them accountable or did not. If the structure failed, the structure must change. Otherwise the next leader will fall in the same way, in the same place, for the same reasons.

**Principle 6: Practice what you preach about grace.**

The church that practices discipline must also be the most grace-saturated community in the city. If the reputation of the church is “those are the people who kick people out,“ the discipline has become an end rather than a means. If the primary message people hear is “you will be held accountable,“ without an equally loud message of “you will be loved fiercely,“ the community has become a courtroom rather than a hospital.

Grace is the air the church breathes. It is the foundation on which everything else is built. Without grace, discipline becomes legalism. Without grace, accountability becomes surveillance. Without grace, confrontation becomes attack. Without grace, the church becomes the very thing Jesus condemned in the Pharisees — a community that loads heavy burdens on people’s shoulders without lifting a finger to help them carry the weight (Matthew 23:4).

The church that disciplines well is the church where confession is safe. Where a member can walk into a small group and say “I am struggling with this“ and know that the response will be compassion, not judgment. Where the pastor can stand in the pulpit and admit his own failures without fear that the congregation will use the admission against him. Where weakness is met with strength and shame is met with the persistent, costly, unrelenting grace of God.

That church can discipline because it has built a reservoir of trust. The person being confronted knows — from years of experience, not from a mission statement — that this community loves them. That the confrontation comes from love. That the goal is their healing. That the arms that pointed out the sin will be the same arms that welcome them back.

Trust is not built in a crisis. It is built in a thousand ordinary moments of faithfulness that precede the crisis by years. The casserole delivered after surgery. The phone call after a hard week. The text message that says “I am thinking about you and praying for you.“ The elder who remembers your mother’s name and asks about her. The pastor who shows up at the hospital at two in the morning because you called and said you were scared.

That is the church that can bear the truth. Not because its members are braver than anyone else. Because they have been loved well enough to believe that the truth, even when it hurts, is coming from a place they can trust.

**The church I want to build.**

I want to build a church where sin can be named without the person being destroyed. Where repentance is met with tears of relief rather than ongoing suspicion. Where the congregation knows what they have committed to and rises to that commitment when it costs them something. Where leaders are held to a standard that matches the weight of their calling. Where the vulnerable are protected before the institution is preserved. Where the watching world looks at us and sees something they have never seen in any other human community — a group of people who take holiness seriously enough to confront and take grace seriously enough to restore.

That church is worth every hard conversation. Every sleepless night. Every relationship strained by the weight of truth. Every person who walks away angry and every person who walks back in tears.

I am not there yet. We are not there yet. But we are building. And the God who gave the church its immune system is the same God who heals the body when that immune system works.

That is enough to keep going.

**Conclusion: The Body That Heals**

I started this book with a man standing at the front of a church. Hands trembling. A decade of deacon service behind him. Two years of hidden sin exposed. A congregation that loved him enough to tell the truth. A pastor who said, “We are not removing David from fellowship because we are better than him.“

I told you he came back.

I did not tell you what it cost.

It cost David his reputation. For fourteen months, he was the man who had the affair. In a town of nine thousand people, that follows you. To the grocery store. To the gas station. To the school where your children are learning to spell. Every eye that lingered a second too long. Every conversation that went quiet when he walked in. He paid for his sin in the currency of social death.

It cost his wife a marriage she thought she knew. She had to decide, in real time, with real children watching, whether the covenant she had made was worth the wreckage. She chose to stay. Not because she was weak. Because she believed something about marriage that most people only say they believe — that the covenant holds when the feelings do not. That decision cost her sleep, dignity, friendships, and years of therapeutic work that nobody saw and nobody applauded.

It cost the church its comfort. The congregation had to sit in the discomfort of knowing that one of their own had fallen. They had to resist the gossip. They had to watch the man they used to trust walk away from the building they all loved and wonder if he would ever come back. They had to keep a seat open for fourteen months and pray that someone would fill it. Some of them were angry. Some of them left. Some of them questioned whether the whole process was necessary.

It cost the pastor a friendship. The relationship between the pastor and David was never the same. It eventually became something better — deeper, more honest, forged in fire rather than convenience — but the easy friendship of before was gone. You cannot tell someone the worst truth about themselves and then go back to casual. The relationship that survives that truth is stronger, but it carries a weight that lighter friendships do not.

That is what discipline costs. And the cost is the point.

The American church has been looking for a version of discipline that does not cost anything. A version where sin is addressed without anyone being uncomfortable. Where confrontation happens without anyone getting hurt. Where restoration occurs without anyone paying a price.

That version does not exist. It cannot exist. Because the disease is real, and real diseases require real treatment. Surgery hurts. Chemotherapy is devastating. Physical therapy is grinding, tedious, painful work. But the alternative to treatment is not comfort. The alternative to treatment is death.

The body that refuses to fight infection dies. That is biology. The church that refuses to address sin dies. That is theology. And the death is slower and less dramatic than the treatment, which is why so many churches choose it — you do not notice you are dying until the dying is done.

I have watched churches die this way. Not in a blaze of scandal. In a slow fade. The moral standards erode one concession at a time. The leadership grows increasingly disconnected from the lives of the congregation. The sermons become more general, less specific, less willing to name the things that need naming. The membership rolls grow while the depth of commitment shrinks. The church becomes a place where people go to feel something for an hour and then return to lives that are unchanged by the experience.

That is not the body of Christ. That is a corpse that has not been told it is dead.

But I have also watched churches live. Churches where the immune system works. Where sin is confronted with tears rather than triumph. Where the person being disciplined knows — because they have experienced it for years before the crisis — that this community would rather lose everything than lose them to destruction. Where restoration is not an afterthought but the driving purpose. Where the leaders are under the same standard as the congregation. Where the vulnerable are protected before the institution is preserved.

Those churches do not make headlines. They are not large, usually. They are not glamorous. They do not have marketing teams or production budgets or celebrity pastors. They have something better. They have the quiet, costly, unglamorous integrity of a community that practices what it preaches.

And the people who find those churches — the broken, the grieving, the skeptics who have been burned by religious institutions, the prodigals who left and are looking for a reason to come back — those people recognize what they have found. Not a perfect community. A honest one. A community where the gap between what is professed and what is practiced is small enough to bear. A community that takes sin seriously and takes grace seriously and holds both together without letting either one win at the other’s expense.

That is the church Jesus built. He built it on a rock and promised that the gates of hell would not prevail against it. He gave it an immune system — the capacity to confront, the authority to bind and loose, the promise of his presence in the process. He told it exactly how the system works. Step by step. Person to person. Private, then witnessed, then congregational, then consequential.

And he told it the goal. Not punishment. Not exclusion. Not institutional purity as an end in itself. The goal is a brother or sister won. A life restored. A body healed.

The church has everything it needs to do this. The instructions are clear. The authority has been given. The Spirit is present. What the church lacks is not competence or theology. What the church lacks is courage.

The courage to tell the truth when the truth is expensive. The courage to lose someone in order to love them. The courage to hold the standard when the standard is costly. The courage to welcome someone home when the welcoming requires absorbing the cost of their failure. The courage to examine the institution as rigorously as it examines the individual. The courage to repent as publicly as it preaches repentance.

That courage is not produced by willpower. It is produced by the Spirit of the God who confronted Israel’s sin through prophets who wept while they spoke. The God who sent his Son to tell the truth about the human condition and then die for the humans he diagnosed. The God who pursues prodigals down roads they chose against his counsel and waits at the end of the driveway for as long as it takes.

That God built the church. That God gave it the tools. That God is present in the process — in the confrontation, in the waiting, in the restoration, in the grief when restoration does not come.

The body that heals is the body that tells the truth. About itself. About its members. About the God it serves. About the world it was sent to reach.

Not the easy truth. The costly truth. The truth that names sin without destroying the sinner. The truth that offers grace without excusing the offense. The truth that holds together what the world insists must be held apart — holiness and mercy, justice and compassion, accountability and welcome.

That truth is the church’s gift to the world. Not its sermons. Not its programs. Not its buildings or budgets or attendance numbers. Its willingness to be honest about what is happening inside its own walls and to do something about it.

The man who stood at the front of that church with trembling hands is now sixty-one years old. He serves on the deacon board again. Not because the church forgot what happened. Because the church remembers what happened — the sin, the confrontation, the exclusion, the fourteen months of waiting, and the day he walked back through the door with nothing left to hide behind.

His restoration did not erase the scar. It redeemed it. The scar became the place where the bone set stronger than before. The place where the body healed. The place where the church proved that the gospel it preached was not decoration on the wall but architecture in the bones.

That is discipline. Not the easy version. The real one. The version that costs everyone something and gives everyone something they could not get any other way.

The church that practices it will not be the most popular church in town. It will not be the most comfortable. It will not be the church where you can hide.

It will be the church where you can heal.
