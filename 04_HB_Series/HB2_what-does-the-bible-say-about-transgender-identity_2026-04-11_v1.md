\# INTRODUCTION: THE QUESTION NOBODY PREPARED THE CHURCH TO ANSWER

\## Gender Identity, the Bible, and What We Owe People Made in God's Image

The email arrived on a Tuesday. A pastor I know. His twenty-three-year-old son. Gay. Living with another man. And now, identifying as a woman.

The pastor had no theology for this. Neither did his church. What he had instead was panic, denial, a few Bible verses deployed like grenades, and a son who stopped calling.

This is the story of American Christianity in 2026.

Gender identity became a dominant cultural question while the church was asleep. Not distracted. Asleep. We woke up already behind, already polarized, already forced to choose between two false options: reduce transgender people to a debate to be won, or abandon biblical clarity in the name of compassion. As if clarity and compassion cannot occupy the same room.

They can. The church just has not done the work.

What happened instead is predictable. Some churches went full dismissal. Mockery disguised as conviction. Using people's deepest confusion as punchlines. Refusing to learn names. Weaponizing Scripture. Making the gospel look like an ideology that serves the already-certain at the expense of the searching. These churches lost any credibility with the person actually struggling.

Other churches went full capitulation. Affirming everything. Asking no questions. Treating doubt about gender ideology as bigotry. Making the gospel sound like "whatever you think about yourself is true." Refusing to do theology because theology might create discomfort. These churches lost any credibility with people who actually want to think.

Both failures produce the same result: the person at the center of the question—the teenager confused about their body, the young adult in genuine distress, the parent torn between love and conviction—has nowhere to turn that feels safe.

The church should be the safest place for the most confused person.

We have not been.

This book exists because the church needs to do what it has refused to do: the theological work. Not the political work. Not the culture-war work. The theology. The hard, careful, sometimes uncomfortable work of asking what Scripture actually teaches about the body, about gender, about identity, about the image of God—and holding that alongside genuine pastoral concern for people in real pain.

This is not a book about whether transgender identity is a sin. The Bible does not speak directly to gender identity as we frame it now. It speaks to other things—the body, sexual difference, the created order, what it means to be made in God's image—and we have to do the theological work of thinking carefully about how those truths apply to people living in a complicated world.

This is a book about theological honesty and pastoral compassion occupying the same space. About what we owe people made in God's image. About what the church looks like when it refuses to reduce anyone to a debate.

Some people will read this and say I am too harsh on cultural positions they hold dear. Others will say I am not harsh enough. Both will be right. The book lives in the tension where most real pastoral work lives—between conviction and compassion, between clarity and care.

But here is what I know after two decades of pastoral ministry: the person in front of you always matters more than the position you think they represent. And the gospel is big enough to handle their questions, their pain, their confusion, without requiring you to either destroy them with truth or abandon truth to make room for kindness.

We have not believed this about gender. We have chosen sides. We have weaponized Scripture. We have made the question into a culture war rather than an opportunity to show what Christian love looks like when it refuses to go soft on either end.

This book rejects both failures. It does the theology. It holds the tension. It insists that biblical conviction and genuine love for confused people are not in competition—they are the same call.

Read it asking one question: What does the church owe the person standing in front of us who is genuinely confused about their body and their identity?

The answer will cost us something. Costly hope always does.

\# CHAPTER 1: MALE AND FEMALE HE CREATED THEM

\## What Genesis 1:27 Actually Teaches About Gender and the Image of God

The verse sits in every Bible the way a stone sits in a riverbed—so familiar that people step over it without looking. Genesis 1:27.

"So God created mankind in his own image, in the image of God he created them; male and female he created them."

For some, this verse settles the question immediately. Men are men. Women are women. Created that way. By God. End of discussion.

For others, the verse settles nothing. How can it? The verse does not address the person who experiences their sex assigned at birth as at its core misaligned with their sense of self. The verse does not address intersex conditions. The verse does not address the feeling of dysphoria. To use Genesis 1:27 as a dismissal of someone's lived experience is to pretend the verse is more specific than it actually is.

The truth is in the middle. Or rather, above both positions.

Genesis 1:27 teaches us something deep about gender. Not everything. Something deep. And if we read it with the care it deserves, we will find we have been thinking about gender at the wrong level all along.

\## The Text and Its Context

Let's start with what the verse actually says.

"So God created mankind in his own image, in the image of God he created them; male and female he created them."

The Hebrew word for "mankind" here is Adam—the generic term for humanity. The verse is not describing the first man. It is describing humanity as God created it. And it says God created humanity in two forms: male and female.

The word male is zakar. The word female is neqevah. These are biological designations. The text is not metaphorical here. When God creates the human, God creates it in sexual difference. One of the most fundamental facts about human existence, according to Genesis, is that we come in two kinds.

And before we talk about what this means, we need to understand what the verse is actually emphasizing.

The emphasis is not on the distinction between male and female. The emphasis is on the image of God.

Read it carefully. The verse repeats the image-of-God language three times in one sentence. "In his own image." "In the image of God." "Male and female he created them."

What is the image of God? Not the body. Not genitalia. The image of God is something else entirely. The image of God is representation. It is relationship. It is rule.

When Genesis says humans are made in the image of God, it means humans represent God. We reflect His character. We are His representatives in creation. Both male and female. The image of God is not gendered. Both male and female image God. Both carry the divine stamp. Both are responsible for how they live.

This is subversive. In ancient Near Eastern contexts, it was not obvious that both male and female would bear divine image equally. The text insists on it.

But here is what the text also insists on: sexual difference matters. God could have created humanity as genderless beings. God could have created one unified human form. Instead, God created humanity in sexual difference. And God called it good.

This raises an immediate question: If the image of God is genderless, why does God create gender at all?

\## The Gift of Sexual Difference

The answer is in the rest of Genesis.

In Genesis 1:27, God creates humanity male and female. In Genesis 1:28, God gives them a command: "Be fruitful and increase in number; fill the earth and subdue it."

Sexual difference is not a side effect of creation. It is built into the structure of what God wants humanity to do. God creates sexual difference and immediately connects it to the human vocation—to be fruitful, to multiply, to fill the earth, to tend creation.

Sexual difference, in the biblical account, is not a curse. It is not a necessary evil. It is a gift. It is the way God structured human life so that we would need each other, depend on each other, and participate together in the ongoing work of creation.

This is important. The Bible does not treat sexual difference as incidental to human identity. It treats it as fundamental. You do not just happen to be male or female. Your sexual difference shapes how you move through the world, how you relate to others, how you participate in God's plan.

But—and this is crucial—being male or female is not the same as what it means to be made in God's image. The image of God is what you share with every other human. Sexual difference is how you, as an image-bearer, live out that calling in a particular way.

This is the distinction we have lost. We have made gender into an identity marker that obscures the more fundamental fact: you are made in God's image. Everything else—male, female, the ways you express those realities—flows from that.

\## What Genesis 1:27 Does Not Say

This is where we need to be honest.

Genesis 1:27 does not say what happens when someone's biological sex and their internal sense of gender do not align. The verse acknowledges that sexual difference exists. It does not describe every possible variation of human experience. It does not address intersex conditions. It does not speak to gender dysphoria. It does not say whether someone who experiences deep distress about their biological sex is sinning, confused, traumatized, or some combination.

That does not make the verse irrelevant. It makes the verse insufficient all by itself to answer every question we face.

This is actually how theology works. You start with what Scripture says clearly, and then you think carefully about how those truths apply to situations Scripture does not directly address. You do not pretend the Bible says more than it does. You do not ignore what it does say. You think.

Genesis 1:27 says: sexual difference is real, created by God, and called good. It also says: both male and female image God equally.

Those truths are not small. But they are not exhaustive. They do not, by themselves, tell us how to love someone whose experience of gender is complicated, confused, or painful.

To use Genesis 1:27 as a conversation-ender is to avoid the real work. The real work is asking: if sexual difference is good and created by God, what does that mean for someone whose relationship to their sexual body is not good? If both male and female image God equally, does that settle what gender means? If the image of God is not gendered but bodies are, how do those two truths coexist?

These are the questions the church has failed to ask.

\## The Image of God Beyond Gender

Let's push deeper into what "image of God" actually means, because this is where Genesis 1:27 becomes useful for the harder conversations.

When the Bible says humans are made in the image of God, it is not talking about physical appearance. God does not have a body until the Incarnation. The image is something else.

The image of God means you are capable of relationship with God. You can know Him. You can hear Him. You can respond to Him. You bear responsibility for how you live. You are not a passive object in creation. You are an agent. A representative. Someone through whom God's character is expressed.

Both male and female bear this image. A woman is not made in the image of God because she is female. She is made in the image of God because she is human. Her femaleness is a particular expression of her humanity, not the foundation of her image-bearing.

This matters because it means the image of God is deeper than gender. And it means gender, while real and good, is not the ultimate category of human identity.

You are made in God's image. That is the ultimate fact about you. Everything else—male, female, your particular personality, your talents, your weaknesses, your confusion—everything else is downstream from that.

If you lose that, you get lost. You start believing that your identity is at its core about your gender, your sexuality, your body as experienced. And you forget the more basic truth: you are a representative of God on earth. That is your calling. That is your identity.

This is not to say gender does not matter. It is to say gender is not what in the end matters.

\## The Body Is Good, But the Body Is Not God

Here is where Genesis 1:27 intersects with a larger biblical claim: the body is good.

God does not create the body and then regret it. God does not create sexual difference and then treat it as shameful. The Bible is not Gnostic. The body is not a prison to escape. The body is part of God's good creation.

But the body is not God. The body does not determine everything about you. The body is the vehicle through which you live, move, relate, and participate in creation. The body matters. But the body is not the final word on who you are.

When someone says "I know my body is female, but I am male," they are making a claim that something internal to their identity supersedes something external to their body. The church has largely refused to engage with this claim carefully. Either the church says "Your body is the final word—accept it," or the church says "Your identity as you experience it is the final word—your body is incidental."

Both responses miss what Genesis 1:27 actually teaches. Your body is good. Your experience of your body matters. But your identity goes deeper than your body. Your identity is rooted in the image of God.

That distinction opens a different kind of pastoral conversation. Not "Your body is wrong" and not "Your body is irrelevant," but "Your body is real and good, and it is also not the ultimate word on who you are. You are made in God's image. That image is not dependent on feeling comfortable in your body. And your confusion about your body does not erase that you image God."

This is costly hope. It does not promise that your dysphoria will disappear if you accept your biological sex. It does not promise that your distress will vanish. It promises something harder: that you matter more than your suffering, that you image God beyond your gender experience, and that the church has a responsibility to help you think about your body and identity from a place deeper than ideology on either side.

\## The Pattern and the Exception

This is where we need to be careful about how we apply Genesis 1:27.

The verse describes a pattern: God creates humanity male and female. God creates sexual difference. Sexual difference is part of God's good order.

Patterns are real. They matter. When you see a pattern throughout Scripture, throughout nature, throughout human experience—male and female, designed to complement each other, designed to reproduce, designed as the fundamental structure of how humans live together—that pattern tells us something true about how God made the world.

But patterns have exceptions. All of them. The pattern of day and night is real. The sun rises and sets. But on the poles, the sun does not rise for months. That does not make the pattern false. It makes the exception real.

In the same way, the pattern of sexual difference—male and female—is real. Scripture emphasizes it. Biology emphasizes it. The structure of reproduction emphasizes it. But exceptions exist. Intersex conditions exist. People experience their gender in ways that do not fit the pattern.

Does the existence of exceptions invalidate the pattern? No. Does the pattern invalidate the exceptions? No.

This is where Genesis 1:27 becomes useful for the actual work of theology. The verse tells us the pattern is real and good. It does not tell us there are no exceptions. And it does not tell us what to do when someone experiences deep dysphoria about the body they have.

Those are questions you have to think through with the rest of Scripture, with pastoral care, with theological precision, and with genuine love for the person in front of you.

Genesis 1:27 is not a conversation-ender. It is a place to begin.

\## Application: What This Means for the Church

If Genesis 1:27 teaches us that sexual difference is good and that both male and female image God equally, what does that mean for how the church responds to someone identifying as transgender?

First, it means we have to stop reducing people to an ideology. Whether someone is affirming everything about a person's gender identity or denying the reality of their experience, the church has largely failed at seeing the person. We have made them a representative of a position rather than a human being made in God's image.

Second, it means we have to do deeper theological work than we have done. We cannot just cite Genesis 1:27 and consider the conversation over. We have to ask harder questions: What does it mean to embrace your biological sex when that sex feels foreign to you? What does it mean to be at peace in a body? How do we distinguish between spiritual growth and capitulation to suffering? What does the church owe someone in genuine dysphoria?

Third, it means we have to see sexual difference as a gift without making conformity to it an idol. Sexual difference matters. But if someone cannot align with their biological sex, that does not make them less made in God's image. It makes them someone who needs the church's help thinking through their identity at a deeper level than gender alone.

Fourth, it means we have to hold both things: sexual difference is real and good. And some people experience their body in ways that feel contrary to who they are. We do not resolve the tension between these by eliminating one or the other. We live inside the tension, as we do with all of Scripture's paradoxes.

Genesis 1:27 is not a dismissal. It is an invitation to think more carefully, to love more sacrificially, and to do the theological work that most churches have been too afraid or too certain to do.

The verse stands. Male and female He created them. Sexual difference is real. Both image God equally.

And the person in front of you, regardless of how they experience their gender, is made in that image.

The question the church has to answer is: what do we owe them because of that?

\## EXPANSION: The Merism Question — Male and Female as Complete Spectrum

Genesis 1:27 uses a rhetorical device that has almost completely disappeared from how we read it. The device is called a merism.

A merism is when two opposite extremes are used to describe everything between them. It is not a list of two things. It is a way of saying "everything in the spectrum between these two points."

When the psalmist says, "From the rising of the sun to its setting, the name of the Lord is praised," the writer is not talking about two locations. The writer is using a merism to describe every location on earth. From sunrise to sunset covers the whole world.

Genesis 1:27 does something similar. "Male and female he created them." This is not a list of exactly two genders. This is a way of describing the full spectrum of human sexual difference. From the strongest expressions of maleness to the strongest expressions of femaleness to everything in between—God created all of it.

This changes something important.

If Genesis 1:27 is using a merism, then it is not claiming there are only two genders. It is claiming that sexual difference exists on a spectrum, and God created all of it. The text acknowledges that variation exists. The text does not try to reduce variation to simplicity.

This is actually what the science shows. Sexual difference is not a binary switch where everyone is either entirely male or entirely female. Sexual difference involves chromosomes, hormones, anatomy, and neurology. All of these can vary. A person can be XX chromosomally but have higher testosterone exposure in utero. A person can be XY chromosomally but be insensitive to testosterone. A person can have one set of characteristics in most ways but variations in other ways.

Sexual difference is a spectrum. That is what biology shows. That is what the merism of Genesis 1:27 suggests.

This does not mean that sexual difference is purely a social construct. It means that while the pattern of male and female is real, the pattern has gradations. The pattern is not an on-off switch. It is a spectrum.

This matters because it means the church can affirm the reality of sexual difference—male and female as the main pattern—while also acknowledging that some people exist in the variations and gradations.

The church has largely failed to make this distinction. The church has treated Genesis 1:27 as proof that everyone fits neatly into either male or female. The church has not acknowledged that sexual difference itself is more complex than that.

Some theologians argue that because Genesis uses a merism, the inclusion of variations is actually biblical. This is worth taking seriously. The text does not say "only male and female." It says "male and female," using the rhetorical device that indicates "from the clearest male expression to the clearest female expression and everything between."

This does not resolve the transgender question. But it does complicate it in a helpful way. It says: sexual difference exists. It is created by God. And it is more complex than we typically acknowledge.

\## EXPANSION: The Fall, the Image, and Why the Merism Matters

Genesis 1:27 uses a merism—male and female covering the full spectrum of sexual difference as God created it. But what happens after the fall? Does the fall change what sexual difference means? This question sits at the intersection of creation theology and fallen reality, and the church has largely failed to think it through carefully.

Some theologians argue that the fall corrupts everything, including sexual difference. The fall corrupts desire. The fall corrupts the body. The fall corrupts how we relate to embodied existence. Sexual difference, which was meant for procreation within covenant, becomes fractured. Desire becomes disordered. The body becomes a site of confusion. Therefore, if someone experiences dysphoria—a disconnection from their biological sex—perhaps that disconnection is itself a result of the fall's corruption. Perhaps dysphoria is evidence of sexual brokenness in the same way that lustful thoughts or twisted desires are evidence of sexual brokenness.

This is not unreasonable theology. The fall does corrupt everything. It corrupts our sexuality, our bodies, our sense of self. The body is subject to decay. The desires that arise from the body are sometimes twisted. The alignment between what we are and what we feel is sometimes fractured. This is all true.

But it takes an important step that needs examination: it treats dysphoria as categorically the same as other post-fall sexual disorders. Lustful thoughts. Twisted desires. Infidelity. Sexual exploitation. Dysphoria gets grouped in with these as a manifestation of sin's corruption. The person experiencing dysphoria is implicitly asked to see their dysphoria as evidence of their own sinful fallen nature.

But is this right? Consider the actual phenomenology of dysphoria. A person experiences dysphoria. They do not choose it. They do not actively desire it. They wake up in a body that feels at its core misaligned with who they are. This is suffering, not sin. This is a consequence of living in a fallen world, not a choice to embrace fallenness. The person experiencing dysphoria is not choosing to rebel against God's design. They are experiencing brokenness in the design.

This is where the distinction between sin and suffering becomes crucial. Not everything that is wrong is sin. Some things are wrong because creation is fallen. A person born with severe mental illness did not sin to create that illness. A person born with a disability did not sin to create that disability. A person experiencing dysphoria did not sin to create that dysphoria. These are consequences of living in a fallen creation, not evidence of personal sin.

Here is where the merism of Genesis 1:27 becomes pastoral. If Genesis 1:27 describes a spectrum from the clearest maleness to the clearest femaleness and everything between, then sexual difference includes natural variations. These variations are not new. They are not modern inventions. They are built into how human sexual development actually works.

Some people are born with variations in chromosomes. Some are born with variations in hormone levels. Some are born with variations in hormone receptivity. Some are born with variations in the development of sexual characteristics. Some are born with variations in the neurology that shapes sense of self. These are not sins. They are part of how sexual difference actually expresses itself in a fallen creation.

The intersex person is not experiencing dysphoria caused by sin. The intersex person is experiencing the reality of biological variation in a fallen world. They did not choose this. They cannot be blamed for it. The merism of Genesis 1:27—male and female—includes them. They are part of the spectrum.

Dysphoria itself is more complex. Some dysphoria may be rooted in trauma. Abuse, rejection, fear—these can create disconnection from the body. Some dysphoria may be influenced by social contagion or peer pressure. Young people are uniquely susceptible to adopting frameworks that make sense of pain. If a young person is depressed or confused, and a peer group is saying "maybe you are transgender," that system can seem to explain everything. Some dysphoria may be rooted in depression or anxiety. The distress about the body is secondary to the mental health condition.

But some dysphoria may be rooted in genuine sexual variation that is not obvious on the surface. A person might be chromosomally one sex but have experienced atypical hormone exposure in utero. The dysphoria might not be a false belief. It might be an accurate sensing that their embodied experience does not match the pattern most people experience. The person's internal sense of sexual self might actually align more closely with the opposite biological sex because their neurology was shaped by unusual hormone exposure.

This is what the medical literature is increasingly showing. Not all dysphoria has the same cause. Not all dysphoria is purely psychological. Not all dysphoria is rooted in ideology. Some dysphoria appears to have legitimate biological roots.

This is what pastorally careful thinking looks like. You do not dismiss dysphoria as sin. You do not assume that dysphoria is always rooted in trauma or social contagion or depression, though it sometimes is. You do not pretend that the body is always a reliable indicator of what the deeper sexual variation might be. You recognize that sexual difference is complex, that dysphoria is real suffering, and that the cause of that suffering might be biological rather than psychological.

The pastoral response is not to offer escape from the body. It is to offer help understanding the body, accepting what is, and finding meaning and identity not dependent on fixing the body's alignment with internal experience. This is different from simple affirmation. It is also different from rejection. It is care that holds both complexity and conviction.

A pastor should help a family think through: Is this dysphoria longstanding or recent? Is it consistent across contexts or does it come and go? Has something happened in this person's life that might be influencing how they think about their body? Are there other conditions—depression, anxiety, autism, trauma—that might be primary? Are peers in this person's life also experiencing gender questioning? Is the media diet filled with gender content that might be providing frameworks for understanding other kinds of distress?

These questions are not dismissive. They are what careful discernment looks like. They honor the person experiencing dysphoria while also refusing to assume that the person has perfect insight into what is causing their own distress.

The gospel does not promise that asking these questions will resolve dysphoria quickly. The gospel promises something different: you are made in God's image. Your suffering is real. Your confusion is understandable. Your body is good, even when it feels broken. Your identity is rooted in Christ, not in whether your body feels aligned with your internal sense of self. Peace comes not from fixing your body but from accepting it and living faithfully within it.

That is the word the church should speak. Not ideology. Not dismissal. Wisdom earned from Scripture and tradition about how to be at peace in embodied existence.

\# CHAPTER 2: WHAT IS GENDER DYSPHORIA?

\## Medical Science, Psychology, and What Christians Need to Understand

A fifteen-year-old sits across from her parents. She is not crying. She is not angry. She is explaining, with a clarity that terrifies them, why she wants to transition. Her parents nod. They say they love her. They are also entirely lost. They love their daughter. Or do they. If she says she is a boy, is she still their daughter. And if they do not call her by the name she chose, are they rejecting her or protecting her.

She says it feels wrong to be in her body. She says she has felt this way since childhood. She says the dysphoria is unbearable.

Her parents do not know what dysphoria is. They do not know if it is a medical condition, a spiritual problem, or a symptom of depression. They do not know if affirming her identity will help or harm her. They do not know what to do.

This is the situation facing thousands of families. And the church has largely failed to help them think clearly.

What you need is not ideology. You need information. You need to understand what gender dysphoria actually is—clinically, psychologically, spiritually. You need to distinguish between gender dysphoria, gender identity, and gender ideology. You need to know what the science says and what it does not say. And you need to understand why the suffering is real even if every response to that suffering is not wise.

\## Defining Gender Dysphoria: The Clinical Reality

The Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders (DSM-5) defines Gender Dysphoria in children as: "A marked incongruence between one's experienced/expressed gender and assigned gender, lasting at least 6 months, manifesting by at least six of the following criteria."

The criteria include: a strong desire to be the other gender, a strong preference for cross-gender clothing and games, a strong preference for playmates of the other gender, a strong desire to participate in the stereotypical games and pastimes of the other gender, a strong desire to have the physical sex characteristics of the other gender, a strong desire to urinate in the sitting position (for assigned males), and a strong desire to exclude stereotypical toys and games of the assigned gender.

For adolescents and adults, the diagnostic criteria shift. The focus moves to the incongruence between experienced/expressed gender and assigned gender. The person experiences significant distress or impairment in functioning. The criteria is the same: at least 6 months duration, and the distress is clinically significant.

Here is what matters: Gender dysphoria is listed in the DSM-5 as a mental health condition. This does not mean it is a mental illness in the sense of being a psychological distortion. What it means is that it causes measurable distress and impairment. It is not trivial. It is not a phase in most cases. It requires clinical attention.

But—and this is crucial—the existence of gender dysphoria as a diagnosis does not settle what causes it or what the best response to it is. The diagnosis names the phenomenon. It does not explain the phenomenon. And it certainly does not prescribe the treatment.

This is where the church has gone wrong. We have either treated dysphoria as if it does not exist (cruelty disguised as conviction) or we have accepted every medical intervention offered to people experiencing it as the obvious next step (affirmation disguised as compassion).

Neither is correct.

\## Dysphoria, Identity, and Ideology: Three Different Things

Before we go further, we need to make distinctions.

Gender dysphoria is a psychological experience of distress related to the incongruence between one's experienced gender and one's assigned sex. It is a subjective suffering. A teenager experiences their body as wrong. As at its core misaligned with who they are. The distress is real.

Gender identity is a person's internal sense of their own gender. It is how someone experiences themselves as male, female, or something else. Gender identity is different from dysphoria. Some people have a strong sense of a gender identity that differs from their assigned sex, but experience minimal dysphoria. Some people experience significant dysphoria without having a clear alternate gender identity. The relationship between the two is not as simple as "different identity equals dysphoria."

Gender ideology is a set of claims about what gender is, what gender means, and how gender should be understood in society. This is where the distinction becomes important. A person can experience dysphoria and search for identity without accepting gender ideology as the explanation for what is happening. And a society can accept gender ideology without accepting that it correctly describes the experience of all people with dysphoria.

The church has largely failed to make these distinctions. We have treated them as if they are all the same thing—one phenomenon that must be either rejected entirely or accepted entirely. In reality, they are three separate questions.

A person can be experiencing real dysphoria (true), be searching for a gender identity that fits them better (true), and still be embracing an ideological understanding of what gender is that does not account for the full complexity of human existence (also possible and true).

Compassion for someone's dysphoria does not require accepting gender ideology. But denying the reality of dysphoria while hiding behind biblical truth is not compassion—it is cruelty.

\## What the Science Shows

This is where we have to be careful. The science on gender dysphoria is real but incomplete. Some facts are solid. Others are contested. Most are more complex than either side of the political debate acknowledges.

What the science clearly shows: Gender dysphoria exists. The prevalence has increased significantly, particularly among adolescent assigned females. The distress is measurable and real. People experiencing dysphoria have elevated rates of depression, anxiety, and suicidal ideation.

Let that land. Young people experiencing dysphoria have suicide rates that the church cannot ignore. This is not a small thing. This is not something to dismiss. This is not evidence that we should do whatever the person wants, but it is evidence that we should not mock them, dismiss them, or refuse them pastoral care.

What the science less clearly shows: the causes of gender dysphoria. Some research suggests biological factors—prenatal hormone exposure, neurological differences, genetic factors. Some research suggests psychological or social factors. Most research suggests it is multifactorial. We do not actually know the primary cause, and pretending we do is false certainty in either direction.

What the science shows about outcomes: Some people who experience dysphoria in childhood desist by adulthood, particularly if assigned female. Some people persist in their dysphoria regardless of social support or transition. Some people transition socially or medically and report decreased distress. Some people transition and later regret it. Some people transition and feel liberated. The outcomes are variable. They are not uniform.

What the science shows about medical interventions: Puberty blockers pause puberty, allowing time for exploration before irreversible changes. Hormones produce physical changes. Surgery is irreversible. But the long-term outcomes of these interventions are not fully studied. We do not have decades of data on how people fare who began medical transition in adolescence. This is the honest truth. We have anecdotal reports, we have shorter-term studies, but we do not have the longitudinal data we would have for other medical interventions affecting people for decades.

This is not an argument against medical transition. It is an argument for medical precision, informed consent, careful psychological evaluation, and age-appropriate decision-making.

The church has largely failed on all of those fronts. Either we have said "medically transition if you want, no questions" or "never medically transition under any circumstances." Both are failures of care.

\## The Suffering Is Real

This needs to be named clearly. The person experiencing gender dysphoria is not making it up. They are not confused about what they are experiencing. They are experiencing real distress.

A fifteen-year-old assigned female at birth who experiences their developing chest as a violation of their body is not being dramatic. They are suffering. A person who has persistent dysphoria into adulthood is not being trendy. They are struggling. When they describe their body as feeling at its core wrong, that experience is not a delusion to be dismissed. It is a suffering to be taken seriously.

This is where many churches have failed. We have treated dysphoria as if it is not real because we did not understand it. We have responded to people in distress with dismissal instead of care. We have made the gospel sound like it does not take seriously the suffering of people in our congregations.

That has to change.

Taking seriously someone's dysphoria does not mean affirming everything they decide to do about it. Parents take seriously a child's depression without affirming every decision the depressed child makes. Pastors take seriously someone's addiction without supporting every behavior that addiction produces. Taking dysphoria seriously means acknowledging the suffering is real, refusing to mock it, and walking with the person through it—not abandoning them into ideology.

\## Children, Adolescents, and the Medical Question

This is the most complicated part. And the church has largely avoided doing the work.

When a child says they are the other gender, parents panic. Schools now have policies requiring they use preferred names and pronouns. Therapy can be hard to find because many therapists feel pressure to affirm quickly without exploring. Some states have laws protecting social transition for children. Other states have laws restricting medical interventions. Everyone is afraid and nobody is doing the careful, patient work.

Here is what we know: gender dysphoria in childhood does not always persist into adulthood. Studies show that 60-90% of children who meet criteria for gender dysphoria in childhood do not persist in that dysphoria by early adulthood, particularly among those assigned female at birth. This is not a small percentage. This is most children.

But some do persist. And for those, the dysphoria can be severe and debilitating.

The question the church should ask: How do we care for children experiencing dysphoria in a way that does not assume it will persist, does not assume it will not, and does not push in either direction?

Some children benefit from social transition—changing names, presentation, and pronouns. For some, this is experienced as relief. For some, it increases dysphoria. The outcomes are not uniform. This is not a simple intervention.

Most children do not need immediate medical intervention. Puberty blockers have been used, but the long-term effects are not fully understood. Hormones create irreversible changes. Surgery is not typically done on minors except in rare circumstances. The question is not whether these interventions exist. The question is when they are appropriate, who should make the decision, and what informed consent actually looks like for a teenager.

The church's responsibility is not to make these decisions. It is to help families think carefully, to refuse to mock the child's suffering, to refuse to pretend we have certainty we do not have, and to walk alongside families as they handle impossibly complicated terrain.

\## The Detransition Reality

There is something the affirming side of this debate rarely acknowledges clearly: some people transition and later regret it.

The numbers are not huge. Different studies suggest somewhere between 1-8% of people who transition socially detransition. But it is not zero. And those people exist. And their experience matters.

Why would someone transition and later regret it? The reasons vary. Some realized their dysphoria was related to trauma or other mental health issues that needed different treatment. Some transitioned under pressure from affirmative communities and later reconsidered. Some found that medical transition did not solve the underlying dysphoria. Some regretted the social consequences or the irreversible medical changes. Some changed their minds about their gender identity.

This is not proof that transition is always wrong. It is proof that transition is not always right for everyone. And it means the church has a responsibility to help people think carefully before making irreversible decisions.

What the church should not do: use detransitioners as a rhetorical weapon to dismiss all transition. What the church should do: acknowledge that some people's best path includes transition and some does not, and the work is discerning which is true for each person.

\## What Gender Dysphoria Teaches Us

If we take gender dysphoria seriously as a clinical and pastoral reality, what does it teach us?

First, it teaches us that the human experience of gender is complex. Not everyone experiences gender in a straightforward way. This is not a new discovery, but it is one we have largely ignored.

Second, it teaches us that suffering is real. Some people experience genuine distress about their bodies and their gender. This suffering deserves a pastoral response, not a dismissal.

Third, it teaches us that we do not have all the answers. The science is incomplete. The theology is underdeveloped. The pastoral care frameworks are inadequate. Admitting this is not weakness. It is honesty.

Fourth, it teaches us that precision matters. Using the word dysphoria carelessly, using it to describe everyone who questions their gender, using it to dismiss the reality of medical conditions—all of this prevents us from thinking clearly.

Fifth, it teaches us that ideology—on both sides—obscures rather than clarifies. When we treat dysphoria as entirely biological or entirely psychological or entirely social, we miss the complexity. When we make it primarily a political issue, we abandon the person. When we make it primarily a spiritual issue, we miss the real suffering. The truth involves all dimensions of human existence.

\## Application: What the Church Should Do

If the church takes gender dysphoria seriously, what does that mean in practice?

First, it means refusing to mock people experiencing dysphoria. If someone says they are suffering, the pastoral response is not dismissal. It is "Tell me more. How can I help?"

Second, it means creating space for people to ask questions. Many people struggling with dysphoria have never had a pastor or counselor ask them careful, compassionate questions. "When did you start feeling this way? What does it feel like? What have you tried? What do you think this means?" These are the questions that help people think, not the questions that push them toward ideology.

Third, it means not rushing into medical transition as the obvious next step. If someone is experiencing dysphoria, the first response should be careful evaluation, counseling, time to think. Not "yes, transition immediately" and not "no, never." But "let's understand this together."

Fourth, it means acknowledging the suicide rates. If young people experiencing dysphoria have higher suicide rates, the church's response cannot be to ignore them or dismiss their suffering. Our responsibility is to walk with them, to help them find meaning and identity that goes deeper than gender, and to refuse to abandon them into an ideological community that promises solutions we cannot deliver.

Fifth, it means doing the theological work. We need theology of the body, theology of identity, theology of suffering, theology of what it means to be made in God's image—all of it held together. Not theology that dismisses dysphoria and not theology that affirms ideology uncritically, but theology that takes both the suffering and the truth seriously.

Gender dysphoria is not a simple thing. It is not a simple sin. It is not a simple medical condition. It is a human being in distress about the most fundamental aspects of their existence. And the church owes them something more than mockery or uncritical affirmation.

The church owes them care. The church owes them truth. The church owes them space to think. And the church owes them the refusal to reduce them to a debate.

This is what taking gender dysphoria seriously means.

\## EXPANSION: The Dutch Protocol, the Cass Review, and Medical Uncertainty

In 2012, the Netherlands began using what is called the "Dutch Protocol" for treating adolescents with severe gender dysphoria. The approach was cautious: early social support, psychological evaluation, and only later consideration of medical interventions like puberty blockers or hormones.

The Dutch Protocol became the model many other countries adopted. The United States followed it. Many European countries followed it.

Then, in 2024, Hilary Cass, a renowned pediatrician, released the findings of a three-year review of gender care for children in the UK. The Cass Review examined thousands of cases, interviewed practitioners, reviewed research, and came to conclusions that complicated the affirming narrative.

Cass found that: The evidence base for medical interventions in young people with gender dysphoria is weak. Psychological evaluation is often inadequate. The speed of transition is often too rapid. Social contagion may be playing a role in the increase in cases, particularly among assigned females.

Most importantly, Cass concluded: "The evidence for the dramatic increase in numbers of children presenting with gender dysphoria is unclear. Many of these young people have other vulnerabilities—trauma, neurodivergence, mental health conditions. These are not being adequately explored before moving toward medical transition."

The Cass Review did not say: "Never use medical interventions." It said: "Slow down. Think carefully. Ensure that psychological evaluation is thorough. Consider whether there are other explanations for dysphoria."

This is where the Western world's medical consensus is moving. Not toward prohibition. Toward caution.

Sweden, in 2023, made a similar move. The Swedish National Board of Health and Welfare reviewed the evidence and recommended that puberty blockers and hormones should be used much more rarely, and only after extensive psychological evaluation and psychological support.

This is important because the affirming side of the debate has claimed that the medical evidence clearly supports early medical transition. That claim is now being questioned by medical professionals in multiple countries.

A Christian parent needs to understand: the medical establishment itself is becoming more cautious. If your doctor is recommending rapid medical transition without extensive psychological evaluation, your doctor may be out of step with the emerging consensus in pediatric medicine.

This does not mean transition is always wrong. It means: ask questions. Request psychological evaluation. Seek second opinions. Do not assume that what is being recommended is the obvious next step. Ensure that your child has actually been evaluated for other explanations—trauma, depression, peer influence, autism spectrum, other mental health conditions.

The emerging medical caution should inform the church's caution. Not as a political statement, but as a pastoral one grounded in medical reality.

\## EXPANSION: The Medical Numbers and What They Tell Us

The statistics on gender clinic referrals are stark enough that they cannot be dismissed as simply better access to care. The numbers are too dramatic. The rise is too sudden. The trajectory tells a story that demands serious examination.

In 2009, the NHS in the UK received approximately 77 new referrals to gender identity clinics. In 2021, the number was 3,359. That is roughly a 4,300 percent increase in twelve years. This was not a gradual rise from underdiagnosis finally being corrected. It was rapid acceleration, particularly among adolescents assigned female at birth. In that demographic, the rise is even more dramatic.

Australia experienced similar trajectories. Referrals to gender clinics increased roughly tenfold in less than a decade. Spain reported similar patterns. Canada reported similar patterns. This is not localized. It is not regional. It is happening across multiple developed countries simultaneously.

This pattern raises a simple question: Did prevalence of gender dysphoria actually increase this dramatically? Or did something else change?

There are several possible explanations. It is possible that prevalence genuinely increased this dramatically. Perhaps increasing social acceptance made it safer for people with dysphoria to present to clinics. Perhaps social media and internet communities made it easier for dysphoric people to find information and community. Perhaps the shift was simply from hiding dysphoria to expressing it.

This is plausible. Social permission matters. A person who has experienced dysphoria but was afraid to say anything might become willing to say it once they believe they will be accepted rather than rejected.

But there are other possible explanations, and the medical establishment in multiple countries is taking them seriously.

Sweden's national health board, in 2023, published a detailed analysis of why they were becoming more cautious. Their conclusion: while there are young people with genuine, persistent dysphoria who benefit from medical intervention, the recent surge in referrals includes many young people with other primary conditions—autism, depression, anxiety, trauma history, recent onset questioning. The dysphoria may be secondary to these other conditions. A young person who is depressed might experience disconnection from their body as part of that depression. A young person who has experienced sexual trauma might experience deep discomfort with their sexed body. A young person on the autism spectrum might experience gender dysphoria as part of their autism-related struggle with identity and social belonging.

This does not mean dysphoria is not real for those experiencing it. It means that rapid rise in numbers suggests something else is happening alongside genuine dysphoria. Either social influence is playing a significant role in whether dysphoria is expressed and acted upon, or identification with gender variance has become a more available structure for expressing other kinds of distress, or some combination of both.

The Cass Review in the UK, released in 2024, made this explicit. The review was commissioned to examine the care of transgender and gender nonconforming children and young people in the NHS. It involved three years of work, interviews with hundreds of practitioners and parents, examination of thousands of cases, and careful review of research literature.

Hilary Cass, a renowned pediatrician who led the review, concluded: "The evidence that the numbers of young people presenting with gender dysphoria reflects a genuine increase in prevalence of gender dysphoria is uncertain." Not rejected. Uncertain. She found that psychological evaluation was often inadequate. She found that the speed of medical transition was often too rapid. She found that alternative explanations for distress—trauma, depression, neurodivergence, peer influence—were not adequately explored before moving toward medical intervention.

Cass wrote: "Many of these young people have other vulnerabilities that are not being adequately recognized and addressed. There is no strong research on the long-term outcomes of early social transition. There is insufficient evidence on the safety and efficacy of puberty blockers in this context."

What did Cass recommend? Not prohibition of medical transition. But caution. Thorough psychological evaluation. Exploration of trauma, depression, neurodivergence, peer influence. Slower timelines. More conservative use of puberty blockers and hormones until evidence is clearer. Space for young people to explore identity without rushing toward permanent medical changes.

This is important because the affirming narrative in the West has claimed that science clearly supports early medical transition. That claim is increasingly contested by pediatricians, psychiatrists, and health policy makers in multiple countries. Not by religious conservatives. By medical professionals trained to evaluate evidence and committed to the welfare of young people.

A Christian parent should understand: the medical field itself is divided. The countries most experienced in treating gender dysphoria over the longest periods are now questioning the speed and breadth of medical interventions. The Netherlands pioneered the Dutch Protocol. Now Dutch clinicians are reconsidering. The UK pioneered Gender Identity Service for adolescents. Now UK pediatricians are recommending more caution. Sweden developed expertise. Now Sweden is moving more conservatively.

If your doctor is recommending rapid transition without extensive psychological work, your doctor may be out of step with what international medical consensus is moving toward. This does not mean your doctor is wrong. It means: ask questions. Seek second opinions. Request psychological evaluation before medical decisions. Request information about long-term outcomes. Ask what research supports the proposed timeline.

The medical uncertainty should inform your caution as a parent. This is not religious ideology. This is medical realism. The evidence is genuinely unclear. The outcomes are genuinely unknown. The long-term consequences of early medical transition are not yet fully understood.

A pastor should understand something similar. You are not qualified to make medical decisions. But you are qualified to encourage families to slow down, to think carefully, to get thorough evaluation, to consider whether there are other explanations for what a young person is experiencing. You are not rejecting the young person by encouraging this. You are being responsible with a young person's future.

You are not saying: "Do not transition." You are saying: "Do not rush. Take time. Get help understanding what you are experiencing. Consider what else might be causing this distress."

The gospel does not require you to defer to any particular medical protocol. The gospel requires wisdom, care, and refusal to rush toward irreversible decisions made under pressure.

A young person in dysphoria needs time. They need professional evaluation. They need community. They need to be known and loved. They do not need quick medical fixes. They need slow, careful discernment.

The emerging medical caution from around the world should inform your pastoral approach. When multiple countries known for careful medical practice are pumping the brakes on rapid medical transition, you should too. Not out of ideology. Out of care for young people.

The problem is not medical intervention itself. The problem is medical intervention without adequate psychological evaluation. The problem is speed without discernment. The problem is assuming that dysphoria has only one cause and one solution.

A medical professional who is cautious has learned from experience. They have seen outcomes. They have watched young people who transitioned and later expressed regret. They have watched young people who transitioned and felt genuine relief. They have watched young people with depression masquerading as dysphoria. They have watched young people with trauma creating disconnection from their body.

The caution is not based in fear. It is based in clinical experience. That is worth taking seriously.

You as a pastor can say to a family: "The medical establishment itself is becoming more cautious about this. That does not mean you should avoid medical help. It means you should seek medical professionals who are thoughtful, who do thorough evaluation, who are willing to slow down."

That is a word worth speaking.

\# CHAPTER 3: THE BODY MATTERS

\## A Biblical Theology of Embodiment and Why It Changes Everything

There is a moment in every church that tells you what it actually believes about the body.

It is the moment you watch someone receive Communion. The pastor places a small piece of bread in their mouth. "The body of Christ, broken for you."

Not a symbol of the body. The body.

This is where Christian theology separates itself from every other religious tradition. Christianity is not Gnostic. Christianity does not treat the body as an obstacle to the spirit. Christianity does not teach that the real you is trapped inside a flesh prison waiting for escape.

Christianity teaches something subversive: the body matters.

God took a body. Jesus of Nazareth had a body—hands, feet, a voice, a face, blood. He ate fish. He walked. He touched people. He bled. The Incarnation is God's declaration that matter is not evil. The body is not shameful. Flesh is not the enemy.

And then, Christianity teaches something even more demanding: we will have bodies forever. The resurrection is not escape from the body. It is resurrection of the body. Paul is clear: "Now we see only a reflection as in a mirror; then we shall see face to face." We will be embodied in eternity.

The body is not temporary. The body is not incidental. The body is where human existence happens. And the church has largely forgotten this.

This has enormous implications for how we think about gender, about dysphoria, about the relationship between identity and embodiment. And if we get this wrong, we will fail people in genuine pain.

\## God Became Flesh

Start here: "The Word became flesh and made his dwelling among us."

Not a symbol. Not an illusion. Not a temporary descent into matter. Flesh. Sarx. The actual, physical body of Jesus Christ, born of Mary, hungry, tired, vulnerable to pain.

Why did God become flesh? Multiple reasons. To redeem us. To show us what humanity was meant to be. To save us from the inside of our existence rather than from outside it. To demonstrate that the body is not the problem.

This matters because there is a very old heresy that wants to say the spirit is good and the body is evil. That the real self is separate from the physical. That matter is fallen in a way that demands we escape it.

This heresy has names. It is Gnosticism. It shows up in different forms throughout history. And it has deeply infected how Christians think about the body—particularly the sexual body, particularly the gendered body.

The Incarnation is God's definitive rejection of this heresy. God takes a body. God calls it good. God says matter is not evil. Flesh is not the enemy. The body is the way human existence happens, and God is willing to become body to redeem body.

If the body is good—actually good, not reluctantly tolerated—then the body matters. What you do with your body matters. How you treat your body matters. What you think about your body matters.

This is where contemporary gender ideology and Christian theology diverge. Gender ideology tends to treat the body as incidental to identity. The ideology says: your gender identity is who you are. Your body is secondary. If your body does not match your internal sense of self, the body is the problem, not the identity.

Christianity says something different. Christianity says: your body is part of who you are. Your body is not a costume you can exchange. Your body is not a mistake to be corrected. Your body is a gift to be inhabited, cared for, and lived from.

This does not mean your body determines everything about you. It does not mean you have no agency regarding your body. It does not mean dysphoria is not real or that all embodied existence is comfortable.

It means the body is not incidental.

\## The Resurrection Is Not Escape

This is the second move. And it is where most Christians go wrong, because we have been trained to think of heaven as escape from the body.

It is not.

Paul writes: "For now we see only a reflection as in a mirror; then we shall see face to face. Now I know in part; then I shall know fully, even as I am fully known. And these three remain: faith, hope and love."

Notice what Paul does not say. He does not say "Then I will escape my body" or "Then I will be pure spirit" or "Then matter will be gone."

He says "I shall see face to face."

A face requires a body.

The Apostles' Creed says: "I believe in the resurrection of the body."

Not the resurrection of the spirit, separated from the body. The resurrection of the body. Jesus was raised bodily. He had a body that could be touched and that could eat. He was not a ghost. He was not pure spirit. He was embodied.

And the promise to those who follow Christ is the same: resurrection of the body. We will have bodies in eternity. Real bodies. Not incidental bodies. Bodies that matter.

This has enormous implications. If your destiny is embodied, if you will have a body forever, if resurrection is not escape but transformation—then what you think about your body now matters. How you treat your body now matters. Whether you can be at peace in your body matters.

It is not about conforming to social pressure. It is about understanding that embodied existence is not temporary. It is not a phase. It is not something to escape. It is the mode of existence God created you for, is redeeming you through, and will resurrect you in.

If someone is experiencing dysphoria—a deep sense that their body is wrong—that is a real problem. But the problem is not the body itself. The problem is the suffering related to the body. And the solution is not pretending the body is irrelevant. The solution is learning to inhabit and be at peace in the body you have.

This is hard. This is costly. And it is different from both the cruelty of "just accept it" and the denial of "your body is not real."

\## The Shadow of Gnosticism

This is where we have to name something directly: gender ideology, as it is typically articulated, is Gnostic.

It teaches that the real self is separate from the physical body. It teaches that your identity is a matter of internal feeling, not external reality. It teaches that if your body does not match your sense of self, the body is the problem, not the identity.

This is the ancient heresy the church spent centuries fighting.

Gnosticism taught that matter is evil, that the body is a prison, that the real self is the spirit trapped inside flesh. To be saved is to escape the body. To be saved is to achieve a purely spiritual existence.

Christianity rejected this completely. Christianity says the body is good. The body is not a prison. Embodied existence is not shameful. Redemption is not escape from the body. Redemption is transformation of the body.

But Gnosticism does not just appear in ancient heresies. It appears whenever the church treats the body with contempt. It appears whenever we treat the body as incidental. It appears whenever we teach that what matters is your internal spiritual state and the body is irrelevant.

And it appears in the claim that your gender identity—your internal sense of what gender you are—is at its core more real than your biological sex. That your body is the mistake. That if you feel female but your body is male, your body is wrong.

This is Gnostic to its core.

But here is what is tricky: Gnosticism appeals to real suffering. If someone is experiencing dysphoria—a deep sense that their body is wrong—then saying "your body is good and you need to accept it" sounds cruel. It sounds like we are denying their suffering.

We are not. But we are refusing to solve the suffering by pretending the body is irrelevant.

\## The Truth About Dysphoria and the Body

Let's be clear about what we are claiming.

We are not claiming that dysphoria is not real. We are not claiming that the suffering is insignificant. We are not claiming that someone who experiences deep dysphoria should just "accept it" and be fine.

What we are claiming is that the correct response to dysphoria is not to pretend the body is not real. The correct response is to help someone find peace in their actual body while taking seriously the psychological and spiritual roots of the dysphoria.

This is hard. This is not simple. This does not work in every case the same way. But it is the Christian response because it is grounded in a theology that says: the body is good. God created your body. Your body will be resurrected. Your body is not a mistake to be corrected. Your body is where your life happens.

Now, some people reading this will say: "But you do not understand dysphoria. You have never felt this way. You are being cruel."

I hear that. And I take it seriously. But cruelty would be to tell someone their body is not real, or to affirm their pain by pretending they can escape their body through gender transition. Cruelty would be to offer a false solution to a problem that requires deeper work.

True pastoral care says: "Your suffering is real. Your dysphoria is real. And the answer is not to pretend your body is irrelevant. The answer is to find your way to peace with the body you have, to understand the roots of your dysphoria, and to build an identity that goes deeper than gender."

This requires courage. It requires refusing to take the easy path of either cruelty or false affirmation. It requires staying in the tension, honoring both the suffering and the truth of the body.

\## What Body Theology Means Practically

If the body matters—actually matters, not just theologically but practically—what does that mean?

First, it means we stop treating the body as incidental. The way you eat, the way you sleep, the way you move, the way you care for your physical health—these are not trivial. These are the way you live. The body is not a vehicle for a disembodied self. The body is you.

Second, it means we take seriously the reality of biological sex. The fact that you have a body that developed in a particular way, that is capable of particular things, that has particular capacities—these are not evil facts to be denied. They are part of your actual existence. To be at peace, you have to be at peace with your actual existence, not a fantasy of different embodiment.

Third, it means we refuse to treat the body as a costume that can be exchanged. The body is not a costume. The body is your flesh, your blood, your material existence. Medical interventions can change the body. But they cannot make you someone other than who you are. They can suppress biological processes. But they cannot make you someone other than someone whose body is what it is.

Fourth, it means we take dysphoria seriously as a problem to solve, not a problem to enable. If someone is experiencing deep distress about their body, the pastoral response is not to tell them their body is irrelevant. The pastoral response is to help them understand why they are experiencing dysphoria, to address the psychological and spiritual roots, and to help them find their way to being at peace in their actual, embodied existence.

This is not the same as forcing someone to accept their body against their will. This is deeper, longer, harder work. This is therapy. This is prayer. This is discipleship. This is learning to live from the inside out rather than constantly rebelling against the material reality of your existence.

\## The Gift Hidden in the Discomfort

This is where the gospel gets interesting.

In every version of Christianity that is worth anything, there is a call to surrender. To die to yourself. To lose your life to find it. To give up your agenda for God's agenda.

This is deeply uncomfortable. This is not what the world teaches. The world teaches: pursue what makes you happy, demand that reality conform to your desires, build an identity around your preferences and feelings.

Christianity teaches something harder: sometimes reality is not what you want. Sometimes your body is not what you wish it was. And sometimes the way forward is learning to surrender, to accept, to find peace with what is rather than constantly battling for what you wish were true.

This is not easy. This is not comfortable. This is not therapy-speak about validating feelings and honoring the self.

But it is the path that leads to actual freedom.

If you are constantly at war with your body, constantly resisting your physical reality, constantly trying to force your embodied existence into a different shape—you will exhaust yourself. You will find no peace. You will keep chasing interventions that promise relief but deliver only a different form of suffering.

But if you can surrender, if you can accept your actual, embodied existence, if you can find peace with the body you have been given—that is where freedom lives. Not in pretending your body is not real. In accepting that it is real and learning to live from that ground.

For someone experiencing dysphoria, this is a costly path. It is not the affirmation that culture offers. It is not the denial that churches offer. It is something harder and more real: acceptance of your actual existence, combined with the help, the prayer, the community, the counseling that makes that acceptance possible.

This is what body theology actually teaches. Not "your body is a prison." Not "your body is irrelevant." But "your body matters, your body is good, your body is where your real life happens, and learning to be at peace in your body is the path to actual freedom."

The church needs to teach this again. And it needs to offer to people in dysphoria not what culture offers or what cruelty offers, but what gospel offers: a way home to their own embodied existence.

\## EXPANSION: Wendell Berry, N.T. Wright, and Incarnational Body Theology

Wendell Berry, the Christian poet and essayist, has written more carefully about the body than almost any contemporary Christian writer. His essay "Health Is a Membership" argues that the body is not yours to manipulate as you wish. The body is part of a web of relationships—to the earth, to community, to the past, to the future.

When someone tries to escape or at its core reject their body, they are rejecting membership in this web. They are claiming the right to treat the body as a possession to be redesigned rather than as a gift to be inhabited.

Berry writes: "The body, as commonly understood, is merely the meat. And the meat, as it is implied, is corrupt. For it is divisible; it will decay; it will die. So to eat, to have bodily needs and desires, to be threatened with pain and death—this is to be fallen, to be in exile from the Garden of Eden."

But Berry argues that the Christian vision is different. The body is not fallen. The body is good. The body is how we participate in God's creation. To be at peace with the body is to accept membership in creation.

N.T. Wright has done similar work with the doctrine of resurrection. Wright argues that the Christian hope is not escape from the body but resurrection of the body. The resurrected body is not a different body. It is the same body, transformed and renewed.

This matters for how the church thinks about dysphoria. The church should offer not escape from the body but reconciliation with the body. Not denial of the body but acceptance of it. Not transformation of the body through medical intervention but transformation of the relationship to the body through grace.

The gnostic heresy treats the body as an obstacle to the spirit. The Christian truth treats the body as the vehicle through which the spirit lives. To be at peace in the body is not to deny the spirit. It is to integrate the spirit and the body.

This is costly. It is not the comfort that either ideology offers. But it is the path that leads to actual peace—not escape, but integration.

Gnosticism sounds compassionate. It says: "Your body is the problem. Escape from it." But escape is not possible. You cannot escape your body. You can only deny it or accept it. Denying it creates constant war with yourself. Accepting it creates the possibility of peace.

This is what body theology from the Christian tradition offers. Not ideology. Wisdom earned through 2,000 years of Christians learning to be at peace in their actual, embodied existence.

\## EXPANSION: The Gnostic Problem in Modern Christianity — Why Both Sides Are Wrong

The greatest heresy infiltrating contemporary Christianity is not about gender specifically. It is about the body. Gnosticism—the ancient belief that the physical world is evil and the spiritual world is the only reality—has returned in new forms. And the church, caught between two ideological poles, has failed to name the heresy that underlies them both.

On the progressive side, the gnostic belief sounds like this: Your true self is your internal sense of identity. Your body is a container that may or may not match your true self. The true you is interior—consciousness, identity, the sense of who you are. The body is external, a temporary vessel that houses that self. If the container does not match your sense of self, the container can and should be changed to match your internal reality. Your body is malleable. Your identity is what matters. The body is material. Identity is real.

This is pure gnosticism. It treats the body as secondary to the true self. The true self is interior—your sense of gender, your internal experience, your consciousness. The body is just matter, just the temporary vessel that houses that consciousness.

The spiritual is the internal sense of self. The material body is the obstacle. This is exactly what the Gnostics believed. And it was condemned as heresy in the early church for good reason.

On the conservative side, the gnostic belief sounds different but is still present: Your body is fallen and corrupted. Your desires are disordered. What you feel about your body is unreliable. You cannot trust your internal experience. You must discipline your body, deny its desires, and submit to what Scripture and tradition say the body is supposed to be. The body is dangerous. The body lies to you. Your feelings are not trustworthy.

This also treats the body as secondary. But instead of privileging the internal sense of self over the body, it privileges external authority over the internal sense of self. Either way, the body is not accepted as the integrated reality of who you are. The body is viewed with suspicion. The body is something to be controlled, not inhabited.

This is gnosticism too. It is the gnosticism of contempt for the flesh rather than the gnosticism of exaltation of consciousness. But it is gnosticism. It says the body is not quite right. The body is not quite acceptable. The body needs to be brought into line with something outside of itself.

Paul offers something radically different. Paul does not treat the body as a container. Paul does not treat the body as something to be controlled or escaped. Paul treats the body as integral to who you are.

"Do you not know that your body is a temple of the Holy Spirit, who is in you, whom you have received from God?" The body is not a temple that houses the spirit. Your body is the temple. The physical and the spiritual are integrated.

In the same passage, Paul writes: "Therefore honor God with your bodies." Not "deny your bodies." Not "escape your bodies." Not "transcend your bodies." Honor them. Accept them. Inhabit them.

This is what the incarnation teaches. God did not send a divine spirit into a body as a temporary housing. God became embodied. Jesus is not a spirit trapped in flesh. Jesus is spirit and body. He ate. He had wounds. He was touched and touched others. He had embodied relationships. He experienced embodied life.

The resurrection is not escape from the body. It is transformation of the body. Paul writes: "We know that when he appears, we shall be like him, for we shall see him as he is." Like Christ. And Christ in resurrection is embodied. Not a ghost. Not pure consciousness. A body.

Paul writes: "Listen, I tell you a mystery: We will not all sleep, but we will all be changed—in a flash, in the twinkling of an eye, at the last trumpet. For the trumpet will sound, the dead will be raised imperishable, and we will be changed." The body is raised. Not escaped. Transformed.

To accept your body—even when that body creates suffering—is not to deny the spirit. It is to follow the pattern Christ established. Integration. Acceptance. Transformation of how you relate to your embodied existence rather than escape from that existence.

Both progressive and conservative gender ideology fail at this point. Both are, at their core, gnostic. Both assume the body is not quite acceptable as it is. Both believe the body needs to be fixed. The progressive ideology says fix it by aligning it with your internal sense of self. The conservative ideology says fix it by submitting it to external doctrine about what the body should be. Neither offers the hard, Christian path.

That path is: your body is good. Your body is created by God. Your body is the vehicle through which you encounter reality. Your body is how you love and are loved. Your body will be resurrected and transformed. In the meantime, your calling is not to escape your body or to perfect your body or to make your body match your internal sense of self. Your calling is to accept your body and to live faithfully within it.

This is not easy. It is not comfortable. It does not promise that you will feel good about your body. It promises something harder and deeper: integration. Peace that comes not from fixing the body but from accepting it and living out of that acceptance.

The disabled person cannot fix their body. The person experiencing dysphoria cannot escape their body. The person with a chronic illness cannot will their body into conformity with their desired self. What all of them can do is accept the body they have and find meaning, identity, and purpose that does not depend on the body being different.

This is what separates the gospel from ideology on both sides. The gospel does not promise to make your body what you want. The gospel promises that you are loved in your body. That you are made in God's image in your body. That your body is good. That transformation happens not by escaping embodied existence but by inhabiting it with faith.

This is why the church has to recover body theology. Not a theology that says the body is fallen and must be denied. Not a theology that says the body does not matter and the internal sense of self is all that matters. But a theology that says: you are embodied. Your body is good. Your body is part of your identity. Your peace comes not from changing the body but from accepting it and living faithfully within it. Your hope comes from the promise that this embodied existence, with all its suffering and difficulty, will be redeemed and transformed by God.

This is what separates the gospel from ideology on both sides.

\# CHAPTER 4: EUNUCHS IN THE BIBLE

\## What Scripture Says About Gender Exceptions and the Kingdom of God

There is a verse that almost nobody brings up in the gender conversation. And the fact that it is almost never cited tells you everything you need to know about how the church has been thinking about this question.

It is Matthew 19:12.

Jesus is answering a question about divorce. He teaches about the permanence of marriage. And then His disciples say: "If this is the situation between a husband and wife, it is better not to marry."

And Jesus responds: "Not everyone can accept this word, but only those to whom it has been given. For there are eunuchs who were born that way; and there are eunuchs who have been made that way by others; and there are those who choose to live like eunuchs for the sake of the kingdom of heaven. The one who can accept this should accept it."

This is the only passage where Jesus directly addresses people who are not typically male or female in the biological sense. And what He says is striking.

He acknowledges that eunuchs exist. He acknowledges that there are different kinds of eunuchs. He includes them in His moral universe. And He offers them a place in the kingdom of God based on how they live, not based on whether they fit the typical pattern.

This passage has been almost entirely ignored by the church when discussing gender. We have preferred to work from Genesis 1:27 alone, as if that verse were sufficient to address every variation of human experience.

It is not. And eunuch theology—biblical theology of eunuchs—opens a door that the church has refused to walk through.

\## What Matthew 19:12 Actually Says

Let's look at the verse carefully.

"For there are eunuchs who were born that way; and there are eunuchs who have been made that way by others; and there are those who choose to live like eunuchs for the sake of the kingdom of heaven."

Jesus identifies three categories of eunuchs.

First: eunuchs born that way. The Greek is "eunouchoi hoi ek koilias metros gennethe houtos"—literally, "eunuchs who were born from the womb this way."

In biblical times, a eunuch was someone who could not engage in typical sexual reproduction. Some were born that way—likely what we would now call intersex conditions or other conditions affecting reproductive capacity or sexual function. Some were made that way by castration.

Jesus acknowledges that some people are born outside the typical pattern of sexual development. They exist. They are part of human experience. And they are part of God's design.

Second: eunuchs made that way by others. This refers to people who were castrated—sometimes as slaves, sometimes as part of court practice, sometimes for religious reasons in various cultures.

Jesus acknowledges that some people's sexual capacity has been altered by human action. He does not affirm the practice of castration. But He acknowledges the reality: some people exist outside the typical pattern not because of their own choice but because of what has been done to them.

Third: eunuchs who choose to live as eunuchs for the kingdom of heaven. This refers to people—likely the unmarried followers of Jesus—who renounce sexual activity and procreation for the sake of spiritual devotion.

Jesus acknowledges that some people choose a life path that removes them from the typical reproductive pattern, and He affirms this as a valid choice if it is done for kingdom purposes.

What is Jesus doing here? He is saying: the pattern of male and female designed for procreation is real. But the pattern is not absolute. There are people who exist outside it. Some by natural condition. Some by circumstance. Some by choice. And all of them have a place in God's kingdom.

This is not a small thing. This is Jesus saying that sexual difference and the capacity for procreation, while they are the pattern, are not the only way to be human. Not the only way to belong to God's people.

\## Isaiah 56 and the Eunuch's Promise

But there is even more in Scripture about eunuchs. And it is even more striking.

Turn to Isaiah 56. This is post-exile Isaiah. The people have been restored to the land. Gentiles are coming to faith. The borders of who belongs to God's people are being redrawn.

And the prophet addresses eunuchs specifically.

"To the eunuchs who keep my Sabbaths, who choose what pleases me and hold fast to my covenant—to them I will give within my temple and its walls a memorial and a name better than sons and daughters; I will give them an everlasting name that will endure forever."

Let that land.

A eunuch—someone who cannot have biological children, someone who is outside the reproductive pattern—is promised something better than children. An everlasting name. A memorial. A place in God's house.

In the ancient Near East, children were your legacy. Your children would carry your name. Your children would remember you. Without children, you were forgotten.

The eunuch was the person most obviously outside this pattern. The eunuch was the one who would have no biological legacy.

And God promises the eunuch: you will have a better legacy than biological children. You will have an everlasting name. You will be remembered not because of your procreative capacity but because of your faith.

What is God doing here? God is saying: the pattern of procreation and biological family is real. But your value is not dependent on fitting that pattern. Your place in God's kingdom is not earned through biological capacity. Your legacy comes through covenant faithfulness, not genetic inheritance.

This is revolutionary. This is God saying: I see you. I know you are outside the pattern. And you are not outside My kingdom.

\## Acts 8 and Full Inclusion

Now, consider what happens in Acts 8.

Philip, one of the apostles, meets an Ethiopian eunuch on the road. The eunuch is a person of authority in the Ethiopian court. He has high position. But he is also a eunuch—outside the typical sexual pattern.

He is reading Isaiah. Specifically, he is reading Isaiah 53, about the suffering servant. And he asks Philip: "Tell me, please, who is the prophet talking about, himself or someone else?"

Philip explains Jesus. The eunuch believes. And he asks: "Look, here is water. What can stop me from being baptized?"

Philip baptizes him. Full membership in God's people. Full inclusion in the Church.

Now, notice what does not happen. Philip does not say: "Well, you need to understand that the standard for Christian membership is male and female. You are outside that pattern. So perhaps you should wait." He does not say: "You are a eunuch. You are unusual. We need to think about whether you really belong." He does not say: "Eunuchs are an exception to the rule."

He baptizes him. Full inclusion. No hesitation.

What is the story saying? It is saying: the eunuch—the person outside the typical sexual pattern—has full access to God's kingdom. Full membership. Full belonging. No qualification.

The story does not require the eunuch to become typically male or female. It does not require conformity to the pattern. It requires faith. It requires following Jesus. And that is enough.

\## What Eunuch Theology Teaches Us

So what does this teach? What does it mean to have a biblical eunuch theology?

First, it means acknowledging that sexual difference is a pattern, not an absolute. Genesis 1:27 describes the pattern. Male and female. Designed to be fruitful. Designed to fill the earth. This is how God made human beings to work.

But patterns have exceptions. All of them. And the Bible acknowledges eunuchs as exceptions to the pattern—not as mistakes, not as less-than, but as exceptions that exist within God's design.

Second, it means understanding that belonging to God's people is not dependent on fitting the sexual pattern. The eunuch's value is not dependent on reproductive capacity. The eunuch's place in God's kingdom is not earned through biological conformity. The eunuch belongs to God through covenant, through faith, through following Jesus.

Third, it means taking seriously that people exist outside the typical pattern. Not everyone is straightforwardly male or female. Not everyone has typical reproductive capacity or sexual function. Not everyone can or wants to have biological children. These are people. They are not mistakes. They are part of human reality.

Fourth, it means refusing to make conformity to the sexual pattern the measure of who belongs and who does not. The eunuch was promised an everlasting name, a place in God's house, not because the eunuch became typically gendered, but because the eunuch kept covenant and held fast to God.

\## How This Changes the Transgender Conversation

So how does eunuch theology apply to the contemporary conversation about gender and transgender identity?

This is where we have to be careful not to overstate, but also not to understate.

Eunuch theology does not say: "Gender is completely fluid and anyone can be any gender they choose." The Bible still teaches that sexual difference is real and good. Genesis 1:27 still stands. Male and female He created them.

Eunuch theology does not say: "Transgender identity is the same as being a eunuch in Scripture." The conditions are not identical. The ancient eunuch was typically someone who could not engage in typical sexual reproduction, either from birth or through castration. That is a different reality from modern transgender identity and gender dysphoria.

But eunuch theology does say something important: the Bible acknowledges that not everyone fits the typical pattern. The Bible does not treat people outside the pattern as less-than. The Bible includes them. The Bible promises them a place. The Bible judges them by their faithfulness, not by whether they conform to the sexual pattern.

For someone experiencing dysphoria, or identifying as transgender, this changes the conversation. It says: the typical pattern is real. Sexual difference matters. But your value is not dependent on fitting that pattern. Your place in God's kingdom is not dependent on whether your body matches cultural expectations about gender. Your worth is as an image-bearer. Your place is as someone loved by Christ.

This is different from both dismissal and uncritical affirmation.

It is not: "You are wrong about your gender and you need to accept your biological sex." It is: "Your experience of your gender is complex. Your belonging in God's community is not dependent on fitting a pattern. And we will walk with you in that complexity, not as judges but as fellow travelers who also do not always fit cleanly into categories."

It is not: "Whatever you think about your gender is true and the church should affirm everything you choose." It is: "Your suffering is real. Your experience of your body is real. And your value and your place in God's kingdom transcend your gender experience."

\## The Limit of the Analogy

This is important to name: the eunuch analogy is not perfect. And pretending it is would be intellectually dishonest.

A eunuch in Scripture was typically someone incapable of reproduction, either from birth or through external action. A transgender person is typically someone with typical reproductive capacity who experiences their gender differently than their biological sex.

These are not identical situations. The eunuch's condition was about reproductive capacity. The transgender person's condition is about gender identity—the sense of what gender one is, not necessarily about reproductive function.

So we should not collapse the categories. We should not say: "The Bible's theology of eunuchs fully addresses transgender identity." It does not. The Bible does not directly address gender identity as we understand it now.

But eunuch theology does give us something important: permission to acknowledge that not everyone fits the typical pattern, permission to include people who do not fit the pattern, and permission to judge people by their faithfulness, not by whether they conform to sexual norms.

That permission changes everything.

\## What the Church Should Learn

If the church takes eunuch theology seriously, what changes?

First, it changes how we speak about gender. We stop pretending the binary is absolute. We acknowledge that sexual difference is a pattern, but patterns have exceptions. Not everyone fits neatly.

Second, it changes how we include people. We stop making conformity to gender expectations a condition for belonging. The eunuch belonged not because he became typically gendered but because he kept covenant. We include people who do not fit the pattern.

Third, it changes how we judge. We stop evaluating people's faithfulness based on whether they fit gender expectations. A eunuch's value was not his reproductive capacity. A Christian's value is not their conformity to gender norms. A Christian's value is their faithfulness to Christ.

Fourth, it changes how we treat dysphoria. If someone is experiencing dysphoria, we do not dismiss them by saying "gender is a binary and you need to accept your place in it." We acknowledge: this person is suffering. This person does not fit the typical pattern. And we offer them belonging and care, not conformity as a condition.

This does not mean affirming every choice someone makes about their body. It does not mean the church has no wisdom to offer about the body and gender and identity. It does not mean we pretend dysphoria is the same as just being an exception to the typical pattern.

But it means we offer the same thing Isaiah offered the eunuch: "You are outside the pattern. You will not have the legacy of children. But I will give you a name and a place in My house that will last forever."

That is the promise. That is the gospel. Not conformity, but inclusion. Not denial of the exception, but embrace of the exception into God's covenant people.

The church has known this about eunuchs for 2,000 years. We just forgot. And remembering changes everything.

\## EXPANSION: The Galli, the Hijra, and What History Teaches

The eunuch theology of Matthew 19:12 is not without historical context. Jesus was not inventing something new. He was drawing on a reality that existed in multiple cultures and religions for centuries.

In ancient Rome, the cult of Cybele included the galli—priests who were castrated and who lived outside typical male gender roles. They wore female dress. They adopted female names. They were integrated into religious life in ways that were recognized and somewhat accepted.

In Hindu and Islamic traditions across India, the hijra have existed for centuries. The hijra are people who are not considered male or female. They have their own communities, their own roles in society, their own spiritual practices. They are not universally affirmed, but they are acknowledged.

In various indigenous cultures, third-gender categories existed. The Two-Spirit people of Native American cultures. The fa'afafine of Samoa. People who existed outside the binary and had recognized social roles.

This does not mean that these cultures had the same understanding of gender as contemporary culture. It means that humans have long recognized that some people exist outside the typical binary. Cultures have handled this recognition differently. Some with respect. Some with disrespect. But most human cultures have somehow acknowledged the reality.

What does this teach? First, that the impulse to fit everyone neatly into male or female is not universal. Humans have often created social structures to account for people who do not fit the binary.

Second, that when a culture does create space for people outside the binary, that space is often religious or spiritual. The galli were priests. The hijra have spiritual significance. The Two-Spirit people had ceremonial roles.

This suggests something important: the human longing to understand gender and identity is not simply modern. It is ancient. And humans have often tried to address that longing through religious frameworks.

The church should understand this history not to affirm contemporary gender ideology, but to understand that the church's refusal to engage with people outside the gender binary is not inevitable. Other religious traditions have found ways to include them.

The question is not whether the church should include people outside the binary. The church should. The question is how. With what theology. With what understanding of what it means to belong to God's people.

Matthew 19:12 suggests the answer: you can belong without fitting the pattern. Your value is not dependent on your conformity to the binary. The eunuch's place in God's kingdom was earned through covenant faithfulness, not gender conformity.

The history of human cultures suggests that this is not strange. Many cultures have found ways to acknowledge and include people outside the binary. The church is not doing something unprecedented by learning to do the same.

What the church is doing, by refusing to learn from either Scripture or history, is being unnecessarily cruel.

\## EXPANSION: The Eunuch's Promise in Isaiah 56 — What Legacy Looks Like Beyond Procreation

Isaiah 56 contains one of the most subversive statements in Scripture about belonging and legacy. A eunuch—someone outside the reproductive pattern—is promised "a memorial and a name better than sons and daughters."

In the ancient world, sons and daughters were your legacy. Your children would carry your name. Your children would remember you. Without children, you were erased from history.

But the eunuch is promised something different. An everlasting name. A memorial. A place in God's house.

What does this promise mean?

First, it means the eunuch's value is not dependent on reproductive capacity. The eunuch does not have to apologize for not being able to have biological children. The eunuch's life is valuable not because it contributes to the continuation of the family line, but because it participates in covenant with God.

Second, it means legacy takes different forms. Not everyone's legacy is biological. Some people's legacy is spiritual. Some people's legacy is what they build in community. Some people's legacy is how they point others toward God.

For someone experiencing dysphoria, this is important. The dysphoria often involves a sense of fundamental wrongness. "My body is wrong. I do not fit the pattern. I cannot participate in what my body is designed for."

The eunuch theology says: your body may not fit the typical pattern. Your role may be different. But your life still has meaning. Your legacy is not dependent on conforming to the reproductive pattern.

A person who accepts their biological sex, even though they experience dysphoria, is not giving up on meaning. They are finding meaning in a different way. Not through reproductive participation, but through spiritual participation. Not through biological legacy, but through covenant faithfulness.

This is what Isaiah 56 promises. And it is what the church has largely failed to teach.

The gospel is not a promise that you will feel comfortable in your body. The gospel is a promise that your body is good, that your identity goes deeper than gender, and that your legacy is found in covenant with God, not in whether you fit the biological pattern.

That is a promise worth hearing.

\# CHAPTER 5: THE GENDER IDEOLOGY DEBATE

\## What Secular Culture Claims and Where the Bible Disagrees

Every conversation about gender eventually comes to this: a disagreement about what gender is.

Is gender an innate, internal sense of identity that should be accepted and affirmed regardless of biological reality? Or is gender rooted in biological sex and shaped by cultural expectations? Is gender performance—roles, presentation, social norms? Is gender identity? Is gender a binary or a spectrum?

People answer these questions differently, and the differences matter.

The church's job is not to police all of these questions. The church's job is to think carefully about what Scripture teaches and to refuse to reduce the gospel to any single ideological answer.

But the church also has a responsibility to name where contemporary gender ideology parts ways with Christian theology. Not because we are trying to score political points. Because the stakes are souls. And if ideology is promising what only the gospel can deliver, the church has to say so.

\## What Gender Ideology Claims

Let's be specific about what we mean by "gender ideology."

Gender ideology is a set of claims about the nature and meaning of gender. Different versions exist, but the core claims are something like this:

Gender is primarily an internal sense of identity. What matters most is what gender you feel you are. Your gender identity is who you are. Your body is secondary.

Gender is separate from biological sex. Your biological sex is one thing. Your gender identity is another thing. They may or may not align. And if they do not align, your gender identity is more fundamental to who you are than your biological sex.

Gender is a spectrum or fluid, not a binary. The categories "male" and "female" are social constructs that do not capture the full reality of human gender experience. Gender exists along a spectrum or shifts over time. People can be non-binary, genderfluid, or belong to categories that do not fit the male/female binary.

If your gender identity does not align with your biological sex, you should transition. Medically if necessary. Socially if helpful. The goal is to align your external presentation and body with your internal sense of gender.

Gender expectations that do not align with a person's gender identity are oppressive. If someone is told they must conform to expectations based on their biological sex, that is oppressive. The liberating response is to allow people to express whatever gender they feel they are.

Now, not every person who has questions about gender accepts all of these claims. But together, they form what is typically called "gender ideology"—a comprehensive structure for understanding gender as internal identity, separate from biological reality, and deserving of social, medical, and legal support.

The church has largely responded in one of two ways: either by rejecting all of it (cruelty disguised as conviction) or by accepting all of it (capitulation disguised as compassion).

Neither is the Christian response.

\## Where the Bible Agrees

Before we talk about disagreement, let's be honest about where Scripture and gender ideology have some common ground.

The Bible teaches that people are more than their biological function. Genesis 1:27 teaches that humans are made in the image of God. That image is not genitalia. That image is something deeper—representation, relationship, rule. So the Bible agrees that there is something in human identity that goes beyond just biological category.

The Bible teaches that gender roles are cultural, not absolute. The Bible is not consistent in what it prescribes about male and female roles. Proverbs 31 describes a woman who is a businesswoman, a trader, someone with significant economic and social power—roles that would be closed to women in many historical periods. So the Bible itself refuses to lock gender roles into a permanent structure.

The Bible teaches that gender expectations can be oppressive. The story of Jesus and the woman at the well (John 4) is a story of Jesus breaking gender expectations to have a genuine conversation. Throughout the Gospels, Jesus breaks cultural gender norms—speaking to women, including women as witnesses, treating women with a dignity that shocked the culture.

The Bible teaches that belonging is not dependent on fitting stereotypes. As we saw with eunuchs, people who do not fit the typical pattern have a place in God's kingdom. So the Bible agrees that you do not have to fit cultural gender expectations to belong.

Where, then, is the disagreement?

\## Where the Bible Parts Ways with Gender Ideology

The disagreement comes in several places.

First, on what is foundational. Gender ideology says: your gender identity is who you are. Your body is secondary. Your internal sense of gender is more fundamental than your biological reality.

The Bible says: your identity is rooted in being made in God's image. That is primary. Everything else—including gender—flows from that. Your body is not secondary. Your body is the medium through which you live. But neither is your body the absolute determinant of who you are. You are a person—body, mind, spirit—made in God's image.

This is different from gender ideology's structure. It is not saying: "Your body is who you are and you must accept it." It is saying: "You are a complete person—body and soul. Your identity is rooted in that complete reality, not in your internal feeling alone and not in your body alone."

Second, on the nature of sexual difference. Gender ideology treats sexual difference as primarily a social construction. Different cultures construct gender differently, so the Bible's teaching about male and female is culturally conditioned and therefore not universally binding.

The Bible says: sexual difference is real and created by God. Genesis 1:27 is not culturally conditioned. It is a claim about how God made humanity. Male and female. Not interchangeable. Not purely socially constructed. Created that way.

Now, the Bible also says gender roles are culturally variable and culturally conditioned. How men and women express their sexual difference varies by culture. But the fundamental reality of sexual difference does not.

Gender ideology collapses the distinction. It treats sexual difference itself as a social construct. The Bible maintains the distinction: sexual difference is created. Gender roles are culturally variable.

Third, on the question of dysphoria. Gender ideology says: if you experience your gender differently than your assigned sex, transition is the answer. Medical transition, social transition, legal transition. That is the path to relief.

The Bible does not address dysphoria specifically. But it offers something different: the gospel. The gospel says: your suffering is real. Your dysphoria matters. But the answer is not to escape your body. The answer is transformation—of your mind, your spirit, your relationship to your embodied existence. The resurrection is not escape from the body. It is transformation of the body. The Christian path is learning to be at peace in your actual embodied reality.

This is not the same as cruel dismissal. This is harder and deeper: actual transformation. Not medical alteration. Not social pretense. But spiritual and psychological transformation that helps you be at peace with who you actually are.

Fourth, on what freedom means. Gender ideology says: freedom is the ability to express whatever gender you feel you are. Freedom is affirming your gender identity. Freedom is access to medical transition if you want it. Freedom is being called by your preferred name and pronouns.

The Bible says something different. Freedom is freedom from sin, freedom from the corruption that distorts human relationships and human existence. Freedom is being reconciled to God and to your own created nature. Freedom is not always getting what you want. Freedom is sometimes learning to surrender, to accept, to find peace with reality as it is.

This does not mean the church should mock people. This does not mean we refuse to use someone's name or pronouns. But it does mean we do not pretend that affirming someone's gender identity is freedom if that affirmation prevents them from doing deeper work on their dysphoria.

\## The Limits of Ideology

Here is the thing about gender ideology: it promises more than it can deliver.

It promises that if you transition, you will finally feel at peace. If you live as your true gender, the dysphoria will go away. You will be happy.

This is not always true. Some people transition and feel liberated. Others transition and feel liberated initially, then experience regret. Others transition and find that dysphoria remains even after transition. The ideology promises certainty the reality does not deliver.

The gospel does not promise that you will feel comfortable in your body. The gospel promises something different: that you will have a resurrection body, that your suffering will be redeemed, that you will find your identity rooted in something deeper than your gender experience.

That is not the promise people want. But it is the promise that delivers.

The church's job is not to mock people for seeking comfort. The church's job is to offer them something better than ideology—a theological grid that takes seriously both their suffering and the deeper truth about who they are.

\## What the Church Should Teach

If the church is going to respond faithfully to gender ideology, what should we say?

First, we should affirm what is true in Scripture: sexual difference is real and created by God. Genesis 1:27 stands. Male and female He created them. This is not culturally conditioned. This is not oppressive. This is good.

Second, we should clarify what the Bible does not teach: that gender roles are absolute. The Bible is clear that how gender is expressed varies by culture and context. Jesus broke gender norms. The early church functioned in countercultural ways regarding gender. So while sexual difference is real, the specific role expectations are culturally variable.

Third, we should name what the Bible does teach about dysphoria's solution: not escape from the body but transformation through the gospel. Not denial of suffering but redemption of suffering. Not medical alteration but spiritual and psychological healing that helps you be at peace in your actual embodied existence.

Fourth, we should insist that people are more than their gender. More important than someone's gender identity is the fact that they are made in God's image. More foundational than your gender experience is your relationship with Jesus. More ultimate than whether you feel comfortable in your body is whether you are at peace with God.

Fifth, we should refuse the false choice. It is not: affirm everything about gender ideology or show cruelty to people with dysphoria. There is a third way. The way of truth-telling combined with genuine love. The way of clear theology combined with pastoral care. The way of refusing to reduce people to an ideology while also refusing to pretend ideology is gospel.

\## The Pastoral Response to Ideology

How do we actually do this in the room with someone?

If someone comes to you and says they are struggling with their gender, you do not start with ideology. You start with their suffering. "Tell me what you are experiencing. How long has this been happening? What have you tried? What do you hope for?"

You listen. You affirm that their suffering is real. You do not mock them. You do not dismiss them.

Then, you ask questions that help them think. Not questions that push them toward ideology but questions that help them explore their own experience. "What do you think this dysphoria is about? Do you think it is about your body not being right, or your relationship to your body? What would it mean to try to find peace in your actual body instead of trying to escape it?"

You offer them community. You offer them prayer. You offer them counseling that is not ideology-driven but person-focused. You offer them time and space to think, not pressure to move quickly toward transition.

You refuse to affirm ideology while also refusing to be cruel. You say: "I love you. Your suffering matters to me. And I believe there is a path toward healing that does not require you to reject your body or embrace ideology. Let's walk that path together."

This is harder than either cruelty or capitulation. But it is the path that actually serves people.

\## Why This Matters

The reason the church has to do this work is not political. It is pastoral.

There are young people in dysphoria right now. Genuine suffering. Real confusion about their body and their gender. They need the church to offer them something more than ideology on either side.

They need the church to say: "You are made in God's image. Your suffering is real. And your answer is not to escape your body or to embrace ideology that promises certainty about your gender. Your answer is the gospel. Transformation. Learning to be at peace in your actual embodied existence. Learning to find your identity in Christ rather than in gender. Learning to live in the tension between the physical reality of your body and your internal sense of self."

This is what gender ideology fails to offer. This is what the gospel does.

The church's job is to know the difference. And to offer people the gospel instead of ideology.

\## EXPANSION: Judith Butler, John Money, and the Distinction Between Sex and Gender

Contemporary gender ideology often traces its intellectual roots to Judith Butler and earlier theorists. To understand the ideology, you need to understand what these thinkers actually said—and how their ideas have been simplified and weaponized.

Judith Butler, in "Gender Trouble," argued that gender is performative. Gender is something we do, not something we are. Butler was arguing against the idea that gender is biologically determined. Butler was right that gender as expressed culturally is not biologically determined.

But Butler's claim that gender is "performative" is often reduced to: "Gender is whatever you perform it as." That is a misreading.

Butler's actual claim is more complex: gender is a repeated performance that creates the illusion of a natural identity. Butler is arguing that we are all performing gender all the time. The fact that you feel your gender as natural does not mean it is biologically determined. It means you have internalized the performance so thoroughly that it feels natural.

This is an argument about how gender is socially constructed. It is not an argument that gender is entirely voluntary or that anyone can claim any gender at any time.

John Money, a sexologist, coined the term "gender identity" in the 1950s. Money argued that there was a distinction between biological sex (the chromosomes, the anatomy, the hormones) and gender identity (the internal sense of being male or female). Money did important work in recognizing that sexual development is complex and that people can have atypical sexual development.

But Money also conducted the infamous David Reimer experiment. Reimer was a boy whose penis was damaged in a circumcision accident. Money convinced the parents to raise the child as a girl, believing that gender identity was purely socially constructed. Money was wrong. Reimer lived as a girl in constant distress, eventually transitioned back to living as a male, and in the end died by suicide.

The Reimer case teaches an important lesson: gender identity is not purely socially constructed. Raising someone as a gender contrary to their sense of self creates deep distress. But this does not mean the opposite—that gender identity is purely biological. It means it is complex.

Contemporary gender ideology has often taken Butler's idea of gender as performative and Money's idea of gender identity as distinct from sex, and combined them into a claim: gender is entirely internal, entirely chosen, entirely performative. If you perform a gender, you are that gender.

This is a step beyond what the original theorists were arguing.

Here is the distinction the church needs to make:

Sex is biological. It is real. It is created. It has chromosomes, hormones, anatomy. People vary in how these develop, but sexual difference is real.

Gender is the cultural expression of sexual difference. How do men act? How do women act? These vary by culture. These are socially constructed.

Gender identity is the internal sense of what gender you are. This seems to be formed through a complex interaction of biology, environment, psychology, social factors. It is not purely biological. It is not purely social. It is complex.

A person can believe all three of these things without affirming gender ideology. You can believe that sex is real, that gender expression is culturally variable, and that gender identity is complex. You can hold all three while still believing that medical transition in young people requires extreme caution, that ideology is not the same as truth, and that the gospel offers a different path.

The church's job is to be precise about what gender ideology actually claims, to distinguish that from what the original theorists claimed, and to refuse to collapse all these distinctions into one claim.

Most of the church has not done this work. That failure of precision has cost credibility.

\## EXPANSION: The Limits of Ideology on Both Sides — What Neither Side Can Deliver

The chapter on gender ideology identifies what the ideology promises and what the church should offer instead. But it is worth being specific about what neither ideology can deliver.

Left-wing gender ideology promises: If you transition, if you live as your true gender, if you find community that affirms your identity, you will be happy. You will finally be yourself. Your dysphoria will go away.

This promise is not always true. Some people transition and feel liberated. Some transition and find their dysphoria remains. Some transition, initially feel liberated, then later regret it. Some transition and regret certain aspects while affirming others.

The ideology does not account for complexity. It does not account for the fact that some dysphoria is rooted in trauma, and transition will not heal trauma. It does not account for the fact that some dysphoria is rooted in depression, and transition will not cure depression. It does not account for the fact that some gender confusion is rooted in peer pressure or social contagion, and that affirming the identity will not resolve the underlying confusion.

Right-wing ideology promises: If you accept your biological sex, if you resist the lie of gender ideology, if you trust God's design, dysphoria will go away. You will find peace.

This promise is also not always true. Some people do find peace by accepting their biological sex. But some people accept their biological sex and still experience dysphoria. Some people spend years in prayer and acceptance and never fully resolve the dysphoria. Some people accept their biological sex and find peace in meaning and purpose, even though the dysphoria remains.

The right-wing ideology does not account for the fact that dysphoria is real and sometimes persistent. It does not account for the fact that accepting something and being at peace with it are not the same thing. It does not account for the fact that some people genuinely need help managing dysphoria, and that help might look like acceptance of what is, not escape from what is.

What the gospel offers is different from both. The gospel does not promise that you will get what you want. The gospel promises that you are loved. That you are made in God's image. That your identity is rooted in Christ. That transformation is possible—not the transformation you might want, but the transformation that leads to actual peace.

This is harder than either ideology. It does not promise quick relief. It promises something deeper: belonging, meaning, identity rooted in something other than gender.

Neither ideology can deliver what the gospel delivers. That is the point.

The progressive side of the gender conversation makes a necessary claim: you cannot reduce a person to their biology. Body is not destiny. A woman is more than her reproductive capacity. A man is more than his testosterone. This claim is important. It is also incomplete.

The conservative side makes its own necessary claim: body matters. It is not incidental to identity. The design of the body is not arbitrary. Male and female are not interchangeable categories but complementary realities with different kinds of meaning and different kinds of responsibility. This claim is also important. This claim is also incomplete.

The progressive side, in making its necessary claim, has often made an unnecessary one: if biology does not determine destiny, then biology does not determine anything. The body becomes functionally irrelevant except as a canvas for the self to decorate. This erases the material reality of embodiment. It treats the body as a choice rather than a given. It leaves no room for the question: what does it mean to live faithfully as an embodied creature? What does my body teach me about what I am?

The conservative side, in making its necessary claim, has often made an unnecessary one: if body matters, then body is destiny. The design of the body determines the shape of a life. Sex is not a category humans handle but a predetermined path humans walk. This erases the mystery of personhood. It leaves no room for the question: what does it mean that I experience my body differently than others experience theirs?

Both sides have gotten something right and something wrong. Both sides have also used their rightness to avoid their blindness.

The conservative instinct to honor the body's design is necessary. The human body is not a mistake. Maleness and femaleness are not neutral categories. They are laden with meaning — not determined by that meaning, but shaped by it. A woman's body is designed for certain kinds of power and vulnerability. A man's body is designed for certain kinds of strength and protection. To ignore this design is not liberation. It is a different kind of lie.

But the conservative side has too often weaponized this truth. It has used the design of the body as an excuse for the abuse of the body. It has said: your body determines your role, and your role is to be compliant. Your body is designed for submission. Your body is designed for service. In doing this, it has confused the created design of the body with the corrupted use of the body. It has treated gender roles as though they descended from heaven rather than emerged from cultures shaped by sin.

The progressive instinct to expand beyond biological determinism is necessary. Not every man is suited for every male role. Not every woman is suited for every female role. Some women are called to leadership and authority. Some men are called to nurture and support. To insist that these callings are violations of biology rather than expressions of human complexity within biology is to build a cage and call it a creation.

But the progressive side has too often abandoned the body entirely. It has treated the experience of being embodied as a problem to be solved rather than a mystery to be inhabited. It has said: biology does not matter at all except that it is oppressive. The solution is to transcend it, to choose your own meaning, to be free from the constraints of the body. In doing this, it has confused liberation from oppression with liberation from embodiment itself. It has created a different kind of exile.

The humility required to hold this conversation is willingness to admit that you have gotten something wrong even in what you are getting right.

The conservative needs to admit: you have used the design of the body to justify the domination of bodies. You have confused what bodies are designed for with what humans are justified in doing to bodies. You have created systems that crush women who were called to lead and men who were called to nurture. Your rightness about the body's design does not excuse your wrongness about the body's dignity.

The progressive needs to admit: you have treated embodiment as an obstacle to overcome rather than a reality to handle. You have created space for people to question their assigned roles, which was necessary. But you have also created pressure for people to transcend their embodiment entirely, which is impossible. You have left people suspended between their body and their chosen identity with no language for integration.

The biblical vision is neither progressive nor conservative. It honors the body without treating it as destiny. It affirms the design of male and female without weaponizing those categories. It makes room for the man who is made for tenderness, the woman who is made for authority, and the complexity that defies easy categorization. It does not treat the body as irrelevant. It does not treat the body as determinative.

What is required is the capacity to hold both truths without one collapsing into the other. Your body is not destiny. Your body also matters. Your sex is not your entire identity. Your sex is not nothing. You are not free to be entirely self-created. You are not imprisoned by your creation.

This conversation will not be resolved by arguments. It will be resolved by people willing to say: I have been wrong in the way I have applied this truth. I have let my rightness become an excuse for someone else's pain. What does faithfulness look like that honors both the design and the dignity, both the given and the chosen?

Until both sides are willing to admit their blindness, the conversation will remain a battle between two incomplete visions each defended by people convinced of their own completeness.

\# CHAPTER 6: CHILDREN, PUBERTY BLOCKERS, AND THE CHURCH'S RESPONSIBILITY

\## What Every Christian Parent Needs to Know

A thirteen-year-old assigned female at birth starts talking about being a boy. The parents are confused. They do not know if this is a phase. They do not know if their child is experiencing dysphoria or if something else is happening. They search online and find two camps: one saying immediate transition is necessary and that waiting causes trauma, another saying it is a phase and if you affirm anything you are harming your child.

The parents are paralyzed. They do not know what to do. They know their child is struggling. They do not know if the struggle is dysphoria or something else—trauma, peer influence, depression, body dysphoria (hating one's body) which is different from dysphoria (experiencing one's gender as misaligned).

The church has largely failed these families. Either we have said: "This is a sin, pray away the confusion," or we have said: "Affirm your child's gender identity and if you do not, you are a bigot." Neither serves the family actually trying to love a child and figure out what is happening.

This chapter is for those parents. And for churches trying to help those parents.

\## The Rise of Gender-Questioning Youth

First, a fact that is not debated: the number of young people questioning their gender has risen significantly in the past decade.

Surveys from 2021-2024 show that approximately 20% of Gen Z identifies as something other than cisgender and heterosexual. This is substantially higher than previous generations. The rise is particularly notable in assigned females who now describe themselves as trans or non-binary.

What explains this rise? There are several factors, and honest analysis means acknowledging all of them:

Social acceptance has increased. A generation ago, a young person with questions about gender could not explore them safely. Now, in many communities, there is space to ask questions. This is not bad. More space to think is better than no space. But it does mean more young people will explore these questions.

Social contagion is real. When a peer transitions or questions gender, it influences other peers. This is not unique to gender. When one friend gets piercings, others often follow. When one friend struggles with depression, the concept becomes more visible to friends. Social influence is part of how young people develop identity. Gender questions are no exception.

Online communities and social media accelerate this. A young person can find community online around gender identity questions in ways they could not before. This creates both opportunity and risk: opportunity to find people with similar questions, risk of being driven toward particular conclusions by algorithms and community pressure.

Mental health is connected. Many young people questioning gender also experience depression, anxiety, autism, or trauma. The relationship between these is complex. But honest analysis means acknowledging that gender questioning sometimes co-occurs with other mental health struggles.

The ideological environment has shifted. Gender ideology has become mainstream in schools, libraries, and some churches. This is not neutral. It influences young people's thinking about their own experience. A young person who a decade ago might have experienced their gender dysphoria privately now has language for it, community around it, and ideological reinforcement that transition is the answer.

None of these factors explain everything. But together, they explain why so many more young people are now exploring gender questions than in the past.

The question for parents and churches is: How do we respond?

\## What Puberty Blockers Actually Are

Let's be clear and specific about medical interventions.

Puberty blockers are medications (typically GnRH agonists) that suppress the hormones that trigger puberty. They pause pubertal development. They do not cause permanent changes. If stopped, puberty typically resumes.

The medications have been used for decades in other contexts: to treat precocious puberty (puberty that happens too early) and, off-label, for gender dysphoria.

What do puberty blockers do? They pause pubertal development. They prevent the development of secondary sex characteristics—breast development, testicular development, voice changes, body hair. They prevent menstruation. They prevent the height growth spurt that typically happens in puberty.

The claimed benefit: they give time for exploration. A young person questioning their gender can take time without their body changing in ways they find dysphoric. This buys time for therapy, for thinking, for determining what is really happening.

The risks: not fully understood. Puberty blockers have not been used in gender dysphoria for decades. We do not have long-term outcome data. We know they can affect bone density. We know they can affect sexual function. We know they can affect fertility. But long-term consequences on developing brains and bodies are not fully known.

The critical point: puberty blockers are not neutral. They are a medical intervention. They change the trajectory of development. To say they are just a pause is to understate what they do. Pausing puberty at age thirteen is not the same as pausing puberty at age seventeen. The window of pubertal development has consequences that are not reversible in the same way the medication is reversible.

Parents are often told: "Puberty blockers are reversible. You can always stop them and puberty will resume." This is technically true. But puberty that resumes at sixteen after being paused at thirteen is different from puberty that happens at thirteen. Development happens in windows. Disrupting those windows has effects.

A Christian parent has to understand what puberty blockers actually are before deciding whether to pursue them.

\## The Detransition Question: What the Data Shows

One thing the affirming side of this debate rarely brings up is detransition.

Some young people transition socially or medically, then later detransition. The numbers vary depending on the study. Some research suggests 1-8% of people who transition socially detransition. The numbers for medical transition regret are higher—some research suggests 8-40% of young people on puberty blockers or hormones later regret it or stop treatment.

Why? The reasons vary. Some realized their dysphoria was related to trauma or abuse. Some realized it was related to depression or anxiety that needed different treatment. Some realized their gender questioning was connected to peer influence or social contagion. Some transitioned under pressure from affirming communities and later felt they were not given space to explore other options. Some changed their minds about their gender. Some regretted the physical changes of hormones. Some regretted the loss of fertility.

This is not proof that transition is always wrong. It is proof that transition is not always right. And it means parents have a responsibility to slow down, to think carefully, to ensure their child is not being pushed too quickly into irreversible decisions.

The church's job is not to forbid transition. The church's job is to help families think carefully. To ensure that medical decisions are made with actual informed consent—not ideology-driven consent where one option is constantly affirmed and others are dismissed as bigotry.

\## What Children Actually Need

Here is what a child or adolescent questioning their gender actually needs.

First, they need safety. They need to know that questioning their gender will not result in rejection, ridicule, or loss of love. If a young person is hiding their feelings about gender because they fear parental response, they will not get help. They will seek it elsewhere—in online communities that may be ideologically driven.

Second, they need time. Not time to transition. Time to explore. Time to think. Time to talk to trusted adults. Time to understand what they are experiencing. Gender questioning in early adolescence often resolves by adulthood. Pushing toward quick conclusions works against time doing its work.

Third, they need actual therapy—not affirmative therapy that quickly affirms whatever gender the young person expresses, but exploratory therapy that helps them understand what is happening. What is the actual dysphoria? Where did it come from? What does it connect to? Is there trauma? Is there depression? Is there peer influence? Is there something else going on? A good therapist asks these questions. An ideology-driven therapist assumes the answer and affirms it.

Fourth, they need information. Young people need to understand what medical interventions actually do. Not the propaganda from either side, but actual information. What do puberty blockers do? What are the risks? What are the benefits? What are the reversible and irreversible effects? Young people deserve informed consent, not one-sided information.

Fifth, they need community. Not an online community of gender-affirming ideology, but actual relationship with adults who love them, who can help them think, who can walk with them through confusion. The church should be that community.

Sixth, they need you to let them be confused. The pressure in current culture is to have an answer. Either "you are definitely trans and you need to transition" or "you are confused and it will pass." Young people are told they need to know who they are, what their gender is, what to call themselves. This is unfair. Adolescence is a time of exploration. Let them explore. Let them ask questions without forcing them to choose.

\## What the Church Should Tell Parents

If a Christian parent comes to you and says: "My child is questioning their gender. What do I do?"

Say this: "First, you love your child. That is the foundation. Nothing changes that."

Then: "Second, slow down. There is no rush. If this is genuine dysphoria, it will persist. If it is a phase, you will know that too. Either way, you have time. Do not let anyone pressure you into quick decisions."

Then: "Third, seek good therapy. Not affirmative therapy that assumes your child is trans and needs to transition. But exploratory therapy that helps your child understand what they are experiencing. Is it dysphoria? Is it trauma? Is it depression? Is it peer influence? A good therapist asks these questions."

Then: "Fourth, get information. Read from sources that are not ideologically driven. Understand what medical interventions actually are. Understand the risks. Understand what reversible and irreversible means."

Then: "Fifth, create safety. Your child needs to know they can talk to you. They need to know that questioning their gender will not result in rejection. But they also need to know that you will tell them the truth, even if the truth is uncomfortable."

Then: "Sixth, hold your convictions. You believe sexual difference is real and created by God. You believe the body matters. You believe gender ideology is not the answer. Hold those convictions clearly and lovingly. Your child needs to know what you actually believe, not just what you think they want to hear."

Then: "Seventh, do not abandon your child into ideology on either side. Do not say: 'Go figure this out with your peers or your online community.' Do not say: 'I wash my hands of this.' Stay involved. Stay loving. Stay engaged. Your child needs you."

\## The Medical Establishment's Caution

This is important to note: even the medical establishment is increasingly cautious about rapid transition for young people.

Sweden, in 2023, recommended that gender-questioning youth be offered talk therapy first, not immediate medical transition. The Swedish National Board of Health and Welfare, after reviewing the evidence, concluded that the evidence for medical transition in young people is insufficient and that psychological support should be primary.

Similar caution is growing in other countries. The evidence is not sufficient to support rapid transition, particularly medical transition, for young people.

This does not mean transition is never appropriate. It means the medical establishment itself is saying: slow down. Think carefully. Do not assume medical transition is the answer. Ensure young people have psychological support and time to think before medical decisions are made.

The church should echo this caution. Not as a political statement, but as a pastoral one. We care about young people. And young people deserve careful, thoughtful, non-ideological medical care.

\## Application: The Church's Role

What is the church's actual responsibility here?

First, be a safe community. Make it clear that young people questioning gender will not be mocked, will not be rejected, will not be abandoned. This is non-negotiable.

Second, teach the theology. Make sure young people understand what Scripture teaches about the body, about sexual difference, about identity in Christ. Do not assume they know this. Teach it clearly and lovingly.

Third, help families. Offer resources to families with gender-questioning young people. Offer counseling. Offer community. Help families think carefully, not ideologically.

Fourth, refuse both failures. Do not let your church be a place where gender-questioning young people are mocked. And do not let your church be a place where ideological affirmation is the only option. Offer something better: truth-telling combined with genuine love.

Fifth, pray. Pray for young people in confusion. Pray for their parents. Pray that they will find their way to clarity and peace—the real kind, not ideology-driven, but the kind that comes from being at peace with their actual embodied existence and their identity in Christ.

Young people questioning gender are not enemies. They are young people in confusion who deserve the church to be the safest place they can be—safe to ask questions, safe to be confused, safe to explore, safe to come to different conclusions than the culture suggests.

If the church can be that place, we will win far more hearts than any other strategy.

\## EXPANSION: Rapid Onset Gender Dysphoria, Social Contagion, and the Questions Nobody Asks

In the past ten years, clinicians began noticing something unusual. A significant number of adolescents—particularly assigned females—were reporting gender dysphoria for the first time without any prior history of gender nonconformity.

The pattern was: typically a young person who had not shown gender dysphoria in childhood suddenly reported dysphoria and requested transition in early adolescence, often after their peer group had affirmed this identity.

Some clinicians described this as "Rapid Onset Gender Dysphoria" (ROGD). The term was controversial because it suggested that the dysphoria was not the primary phenomenon but rather a response to social influence.

The research on ROGD is contested. Some studies support the reality of this pattern. Others suggest that the data is more complex. But honest analysis requires acknowledging: some young people experience dysphoria that correlates with peer group influence.

This does not mean all gender dysphoria is socially influenced. This does not mean that social contagion is the only explanation. But it does mean that social contagion is one factor.

Consider what the social environment does now that would not have existed ten years ago:

Social media algorithms. If a young person expresses any uncertainty about gender, the algorithm will show them content from trans communities, trans creators, and trans ideology. The algorithm will create a feed that affirms one particular conclusion.

Online communities. A young person can find community online around being transgender. These communities can be supportive. They can also push toward particular conclusions. The influence is real.

School policies. Many schools now have policies that allow students to use preferred names and pronouns without parental notification. The school environment actively affirms gender identity exploration.

Peer effects. When one peer transitions, other peers explore their own gender. This is not unique to gender. Adolescents have always influenced each other. But the speed and intensity of influence is amplified by social media.

A parent needs to understand: when your adolescent comes out as transgender, it is not enough to ask "Is this real dysphoria?" You also need to ask: "What is the role of peer influence, social media, online communities, and school environment in shaping how my child is thinking about their gender?"

This is not to deny the reality of dysphoria. It is to understand that adolescence involves exploration, and that exploration is now taking place in an environment that is actively shaped to push toward certain conclusions.

The church needs to help families ask these questions without shame. "Your child may be experiencing real dysphoria. Your child may also be experiencing social influence. These are not mutually exclusive. How do we understand both?"

That is the question the church should help families think through.

\## EXPANSION: The Parental Dilemma — When You Have to Make Decisions Without Certainty

A parent whose child is expressing gender dysphoria faces an impossible situation. Whatever decision the parent makes—whether to support social transition, whether to consider medical intervention, whether to seek particular kinds of therapy—could be wrong.

The parent cannot get certainty. The science is incomplete. The theology is contested. The child's future is unknowable.

Yet the parent has to decide. The child cannot wait while the parent achieves certainty that will never come.

What should the parent do?

First: Slow down. You do not have to decide immediately. You do not have to choose a path in response to cultural pressure from either side. Take time. Think carefully. Get information.

Second: Seek good professionals. A therapist who will help your child understand their experience, not someone who will quickly affirm or quickly condemn. A doctor who is cautious, not someone who sees your child's dysphoria as an obvious indication for medical intervention.

Third: Talk to your child. Not with the goal of convincing them to see things your way. With the goal of understanding what they are experiencing. "Tell me about this. When did you start feeling this way? What does it feel like? What do you hope will happen?"

Fourth: Remember that your child's questioning is not the same as permanent identity. Adolescence involves exploration. Gender questioning in adolescence often resolves. This does not mean your child is confused. It means your child is normal. Let them explore without rushing them toward permanent decisions.

Fifth: Hold your convictions. You believe some things about the body and gender. Hold those convictions. Teach them to your child. But do not use your convictions as a weapon. Use them as a foundation.

Sixth: Create safety. Your child needs to know that questioning their gender will not result in rejection or loss of love. If your child fears your response, they will not come to you. They will go elsewhere.

Seventh: Be honest about uncertainty. You do not have all the answers. The world does not have all the answers. Admitting this is not weakness. It is honesty.

The parent's job is not to solve this immediately. The parent's job is to love the child, to help the child think carefully, to refuse to abandon the child to ideology on either side, and to walk with the child through a genuinely confusing time.

That is enough. That is not everything. But it is enough.

\## EXPANSION: The Child, the Adolescent, the Adult — Three Different Conversations

The question of what the church should do when a young person experiences gender dysphoria requires three at its core different answers depending on age, because the developmental reality is at its core different. The same response that would be appropriate for a child would be harmful for an adolescent. The same response appropriate for an adolescent would be inappropriately patronizing for an adult.

A child around seven years old is expressing that they think they are a different gender from what adults assigned at birth. This is worth taking seriously, but it requires a different response than an adolescent's questioning or an adult's persistent dysphoria.

At seven, a child is still developing a sense of self. The child's identity is not yet formed. Children at this age engage in imaginative play, question everything, and often change their minds about identity, interests, and preferences. A child might insist they are a dinosaur. A child might insist they are a different gender. Both are part of how children explore and understand reality.

Some children at this age who express gender questioning later identify with their biological sex. Some continue to experience dysphoria. Some express dysphoria for a period and then it resolves. The pattern is not predictable.

The pastoral response to a family with a seven-year-old expressing gender dysphoria should be: slow down. This is not an emergency. This does not require immediate social transition. This child needs psychological evaluation and support, not ideology on either side. The family should be helped to understand what the child is experiencing.

Questions worth exploring: Does the child feel comfortable in their body? Is the dysphoria consistent across contexts or does it come and go? Has something happened—trauma, peer influence, media exposure—that might have triggered this questioning? Are there other signs of neurodivergence or anxiety that might be playing a role? Are there other children in the peer group also expressing gender questioning? How did the child learn this language? Did they generate it themselves or pick it up from others?

The church's job is not to diagnose. The church's job is to encourage the family not to panic, to seek professional help, to give the child time to understand themselves, and to ensure the child knows they are loved regardless of how this unfolds. A seven-year-old who is confused about gender does not need to make permanent decisions. The child needs time.

An adolescent around fourteen or fifteen is requesting social transition and raising the question of hormones. This is developmentally different from the seven-year-old. Adolescence is a time of identity formation. Questions about gender are normal. Some adolescents work through questioning and end up identifying with their biological sex. Some do not. Some explore for years before deciding. Some change their minds multiple times.

But adolescence is also a time of significant physical change, psychological vulnerability, and peer influence. The adolescent's brain is not fully developed, particularly in areas related to long-term decision-making and impulse control. The prefrontal cortex—the part of the brain responsible for evaluating long-term consequences—is not fully mature until the mid-twenties. The adolescent's sense of identity is not yet stable. The adolescent is uniquely susceptible to peer influence and to adopting identities that make sense of distress.

The pastoral response should be more deliberate. This is a time for real conversation. The family should understand what the adolescent is experiencing. When did the dysphoria start? Was it gradual or sudden? Are there other adolescents in the peer group expressing similar things? Is the adolescent following certain content creators or online communities that normalize gender questioning? Has the adolescent experienced trauma? Is there depression or anxiety underlying the dysphoria? What does the adolescent hope will happen? What does the adolescent fear?

This requires professional psychological evaluation—not therapy designed to affirm the identity quickly, but therapy designed to help the adolescent understand themselves. The church should encourage this. The church should also encourage the family not to make permanent medical decisions before the adolescent is older.

What should this look like practically? Social transition—using chosen names and pronouns—can happen without medical intervention. This allows the adolescent to explore identity without irreversible physical changes. This is not rejection. It is wisdom. The adolescent can explore at a pace that allows for genuine discernment.

Medical decisions about puberty blockers or hormones should wait until the adolescent is older, ideally until mid-to-late adolescence or early adulthood, and should require thorough psychological evaluation. This is not prohibition. It is caution appropriate to the developmental stage. The adolescent's brain is developing. The adolescent's identity is developing. It is reasonable to allow that development to occur before making irreversible physical changes.

A young person around sixteen or seventeen who has experienced persistent dysphoria since early adolescence might be different. But even then, the standard should be high. Extensive psychological evaluation. Exploration of other conditions. Second opinions. Slower timelines than what is often recommended.

An adult around eighteen or older who has experienced persistent dysphoria for years and is now requesting medical transition is in a different situation. The person has achieved legal adulthood. The person has had time to think through options. The dysphoria is not new. The person has presumably had time to consider what other conditions might be playing a role.

The church's response should be different. This is less about protection from rushed decision-making, though that still matters. This is more about honoring the autonomy of an adult while maintaining the church's theological convictions.

The church can say: "We believe that bodies are created good by God. We believe that accepting embodied existence, rather than escaping it, is the path to peace. We believe that transformation comes not through medical fixing of the body but through grace and acceptance. We also honor your right to make decisions about your own body. We do not fully affirm this choice, but we do not reject you. We remain in relationship with you. We will call you by your chosen name. We invite you into our community. But we cannot pretend to believe what we do not believe about what it means to accept embodied existence. And we want to walk with you toward actual peace, not escape."

This is harder than either complete affirmation or complete rejection. But it is the path of genuine pastoral care that holds conviction and compassion.

The mistake churches often make is treating all three situations the same way. A seven-year-old needs time and evaluation and space to develop. A fifteen-year-old needs space to explore without permanent medical changes. An eighteen-year-old deserves respect for their autonomy. The pastoral response in each case should be proportionate to the actual situation.

A pastor should be trained to ask: How old is this person? How long have they experienced dysphoria? What other conditions might be present? What professional evaluation have they received? What does their family think? What does the broader community look like? Are they under pressure to make decisions? Is there time to slow down?

These questions are not dismissive. They are what care looks like.

\# CHAPTER 7: PRONOUNS, BATHROOMS, AND THE DAILY QUESTIONS

\## Practical Ethics for Christians in a Changing World

A coworker tells you they are transitioning and asks you to use they/them pronouns.

A teacher in your church asks if it is appropriate to use a student's chosen name instead of their given name.

Your church leadership is debating whether to allow transgender people to use bathrooms that match their gender identity.

A family member comes out as trans and your extended family is fighting about how to respond.

These are the questions that the church debates endlessly—pronouns, bathrooms, names. The questions seem small until they are not. Until they are the difference between feeling welcome and feeling rejected. Until they are the thing that keeps someone from church. Until they are the line in the sand that determines whether someone feels the church sees them as human.

The church has not done the work to give clear, thoughtful, non-ideological guidance on these daily questions. So people default to their politics. Or their feelings. Or the loudest voice in the room.

You need something better. You need theology that tells you what to do when you encounter these questions in real life.

\## The Pronouns Question

Let's start with the simplest and somehow most contentious: pronouns.

Someone asks you to use they/them pronouns instead of she/her. What do you do?

The political right often treats pronouns as a line in the sand: "I will not do this. Pronouns are biology. I will not participate in a lie." The political left treats it as a basic human dignity: "If you refuse to use someone's pronouns, you are committing violence against them."

Both positions miss something important.

First: pronouns are not nothing. Language matters. The way we refer to someone affects how they are treated. If someone asks you to use pronouns different from their biological sex, they are asking for something that matters to them. To dismiss that as trivial is to dismiss what matters to that person.

Second: pronouns are not everything. Pronouns refer to biological reality or they do not. Using they/them instead of she/her does not change anyone's biology. If biology is real and created by God, then pronouns are descriptive or they are not. To treat pronouns as if they are more important than they are is to make them into an idol.

Here is what I think pastorally and theologically: if someone asks you to use pronouns different from their biological sex, you can do it. It is not a compromise of your faith. It is not affirming gender ideology. It is treating someone with basic dignity.

But you do not have to do it. It is not sinful to use pronouns that correspond to biological sex. It is not bigotry. It is one option among several.

What you cannot do, if you love the person, is mock them for asking. You cannot treat the request as an attack. You cannot use their request as an opportunity to declare your beliefs about gender. You can respectfully use their pronouns, or you can respectfully decline and suggest referring to them by name instead.

But if someone is in genuine dysphoria, and using different pronouns helps them feel more at home in community, and that costs you nothing except a small linguistic shift, the gospel counsel is clear: lay down your preferences for someone else's peace.

This does not mean capitulating to ideology. It means choosing kindness when kindness costs little.

\## The Practical Counsel on Pronouns

Here is what I suggest to churches:

Create space for people to share pronouns if they want to. Some trans or non-binary people want their pronouns known. Some do not. Let it be a choice, not a requirement.

Use the pronouns people ask for when you are with them. This is basic respect. You call someone by the name they prefer. You use the pronouns they request when you are in their presence. This is what we do for people we love.

But do not demand it from everyone. Do not make pronoun agreement a test of faith or orthodoxy. Do not shame someone who uses pronouns that correspond to biological sex.

Do not use preferred pronouns as a way to mock someone. Do not say you are using pronouns you do not "believe in" as a cynical performance. Do not create an us-versus-them dynamic around pronouns.

Teach people that pronouns are courtesy, not theology. They are a way of showing respect, not a declaration of theological truth.

And teach this: if someone cannot remember the pronouns you request, and they are genuinely trying, extend grace. Memory fails. People make mistakes. If someone is trying to honor your request and stumbles, that is not violence. That is human limitation.

The goal is simple: make space for people to be known, to be called by the names and pronouns that matter to them, without making pronouns into a battlefield.

\## The Bathroom Question

Then there are bathrooms.

This is where theology becomes policy. And policy becomes a cultural battle.

The question: Should a transgender person use the bathroom that corresponds to their gender identity or their biological sex?

There is genuine tension here. Someone who is early in transition, whose appearance does not yet match their identity, will feel unsafe in either bathroom. The church's job is not to eliminate that discomfort—you cannot—but to minimize it and to ensure safety.

Here is what I think theologically: the body is real. Biological sex is real. Some people are uncomfortable being naked in spaces with people of a different biological sex. That discomfort is understandable. But it is also not the end of the conversation.

Here is what I think pastorally: if a trans person in your congregation needs to use the restroom, the job of the church is to make a decision that protects their dignity and minimizes risk to others. This might mean a private restroom. It might mean signage that says "All Stalls Have Full Privacy." It might mean a policy that allows people to use the restroom that matches their identity. It depends on your context.

What you cannot do: treat a trans person in your church as a threat. What you cannot do: make bathroom access a test of acceptance. What you cannot do: design policies that protect straight people by harming trans people.

The rule is simple: love your neighbor as yourself. That neighbor is a trans person who needs to use the bathroom. How do you treat them with dignity while also respecting the comfort of others? That is a practical question, not an ideological one.

Different churches will answer it differently. But the answer should emerge from love, not from fear or ideology.

\## The Names Question

Then there is the question of names.

Someone you have known since childhood comes out as trans and asks you to use a different name. What do you do?

You use the new name. This is not complicated. You called someone by a nickname. You called someone by their maiden name. You learned to call someone by the name they preferred. You can learn to call someone by their new chosen name.

But—and this is important—you do not have to do it immediately, and you do not have to be ashamed if you stumble. Learning a new name takes time. If someone has been "Sarah" for thirty years and is now "James," your brain will stumble. You will accidentally use the old name. You will catch yourself and correct. This is human and normal.

What matters is: are you trying? Are you making an effort? Do you show respect for the name change?

In churches, this gets complicated because of how names are used. Prayer lists. Worship announcements. Email communications. Bureaucratic systems built around names. The church can make a decision about when and how to use the chosen name in different contexts.

But when you are talking to the person directly, you use the name they prefer. This is not a political statement. This is basic respect.

\## Workplaces, Schools, and the Harder Questions

These daily questions get harder in institutional contexts.

An employee transitions and asks to use a different name and pronouns at work. An administrator has to decide: do we change all systems? Do we use the preferred name in some contexts and not others? Do we make this announcement to the whole workplace?

A teacher in a public school is trans and students use pronouns the teacher prefers. Do parents have a right to know? Can the teacher insist students use preferred pronouns?

A child in a Christian school transitions and the parents ask the school to support the child's identity. The school leadership disagrees with gender ideology. How do you handle this?

These are genuinely hard. They involve competing values, institutional policy, legal considerations, and pastoral care all at once.

What I suggest: take each situation individually. Do not make grand ideological pronouncements that force specific outcomes. Work with the person involved to find a solution that respects their dignity while also respecting the institution's values and other stakeholders.

In a church context, you have more freedom to be explicitly Christian. You can say: "We believe biblical truth about gender. And we also believe you are made in God's image and we will treat you with respect." That is clear and it is both true.

In a secular workplace, the same principle applies but with different language: "We respect your identity and we will use the name and pronouns you prefer. We also have policies about bathrooms and privacy that apply to everyone."

The point is: you do not have to choose between ideology on one side or complete capitulation on the other. You can hold your values and treat people with respect.

\## What To Do In The Room

Here is what I tell people when they are actually in the room with someone who is trans or questioning:

First, listen more than you speak. Listen to their story. Listen to their suffering. Listen to why this matters to them. Do not assume you understand.

Second, ask genuine questions. Not rhetorical questions designed to trap them, but real questions: "Tell me your story. How long have you felt this way? What does it mean to you to be called by this name? What made you decide to transition?"

Third, honor what you can without compromising what you cannot. You may not agree with gender ideology, but you can use someone's preferred name. You may not believe in transition, but you can respect the person in front of you. You may not affirm their choices, but you can affirm their humanity.

Fourth, do not perform your convictions. Do not use the conversation as an opportunity to declare what you believe about gender. Do not make it about you. Make it about them. Later, in appropriate contexts, you can share your views. But when you are with them, the conversation is about understanding them, not educating them.

Fifth, love them as they are. Not after they repent. Not after they change their mind. Not after they come back to biological reality. Right now. As they are. This is what the gospel teaches. God loves us while we are still sinners. The church should love people while they are in whatever situation they are in.

Sixth, do not abandon them. This is where the church has failed most dramatically. Trans people have been abandoned by churches. Cast out. Condemned. Mocked. The church should be the opposite: the place that does not abandon you no matter how confused you are, no matter how far you have strayed from what we believe is true. You belong. God loves you. The church loves you. Even if we disagree about your choices.

\## The Limits of This Counsel

I want to be clear about what I am not saying.

I am not saying: affirm every choice. Medical transition is not something the church should simply affirm as obvious and good. The church should help people think carefully about the costs and consequences.

I am not saying: the church has no wisdom about the body and gender and identity. We do. Biblical theology of the body, of sexual difference, of what it means to be image-bearers—this matters. We should teach it.

I am not saying: there are no policies to set or decisions to make. Churches and institutions have to make real decisions about bathrooms, names, policies. Those decisions should be made thoughtfully, not ideologically.

What I am saying: the basic posture toward the person in front of you should be respect, not suspicion. Should be love, not judgment. Should be "how can we serve you in this community" not "how do we protect ourselves from you."

The daily questions—pronouns, bathrooms, names—these are the things that determine whether someone feels welcome. They are small in the scheme of theology. They are enormous in the scheme of someone's felt belonging.

The church's job is to get them right. Not politically right. Pastorally right. In a way that honors both truth and the person in front of you.

\## EXPANSION: Title IX, Bostock v. Clayton County, and the Legal Landscape Christians Navigate

The church does not exist in a vacuum. Christians work in places governed by law. A Christian employer has to understand employment law. A Christian teacher in a public school has to understand Title IX. A Christian healthcare provider has to understand healthcare law as it applies to gender.

In 2020, the Supreme Court ruled in Bostock v. Clayton County that the prohibition on sex discrimination in Title VII of the Civil Rights Act of 1964 includes discrimination on the basis of sexual orientation and gender identity.

What does this mean practically? It means a Christian employer cannot legally fire someone for being transgender. It means a Christian-owned business cannot legally refuse service based on transgender status. It means many of the positions Christians want to take on gender have become legally impossible.

This creates real tension. A Christian believes some things about gender and the body. A Christian also has to operate within the law.

How should a Christian handle this?

First, by understanding what the law actually says. Bostock covers employment discrimination. It covers public accommodations in some contexts. It does not require affirmation of ideology. It requires treating people with legal equality.

Second, by distinguishing between what the law requires and what the gospel requires. The law might require that you use someone's preferred name in employment context. The gospel requires that you treat that person with love and respect. These are not the same, but they can coexist.

Third, by making decisions as an institution. A Christian school might decide: "We believe sexual difference is created by God. We also serve students of all backgrounds. We will treat all students with respect and will use preferred names. But we will not require teachers to affirm gender ideology in curriculum." That is a coherent position.

Fourth, by accepting that the world is not friendly to Christian convictions on this issue, and by choosing your battles. You cannot win every battle. You can make decisions about where you stand clearly.

A Christian in HR has to learn the law so that you do not inadvertently discriminate while also maintaining your convictions about what you believe. A Christian teacher has to handle curriculum requirements while maintaining your theology. A Christian healthcare provider has to work within medical law while also thinking carefully about your own ethical boundaries.

The church should prepare Christians for this. Not by telling them to ignore the law. By helping them understand the law and think carefully about how to handle it with integrity.

Most churches have not done this work. The result is that Christians in professional contexts are left to figure this out alone.

\## EXPANSION: Practical Church Policy — Names, Bathrooms, Sports, Camps

The chapter on harm identifies specific harms. But what does repentance look like in concrete policy decisions? A church has to decide: how do we actually handle these questions in real time? Policy is not the same as theology, but policy flows from theology and creates the lived experience that shapes how people encounter the gospel.

Start with names. A young person in your youth group requests to be called by a name different from their legal name. Your policy should be: we will honor that request. Not as an affirmation of ideology, but as a basic matter of respect. Learning someone's chosen name is not the same as affirming a metaphysical claim about gender. It is basic courtesy.

This matters more than it might seem. When you use someone's name, you are honoring their identity. When you refuse to use someone's name, you are communicating something specific: "I do not fully accept who you are claiming to be. I am choosing to use the name you were assigned rather than the name you have chosen. This is an act of refusal."

A young person in dysphoria knows they are misaligned with the gender they were assigned. Every time someone uses their birth name, it reinforces the misalignment. Every time someone uses their chosen name, it is an act of respect that says: "I see you."

Your policy should be: a name change in your records. Counselors, youth leaders, volunteers are informed and expected to use the chosen name. If someone slips up occasionally, that is human. If it becomes a pattern, that is a problem and should be addressed. The message your church sends is clear: we see you and we honor your name.

This does not require theological affirmation. It requires basic respect.

Pronouns are more complex. A young person requests they/them pronouns. Your church should ask: can we accommodate this? Many churches can. Many leaders will find they/them pronouns unfamiliar, but learning them is not impossible. Your policy should allow for accommodation while also recognizing that some people may have genuine struggles with this.

What matters is the spirit in which you make the decision. If your church uses preferred pronouns as a form of pastoral care and respect, that is one thing. If you refuse to use preferred pronouns as a form of ideological resistance, that is something else. It is not a theological principled stand. It is a refusal to extend basic respect.

The argument that "they/them is not grammatically correct" is a bad argument. English is a living language. Language changes. Singular they has been used in English since the fourteenth century. "If someone comes to the door, ask them to wait" uses singular they and sounds grammatically correct to any native English speaker. If you can learn to say "you all" in the South, you can learn "they" as singular. If you refuse, it communicates rejection, not theological principle.

Your policy might be: preferred pronouns are used in private pastoral settings, in communications to the young person, and in settings where it does not create conflict. In public settings, the church uses respectful language that honors the person without necessarily using pronouns they object to. This is a reasonable middle ground.

What your policy should not do is use pronoun refusal as a form of ideological resistance.

Bathrooms require more complexity because there is a genuine practical issue. Single-stall bathrooms solve most of the problem. If your youth center has individual bathrooms, there is no conflict. Young people who are not socially transitioned use the bathroom matching their biological sex. Everyone maintains privacy and dignity.

If you do not have single-stall bathrooms, you have to think carefully. Your policy might be: young people who are not yet medically transitioned use the bathroom matching their biological sex. Young people on hormone therapy may request to use the bathroom matching their gender identity. You make decisions case by case, always with the goal of safety and dignity for everyone.

This is not ideology. This is pragmatism grounded in concern for physical safety and reasonable accommodation.

What matters is that your policy is clear and applied with respect. A young person should know what the rule is before they encounter it. And the rule should be applied consistently.

Sports and camps require similar thought. A young person who was assigned male at birth but identifies as female requests to continue playing on the girls' soccer team after social transition. Your decision depends on whether your league rules allow it. If your league follows state high school athletic association rules, check those rules. They are evolving. If your church organizes the league, you get to decide.

A reasonable policy might be: for recreational leagues where physical differences do not significantly impact fairness or safety, we prioritize inclusion. For competitive leagues where there are significant physical differences that create fairness issues, we follow biological sex. This is not rejection. It is a recognition that different contexts have different considerations.

At camps, the issue is usually sleeping arrangements and bathroom facilities. A reasonable policy might be: young people sleep in cabins based on the gender they identify with, unless there is a significant safety concern. All sleeping areas are supervised appropriately. All bathroom facilities offer privacy. The goal is dignity and safety for everyone.

What matters in all of this is that your church is not primarily driven by either ideology. You are driven by the question: how do we love people? How do we treat them with dignity? How do we help them belong?

That requires policy. But policy should flow from pastoral care, not from political positioning. The goal is not to be right. The goal is to be faithful.

The deeper question is this: how do we hold conviction about what we believe regarding embodiment while also holding space for people whose embodied experience is confused and painful? This is the tension every Christian community faces.

One church solves this by abandoning conviction. They affirm everything because they fear rejection is cruelty. They offer belonging but no truth. A young person can be part of that community. But they are not being helped. They are being left alone in their confusion.

Another church solves this by abandoning compassion. They hold clear doctrine but refuse to build bridges to people living differently. A young person in dysphoria is not welcome. Or if they are welcome, it is conditional on agreeing with the church's theology. That is not welcome. That is coercion.

A third way exists. Your church can say: We believe something. We hold it carefully. We know what Scripture teaches. But we are bigger than our agreement on this one issue. You can be part of our community while you are wrestling. You can disagree with us and still belong. We will use your name. We will treat you with respect. We will help you think. We will walk with you.

This requires training. It requires clarity. It requires your leadership to be unified in conviction while also being unified in compassion. It requires your small groups to be places where people do not have to pretend. It requires your pastoral staff to be equipped to have complex conversations. It requires your congregation to see disagreement as normal, not as failure.

This is not easy. Churches that do this will face criticism from both sides. Progressive advocates will say you are not affirming enough. Conservative advocates will say you are compromising. You will disappoint everyone. And that is the mark that you are doing something right.

Policy matters because it shapes daily experience. A policy to honor chosen names shapes how a young person is treated every single week they come to church. A policy on pronouns shapes whether that young person feels respected or rejected. A policy on bathrooms shapes whether that young person feels safe in your building. A policy on sports shapes whether that young person can participate in youth group activities.

These are not abstract theological questions. These are practical decisions that say to a young person: you matter. You belong. We see you.

That is what the church should offer. Not ideology. Not dismissal. Belonging with honesty.

\# CHAPTER 8: HOW THE CHURCH HAS HARMED TRANSGENDER PEOPLE

\## Confession, Repentance, and Beginning Again

There is a moment that repeats itself in every church that takes gender seriously.

A young person, usually assigned female at birth, is sitting in the youth group or the worship service. They are listening to a sermon about God's design. A sermon about how God made male and female. A sermon about how God's design is perfect and anyone who questions it is rejecting God.

And they think: if I tell anyone who I am, I will be rejected by this community. If I admit I am struggling with my gender, I will be the sermon illustration. I will be the cautionary tale. I will be the evidence of what happens when you reject God's design.

So they do not tell anyone. They suffer alone. They search online for community. They find people who affirm them. They find ideology that makes sense of their suffering. And eventually, they leave the church.

The pastor in the pulpit never knew. The church never knew. The young person is gone.

This is how the church has harmed transgender people.

Not with policy. Not with doctrine. With something more insidious: with the casual dismissal of their existence. With mockery disguised as conviction. With refusal to see them as people rather than as problems to be solved or positions to be condemned.

The church has to confess this. Not because confession is comfortable. Because confession is the only path to repentance. And repentance is the only path forward.

\## The Mockery

Let's start with the simplest harm: the mockery.

In some churches, transgender people are punching bags. Punchlines in sermons. Evidence of cultural decay. Examples of what happens when people rebel against God's design.

A pastor makes a joke about someone's chosen name. The congregation laughs. A youth leader refers to "these people confused about their gender." The young people hear that message: your confusion is not legitimate. It is ridiculous. It is the kind of thing we mock here.

When you are the young person in the room whose confusion is being mocked, the message is clear: you do not belong here. Your experience is not taken seriously. Your suffering is a joke.

The suicide rates among transgender youth tell you what this does. While there are many factors, one of them is clear: the experience of rejection and mockery in faith communities contributes to despair.

The church did this. The church mocked people in genuine dysphoria. The church turned their suffering into a cautionary tale.

This has to be confessed. Not as ancient history. As current reality.

\## The Refusal to Learn

Then there is the refusal to learn.

A young person comes out as trans. They give you a new name. And you ignore it. You "accidentally" use the old name. You use the old pronouns. You make it clear through your persistent refusal that you do not accept this person's reality.

This is different from stumbling. This is different from the difficulty of learning a new name. This is strategic refusal. This is saying: I do not accept you. I do not see you as you are. I am refusing to call you by the name you have given yourself.

To someone experiencing dysphoria, to someone in genuine confusion, to someone who has risked so much to tell you who they are, this refusal is devastating. It is one thing to believe they are wrong. It is another to refuse to honor the name they have chosen. It is one thing to disagree about transition. It is another to name them as if their name is irrelevant.

The church has done this routinely. And then the church has wondered why trans people leave.

\## The Abandonment

The deepest harm, though, comes from abandonment.

A young person in the church is struggling with dysphoria. They do not know who to talk to. They fear rejection. They eventually come out as trans to their small group. And immediately, the response is either: "You are sinning and you need to repent," with no offer of support, or, "We affirm you completely," which sounds like welcome but is actually just ideological affirmation without any genuine relationship.

What they needed was: "We love you. We are confused about this, but we are not going anywhere. Tell us what you need. We want to walk with you."

Instead, they got either judgment or empty affirmation. Either way, they got abandonment. The judgment was clear abandonment. But the affirmation was also abandonment because it was ideological rather than relational. It said: "We affirm your gender identity," not, "We know you. We love you. We are here."

The young person, feeling they have no real community in the church, seeks community elsewhere. They find it in online spaces. In gender-affirming communities. In ideology. These communities may affirm them in ways the church could not. But they are often communities that push toward particular conclusions—medical transition, social identity change, ideological commitment—without the kind of long-term pastoral care that can help someone think clearly.

The church's failure to show up for people in genuine confusion created the void that ideology filled.

\## The Silence When They Died

The most devastating failure is this: young people died. Transgender youth with elevated suicide rates. Young people in the church who experienced dysphoria and in the end took their own lives.

And the church, by and large, said nothing. Said nothing at the funeral. Said nothing from the pulpit. Said nothing about how the church might have failed them. Said nothing about what we should do differently.

When the young person was alive, the church had no space for their confusion. When they died, the church had no space for the grief about what we lost and how we failed them.

This is perhaps the most damning failure. Not just that we were unkind. But that we were so unkind that we drove some people to the edge. And then we had nothing to say about it.

\## What Repentance Actually Looks Like

So what does the church do now?

Repentance means more than saying "sorry." It means changing direction. It means concrete actions that show the church is different.

First, it means acknowledging the harm. Not defensively explaining that we meant well. Not hedging by saying "some churches were unkind but not all." Acknowledging clearly: the church mocked transgender people. The church refused to learn their names. The church abandoned them when they needed us. This is true. We have to say it.

Second, it means creating actual space for people who are struggling. Not ideologically affirming everything, but pastorally present. Creating a context where someone questioning their gender can come and say: "I am confused. I do not know who I am. I do not know what to do," and the response is not judgment, not false affirmation, but genuine care.

Third, it means training leaders differently. Teaching pastors and youth leaders about gender dysphoria. Teaching them how to listen to someone without immediately trying to fix or affirm them. Teaching them that the first job is to understand, not to judge.

Fourth, it means teaching theology of the body. Teach what Scripture says about sexual difference, about embodiment, about identity in Christ. Teach it clearly, without apology, but also teach it in a way that creates space for people struggling with these questions.

Fifth, it means changing the tone. Removing mockery from sermons. Removing casual dismissal of people questioning gender. Speaking about this issue with the seriousness it deserves and the compassion it requires.

Sixth, it means supporting families. When a family has a young person questioning gender, the church should show up. Offer counseling. Offer prayer. Offer community. Not to push them toward a particular conclusion, but to walk with them as they handle genuinely hard terrain.

Seventh, it means prayer. Actual intercession for young people in dysphoria. Actual grief about those we failed. Actual turning to God and saying: we got this wrong. Help us do better.

\## Where We Are Now

The church is at a crossroads on this issue.

One path is to double down on the approach of mockery and dismissal. To make gender a line in the sand. To treat anyone questioning gender as someone who needs to be defeated ideologically rather than accompanied pastorally.

This path will continue to drive people away. Will continue to push young people in dysphoria toward ideology rather than toward faith communities. Will continue to build walls between the church and a generation that is already leaving in large numbers.

The other path is the one of repentance and change. To confess how we have failed. To acknowledge the real harm we have done. To create genuine space for people in confusion. To teach our theology clearly while walking alongside people in pain. To refuse both cruelty and ideology.

This path is harder. It requires saying: "We were wrong. We have some things to learn. We still believe some things about the body and gender, but we are going to hold those convictions alongside genuine love for people who are struggling."

The gospel requires the second path. Not because it is easier. Because it is true.

\## The Bigger Picture: American Christianity's Crisis

This issue, while specific, points to something larger about the American church.

We have largely lost the ability to hold conviction and compassion simultaneously. We believe we have to choose. Either we mock people to show we have convictions, or we abandon convictions to show compassion.

We cannot do both at once. Or so we think.

But Jesus did it. Jesus held uncompromised truth and genuine love for people simultaneously. Jesus told the woman caught in adultery: "I do not condemn you," and also, "Go and sin no more." Both true. Both spoken to the same person.

The church needs to recover this capacity. To say: we believe sexual difference is real and created by God. And we also believe you, the person in front of us, are made in God's image and we will treat you with love. Both things. Not one at the expense of the other.

If the church can recover this, it will recover its credibility with a generation that has been trained to believe that conviction and compassion cannot coexist.

And if we cannot recover it, we will continue to be a church that young people in confusion flee from rather than flee to.

The choice is ours.

\## The Call to Repentance

So I am calling the church to repentance.

Not repentance from believing what Scripture teaches about the body and sexual difference. Not repentance from holding biblical convictions.

Repentance from cruelty. Repentance from mockery. Repentance from abandonment. Repentance from the refusal to see people as people rather than as problems to be solved.

Repentance means: the next young person who walks into your church struggling with dysphoria will experience something different. Will experience a community that says: we do not have all the answers. We believe some things about the body and gender. But you belong here. We love you. We will walk with you.

That young person might still leave the church. Might still make choices you believe are wrong. But at least they will know that the church loved them. That is not everything. But it is something.

And it is the only path the gospel offers.

\## EXPANSION: The Specific Harms — Names, Pronouns, and What Refusal Communicates

The chapter on harm identifies the mockery, the refusal to learn names, the abandonment. But the specifics matter. What exactly does it mean to refuse someone's chosen name?

Consider: a young person assigned male at birth transitions and asks to be called Sarah. The church leaders agree to this. But in prayer list updates, in worship announcements, in pastoral visits, the old name occasionally slips. The young person notices. Every slip sends a message: "I do not fully accept who you are claiming to be."

Now, sometimes slips are genuine. Memory fails. But if the slip happens repeatedly, if it happens more when the person is not in the room, if it happens from people who seem like they are deliberately using the old name—that is not failure. That is refusal. And the person feels it.

This refusal communicates something specific: "I see you as someone who is confused about their identity, not someone who has made a clear choice about who they are. I do not fully honor that choice."

Is this always accurate? Not necessarily. But it is how the person experiences it. And the experience is real.

The same is true with pronouns. A youth leader says: "I just do not feel comfortable using they/them pronouns because I do not think they are grammatically correct." What the young person hears is: "Your identity is grammatically incorrect. Your existence does not fit the rules I understand. Therefore I do not have to honor your request."

Again, the leader probably did not intend this. But the message is received.

The church's refusal to learn has been one of its most damaging failures. Not because using someone's name is the same as affirming ideology. But because the refusal communicates something the church should never communicate: "You are not worth the small effort it takes for me to learn your name."

In the specific case of the young person in dysphoria, that message is devastating. Everything in the culture is telling them they are wrong. Everything in their family might be telling them they are wrong. The church had a chance to communicate something different: "I may not fully understand your experience. But I see you. I will call you by the name you have chosen. You matter to me."

The church failed to do this. That failure has cost real relationships and real safety.

Repentance has to start with understanding exactly what was communicated by the refusal. Then committing to something different.

\## EXPANSION: Prophetic Resistance and Pastoral Presence — Holding Both

The chapter on how the church has harmed identifies specific failures. But it does not resolve a harder tension: How does the church maintain prophetic witness while also being pastorally present?

If the church teaches that sexual difference is real and created by God, and that the gospel calls people to accept their embodied existence, is that position already rejecting transgender people?

Some would say yes. They would argue that any theology that questions the affirmation of transgender identity is inherently harmful.

But consider: Is a church teaching that singleness is a valid calling inherently rejecting to people who are single? Is a church teaching that children are a blessing inherently rejecting to people who are infertile? Is a church teaching that marriage is between a man and a woman inherently rejecting to people experiencing same-sex attraction?

These are complex questions. A church can hold convictions without being cruel about them. A church can teach something and also create space for people navigating the complexity of living with that teaching.

The church's job is to do both simultaneously. To teach what Scripture says about the body and sexual difference clearly, without apology. And also to create a context where someone dealing with dysphoria can come and know: You are welcome here. You will be heard. You will not be mocked. We will help you think.

This is hard because it requires holding tension. It requires saying: "I believe sexual difference is created by God. I also believe you are made in God's image and I will treat you with respect. I do not fully understand what you are experiencing. I want to understand. Help me."

Most churches cannot hold this tension. They collapse into one side or the other. The church either becomes a place of prophetic witness that fails pastorally, or a place of pastoral warmth that fails prophetically.

The church needs to learn to hold both. That is the work.

\# CHAPTER 9: INTERSEX CONDITIONS AND THE BINARY QUESTION

\## What Biology Teaches Us About the Limits of Categories

There is something that both sides of the gender debate avoid discussing: the fact that biological sex is not always a clean binary.

The right avoids it because it complicates the claim that sex is absolute and unambiguous. The left avoids it because it separates the question of biological reality from the question of gender identity.

But intersex conditions exist. Real conditions. Real people. And if we are going to think theologically about gender, we have to understand intersex.

This chapter is not going to resolve the gender debate. But it will complicate it. And complication is what we need.

\## What Are Intersex Conditions?

Intersex conditions, also called Differences of Sex Development (DSD), are variations in reproductive anatomy, chromosomes, or hormones that do not fit the typical pattern of male or female development.

Some examples:

Androgen Insensitivity Syndrome (AIS): A person with XY chromosomes is born with insensitivity to testosterone. Their body developed according to female patterns despite having male chromosomes. The person typically has female external genitalia, but different internal anatomy. They are often assigned female at birth and raised as girls.

5-Alpha Reductase Deficiency: A genetic condition that affects how testosterone is processed. Babies born with this condition may be assigned female at birth because their genitalia appear female, but then during puberty, testosterone production increases and their bodies develop male secondary sex characteristics.

Klinefelter Syndrome: A person with XXY chromosomes. They typically have male anatomy but may have some female physical characteristics.

Turner Syndrome: A person with a single X chromosome instead of two. They typically have female anatomy and are raised as girls, but their development is often different from typical female development.

Congenital Adrenal Hyperplasia: A condition where the adrenal glands produce excess androgens. A person with XX chromosomes may have external genitalia that appear more male than female, or ambiguous.

Ovotesticular Differences of Sex Development: A person has both ovarian and testicular tissue. This is rare.

Müllerian Agenesis: A person with XX chromosomes has no uterus or fallopian tubes but has female external genitalia and is assigned female at birth.

These are real conditions. They exist. They affect real people. Some are more common than others. Some cause significant medical issues. Some cause minimal health consequences. But they all exist.

\## How Common Are They?

This is debated, but various estimates suggest that somewhere between 0.5% to 1.7% of people have some form of intersex condition or ambiguous genitalia.

To put that in perspective: if 1% of the population is intersex, that means in a congregation of 500 people, roughly 5 people have some intersex condition, likely without anyone knowing about it.

These are not rare edge cases. These are real people in your church, your neighborhood, your world.

\## Do Intersex Conditions Disprove the Gender Binary?

This is where the conversation gets complicated.

Some have argued that intersex conditions prove the gender binary is a social construct. If biology is not always binary, they argue, then the binary is false. Gender is a spectrum. Sex is fluid.

This argument misunderstands both biology and logic.

Intersex conditions do not prove the binary is a construct. They prove the binary has exceptions. Two different things.

Consider another binary: day and night. Day is defined by the presence of the sun. Night is defined by the absence of the sun. It is a pretty clear binary. But what happens at dawn? Or dusk? The light gradually shifts from night to day. Are dawn and dusk proof that the day/night binary is false? No. They are exceptions that prove the pattern is real but not absolute.

Similarly, intersex conditions prove that the male/female binary is real but not absolute. Most people are clearly male or clearly female. The biological patterns are real. But some people develop outside the typical pattern. The exception does not invalidate the pattern. It just shows the pattern has limits.

\## What Biology Actually Teaches

Here is what honest biology says about intersex conditions:

First, sexual difference is real. The pattern of male and female exists. Chromosomes typically determine this. Hormones typically determine this. Anatomy typically determines this. These patterns are real.

Second, development sometimes does not follow the pattern. For complex biological reasons, sometimes development goes differently. The result is a person whose sex characteristics are not typical.

Third, in these cases, doctors and families have to make decisions about sex assignment, medical care, and how to raise the child. These decisions are genuinely hard. There is not always a clear "correct" answer.

Fourth, intersex conditions say nothing about gender identity. A person with AIS, assigned female at birth, with female external genitalia, who is raised as a girl and identifies as female, is not transgender. They have an intersex condition. These are different things.

The two questions are separate: What is your biological sex? (complicated in intersex conditions). What is your gender identity? (complicated in transgender experience). A person can have an intersex condition without being transgender. A person can be transgender without having an intersex condition. The categories do not collapse into each other.

\## The Theological Implication

So what does this teach us theologically?

The binary is real. Genesis 1:27 stands. God created humanity male and female. This is the pattern. This is how human reproduction works. This is a fundamental reality.

But the pattern has limits. The created world is sometimes more complex than the pattern. This is what it means to live in a fallen world. Creation is good, but creation is also broken. Sometimes development does not follow the intended pattern.

When that happens, we do not pretend the pattern did not exist. We acknowledge the pattern and acknowledge the exception. We ask: what does love and wisdom require here? How do we care for this person? How do we honor both biological reality and the dignity of the person in front of us?

This is different from the gender-ideology position: "Intersex conditions prove the binary is false, therefore gender identity is more real than biological sex, therefore you should transition if that matches your identity."

But it is also different from the rigid position: "The binary is absolute. There are no exceptions. If intersex conditions exist, we will just pretend they do not, or we will force them into the binary anyway."

The Christian position is more humble: "The pattern is real. The exceptions are real. We do not fully understand why exceptions exist. We respond with wisdom, compassion, and honesty."

\## Application: Intersex People in the Church

If intersex people are in your church—and they likely are—what does this mean?

First, it means understanding that intersex is not the same as transgender. Intersex people may or may not be transgender. Most intersex people are not questioning their gender. They were assigned a sex at birth. They were raised as that sex. They identify as that sex. They happen to have a medical condition.

Some intersex people are transgender, but that is a separate thing.

Second, it means medical decisions are genuinely hard. Many intersex people have had surgeries as infants to "normalize" their genitalia. Some of these decisions, made by doctors and parents, have been controversial. Some people are grateful for the decision. Some regret it. There is no universal "right" answer.

The church's job is not to judge families who made these decisions. The job is to support intersex people as they handle whatever their condition is and whatever decisions have been made.

Third, it means understanding that intersex conditions can affect identity, but in complex ways. Someone assigned female at birth with AIS will experience their body differently than a typical female. That difference may create confusion. But it does not necessarily create dysphoria. It may just be: "My body is different than I thought it was."

Fourth, it means inclusion. Intersex people have often been invisible in church conversations. They have not been welcomed to talk about their condition. They have not been included in conversations about gender and the body.

The church should create space where intersex people can be known. Can talk about their conditions. Can explore what it means to be intersex and how that shapes their faith.

\## Why This Matters for the Gender Conversation

So why include a chapter on intersex in a book about transgender identity?

Because intersex conditions complicate the conversation in ways both sides need to face.

If you believe gender is absolutely binary and has no exceptions, you have to reckon with intersex conditions. They exist. They are real. They do not fit neatly.

If you believe gender is fluid and identity is primary, you have to reckon with the fact that intersex conditions exist but many intersex people do not have gender dysphoria or question their gender. Most intersex people identify with the sex they were assigned at birth. So even if gender were completely fluid, intersex conditions would not prove it.

Intersex conditions teach both sides humility.

\## What We Do Not Know

This is important: there is much about intersex development that we do not fully understand.

Why do these conditions happen? We have some answers. Genetic variations. Hormonal disruptions. But we do not fully understand the scope or cause of all intersex conditions.

What are the long-term outcomes? For some conditions, we have good data. For others, we do not. We do not have decades of data on how all intersex people fare across their lifespans.

How should medical decisions be made? Different doctors, different families, different intersex people answer this differently. There is honest disagreement about when surgery is appropriate, when to wait, how to support intersex young people in puberty.

The church's job is to acknowledge: we do not know everything. We should be humble about the complexity. We should listen to intersex people about what they actually experience rather than imposing categories.

\## The Limit of the Analogy—Again

I want to be clear: while intersex conditions are relevant to conversations about gender, they do not settle the transgender question.

Intersex people have biological reasons for their sex being atypical. Transgender people typically have typical biological sex but experience their gender differently. These are not identical situations.

Using intersex people as evidence for gender ideology is using intersex people's existence as a prop for a political argument. Intersex people deserve better than that.

But ignoring intersex people because they do not fit neatly into either camp is also wrong.

The church should include intersex people. Should understand intersex conditions. Should recognize that biology is sometimes more complex than our categories. Should hold the complexity without collapsing the distinctions.

\## Application: What the Church Should Do

If the church is going to think honestly about gender and the body, what about intersex?

First, educate yourself. Learn what intersex conditions actually are. Learn that they are not about gender identity but about biological sex development.

Second, create space for intersex people to be known. Not as a political issue. As people with particular medical realities.

Third, support families navigating intersex conditions. These are genuinely hard situations. Families need pastoral care, not judgment.

Fourth, teach that complexity is okay. Not every theological issue has a simple answer. The existence of intersex conditions means we have to think carefully about what sex is and how to handle cases where sex development is atypical.

Fifth, listen to intersex people. Let them tell you what their experience is. Do not impose ideology on their experience.

Sixth, refuse to use intersex people as a rhetorical tool for either side of the gender debate. Intersex people exist for their own sake, not to be evidence for someone's politics.

The binary is real. Exceptions exist. Both things are true. The church should be the place where that complexity is acknowledged and where people navigating that complexity are welcomed.

\## EXPANSION: Klinefelter, Turner, Congenital Adrenal Hyperplasia — The Specific Conditions

The chapter on intersex conditions names them but does not go deep into specifics. Understanding the specifics matters because it reveals just how complex sexual development actually is.

Klinefelter Syndrome (XXY): A person is born with an extra X chromosome. Most Klinefelter individuals are assigned male at birth, raised as boys, and identify as male. They have male anatomy. But they may have reduced testosterone production, less body hair, wider hips, or other variations. They are typically infertile.

How does a Klinefelter person relate to their body? Like everyone else—it is their actual body, the one they live in. But the body may not match what they expected based on being told they are male. They may feel confusion. But most do not experience dysphoria. They may feel difference. But difference is not the same as wrongness.

Turner Syndrome (45,X — single X chromosome): A person is born with only one X chromosome instead of two. Turner individuals are typically assigned female at birth, raised as girls, and identify as female. But they have short stature, may have cardiac differences, may have infertility, and have a particular physical presentation.

Like Klinefelter individuals, Turner individuals are typically not transgender. They are assigned female, identify as female, but their bodies developed differently than typical. The question is: how does the church help someone understand that their body is different but still good? That variation does not make you less female or less male?

Congenital Adrenal Hyperplasia (CAH): A person with XX chromosomes produces excess androgens due to an adrenal gland condition. The external genitalia may appear more male than female, or be ambiguous. A child might be assigned male or female at birth depending on the appearance of the genitalia.

Some CAH individuals grow up with confusion about their sex because what they were assigned does not match what they expected. Some people assigned female with CAH grow up thinking they are male. Some people assigned male with CAH identify as female.

The point: these are not cases of gender identity different from biological sex in the way we think about transgender identity. These are cases of sexual development that did not follow the typical pattern, leading to confusion about sex itself, not just about gender identity.

The church needs to understand this distinction. An intersex person is not necessarily transgender. They may be. But the conditions are different. One is about sexual development being atypical. The other is about gender identity being different from assigned sex.

Both people deserve to be seen and welcomed. But the church needs to understand what it is actually seeing and responding to.

\## EXPANSION: The Specific Biology — DSD, Medical Intervention, and Intersex Realities

Approximately 1 to 2 percent of the population has some form of Disorder of Sexual Development (DSD). This is not rare. This is common. These are not edge cases. These are real people with real bodies that do not fit the typical binary.

The specifics matter because they challenge the assumption that everyone fits neatly into two categories. They also reveal the complexity of sexual development and remind us that sexual difference is not as simple as chromosomes or anatomy.

Klinefelter syndrome (XXY): A person has an extra X chromosome. They typically have male anatomy and typically identify as male. They may have some feminine physical characteristics. They may be infertile. They may experience reduced testosterone production. Many people with Klinefelter never receive a diagnosis because their expression of masculinity seems normal. They go through life not knowing about the extra chromosome.

Turner syndrome: A person has a single X chromosome instead of XX. They typically have female anatomy and typically identify as female. They may have various physical differences—shorter stature, different facial features, infertility. They may have kidney or heart differences. Like Klinefelter, many people with Turner live without diagnosis or with late diagnosis.

Congenital adrenal hyperplasia (CAH): A person with XX chromosomes experiences higher testosterone exposure in utero due to a genetic condition. This can result in a range of physical presentations from mild to severe. Some people with CAH have female anatomy that is partially or fully virilized. Some are raised as female despite having been exposed to significant testosterone. Some are raised as male. The condition is heritable and can be managed medically.

Complete androgen insensitivity syndrome (CAIS): A person with XY chromosomes is completely insensitive to testosterone. Their body cannot respond to the hormone. They develop typically female anatomy and typically identify as female despite being chromosomally XY. They do not develop typical male anatomy even though they have the Y chromosome. Many people with CAIS do not discover their condition until they fail to menstruate and seek medical evaluation.

Partial androgen insensitivity syndrome (PAIS): Similar to CAIS but the person has some sensitivity to testosterone. This results in a range of physical characteristics that may not fit typical patterns. A person might have some typical male characteristics and some typical female characteristics. The variation is almost infinite.

5-alpha-reductase deficiency: A person with XY chromosomes lacks an enzyme needed to convert testosterone to its more active form. At birth, external anatomy may appear female or ambiguous. At puberty, testosterone production increases and typical male characteristics develop. Some people with this condition have been raised as female and then experienced male puberty at adolescence.

Ovotesticular DSD: A person has both ovarian and testicular tissue. Anatomy may be ambiguous. Sex chromosomes might be XX, XY, or mosaic. Gender identity development is unpredictable. These are real people with genuinely unique embodied experiences.

What do these conditions teach? Sexual difference is not determined by any single factor. Chromosomes, hormone exposure, hormone receptivity, anatomy, neurology—all of these can vary independently. A person can be chromosomally one sex and have physical characteristics typical of another sex. A person can be genetically male but have complete female development. A person can be genetically female but have experienced extensive male-typical hormone exposure.

Sexual difference is not binary. It is a spectrum. The merism of Genesis 1:27—male and female—starts to look less metaphorical and more literally biological.

Now here is the pastoral dimension: many of these conditions in the past were treated with unnecessary medical intervention. Intersex infants were often assigned to one sex and surgically altered to match that assignment. Sometimes the assignment was wrong. The child grew up with anatomy that did not match their sense of sexual self. The medical intervention created dysphoria rather than resolving it.

Medical professionals in the past made decisions about children's bodies without their consent, based on assumptions about what sex they should be, often creating lasting harm. Intersex advocates have spent decades telling this story. Describing the surgery they did not consent to. Describing the shame and confusion of not understanding their own body. Describing the dysphoria that resulted from medical intervention.

The church learned nothing from this. And now the church watches the conversation shift to transgender youth and does not see the parallel. A young person expressing dysphoria. Adults making decisions about medical intervention based on assumptions about identity. Irreversible physical changes.

The church should learn from the intersex history: be very cautious about medical intervention on young people's bodies. Be willing to honor what a person's sense of self tells them. Be willing to acknowledge that some people have genuinely unusual embodied experiences. But do not assume that medical intervention is always the answer. Ensure thorough evaluation before permanent changes.

The intersex person who has been harmed by unnecessary surgery did not need their body changed. They needed help understanding their unusual body and accepting it. They needed community that did not shame them for embodied variation. They needed friends, family, and faith communities that accepted them as they were.

That same wisdom applies to many people experiencing gender dysphoria. Some need medical help. Some need psychological help. Some need community that accepts embodied variation and does not demand that everyone fit the typical pattern. Some need to learn to accept what is rather than trying to fix it.

The church should be a place where embodied variation is acknowledged and accepted. Where intersex people do not feel obligated to hide. Where transgender people are not rejected. But also where the impulse toward medical fixing is questioned carefully. Where the goal is peace with embodied existence, not escape from it.

This is pastoral care grounded in wisdom earned from the actual mistakes of medical history.

What the intersex advocate is saying that the church needs to hear: Do not make decisions about people's bodies without their consent and without thorough understanding of what is really happening. Do not assume that medical intervention is always the answer. Do not treat embodied variation as a problem that needs to be fixed.

That same advice applies to churches thinking about transgender people. Do not rush. Do not assume. Do not treat every question as though there is only one right answer. The intersex person and the transgender person have this in common: they have bodies that do not fit the typical pattern. The church's job is not to fix that. The church's job is to help them find peace with what is.

For some, that peace includes medical intervention. For others, it means accepting what is and finding meaning beyond fixing the body. Either way, the path to peace does not come through ideology. It comes through wisdom. It comes through careful discernment. It comes through community that does not shame.

This is what the intersex history teaches. And it is what the church should learn.

\# CHAPTER 10: TRANSGENDER PEOPLE IN YOUR CHURCH

\## Membership, Baptism, Leadership, and Belonging

The moment comes in every church eventually. A person in transition starts attending. Or someone already in the church comes out as transgender. And suddenly, the church has to answer practical questions it has been avoiding.

Can they be baptized? Should they be baptized? Do we require some kind of repentance first—repentance from what? What do we do about leadership? Can they teach? Can they lead worship? What about church membership?

Different churches answer these questions differently. And the answers reveal what the church actually believes about gender, about grace, about what it means to belong to God's people.

There is no perfect answer that everyone will agree with. But there is a way of thinking about it that is more faithful than others.

\## The Fundamental Question

Before we answer specific questions, we have to answer the foundational one: Is a transgender person a legitimate member of the body of Christ?

The gospel answer is: yes.

A transgender person is made in God's image. A transgender person can believe in Jesus. A transgender person can repent of sin and trust Christ. A transgender person can be baptized and united to the body of Christ.

This is not complicated theologically. A person's gender identity or gender confusion does not disqualify them from the gospel. Sexual confusion is not the unforgivable sin. Dysphoria is not a barrier to grace.

If a transgender person comes to faith in Christ, confesses Jesus as Lord, and wants to be baptized, the church's response should be baptism.

Now, that does not mean the church has no other wisdom to offer. It does not mean the church should not help someone think carefully about medical transition or identity questions. It does not mean the church should simply affirm all choices without reflection.

But it does mean: do not use transition as a barrier to belonging to God's people.

\## Membership and Hospitality

What does it mean to welcome a transgender person into church membership?

It means: you are part of us. We do not pretend to understand everything about your experience. We believe some things about the body and gender that you may not agree with. But you belong here. You are our family now.

When someone joins the church, they are making a commitment to be part of a community that is working together toward holiness, toward Christ-likeness, toward understanding what it means to follow Jesus.

A transgender person can make that commitment. Can grow in faith. Can be discipled. Can grow toward Christ-likeness.

Now, Christ-likeness might look different than the person expects. It might involve learning to be at peace in the body they have, not the body they wish they had. It might involve discovering that their identity is deeper than their gender. But it might also involve learning that the gospel offers resources for peace and transformation that ideology cannot.

Membership means: we are going to walk together. We are not perfect. You are not perfect. But we are committed to following Jesus together.

That is hospitality. That is what the church should offer.

\## Baptism: The Public Commitment

Should a transgender person be baptized?

The only legitimate reason to withhold baptism is if someone has not come to faith in Christ. Baptism is the public declaration of faith in Jesus. If someone has believed, they should be baptized.

A transgender person's gender identity is not a barrier to baptism. Their confusion is not a barrier. Their dysphoria is not a barrier. If they have believed in Jesus, they should be baptized.

Now, what happens when they are baptized? The same thing that happens for anyone: they are united to Christ and to His body. They are publicly declaring allegiance to Jesus.

Does baptism mean their dysphoria will disappear? No. Does it mean they will suddenly "correct" their gender identity? No. Does it mean the church has affirmed all their choices? No.

What it means is: you belong. You are mine (Christ is saying to them). You are part of my people. We are going to walk together.

That is what baptism is. Not ideological affirmation. Belonging.

\## Leadership and the Harder Question

Then there is leadership.

Can a transgender person be a pastor? Can they teach? Can they lead worship?

This is where churches have to think carefully.

On one level, it is the same as any other leader question: Does this person have the spiritual maturity, the biblical knowledge, the character to lead? Is this person walking in alignment with Christ? Are they calling others to follow Jesus and to grow in faith?

A transgender person could theoretically answer yes to all of these. Could be spiritually mature, biblically faithful, calling people toward Christ.

But there is something else at stake here.

Leadership in the church is about representing Christ and calling people to follow Him. It is about modeling Christ-likeness. When someone is leading the church, people are watching them. Learning from them. Following their example.

If someone is in transition, or has transitioned, or is living in a gender identity that differs from their biological sex, are they best positioned to call people toward the biblical understanding of the body, gender, and identity?

Different churches answer this differently.

Some say: no. If we affirm transgender identities, we are contradicting what we teach about the body. The inconsistency is too great. Leaders need to model alignment between what we teach and how we live.

Others say: yes. What matters is someone's spiritual maturity and calling, not their gender. To exclude transgender people from leadership is to suggest they cannot be fully faithful followers of Jesus.

Both have a point.

What I think: this requires honest conversation about what your church actually teaches and whether you are willing to live with the tension.

If your church teaches that sexual difference is real and created by God, and that the gospel calls people to accept their biological sex and find peace there—then having a leader whose life represents something different creates confusion. It suggests either the teaching is wrong or the leader is not fully submitted to it.

But if your church is thoughtful about this, and if the leader has thought carefully about it, it is possible to hold both things: "This is what we teach about the body. This is this particular person's journey with that teaching. They are a faithful follower of Jesus. And we can all grow together."

The key is honesty. Do not pretend you are not teaching something you are. Do not pretend the person in leadership has some other relationship to that teaching than they actually do. Let people see the actual complexity and make their own decisions.

\## Different Church Models

Let me outline a few different approaches different churches might take.

\*\*Model One: Affirming Churches\*\*

Some churches explicitly affirm transgender identities as valid expressions of human experience. They may or may not have biblical theology they are trying to work from. But the baseline is: we affirm you. We welcome you. We use your preferred name and pronouns. You can be baptized, can be members, can be leaders.

This model is clear and consistent for people who share the theology. But it abandons the biblical teaching about the body and sexual difference. If you choose this model, know what you are choosing.

\*\*Model Two: Exclusionary Churches\*\*

Some churches take the opposite approach. Transgender people are not fully welcome. They can attend, but membership, baptism, and leadership are closed to them. The logic is: we believe strongly in biblical teaching about gender, and we cannot have people in visible roles who are living in contradiction to that.

This model is also clear, but it is painful for transgender people who genuinely want to follow Christ. It says: you cannot fully belong here until you stop being transgender. That may be what your church believes, but you should be honest about it.

\*\*Model Three: Welcoming but Cautious\*\*

Some churches say: we welcome you as a person. You can attend. You can be baptized if you have come to faith. You can be a member. But we need to be careful about leadership because leadership is about modeling and calling people toward Christ, and we teach that the gospel calls people to accept their biological bodies. Let's talk about what that means for visible roles.

This model says: you belong. You are not excluded. But we are also going to think carefully about what leadership looks like given what we actually teach.

\*\*Model Four: Honest about Complexity\*\*

Some churches say: we are working through this. We believe some things about the body and gender that we think Scripture teaches. We also believe that anyone who has come to faith in Christ has a place in God's people. We do not fully understand how to hold both things, but we are trying. Come be part of us. Help us think. We will help you think. We will figure this out together.

This model admits we do not have everything figured out. But it offers genuine community while maintaining honest theology.

\## What I Believe

My conviction is this: a transgender person who comes to genuine faith in Christ should be baptized. Should be welcomed into membership. Should be part of the church.

But the church should also be honest about what it teaches regarding the body and gender. The church should not pretend that affirming transgender identity is neutral. It is a theological choice. If you make it, own it. If you do not make it, be honest about that too.

And leadership requires thinking carefully. Not because transgender people cannot be faithful. But because leadership is about representation and influence. You have to be honest about what your leadership is representing to the people watching.

The goal is not exclusion. The goal is honesty. Let transgender people know what they are signing up for. Let your church know what you are teaching. Let the community be united around something real, not pretending complexity does not exist.

\## The Practical Work

So what does this actually look like in the room?

First, someone who is transitioning or has transitioned comes to your church.

Second, you welcome them. You mean it. You do not make them feel like a sociology experiment.

Third, you have a conversation. Not an interrogation. A conversation. "Tell us about your road. How did you come to faith? What does it mean to you to be part of a church community?"

Fourth, you explain what your church teaches. "We teach that sexual difference is real and created by God. We teach that the gospel calls people to accept their embodied existence and find peace there. That is our theological perspective. It may or may not align with your experience. But that is what we teach here."

Fifth, you offer them choices. "Given that, here is what membership might look like. Here is what baptism means. Here is how we think about leadership. Where do you want to fit?"

Sixth, you walk with them. Through confusion. Through questions. Through the actual work of discipleship.

You do not pretend you understand their dysphoria. You do not pretend you have answers you do not have. But you offer the gospel. Community. The patient invitation to let Christ shape who they are.

This is what the church owes people—transgender or otherwise.

\## The Gospel, Not Ideology

The core conviction has to be this: the gospel is the answer, not ideology on either side.

Not ideology that says: "Affirm their transgender identity and everything will be fine." That is false. It promises what it cannot deliver.

Not ideology that says: "Just accept your biological sex and your dysphoria will disappear." That is also false. It promises what it cannot deliver.

The gospel says: "You are made in God's image. You will have a resurrection body. You matter more than your suffering. Your identity is rooted in Christ. Let me help you find your way to peace—the real kind, not ideology-driven, but the kind that comes from being reconciled to God and to your own embodied existence."

That is the message. That is what the church offers.

If your church can offer that—with honesty, with clarity, with pastoral care—then you have something to say to transgender people that culture cannot say.

And that might actually change something.

\## EXPANSION: Catholic, Anglican, Baptist, Presbyterian — Denominational Approaches and Their Strengths

Different Christian traditions approach transgender people differently. Understanding these approaches is helpful not because one is obviously right, but because each tradition has something to offer and each has blind spots.

The Catholic approach has historically emphasized natural law theology. The body is created by God. Sexual difference is part of God's design. The church's teaching is that transition is not consistent with accepting the goodness of creation. However, the Catholic tradition also emphasizes that all people are made in God's image and deserve dignity. So you have official teaching that is restrictive, but also pastoral voices within Catholicism calling for compassion and seeing people first.

The strength of this approach: it has clear theological grounding. It is not negotiable. But it also tries to hold clarity with compassion.

The weakness: for a transgender person who has made different choices, there is not much room for inclusion in leadership or full belonging. The theology holds. But the practice can feel exclusionary.

The Anglican approach is more diverse, depending on the diocese and the priest. Some Anglican churches fully affirm transgender identity. Others take a more traditional view. The Anglican strength is that it has tolerance for theological difference. The weakness is that this tolerance can look like the church does not actually believe anything.

The Baptist approach traditionally has emphasized local church autonomy. So Baptist churches vary widely. Some Baptist churches are very affirming. Others are very restrictive. The strength of this is that churches can respond to their particular contexts. The weakness is that there is no denominational guidance, so individual churches are left to figure this out alone.

The Presbyterian church has more centralized teaching. Presbyterianism has historically emphasize confessional standards—what the church actually teaches. But Presbyterianism has also emphasized scholarship and careful thinking. So you have Presbyterians doing some of the most careful theological work on this issue, while official teaching is still developing.

Each tradition has lessons.

From Catholicism: theology needs to be grounded, not just feelings-based. The church should teach what it actually believes, not pretend to be neutral.

From Anglicanism: the church should be able to hold some theological diversity without claiming that all positions are equally true.

From Baptist tradition: local communities should have some freedom to work out what they believe in their particular context, not be controlled by distant bureaucracy.

From Presbyterianism: the church should do careful, scholarly thinking about hard questions, not just resort to ideology or feelings.

If the church could learn from all of these traditions, you would have a church that taught clear theology, honored scholarship, allowed for some local variation, and held conviction with humility.

That is not what most churches look like. Most churches default to one of two positions: complete exclusion or complete affirmation. Both claim to be based on tradition. Both are actually failures to learn from what the traditions actually offer.

\## EXPANSION: Baptism, Communion, Membership, and Belonging

The question of belonging extends to the most central acts of the church: baptism, communion, and membership covenant. These are not marginal rituals. They are the acts that constitute what it means to be the church. If a young person experiencing gender dysphoria cannot participate in these acts, they cannot fully belong. And if they can participate but only by hiding who they are, that participation is corrupted.

Consider: a young person who has experienced gender dysphoria, who has worked with their family and church community, decides to be baptized. They are making a covenant with Christ and with the church. They are saying: "I believe in Jesus. I want to follow Him. I want to be part of His people." Should the church baptize them?

The answer is almost certainly yes. Baptism is not a reward for theological correctness. Baptism is not a seal for people who have all the answers. Baptism is a sign that you are claimed by Christ, that your sins are forgiven, that you belong to His people. It is an act of grace that precedes understanding.

The theological complexity would be this: if you believe that accepting one's biological sex is part of accepting God's creation, does baptizing someone who is rejecting their biological sex contradict your theology? Possibly. But baptism is also an act of grace that operates independently of the baptismal candidate's current understanding. The baptismal candidate does not have to have perfect theology to be baptized. They have to be willing to follow Christ. The church accompanies them from there. They learn over time what it means to follow Christ. Including what it means to accept embodied existence.

What might be true: the pastor or church leader could have a conversation with the young person before baptism. Not to determine whether they are worthy—no one is—but to understand what they believe and to explain what the church believes. "We baptize you into Christ. Christ teaches us that our bodies are good, that we are called to accept and inhabit our embodied existence, not escape it. As you follow Christ, you will wrestle with what that means for your particular journey. You may come to different conclusions than some of us have come to. But you are welcomed into that wrestling. Baptism is not a stamp of approval on all your beliefs. It is a sign that you belong to Christ and to His people. And we are glad to welcome you."

This is different from rejection. It is grace with honesty.

Communion presents similar questions. A person who is part of your church community, who is actively following Christ, who is working toward peace in their embodied existence—do they belong at the Table? The answer is yes. Communion is not a reward for having your theology figured out. Communion is not a reward for agreeing with the church's theology on gender. It is the meal of the reconciled, where we remember Christ's broken body and shed blood.

If anything, the person struggling with embodiment needs the symbolic power of communion more, not less. To eat the body and drink the blood. To participate in the mystery that the body matters, that physical reality carries spiritual significance. To be reminded that in the end, transformation will come not through escape from embodiment but through resurrection of the body.

A person who rejects their body does not need to be excluded from the Table. They need to be invited to the Table and, through participation in this ancient meal, to be reminded that bodies matter. That physical reality is sacred. That God came in the flesh and will redeem all flesh.

Membership covenant is where ongoing relationship gets formalized and expectations are stated. A young person who has experienced dysphoria, who has worked with their family and church community, who is following Christ—do they sign a membership covenant? Yes. But the covenant is worth being explicit about.

Your membership covenant might include something like this: "As members of this church, we believe that we are made in God's image. We believe that bodies are created good by God. We believe that we are called to accept and integrate our embodied existence, that this acceptance is not the same as agreement about contemporary gender ideology, that transformation comes through grace not ideology, that all kinds of people—including people with complex experiences around gender and identity—can follow Jesus and belong to His people. We commit to walking with you through genuine discernment. We will not pretend to have all the answers. We will help you think carefully about what you are experiencing. We believe your struggle is real. We believe your belonging is not conditional on having it all figured out."

This is costly belonging. It does not pretend to have all the answers. It does not sidestep the theological questions. But it says clearly: you are ours. We are yours. We will figure this out together. We believe something about what it means to be embodied and to follow Christ. And we want to help you understand that too.

Most churches have not developed these conversations. The result is that young people in dysphoria feel that church membership requires either complete affirmation of their gender identity or complete rejection of themselves. There is a third way: belonging that holds conviction and compassion simultaneously.

That is what the church should offer. Not ideology. Belonging.

The power of baptism, communion, and membership covenant is that these acts are not conditional on theological perfection. Baptism does not require you to understand everything about what it means to be in God's image. Communion does not require you to have your life figured out. Membership covenant does not require perfect alignment with the church's theology.

What these acts do require is willingness to belong. Willingness to follow Jesus. Willingness to be part of the community that has decided to live under His lordship. Everything else flows from there.

A young person experiencing gender dysphoria might not have that figured out yet. But they can be willing to follow Jesus. They can be willing to be part of the community. They can come to the waters of baptism and be baptized into Christ. And through that act, they belong. Fully. Not conditionally.

This changes what is possible. A person who belongs fully to the church can be in conversation with the church about hard questions. A person who is welcomed to the table can be transformed slowly by the symbolic power of eating and drinking the body and blood of Christ. A person who is a member of the covenant community has a place from which to ask questions.

But if you make belonging conditional—conditional on affirming gender ideology or on being certain about embodiment or on agreeing with your interpretation of Scripture—then you cut off the very people who most need the church's help.

The church at its best is the place where the most broken people find the most generous welcome. Not a welcome that requires them to hide. But a welcome that makes no final demands except: follow Jesus.

That is what baptism, communion, and membership covenant offer. A way in. A place to belong. A community that will walk with you, even when the walking is slow and the path is unclear.

\# CHAPTER 11: WHAT TO SAY WHEN SOMEONE YOU LOVE SAYS THEY'RE TRANSGENDER

\## A Guide for Families and Friends

The phone call or the conversation comes. Someone you love—a child, a sibling, a friend, a nephew—tells you they are transgender.

Your first feeling is probably some version of: I do not know what to say. I do not know if I understand this. I do not know if I agree with this. I do not know how to respond in a way that honors both my love for this person and what I believe to be true.

That feeling is honest. And it is also paralyzing. Because whatever you say in the next few minutes will matter. It will be remembered. It will shape your relationship.

This chapter is for that moment. Not to give you easy answers. To give you a way of thinking about what to say, what not to say, and how to handle what comes next.

\## What Not To Say

Let's start with what you should not do.

Do not say: "You are confused. You will grow out of this. This is a phase."

This response dismisses what the person is telling you. You do not know if it is a phase. It may be. It may not be. But immediately claiming it is a phase says: I am not going to take you seriously. I am not going to listen. I have already decided what this is.

Do not say: "I need to pray about this" and then disappear into prayer while your relationship distance increases.

Prayer is good. But prayer cannot replace presence. The person in front of you needs to know you are still there, not praying them away from their identity, but praying that you will understand, that the relationship will survive, that you will both find wisdom.

Do not say: "This is a sin. You need to repent."

You may believe that gender transition is wrong. You may believe that living outside your biological sex is sin. But the first conversation when someone is vulnerable enough to tell you they are transgender is not the time to lead with condemnation. Lead with love. The theology comes later.

Do not say: "If you were my child, I would not allow this."

You are not their parent (probably). You do not know their family situation. You do not know the complexity of their life. Making it about what you would do centers you, not them. It is not helpful.

Do not say: "You look fine the way you are."

The person did not ask for your aesthetic opinion. They asked you to hear them. They asked you to acknowledge something real about their experience. Saying "you look fine" suggests you do not understand that this is not about how they look. It is about how they experience themselves.

Do not say: "Are you sure? Maybe you should wait longer before deciding."

Probably they have thought about this longer than you know. Probably they have been dealing with this internally for years before telling you. Suggesting they are not sure enough adds pressure and suggests you do not trust their self-knowledge.

Do not say: "What about the Bible?"

The Bible will come up. But not in the first conversation. Not as a weapon. Not as a way to shut them down. The gospel is important. But it is not the first word. Presence is the first word.

Do not say: "I support you no matter what, but I am very uncomfortable with this."

This sounds supportive but is actually contradictory. You are either uncomfortable and want to change the situation, or you support them. Putting it both ways creates confusion. Pick one. If you are genuinely going to support them, do not lead with your discomfort. If you are genuinely uncomfortable, be honest about that later, in a different conversation.

Do not say: "I understand. I have always wondered about my gender too."

You may have. But this is their moment. This is not about you. Do not make it about your own questions or uncertainties. That centers you when you should be centering them.

\## What To Say (First)

Okay, so what should you actually say?

"Thank you for telling me. I know that took courage."

This is the place to start. Not agreement with their choices. Not disagreement. Just acknowledgment: you trusted me with something real. That matters.

Then: "I love you. That does not change."

Say it. Mean it. Let them hear it. This is the foundation. Everything else builds on this. If they do not know that your love is not conditional on whether you understand or agree with them, they cannot hear anything else you have to say.

Then: "I want to understand. Tell me what you have been experiencing."

Open a real conversation. Ask them to help you understand. What does it feel like to experience their gender differently from their biological sex? When did this start? What made them decide to tell you?

Listen more than you talk. Your job is not to defend your position. Your job is to understand their experience.

Then: "I have some beliefs about the body and gender that are important to me. But I want to work through this together."

This is honest. You are not pretending you do not have convictions. But you are also not leading with those convictions as a weapon. You are saying: I have some things I believe, and I also love you, and we are going to figure out how to hold both.

\## What Comes Next

So you have had the first conversation. What comes next?

First, do not immediately make decisions about what you will or will not accept. You do not need to decide about pronouns in this moment. You do not need to decide about social transition or medical transition. You do not need to have your position locked in stone.

You can say: "I am going to need some time to process this. I want to understand better. Can we talk more?"

Taking time is honest and wise. Do not pretend you have processed something you have not.

Second, educate yourself. Not from internet rabbit holes. From actual resources. Read what medical professionals say about gender dysphoria. Read theology about the body. Read testimonies from transgender people. Read about detransition. Get actual information.

Third, find people to talk to. A pastor you trust. A counselor. People who have walked through this with their own family members. You need support. You need to process your own feelings. Do not burden the person who just came out with managing your feelings about their coming out.

Fourth, get help for your loved one. Not conversion therapy. Not ideology-driven affirmation. Actual psychological care. A therapist who will help them understand what they are experiencing, not someone who will immediately affirm medical transition or someone who will immediately condemn their identity. Just someone who will help them think.

Fifth, maintain the relationship. You may not understand. You may not agree. You may need to have conversations where you express what you believe. But do not disappear. Do not punish them with silence. Stay in the relationship.

\## When They Make Choices You Think Are Harmful

Eventually, your loved one may make choices you believe are harmful. They may want to medically transition. They may want to change their legal name. They may want to make decisions about their body that you think are wrong.

What do you do?

First, distinguish between you disagreeing and them being wrong. You might believe they should not transition. You might believe it is not in their best interest. But that is your conviction, not necessarily universal truth. Your job is not to impose your conclusion. Your job is to help them think carefully.

Second, ask good questions. "What makes you want to do this? Have you talked to a therapist? What are the risks you understand? What do you hope will happen?" Help them think without shaming them.

Third, do not make their choices about you. Do not say: "If you transition, I cannot accept that." That makes it about your feelings. Instead, say: "I think this decision is serious and I want to make sure you have thought it through carefully. How can I help you think?"

Fourth, offer your perspective if asked. You believe some things about the body. You believe the gospel offers a different path. If they ask, you can share. But it is not your job to convince them. It is your job to love them.

Fifth, hold the line on your actual boundaries. There may be things you cannot do. You might not be able to call your child by a new name. You might not be able to support medical transition. You can be honest about that. But be honest gently. Not as punishment. As boundary.

\## The Hardest Situation: When You Think They Are Being Harmed

What if you genuinely believe the choices your loved one is making are harming them?

This is where it is hard. You do not want to be judgmental. But you also do not want to be a cheerleader for choices you believe are dangerous.

Here is what you do:

You speak the truth in love. You say: "I love you. And I also believe this choice may not be good for you for these reasons. Can we talk about that?"

Then you listen. Maybe your loved one has thought about these concerns. Maybe they have reasons you have not considered. Maybe they disagree with your assessment. That is okay. They can disagree with you.

Your job is not to control their choices. Your job is to make sure they are making informed choices with the support of actual professionals, not ideology.

So you ask: "Are you working with a therapist on this? Have you talked to a doctor? Have you thought about the reversible and irreversible aspects? Can you help me understand what you have considered?"

If they are making choices without professional support, without having thought them through, under pressure from a community that is pushing them toward particular conclusions—then you have something to say. Not judgment. But honest concern.

"I am worried that you are being pushed toward this by an ideology that promises certainty I am not sure exists. I want you to have professional support as you think through this. Not a therapist who will affirm whatever you decide. A real therapist who will help you understand what is happening."

This is not rejection. This is love that has teeth.

\## How To Hold Conviction and Relationship

This is the harder work. How do you maintain a relationship with someone whose choices you believe are wrong?

You do it the same way Christians have always done it: by separating the person from the choice.

Jesus did this. He was critical of people's choices—the rich young ruler, the money changers in the temple, those who wanted to stone the woman caught in adultery. But He did it while maintaining their dignity.

He said to the woman caught in adultery: "Neither do I condemn you. Go and sin no more." Both true. Both spoken to the same person. No condemnation. And clear about what righteousness looks like.

You can do something similar. You can say to your loved one:

"I love you. You are made in God's image. I believe some things about the body and gender that I think matter. I also think some of the choices you are considering may not be best for you. And I am going to stay in this relationship. I am going to listen. I am going to share what I believe when appropriate. I am going to pray for you. And I am going to be here."

This is not easy. It is also the only thing that actually works. Control does not work. Judgment does not work. Unconditional affirmation of every choice does not work.

What works is love that has conviction. Relationship that does not require the other person to already agree with you.

\## What You Owe Them

As you work through this, remember what you owe the person who came out to you.

You owe them the truth. Not just about gender ideology, but about what Scripture actually teaches. About what the gospel actually offers. About what peace actually looks like. You do not owe them false agreement, but you owe them honesty.

You owe them your presence. You do not have to understand everything. But you have to show up. You have to maintain the relationship. You have to refuse to abandon them into ideology on either side.

You owe them time. You do not have to process this quickly. You do not have to have your position locked in stone. But you do have to take the time to think, to pray, to learn, to move toward them.

You owe them respect. They are a human being. They are made in God's image. Even if you think they are wrong, they deserve respect.

You owe them the gospel. Not ideology that promises certainty. Not ideology that affirms every choice. But the real gospel: you are loved by God. You are made in His image. He invites you into transformation that goes deeper than any identity you can claim.

If you can offer those things, you can handle this. It will be hard. You will make mistakes. You will say things you wish you had not said. But you can maintain love and conviction simultaneously.

That is what the church should teach. That is what families should practice. That is what the world needs to see.

\## EXPANSION: The Difference Between Boundaries and Rejection — How Long-Term Love Works

The chapter on what to say to loved ones identifies that families should hold conviction and relationship simultaneously. But how does this actually work over years, not just in initial conversations?

A mother believes that gender transition is not the path of healing. Her son transitioned three years ago. They still talk regularly. She still loves him. But the love is not simple.

The mother has learned to use the chosen name and pronouns. That was not difficult once she decided to do it. What is difficult is the ongoing navigation of her actual beliefs and her son's choices.

When her son mentions his hormone therapy, she does not say, "That is good. I am glad you are doing that." She also does not say, "That is sinful and you are ruining your body." She says something like, "I know this is important to you. I still have concerns. But I love you and I want to understand how you are doing."

This is the difference between boundaries and rejection.

A boundary is: "This is what I believe, and I am going to live consistently with what I believe." A mother can say: "I believe your biological sex is good and God-designed. I also love you and want to be in relationship with you. The tension between those things is real, but I am going to stay in the relationship."

Rejection is: "I will not be in relationship with you unless you change." This is different. This breaks the relationship.

The mother in this example has boundaries. She does not pretend to believe things she does not believe. She does not say, "I fully affirm your transition." But she also does not punish her son for his choices with withdrawal or silence.

Over years, what happens? Sometimes the son changes his mind about transition. Sometimes he does not. But either way, the relationship survives because it was never made conditional on his agreement.

This is how long-term love works. Not by capitulating on what you believe. But by refusing to make relationship conditional on agreement.

The mistake many families make is thinking they have to choose. Either "I accept my child's choices," meaning I affirm all of it. Or "I maintain my convictions," meaning I withdraw from the relationship.

There is a third way. You maintain your convictions and you maintain the relationship. Both.

This requires something the church has largely failed to teach: how to disagree with someone without rejecting them. How to believe they are making a wrong choice without making them an enemy. How to hold both concern and love simultaneously.

Jesus did this. Jesus had sharp disagreements with people he loved. He also refused to abandon them.

The church needs to recover this capacity.

The family member who comes out as transgender is a permanent disruption to the world you thought was stable. You have spent years understanding your relationship as one thing — sister, brother, parent, child. You have understood that person as one thing. Now that person is asking you to understand something different. And if you have deeply held convictions about what sex and gender mean, you are being asked to choose between your love for the person and your belief about the reality.

This is not a small thing. Do not let anyone tell you it is small. The people who say "just be supportive" are often the same people who have never had to hold this choice. They are offering you false comfort.

The first thing to know: you cannot control your relative's choices. You cannot convince them back. You cannot therapy them back. The fantasy that you can fix this will destroy you if you hold it. Let it go. The only work that is yours to do is the work of staying in relationship with a person you profoundly disagree with.

The second thing to know: you can stay in genuine relationship across this disagreement. Not by pretending you don't have convictions. Not by becoming affirming in your theology to ease the conversation. But by choosing to remain in the room with someone you love even though you believe they have made a choice you believe is contrary to Scripture. You can do this. It is hard. It is possible.

Here is what that looks like practically. You maintain the relationship by not making every conversation about the disagreement. You ask about their life, their work, their relationships — not the things that divide you but the person in front of you. You remember their birthday. You show up for their graduation. You do not pretend to celebrate what you believe is harmful, but you celebrate them.

When the conversation does happen — and it will — you speak your convictions clearly and you absorb the anger that comes back. You do not defend yourself by attacking their motives. You do not say "you're just being rebellious" or "you're running from God." You say what you actually believe about what Scripture teaches, and you receive their response as someone who has earned the right to respond because you have stayed in relationship rather than fled it.

You also name what is true: the church has harmed LGBTQ+ people in ways that were unjustifiable. Even if your theological position is correct, the way it has often been enforced has been cruel. You can hold both of these truths. Your relative can hold both of these truths too.

There is a difference between unconditional love and unconditional approval. This distinction is critical because one is required and the other is impossible. Unconditional love means you do not withdraw your presence because your relative disappoints you. It means you show up even when you believe they have made a choice you think is wrong. Unconditional approval means you pretend to agree with them when you don't. This is a lie, and it is not actually loving. It is abandonment dressed as acceptance.

The years will teach you things that theology alone cannot. You will see your relative's pain decrease or increase. You will see their faith deepen or dissolve. You will see their relationships flourish or collapse. You will have access to information that internet arguments do not provide. And you may find yourself holding convictions you are less certain about than you were when this began.

This does not mean you change your theology because you love someone. It means you hold your theology with more humility because you have faced the human cost of being wrong about people who are shaped by your convictions. It means you cannot unsee what you have seen.

The hardest part is accepting that you cannot resolve this. You cannot create a neat ending where your relative comes back to traditional sexual ethics or where you become affirming. You might get decades of faithful presence with an unresolved disagreement. This might be the only resolution available. And this might be enough.

Enough because the relationship itself becomes the testimony. The fact that you stayed. That you did not make it conditional. That you told the truth about what you believe and did not abandon someone for disagreeing. That you grieved what you believed was loss while loving the person you believed was lost.

The faith required here is larger than the faith required to stick with people who agree with you. It is faith that love is possible across genuine disagreement. It is faith that the presence of Christ matters more than the agreement of doctrine. It is faith that years of showing up is a form of evangelism that no argument can replicate.

Not every family finds this kind of resolution. Some families break. Some choose sides and never reconvene. But some families discover that love is more durable than disagreement, and that showing up year after year in the face of deep conviction holds a power that force never could.

\# CHAPTER 12: A CHURCH THAT HOLDS THE TRUTH AND WEEPS WITH THOSE WHO WEEP

\## The Way Forward

The church needs to be the safest place for the most confused person.

Not the place where confusion is affirmed uncritically. Not the place where confusion is mocked. But the place where someone in genuine dysphoria, in genuine questions about their body and their gender, can come and know: I will be heard. I will be loved. I will be helped to think clearly. I will not be abandoned.

This has to be the reputation of the church. Not the reputation it currently has.

Right now, for transgender people, the church is either the place where you get condemned and mocked, or the place where you get ideological affirmation without any real relationship. Both drive people away. Both fail the person in front of you.

The church needs to be something else. A place that holds the truth and weeps with those who weep.

\## What This Actually Looks Like

Let's be concrete about what this means in practice.

\*\*In the pulpit\*\*: The gospel is preached clearly. Biblical truth about the body, about sexual difference, about what it means to be made in God's image—this is taught without apology. But it is taught as good news, not as condemnation. It is taught in a way that creates space for people who do not fit the pattern to know they still belong.

The sermon does not mock gender-questioning people. The sermon does not use dysphoria as a cautionary tale. The sermon teaches what Scripture says and lets the weight of that truth stand on its own. It does not need mockery to be powerful.

\*\*In counseling\*\*: The church offers access to good therapists. Not affirmative therapists who quickly affirm every gender identity. Not conversion therapists who try to pray away dysphoria. Actual therapists who help people understand what they are experiencing—is this dysphoria, is it trauma, is it depression, is it peer influence, is it something else? Therapists who help people think without pushing them toward a predetermined conclusion.

\*\*In small groups\*\*: The church creates space where people can ask questions. Where someone dealing with dysphoria can say: "I do not know who I am. I do not know what my gender is. I am confused." And the response is not "you need to repent" and not "you need to transition," but "tell us more. What are you experiencing? How can we help you think through this?"

\*\*In leadership\*\*: The church trains leaders to understand gender dysphoria. To listen without immediately trying to fix. To distinguish between pastoral presence and ideological certainty. To know that the first job is to understand, not to judge.

\*\*In pastoral care\*\*: When someone in the congregation is dealing with gender questions—whether they are the person questioning or a parent of someone questioning—the church shows up. Offers support. Offers community. Offers prayer.

\*\*In hospital visitation\*\*: When someone transitions, medically or socially, the church does not disappear. The church is present. Not to affirm every choice. To offer Christ's presence.

\*\*In education\*\*: The church teaches theology of the body starting with children. Teaches what Scripture says about sexual difference, about embodiment, about identity in Christ. Not in an abstract way. In a way that helps young people think about themselves as people made in God's image, with bodies that are good, with identities rooted in Christ that go deeper than gender.

\## The Posture: Truth and Love Simultaneously

This is the core move: truth and love simultaneously.

Not truth that is loveless. Truth stated in a way that assumes the person in front of you matters.

Not love that abandons truth. Love that is willing to say hard things, but says them to the person's face, not behind their back, and says them because you care about their good.

What does this actually sound like?

You meet with someone who is struggling with dysphoria. You say:

"I love you. You are made in God's image. That does not change based on how you feel about your gender or what choices you make.

I also believe some things about the body. I believe the body is good. I believe sexual difference is created by God and called good. I believe the gospel invites us to accept and find peace in our actual embodied existence, not to escape from the body.

I do not know exactly what you are experiencing. I know you are suffering. And I believe the answer to your suffering is not to escape your body but to find your way to peace with your actual embodied existence.

I also do not know if this will happen through acceptance of your biological sex or through some other path. What I know is that you need help thinking through this—actual professional help, not ideology from either side.

And I want you to know: no matter what path you take, you belong to God. You are part of His people. And the church is a place where you can explore, ask questions, and work toward understanding who you are."

This is truth. And it is love. Not one at the expense of the other.

\## What the Church Should Not Do

Let me be clear about what this does not mean.

The church should not affirm every choice a person makes about their body. The church should not pretend that dysphoria is solved by transition. The church should not say: "Whatever you think about your gender is true."

The church should not pretend it does not have theology about these questions. The church should not try to be neutral. Neutrality is not possible. The question is whether your theology is biblical or not.

The church should not make people choose between their faith and their confusion. The church should not say: "You have to figure out your gender before you can be part of us."

The church should not abandon people into the ideology that will promise certainty the church cannot deliver. The church should stay in the relationship.

The church should not treat dysphoria as a matter of insufficient faith. Dysphoria is not a spiritual problem that prayer will solve, though prayer is good. Dysphoria is a psychological and spiritual problem that requires actual care.

The church should not pretend medical transition is neutral or inevitable. The church should help people think carefully about the costs and consequences.

The church should not use transgender people as a way to prove a theological point or win a culture war argument.

\## Specific Practices

Here are some specific things the church can do:

\*\*Create a resource library\*\*: Books, articles, and videos from multiple perspectives on gender dysphoria, transgender experience, and biblical theology. Let people read. Let people learn. Do not just give them one ideological perspective.

\*\*Offer support groups\*\*: For people questioning their gender. For parents of transgender children. For people dealing with regret after transition. For people working toward peace in their actual embodied existence. Let people know they are not alone.

\*\*Train leaders on trauma\*\*: Many people with dysphoria have experienced trauma. Many trans people have experienced rejection and harm. The church needs to understand trauma and how to respond with wisdom.

\*\*Create mentorship\*\*: Pair people who are struggling with people who have walked similar paths and found faith and peace. Not to convert them to a position. To show them what is possible.

\*\*Teach in youth group\*\*: Teach young people what Scripture actually says about the body, about gender, about identity in Christ. Teach it clearly. Give them theology they can hold onto when they are confused.

\*\*Have good exit conversations\*\*: When someone decides to transition, or to identify differently, or to make a major choice about their gender—do not let them leave the community silently. Have a conversation. Tell them: "We love you. We still believe what we believe. But you belong. The door is open if you want to come back."

\## The Cost of This Way

I want to be honest: doing this right is harder than either dismissal or uncritical affirmation.

It requires patience. It requires long-term commitment to people. It requires you to stay in the hard conversations instead of ending them quickly.

It requires you to hold conviction while also being willing to learn. To be humble about what you do not know while being clear about what you do know.

It requires you to refuse both camps: the right-wing exclusionists and the left-wing ideologues. It requires you to say: "We are not going to mock. And we are not going to pretend ideology is gospel."

That is a harder position to hold. But it is the position worth holding.

\## The Gospel Is Big Enough

At the root of all this is a conviction: the gospel is big enough.

The gospel is big enough for gender dysphoria. Big enough for the questions. Big enough for the confusion. Big enough for the suffering.

The gospel does not need to be protected from transgender people. The gospel does not need to be simplified to fit ideology. The gospel is strong enough to hold complexity, to honor truth, to extend grace, and to call people toward transformation.

What the church needs to believe—and demonstrate through its practices—is that the gospel is the answer. Not ideology from the right or the left. The gospel. Christ. The gospel's insistence that you are made in God's image, that your body is good, that your identity is rooted in something deeper than gender, that transformation is possible, that belonging is not conditional.

If the church can offer that—with clarity, with love, with patience, with persistence—then the church has something to say to transgender people that culture cannot say.

Then the church is the safest place for the most confused person.

\## The Kingdom Vision

Let me paint a picture of what this could look like.

A young person, assigned female at birth, is struggling with their body. They do not know who they are. They are experiencing dysphoria. They are confused.

They come to church. And they find a community where:

People listen. They ask questions. They do not assume they understand. They ask what the person is experiencing.

Leaders have expertise. They help the person think. They connect them with a good therapist. They do not push them toward transition or away from transition. They help them think.

Theology is taught. The person learns what Scripture says about the body, about sexual difference, about identity in Christ. Not as condemnation. As truth. As the foundation for understanding who they are.

Community is offered. They are part of a group of people working toward Christ-likeness. They meet people who have struggled with similar things. They are not alone.

Prayer is real. People pray for them. Not prayers that God will make them "normal." Prayers that God will help them find peace. That God will lead them to truth. That God will help them understand who they are.

Belonging is unconditional. They can ask questions. They can be confused. They do not have to have it figured out. They are welcomed as they are.

And over time, through prayer, through therapy, through community, through encountering Christ, they find their way to peace. Not peace that comes from denying dysphoria. Peace that comes from understanding their identity goes deeper than gender. Peace that comes from being at peace in their actual embodied existence.

That is the vision. That is what the church could offer.

For this to happen, the church has to:

\*\*Repent\*\* from the harm we have done—the mockery, the abandonment, the refusal to see people.

\*\*Learn\*\* what we have refused to learn—about dysphoria, about transgender experience, about the complexity of human existence.

\*\*Believe\*\* that the gospel is enough. That Christ is enough. That we do not need culture-war strategies. We need Christ.

If the church does this work, the church becomes what it is supposed to be: a place of refuge. A place of truth. A place of love. A place where the most confused person can come and find their way home.

That is the way forward.

The chapter describes what a church holding truth and love looks like. But how does a church actually build infrastructure to make this possible?

Books on the theology of the body. Books on detransition. Books on psychiatric treatment of dysphoria. Let people read. Let people learn from multiple sources.

Where people who transitioned and regret it can gather. Where people processing questions can gather. These groups do not exist in most churches because churches have largely failed to acknowledge these communities.

Fifth: A clear statement of belief. What does the church actually teach about the body, about sexual difference, about transgender identity? This statement should be clear. It should be held with humility. But it should be clear. Ambiguity is cruel because it suggests to people that the church does not actually believe what it says.

Sixth: Clear policies about inclusion and boundaries. A transgender person can be baptized if they have come to faith. A transgender person can be a member. Leadership is something the church thinks carefully about, and different churches will come to different conclusions. But these decisions should be made thoughtfully and clearly, not avoided.

Seventh: Ongoing education for the congregation. Not a one-time sermon. Ongoing teaching about what the Bible says about the body, about sexual difference, about identity in Christ. This teaching should shape how the congregation thinks about gender issues.

Eighth: Care when people leave. If someone transitions or makes choices the church does not affirm, and they choose to leave the church, the response is not to let them disappear silently. The response is to have a conversation. "We love you. We still believe what we believe. The door is open. You matter."

These are concrete things a church can do. Most churches do none of them. The result is that the church has no infrastructure to actually care for people dealing with gender questions.

That has to change.

\# CONCLUSION: IMAGE-BEARERS FIRST

\## Why the Gospel Refuses to Let Us Reduce Anyone to a Debate

There is a line in Genesis that settles something before any other question arises.

"So God created mankind in his own image, in the image of God he created them; male and female he created them."

Before the categories of male and female are explained, they are placed inside a larger truth: image-bearers. That is the foundation. That is what does not move.

Every person you know who is struggling with gender. Every person questioning their identity. Every person in dysphoria, in confusion, in pain about their body. Every person you are tempted to reduce to a position in a culture war.

Image-bearer first.

This is where the gospel starts. Not with gender ideology. Not with cultural positions. Not with certainty about what someone's problem is or what the solution must be.

With the simple, stubborn fact: you are made in God's image.

\## The Gospel Cannot Be Held Hostage

The church has allowed the gospel to be held hostage by ideology on both sides.

The right has used the gospel to justify mockery. To turn transgender people into evidence of cultural decline. To use them as punchlines in sermons and confidential conversations. The gospel became a club with which to beat people in confusion.

The left has used the gospel to justify uncritical affirmation. To pretend that every desire a person articulates is truth that must be affirmed. To treat ideology as gospel. To make belonging conditional on agreement with a particular understanding of gender.

Both have taken the gospel and chained it to something it should not be chained to.

The gospel does not belong to the right. The gospel does not belong to the left. The gospel belongs to Christ. And Christ does not reduce people to positions in a debate.

What the gospel does is name the person. See the person. Insist that the person is more than any ideology can capture.

\## What We Know and What We Do Not Know

Let me lay out what the church knows and what it does not know.

\*\*What we know:\*\*

Sexual difference is real. It was created by God. Male and female is how God made humanity. This is true. Genesis 1:27 is not culturally conditioned. This is God's design for the human race.

The body matters. It is good. God took a body. God will raise a body. Embodied existence is not a condition we escape. It is the mode of existence we are created for and resurrected for.

Image-bearing is what gives human worth. Not sexual function. Not gender identity. Not reproductive capacity. Being made in God's image is what gives you worth. Both male and female equally.

Identity in Christ is deeper than any other identity. Your politics do not define you. Your gender does not define you. Your sexuality does not define you. Who you are in Christ—that is what is ultimate.

\*\*What we do not know:\*\*

We do not know exactly what causes gender dysphoria. Biology, psychology, social factors, trauma—all of these may play a role. We do not have a complete picture.

We do not know what the long-term outcomes are for all the medical interventions being used to treat gender dysphoria. We have data on some interventions. On others, we are still gathering data.

We do not know how to help every person in dysphoria find peace. What works for some may not work for others. There may be multiple valid paths toward healing and peace.

We do not know how to handle every edge case, every variation of human experience, every person who does not fit neatly into categories.

What we should do with what we know and do not know is: hold our convictions clearly while extending grace broadly. Teach the truth. Refuse ideology. Stay in relationship with people instead of abandoning them to ideological communities.

\## The Gospel Response to Gender Confusion

Here is what the gospel offers to someone in gender confusion.

Not ideology that says: "Your internal sense of identity is absolute reality and your body is secondary." That is Gnosticism wearing modern clothes.

Not cruelty that says: "Your confusion is ridiculous and you need to stop." That is the failure of compassion.

But the gospel says:

You are made in God's image. That is your foundation. Not your body. Not your identity as you experience it. Being made in God's image. That is what gives you worth. That is permanent. That is not dependent on fitting any category or understanding yourself in any particular way.

Your body is good. God created it. God will resurrect it. Your body is not a costume to be exchanged. Your body is not incidental. Your body is where your life happens. The gospel invites you toward peace with your actual embodied existence, not escape from it.

Your identity is rooted in Christ. Not in your gender. Not in your sexuality. Not in what you feel about yourself on any given day. But in the fact that Christ claims you, Christ loves you, Christ is forming you into His likeness. That is who you are.

Transformation is possible. Not transformation that makes you someone other than who you are. But transformation that helps you be at peace with who you are. That helps you find freedom in your actual existence rather than constant rebellion against it.

Community is available. Not an online community that will affirm ideology. Not a church that mocks you. But a real community of real people trying to follow Jesus, trying to understand themselves and the world, trying to grow toward Christ-likeness. A community that will stay with you in your confusion. That will not abandon you.

That is the gospel response.

\## The Scandal of the Gospel

The gospel is scandalous in a different way than the culture wars assume.

The scandal is not that the church is too strict about gender. The scandal is not that the church is too affirming about identity. The scandal of the gospel is that it insists you are more than what you think you are.

The gospel says: you are trying to find your identity in gender, in sexuality, in political position, in personal preference. And while those things are part of your life, your identity goes deeper. Your identity is rooted in being made in God's image and being claimed by Christ.

That is scandalous. That is offensive to every ideology. Right and left. Because it decentralizes the self. It says: you are not the ultimate meaning-maker about your own existence. God is.

That is harder than either ideology. That is also more liberating.

\## EXPANSION: The Eschatological Question — What Will Bodies Look Like?

The conclusion invokes the promise of resurrection. The gospel says you will have a resurrection body. But what does that mean for someone dealing with gender dysphoria right now?

The resurrection is not escape from the body. It is transformation of the body. Paul says: "We know that when he appears, we shall be like him, for we shall see him as he is." We will be like Christ. And Christ is embodied. He has a body in resurrection.

But the resurrection body is also transformed. Paul writes: "It is sown a perishable body; it is raised an imperishable body." The body is the same body, but transformed. Not destroyed. Not replaced. Transformed.

What does this mean for gender?

Here is what we know: In the resurrection, we will not need to procreate. The biological function of sexual difference—reproduction—will not be necessary. So it is possible that gender as we understand it, inextricably tied to reproductive capacity, might be different in resurrection.

Here is what we do not know: whether gender will disappear entirely, whether it will persist in different form, whether it will be revealed to be something other than we understood it. The resurrection remains mysterious.

But here is what matters: The promise of resurrection means that your struggle with your body now is not the final word. Your dysphoria now is not eternal. Your confusion about your gender now is not the ultimate reality.

For someone in dysphoria, that is either hopeful or terrifying, depending on what they believe about their gender.

If someone believes their true gender is different from their biological sex, the promise that bodies will be transformed in resurrection could mean: "Maybe in the resurrection, my body will finally match who I am."

If someone believes that accepting biological sex is the path to healing, the promise of resurrection could mean: "Maybe in the resurrection, the suffering connected to my body will be redeemed, and I will finally be at peace."

Both are hopeful. Both are based in eschatology. Both invite the person toward trusting God's future rather than being controlled by present suffering.

The church should offer this eschatological perspective. Not as a way to dismiss present suffering. But as a way to contextualize it. Your dysphoria is real now. But it is not eternal. Your confusion is real now. But it is not final.

God will redeem your suffering. God will transform your body. God will make all things new. In that light, what you are struggling with now, while real, is not the whole story.

This is what gives people the freedom to handle present suffering without being consumed by it. Not denial. Eschatological hope.

\## EXPANSION: The Mercy in Boundaries — When No Is an Act of Love

The conclusion affirms both conviction and love. But conviction sometimes has to say no. The church has to be able to say no without that no being cruel.

Consider a parent who believes that their adolescent child is moving toward transition too quickly, without adequate psychological evaluation. The parent says: "I love you. I will not support medical transition right now. I think you need more time and more professional help to understand what you are experiencing."

This is a no. The child hears it as rejection. The culture around the child tells them it is rejection. "Your parent does not affirm you. That is violence. That is abuse."

But is it?

What the parent is actually saying is: "I love you too much to affirm a medical decision I think is happening too quickly. I think you need to slow down and think carefully. I will help you get good psychological care. But I am not going to affirm something I believe could harm you."

This is what mercy sometimes looks like. Not affirmation of every choice. But boundaries set out of love.

The problem is that the culture has redefined this. The culture has said that boundaries are a form of rejection. That saying no is not loving.

But the gospel knows better. The gospel sets boundaries. God says no to certain things. The church says no to certain behaviors. This is not unloving. It is the only way love actually works.

If you affirm everything someone wants, you are not loving them. You are abandoning them to their own confusion. You are refusing to offer wisdom. You are refusing to stand for anything.

But if you set boundaries—clearly, lovingly, with full knowledge that the boundary might cost you the relationship—that is love.

The parent who says, "I will not affirm medical transition without adequate evaluation," is setting a boundary out of love. The parent might be wrong. The parent might be right. But the boundary itself is not unloving. It is the expression of love that has conviction.

The church needs to recover the understanding that no, said with love, is merciful. Not cruel.

This is one of the hardest things the church has to learn. In a culture that has redefined all boundaries as oppressive, the church has to insist: there are boundaries that are loving.

\## EXPANSION: The Monster Is Never in the Mirror — How the Church Can Be Different

The conclusion references "The Monster in the Mirror," the book-within-the-book. The central claim is this: the monster is never in the mirror. The slaveholder was not a monster. He was convinced of his own virtue. He was part of a community confirming him. He read the Bible his culture had translated. He had no access to the information that would have changed his mind.

What does this have to do with gender?

It means: on both sides of this issue, people are convinced they are right. The person transitioning is convinced that transition is the path to freedom. The pastor affirming gender ideology is convinced that affirmation is the path to compassion. The parent opposing transition is convinced that protection is the path to love. The pastor opposing any dialogue on these issues is convinced that conviction is the path to truth.

All of them could be wrong. All of them could be partly right. All of them are embedded in communities that are confirming them. All of them are reading the same Bible and coming to different conclusions because of their interpretive lens.

The church's job is to refuse to be a community that confirms people in wrongness. The church's job is to be a place where the lens gets examined. Where assumptions get questioned. Where we ask: Are we missing something? Are we reading the Bible through a cultural lens rather than letting the Bible challenge our culture?

This is hard. It is much easier to be part of a community that confirms you. It requires courage to say: "Maybe I am missing something. Maybe my culture has shaped my reading of Scripture in ways I am not seeing. Maybe the person I thought was clearly wrong is partly right."

The beauty of the gospel is that it offers this. The gospel does not require certainty. The gospel requires repentance. The willingness to turn around. The willingness to see things differently.

If the church could offer that—a community where people are free to question their assumptions, where Scripture is engaged freshly rather than through ideological lens, where people in confusion are welcomed rather than condemned—the church would be different. The church would be redemptive.

Instead, the church has largely become another ideological space. Left-leaning churches affirm everything. Right-leaning churches condemn everything. Both are the same failure: ideological certainty substituted for the harder work of faithful discipleship.

The church could be different. If it would ask: What does Scripture actually say? How is my culture shaping what I see? Who am I missing? What am I blind to?

That posture would change everything. Not the doctrine. The doctrine would stay the same. But the posture. The humility. The genuine openness to learning.

That is what the church could offer that the world cannot.
