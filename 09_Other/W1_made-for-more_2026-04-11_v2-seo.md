**INTRODUCTION**

The Argument Nobody Wants to Have

What the Bible Really Says About Women, Leadership, and the Gospel

She was already crying when she sat down across from me. Not the sharp, angry kind of crying—the kind that comes from betrayal after you've given someone a chance to be different. She had been at First Baptist for seven years. She knew the doctrines. She could articulate the gospel better than most pastors I know. She had gone to seminary. She carried a master's degree in biblical studies. But two days before this conversation, she had written an email to our leadership team saying she was leaving the church.

I asked her why.

She told me what I should have been ready for but wasn't. She told me about the conversation with one of our staff members—a good man, a faithful man—who had approached her about a teaching role in our church. She was excited. She had been waiting for an opportunity like this. But when they talked details, it became clear that the role had a built-in ceiling. She could lead women's Bible studies. She could lead children's ministry. She could do hospitality. Those were the lanes. Teaching the broader congregation, preaching to the whole church, shepherding people across gender lines—those were not available to her.

When she asked why, the answer came back the way it always does. It was about protection. It was about order. It was about what the Bible says.

She had studied what the Bible actually says. That was the problem.

She knew the difference between 1 Corinthians 14:34-35 and Paul's actual theology of women. She knew those verses appear in some manuscripts and not others, and that they contradict what Paul wrote everywhere else—including in 1 Corinthians 11, where he assumes women are praying and prophesying in the gathered church. She knew about Priscilla teaching Apollos. She knew about Deborah. She knew about Phoebe the deacon. She knew about the women who hosted churches in their homes. She knew about Junia, whom Paul might have called an apostle.

She knew, in other words, that the ceiling was not biblical. It was cultural. And the culture it served was ours.

So she was leaving. Not because she had stopped believing in Jesus. Because she had stopped believing the church actually believed in Jesus—at least not the Jesus who elevated women to scandal-level prominence, who broke every social code to honor women, who appeared first to a woman after the resurrection, who chose a woman to announce the most important news in history.

I want to tell you about two conversations I have watched Christians have about this topic. Both conversations are wrong. And the damage they do—to the church, to women, to the gospel itself—is why this book needs to exist.

The first conversation is the one the progressive church is having. It goes something like this: The Bible is a product of its time, shaped by the patriarchy it inherited. We have evolved. We have science now. We have psychology. We understand gender differently. So we discard the parts of Scripture that don't fit our current understanding of equality and keep the parts that do. Womanhood is whatever a woman decides it is. There is no design, no created order, no account to God for how we image Him in our sexuality. There is only authentic self-expression and the freedom to construct identity according to personal preference. If the Bible disagrees, we have outgrown the Bible.

This conversation empties the category. It tells women they can be anything, do anything, become anything—which sounds like freedom until you realize it means nothing in particular. It tells women that their womanhood is irrelevant to their identity. It tells them the body doesn't matter, sexuality is infinitely malleable, and God has nothing to say about any of it. It tells women they get to define themselves with no reference to anything outside themselves. That is not freedom. That is orphaning.

The second conversation is the one the conservative church is having. It goes like this: God designed women for specific roles. These roles are not inferior—they are different. Complementary, we like to say. This design is permanent and non-negotiable. Women are called to be wives and mothers and to support the male leadership of the church and home. Any woman who chafes against these boundaries is not chafing against God's design—she is rebelling against it. Her dissatisfaction is her problem, not Scripture's problem. Accept your role. Find joy in limitation. Stop trying to lead. Stop trying to speak. Stop trying to be something you were never made to be.

This conversation trades freedom for order. It promises peace through submission. It tells women that questioning their boundaries is the same as rejecting God's Word. It turns limitation into virtue. It confuses silence with holiness. And it has produced generations of women who love Jesus while being simultaneously diminished by His church.

Both conversations have one thing in common: they both believe the Bible is the problem.

The progressive says the Bible is antiquated. The conservative says the Bible is clear—and that clarity means women have permanently limited roles. One deconstructs Scripture to affirm gender autonomy. The other weaponizes Scripture to maintain male authority. Both assume the same thing: that the Bible's vision of womanhood is either irrelevant or restrictive.

What if they are both wrong?

What if the Bible's vision of womanhood is neither progressive deconstruction nor patriarchal submission, but something far more subversive to both sides? What if the gospel actually liberates women from both the false freedom that demands they become men and the false submission that demands they become invisible?

This book exists because a woman is sitting in my office, seminary degree in hand, theology sharp enough to correct pastors, and she is crying because the church that baptized her into Christ has spent seven years making it clear that her gifts, her calling, her voice, and her intellect are too dangerous to be trusted in the spaces where the real work happens.

This book also exists because I am a man who has benefited from every system that diminished her. I have stood in pulpits that were closed to women. I have made decisions in leadership meetings where no woman had a seat. I have taught Scripture my entire adult life without ever questioning whether my hermeneutic about gender was actually biblical or simply inherited. I have preached sermons about women's roles that I never would have preached about men's roles, and I did not notice the inconsistency.

I know what it is like to be so embedded in a system that benefits you that you cannot see it is a system at all. I was an atheist once. I was convinced of my own logic, certain of my own reasoning, absolutely confident that I could think my way to truth. I could not see the frame I was standing inside until someone outside the frame showed it to me. It is the same with this. I could not see the patriarchy because I was living inside it. It looked like truth. It looked like Scripture. It looked like the natural order of things.

I am writing this as a pastor who has counseled women the church has failed. The woman in my office is not alone. There is the pastor's wife told that her calling is to support her husband's calling—and when her calling is different, she is made to feel unfaithful. There is the single woman told that her life is incomplete until she finds a husband, even though the apostle Paul disagrees with this theology entirely. There is the mother told that working outside the home is a betrayal of her design, even though Priscilla ran a business, and the Proverbs 31 woman was a real estate investor and textile merchant. There is the woman with prophetic gifting told to keep quiet in church, even though the Bible is full of female prophets. There is the woman with theological acuity told she could lead women but not mixed gatherings, even though there is no scriptural basis for this distinction.

I am also writing this as a husband to Susanna—a woman whose intellect matches mine, whose theological conviction exceeds mine in places, whose calling is not to support my calling but to be alongside it. I have watched her move through a church culture that treats gifted women as problems to be managed rather than resources to be released. I have watched her work harder than I work to stay within the lanes the church has drawn. I have watched her diminish herself to fit a mold that was never designed for her.

This book is for the woman who loves Jesus and is tired of being diminished by His church. It is for the woman whose gifts are real but whose opportunities are limited by her gender. It is for the woman who has read Scripture and cannot find the ceiling everyone keeps telling her exists. It is for the woman who has been told to accept her role and has tried to accept her role and has discovered that acceptance of injustice is not a spiritual virtue—it is spiritual compromise.

But this book is also for the man. Specifically, for the man who loves Jesus and has never questioned whether his theology of women is actually biblical or simply cultural inheritance. For the man who has benefited from systems of male authority and has never had to think about what those systems cost others. For the man who preaches complementarianism without ever examining whether his hermeneutic would survive the same scrutiny he applies to every other doctrine. For the man who believes he is defending Scripture when he is actually defending patriarchy.

The argument nobody wants to have is this: What if the fall introduced patriarchy, not God? What if gender hierarchy is part of the curse, not part of creation? What if the entire gospel narrative is God redeeming what sin broke, which means undoing the dominion of men over women is part of the redemptive arc? What if women in leadership is not rebellion against Scripture but obedience to it?

That is the argument we are about to have.

And if you have benefited from the status quo, it will feel dangerous. It should.

The two lies that colonized the church arrive from opposite directions, but they converge at the same destination: a woman who is less than fully human.

The first lie comes from the progressive impulse. It says gender is a social construct with no theological tether. Woman can mean anything—or nothing. We can deconstruct it entirely, we can make it fungible, we can declare it theologically irrelevant because every assertion about it was merely patriarchy wearing a Bible. There is liberation in this move, or the appearance of it. A woman does not have to be a mother. A woman does not have to be subordinate. A woman does not have to limit herself to anything. But here is what gets lost in this thoroughgoing deconstruction: if woman can mean anything, it means nothing. The category collapses. The particularity vanishes. And what was meant to be liberation becomes a kind of erasure—the woman who fought to teach becomes a voice saying she was never distinctly a woman, only a human who happened to have been called "woman" by accident of biology.

I have sat in rooms where this deconstruction happens. I have watched women who were wounded by complementarian churches arrive at a place where they reject not just the distortion but the category itself. The woman who spent her thirties being told by her pastor that her teaching gift was a temptation to rebellion. The woman whose church informed her that female leadership was a departure from Scripture, a capitulation to worldly feminism. The woman who finally stood up and said, "Then I reject not just your theology but the entire structure." And I understood the wound that drove her there. But I also saw something get lost when the category of woman—not as the church had twisted it, but as Scripture names it—became something to be deconstructed rather than redeemed.

The second lie comes from the conservative impulse. It says woman is deeply maternal, domestic, supportive—and that these are biblical categories rather than what they actually are: 1950s American cultural assumptions baptized as eternal truth. A woman's highest calling is in the home. Her submission is not a fallen distortion but an unfallen design. Her greatest fulfillment comes through serving her family. The church took a cultural moment—postwar domestic ideology—and froze it in amber and called it Scripture. I benefited from this arrangement. I was taught it. I preached it. I became a pastor who told women that their disappointment with these constraints was actually a failure to embrace their design. That the woman who felt called to teach or lead or work in the public sphere was resisting God's order for her life.

Both lies are cultural products masquerading as theology. Both were produced in specific moments by specific people with specific interests. The progressive lie emerges from Enlightenment assumptions that the individual self is the fundamental unit of reality and that particularity of any kind is merely oppression dressed in tradition. The conservative lie emerges from industrial-era assumptions about separate spheres and the need for stable domestic structures that coincided with—and served—male economic power. Neither arrived from Scripture. Both arrived wrapped in Scripture.

The damage each one does is distinct, and I have seen both up close.

The damage of the conservative lie is the woman who stayed in a marriage where her husband committed adultery repeatedly, whose pastor told her that divorce was unbiblical, that submission meant remaining, that her suffering might be redemptive. She stayed for thirteen years. Her children grew up watching her slowly disappear. When she finally left, she left also from the church, because the church had become synonymous with the institution that held her captive. She told me, years later, that what wounded her most was not the pastor's ignorance but his certainty. He spoke with the authority of Scripture when he was actually speaking from 1950s patriarchal panic about women who might step outside their assigned roles. He did not know the difference. He is not unusual. Thousands of women know exactly the speech pattern I am describing because they have sat across from it in a pastor's office, being told that their unhappiness is a spiritual problem.

The damage of the progressive lie is quieter and harder to name because it wears the mask of liberation. It is the woman who deconstructed her faith because the church would not ordain her, and in deconstructing her faith she lost not just the institution but the gospel. She was right to reject the church's prohibition. She was right to see the arbitrary nature of complementarian restriction. But somewhere in the process of rejecting the distortion, she lost the ability to see anything redemptive in the category of woman itself. She became convinced that if the church had lied about womanhood, then womanhood itself must be a lie—merely biology with no theological weight, no beauty particular to it, no strength worth naming as distinct. She gained access to male domains and lost the confidence that there was anything worth knowing about being a woman.

I came to this conversation as a man who inherited the conservative version. My mother raised me without a father. She was, by necessity, everything—provider, protector, spiritual guide, moral exemplar. I never heard anyone tell her she was acting outside God's design for womanhood. But then I grew up and sat under teaching that told me women belonged in the home, that female leadership was a compromise with the culture, that my mother had done what necessity demanded but that necessity had created an exception to a rule that was otherwise absolute. I learned to see my mother as admirable despite her womanhood, not because of it. I preached that structure for years. I told the women in my congregation that their gifts were real, but that the proper expression of those gifts was primarily domestic. I meant it kindly. I was certain it was biblical.

I was wrong. And my wrongness was not incidental. It was part of a system that diminished women in the name of protecting biblical truth. I benefited from women's diminishment. I gained authority I would not have had in a world where women could claim equal platform. I spoke with the weight of the institution behind me. I did not have to earn the same way women do, because the system said women were not supposed to be earning that kind of authority in the first place.

To name this now feels like it should be cheap. Like I should be able to say "I was wrong" and move on. But this is the tricky part of seeing your own complicity. You cannot unknow it. You cannot un-preach those sermons. You cannot restore the opportunities that were closed to women because men like me occupied the space where women might have stood. I have daughters-in-law. I have sisters. I have watched women in my church recognize their own gifting, their own call, their own capacity to speak truth to power—and then watch them negotiate the minefield of whether they could actually do any of that without betraying their standing in the community. One of them asked me, five years ago, whether she could teach. She was educated. She had thought through Scripture carefully. She had spoken words that changed people's trajectories. I had to tell her the official answer was no. What I should have said was: I have taught you to accept a restriction that has nothing to do with your capacity and everything to do with my comfort.

The book you are about to read is an attempt to speak differently. Not to defend the progressive deconstruction, which loses the beauty of the category by dissolving it entirely. But to recover what Scripture actually says about womanhood from beneath the cultural debris that the church has piled on top of it. To name what both lies get wrong. To sit in the tension of being a man who benefits from patriarchy while acknowledging that patriarchy is a corruption, not a creation. To say to the women I know and the women I do not know: your particularity as a woman is not a problem to be solved or deconstructed. It is not a spiritual handicap. It is not a cultural construct. It is a theological reality, and the church's refusal to see it clearly has done immeasurable damage.

I am a complementarian writing a book that challenges complementarianism. That sentence contains the entire cost of this project.

When I began the theological work that led to this manuscript, I did not set out to write a deconstruction. I set out to read Genesis more carefully. What I discovered—what the actual text says versus what I had been taught it says—forced a choice: defend the system I inherited, or follow the evidence where it leads. There is a real cost to the second option, and I want to be honest about it before we proceed.

I will lose readers. Not all of them, but some. The ones who came to this book looking for theological permission to maintain the hierarchy they already prefer. The ones who built their marriage on a particular reading of Ephesians 5 and hear any revision of that reading as a threat to their stability. The ones whose entire worldview depends on the complementarian structure—and whose belonging in their own church communities depends on staying within it. I understand that loss is real, and I am not pretending it is not.

I will be accused of capitulating to culture. That is already happening in manuscript form, from readers I trust. "James, you're just bowing to progressive pressure." "The secular world is winning you over." "You're choosing expediency over fidelity to Scripture." These accusations will sting more from some voices than others. When they come from men I respect, men I have learned from, men whose approval I still want—that is when I have to remember why I did this work at all. It is not because the culture demanded it. It is because the text demanded it.

The hardest part is this: I have built my pastoral credibility, in significant part, on a complementarian reading. I have taught it from the pulpit. I have mentored young pastors with these assumptions intact. I have counseled couples with these frameworks in place. There is no way to write this book without admitting I got something wrong. Not just wrong in a minor exegetical detail that doesn't matter much. Wrong in a way that shaped how I told women what their lives could be. Wrong in a way that limited voices I should have been amplifying. Wrong in a way that was easier to maintain than to examine.

That admission is not rhetorical for me. It is the texture of this entire manuscript. I am not standing above the complementarian position looking down at it with detached clarity. I am standing in it, admitting it shaped me, and showing the work of getting out of it.

The moment that broke this open came five years ago. Not in an academic article. Not in a conference. In my office. A woman from our congregation—a woman with a Master's degree in Biblical Theology from a seminary I respected—came to ask why she could not teach in the adult Sunday school class. She was not asking to be an elder. Not asking to be the lead pastor. Asking to teach adults what she actually knew. The pastoral leadership—and I was the one who held the line on this—said no. The role of women in public teaching was limited by our understanding of 1 Timothy 2 and 1 Corinthians 11.

I remember her face when I explained this. Not anger. Confusion. Not that she disagreed with my interpretation—though she did. But that she could not understand how a church could look at her training, her knowledge, her call, and say: your gifts are real, your study is serious, your faith is genuine. We just can't let you use any of it where it actually matters.

She did not argue with me. She just asked a question: "If I had been a man with my same credentials, would you have said no?" I lied and said yes. We both knew I was lying. If she were a man, she would be teaching. She would be on the elder track. Her voice would be in the room where decisions get made.

After she left, I sat with that lie for a long time. I sat with it in seminary classrooms I visited. I sat with it reading the actual Greek of the passages I thought I understood. I sat with it in marriage, asking my wife—who has read the Scripture more carefully than I have—what she thought I should have said to that woman. And the more I sat with it, the more I realized I had been reading these texts not as an exegete but as a defender of a system. I was not asking what the text says. I was asking what the text can be made to say in defense of something I already believed.

That is a form of idolatry. Not in the sense that complementarianism itself is worship of a false god, but in the sense that I had made the system itself into something that mattered more than the actual text, more than the actual women in my congregation, more than the image of God I was reflecting by my decision.

This book is not a capitulation. It is a return. A return to the actual text. A return to what Scripture actually says about women, unfettered by centuries of male theological anxiety about what that might cost us. I am not abandoning Scripture. I am reading it more carefully than I was taught to. And that careful reading is devastating to complementarianism—not because I am reading culture into the text, but because I am reading the text on its own terms.

The cost of that honesty is that I have to admit: I was wrong. I told women their gifts were limited by God when, in fact, their gifts were limited by me—by my theology, by my church's tradition, by my unwillingness to ask hard questions about whether we were reading rightly. That admission is not something I get to take back. It is permanent. It is the soil this book grows from.

And there is another cost, more subtle: I know that some of the young men I have mentored will feel betrayed. They came to learn what they thought was biblical complementarianism. They built their marriages and their ministries on these frameworks. Now their mentor is saying: that frame was wrong, and here is why. That is disorienting. That feels like a bait-and-switch. I understand that anger, and I do not have a way to make it softer.

But here is what I have learned: the men whose faith is actually grounded in Scripture will do the work. They will go back to the text. They will read the Greek themselves. They will sit with the hard questions. And some of them—not all, but some—will come to the same conclusions I have come to. The ones whose faith was actually grounded in the system, in the comfort of a hierarchy that benefited them, those men will have to choose between the system and following evidence. I cannot make that choice for them. I can only make it for myself.

This book exists because I made that choice. I made it imperfectly. I made it late. I made it after women like that seminary-trained member of my congregation had already spent years being told their gifts did not matter in the places where it counts. But I made it. And the cost of being honest about that choice, about what it has cost me in credibility and ease and the approval of people whose approval I value—that cost is what gives this book its integrity.

If this had been easy to write, it would not be worth reading. If I had written it without loss, it would be one more attempt to have it all ways—to be prophetic without paying a price, to change my mind without admitting I was wrong, to challenge my tribe without risking my place in it. That is not what happened here. I am risking my place. I have already felt the cost. And I am doing it because the alternative—defending a system I no longer believe in, protecting a theology that is harming women I love—is a bigger betrayal than any accusation my former allies can level at me.

This book is not the culmination of my thinking on these matters. It is the beginning of a different kind of pastoral work. Work that takes seriously what it means to pastor women who have been systematically told their gifts do not matter. Work that means admitting the church has sinned against them—that I have sinned against them—in the name of God. Work that means being willing to be wrong and to say it clearly.

That is what you are reading. Not a polished argument from a position of certainty. A confession. A reorientation. A pastor admitting he got it wrong and inviting you to get it right—to read Scripture not through the lens of what benefits us, but through the lens of what the text actually says when we stop defending a system and start listening.

CHAPTER ONE

Eve Before the Fall

Biblical Womanhood, Purpose, and the Original Image of God

The first time I actually read Genesis 1 and 2 as they were written—not as they had been explained to me—I was thirty-two years old. I was preparing a sermon on creation. I had a Hebrew lexicon in front of me. And I realized I had been reading a text I thought I knew completely.

What struck me first was not what the text said. It was what the text did not say.

It did not establish hierarchy. It did not assign the woman to a secondary role. It did not set up a chain of command. This is what biblical womanhood actually looks like before sin enters the picture. God looked at creation and said it was good. God looked at the man alone and said this was not good. The man needed a corresponding strength. And God created the woman.

The word used here is ezer. It appears in the original Hebrew text exactly nine times when describing the woman's relationship to the man. And I had spent my entire adult life—all of it—translating that word as "helper" in my mind. The word made sense to me. It felt right. A helper supports, assists, makes the leader's job easier. There was nothing threatening about it. There was nothing that challenged the way I had been taught women should function in the church and home.

But I was looking at a Hebrew lexicon. And what I discovered that afternoon changed how I read Scripture.

Ezer is the word the Old Testament uses for God.

Not sometimes. Regularly. Persistently. When Israel is in distress and calls out to God, the response is that God is their ezer. Psalm 121:1-2: "I lift my eyes to the hills. From where does my help \[ezer\] come? My help \[ezer\] comes from the Lord, who made heaven and earth." Deuteronomy 33:29: "Blessed are you, O Israel! Who is like you, a people saved by the Lord, the shield of your help \[ezer\] and the sword of your majesty!" The same word. The word used to describe how God relates to His people is the word used to describe how the woman relates to the man.

God is not a helper. God is the one in whose strength we trust. God is the one without whom the mission cannot be accomplished. God is corresponding strength—the strength that meets another's need at the exact point of insufficiency.

The woman is not called a helper. She is called an ezer.

This is not semantics. This is the difference between being a subordinate and being an indispensable counterpart. This is the difference between supporting someone's vision and standing alongside them with your own. This is the difference between being an accessory to the story and being a co-author of it.

When I began to understand what the text actually said, I began to understand how thoroughly it had been distorted.

The translational history here is not accidental. It is not neutral. It is the history of how male interpreters read texts through the lens of male assumptions and then presented those assumptions as if they were exegesis. The King James Version translated ezer as "help meet," which itself is telling—a "meet" is an appropriate partner, which captures something closer to the actual meaning, but even "help" undermines the force of the original word. Subsequent translations locked us into "helper" as the standard English rendering. And every generation of pastors taught their congregations that the woman's role was to help the man accomplish his vision, not to stand alongside him as a corresponding strength with her own vision.

The medieval theologian Jerome had access to the original Hebrew. He could have translated ezer with precision. Instead, he translated it according to the patriarchal theology he inherited from Augustine and the surrounding culture. The King James translators did the same. They were not ignorant of the Hebrew. They were not attempting to obscure meaning. They were simply translating what seemed obviously true to them—that women were subordinate to men, so the word describing woman's relationship to man must mean subordinate.

And once a translation is locked into the King James, it becomes Scripture in the English-speaking church. It becomes hard to challenge because it is Scripture. That is the power of translation. It is also the danger.

But even beyond the word ezer, the entire narrative of Genesis 1-2 contradicts what has been taught. Read it without commentary. Read it as the text stands. What does it actually say?

Both the man and the woman are created in the image of God. Not the man first and the woman as an afterthought. Not the man as the primary image-bearer and the woman as a secondary one. Genesis 1:27 uses one sentence to describe both: "So God created mankind in his own image, in the image of God he created them; male and female he created them." The parallelism is absolute. Both. Together. One image in two expressions.

Both are given dominion. Genesis 1:28: "God blessed them and said to them, 'Be fruitful and increase in number; fill the earth and subdue it. Rule over the fish in the sea and the birds in the sky and over every living creature that moves on the ground.'" This is not directed to the man with the understanding that the woman participates under his authority. This is directed to both. The mandate to rule is given to both. The command to be fruitful is given to both. The responsibility to steward creation is given to both.

Both are given work. Genesis 2:15 says the Lord placed the man in the Garden to tend and keep it. It does not say the woman's role is to tend the man while he tends the garden. It says both are placed there, both are given work, both have a role in God's cosmic purposes.

There is not a hint of hierarchy in Genesis 1-2. There is no chain of command. There is no indication that one is meant to rule the other. There is complementarity—they are different, sexually and otherwise—but complementarity and hierarchy are not the same thing. A man and a woman can be different without one being subordinate to the other. Indeed, ezer suggests they are different precisely because they bring different strengths to the task.

What Genesis 1-2 shows is a man and a woman, both bearing God's image, both given dominion, both given work, both having something essential to bring to the project of stewardship. What it shows is partnership. What it shows is correspondence—strength meeting strength, vision meeting vision, leadership meeting leadership.

And then God blessed them and called this good.

This is the creation order. Not subordination. Not hierarchy. Not the man as leader and the woman as support. This is man and woman together, equal in dignity, equal in responsibility, equal in their commission to image God and rule creation.

What comes next—what happens in Genesis 3 and beyond—is not an intensification of God's design. It is a corruption of it.

But the Church has spent two thousand years reading the fall back into creation, then reading patriarchy into Scripture as if it were the natural order rather than the fallen order. We have taken what sin introduced and called it what God intended. We have made women apologize for being made in God's image. We have told them their strength is a problem to be managed rather than a gift to be released.

When I was preparing that sermon, I had been a pastor for sixteen years. I had read Genesis 1-2 hundreds of times. I had preached from it. I had taught it to others. But I had never actually read it. I had read what I had been taught it said. And there is a significant difference.

This is what the patriarchal theology of the church has cost us: the actual reading of Scripture.

We have been so certain that the text supported male authority that we have never bothered to examine whether it actually does. We have been so committed to defending the system that has benefited us that we have not considered whether the system is biblical or simply cultural. We have inherited patriarchal interpretations and passed them down as if they were exegesis, never stopping to ask whether our teachers had actually read the text or only read it through the grid of their own assumptions.

The invitation of Genesis 1-2 is to stop. To slow down. To read what the text actually says rather than what we have been told it says. And to come to terms with the possibility that the structure we have defended for so long might not be what Scripture actually commends.

The woman was not created subordinate. She was created as corresponding strength. She was created as an ezer.

If we want to know what women are made for, we have to begin here—not in Genesis 3, where the fall introduces hierarchy, not in 1 Corinthians 11, where Paul is addressing cultural confusion, not in 1 Timothy 2, which may not even be Paul's writing and which contradicts everything he says about women elsewhere.

We have to begin with what God said in the beginning, before anything was broken, before sin introduced its curse, before patriarchy was imposed on creation.

We have to read Genesis 1-2 as if we actually believe what it says.

The word that Scripture uses to describe the second human being God created is ezer. It appears in Genesis 2:18, and most English translations render it as "helper." That word choice has done more damage to women than perhaps any other single translation decision in the history of the church. Because "helper" carries a freight of subordination that ezer does not carry in Hebrew. A helper assists. A helper supports. A helper stands behind. When you read "helper," you picture a supporting role in a narrative where someone else is the protagonist. The problem is that ezer does not mean any of that.

The Hebrew word ezer appears approximately 21 times in the Old Testament, and in the vast majority of those occurrences, it refers to God. God is the ezer of Israel. God is the military strength that rescues, the power that saves, the one who stands with you in battle. In Psalm 33:20, the psalmist says, "We wait in hope for the Lord; he is our help and our shield; he is our help and our shield." The word translated "help" is ezer. The psalmist is not describing emotional support. He is describing military aid. He is describing the overwhelming force that stands between you and your enemies. In Numbers 21, when Israel is traveling through the wilderness and is threatened by other nations, Moses calls out to God as ezer—the one who will fight for them, who will stand with them against overwhelming odds, who will make victory possible. This is the word. This is what it means.

The woman in Genesis 2:18 is ezer. She is not the supporting player in the man's narrative. She is the power that makes his story complete.

This becomes clearer when you attend to the literary structure of the creation account. Genesis 1 establishes God's pattern: God sees that something is not yet complete in creation, and God makes what is necessary to complete it. God sees that there is light without darkness, and God creates night. God sees that there is dry ground without water, and God creates the seas. God sees that there is earth without life, and God creates vegetation. The pattern is relentless and deliberate: incompleteness is met with creative provision until the entire creation is good. In Genesis 2, the pattern continues. God sees the earth without sufficient water, and God causes streams to water the ground. God sees the garden without a human to tend it, and God creates the man. Then comes the crucial moment.

God surveys creation—the land, the animals, the fullness of what has been made—and God's assessment is this: it is not good for the man to be alone. Not good. This is God's evaluation. The creation that God had pronounced "good" in Genesis 1 is now pronounced "not good." The incompleteness is not the woman. The incompleteness is the man. The man by himself, in the midst of a good creation, is insufficient. God has made an error, or rather, God has made an intentional incompleteness. The man needs something that does not yet exist.

God brings the animals to the man to see if one of them could be the ezer. The man names them. But among all the animals, there is no one whose structure matches his structure, whose capacity corresponds to his capacity, whose existence can complete what is incomplete. The text is explicit: no suitable helper was found. The word translated "suitable" is neged, which means opposite or counterpart. The man needs someone who is opposite him, equivalent to him, someone whose existence brings proportion to creation.

So God does something extraordinary. God puts the man into a deep sleep. God takes what is inside the man—bone of his bones, flesh of his flesh—and from that inner reality, God creates woman. The woman emerges not from the earth, like the animals, but from the man himself. She is continuous with him even as she is distinctly her own being. She is opposite him, and in being opposite, she completes him. The man wakes and recognizes her. "This is now bone of my bones and flesh of my flesh; she shall be called woman, for she was taken out of man." His recognition is not dimly conscious—it is the joy of one who sees that the incompleteness has been met.

This is the first relationship Scripture records. Not a hierarchy. Not a servant and master. Not a supporter and leader. A completion. An ezer who stands beside the man as the one without whom the creation, and the man within it, remains insufficient.

The damage begins when the church translates ezer as "helper" and then reads that translation back into Scripture as if that translation were the original meaning. Suddenly the woman becomes supplemental. She becomes optional, in a sense. She becomes the one who assists in someone else's life rather than the one without whom that life is incomplete. But that is not what the Hebrew says. That is what our cultural anxiety about female equality caused us to impose upon the Hebrew.

Consider the implications if we simply took the Hebrew seriously. If woman is ezer—military strength, rescue, power, the one who makes victory possible—then woman is not made to be passive. She is made to be active. She is not made to be protected; she is made to protect. She is not made to be directed; she is made to direct. She is not an addition to the man's life; she is the completion of it. If the man is alone, creation is not good. When she arrives, not good becomes good.

The ancient world would not have heard this as unusual. The cultures surrounding Israel understood ezer language. A king called his ally an ezer. A warrior called his shield-bearer an ezer. A general called his general an ezer. The term carried weight and authority and active power. It would not have meant "help with the dishes." It would have meant "strength in my struggle." It would have meant "the one without whom I cannot succeed."

I preached for years without really seeing this. I preached "helper" because I learned it that way, because the commentaries I read said it that way, because the cultural moment in which I was shaped interpreted it that way. I did not ask the question that should have been obvious: why is the only creature explicitly called ezer beside the man deeply different from the way we think about ezer everywhere else it appears? We do not say that God is passive because God is Israel's ezer. We recognize that ezer means active power. But we have somehow convinced ourselves that when a woman is ezer, she is passive support.

The reason is simple. We had already decided what women were supposed to be. And then we read our decision back into Scripture and called it exegesis. We translated ezer as "helper" because we needed ezer to mean helper. The woman could not be the completion of the man if she were understood as military strength and rescue. That would have been too much power. So we changed the translation, and we changed it without even noticing we had done so. We believed we were simply being faithful to the original meaning when we were actually protecting patriarchal assumptions.

What if the meaning is actually what the Hebrew says? What if woman is the ezer—the strength, the rescue, the active power that makes the man and the creation complete? What if that particularity of hers is not something she needs to overcome to be equal to men? What if that particularity is the way the ancient text has always named her dignity, her capacity, her irreplaceable role?

This reframing changes everything about how we read the rest of Scripture. It means that women are not asked to step into male roles by being equal; they are named to stand in their own roles with full authority. It means that the question is not whether women can lead, but why the church ever thought leadership was inherently male. It means that female strength is not an accident or an exception. It is the ezer standing where ezer has always stood—opposite the man, making complete what is incomplete, providing what cannot be provided by any other force.

CHAPTER TWO

The Damage Done

Gender Hierarchy, the Fall, and What Sin Really Introduced

Genesis 3 changes everything. But not in the way the Church has taught. When we understand what Genesis 3 actually describes, we see that gender hierarchy is not part of God's original design—it is part of the curse, a consequence of sin that Christ came to reverse.

The serpent comes to the woman. Why the woman first? Commentators have debated this for centuries. Some suggest she was weaker in faith. Some suggest the serpent targeted her because she was more gullible. These interpretations reveal more about the prejudices of the interpreters than about the text. The narrative does not explain why the serpent approached the woman. It only says he did. And what happened next was disobedience—and it involved her making a choice.

The serpent introduces doubt about God's word. "Did God really say...?" It is the fundamental challenge: Can you trust what God said? The woman considers the prohibition. She sees the fruit is good for food, pleasing to the eye, desirable for gaining wisdom. She takes it. She eats. She gives some to her husband, who is with her, and he eats too.

This is the moment when the world breaks. This is the moment when creation begins to decay. This is the moment when relationships become complicated, when power becomes coercive, when difference becomes hierarchy.

The consequences are immediate and catastrophic. Their eyes are opened. They see they are naked. They are ashamed. They hide from God. When God calls, they blame. Adam blames the woman. The woman blames the serpent. The chain of accusation forms, and it will echo through the rest of history.

Then God speaks the consequences. To the serpent—enmity, defeat, the promise of a seed who will crush the serpent's head. To the woman—pain in childbearing, conflict in marriage. To the man—toilsome work, the sweat of his brow, the return to dust.

And then God says to the woman: "Your desire will be for your husband, and he will rule over you."

This is the verse that has haunted Christian women for two thousand years. This is the verse that has been weaponized to justify male authority in the home and the church. This is the verse that the conservative church holds up and says: "See? This is what God intends. This is the divine order."

But read it carefully. Read it in context. This is not instruction. This is consequence. This is not what God prescribes. This is what sin produces.

The Hebrew word here is teshuqah—desire. It is used elsewhere in Scripture only once, in Genesis 4:7, where it describes sin's desire to control Cain. "Sin is crouching at your door; it desires to have you. But you must rule over it." The same word. The desire of sin is to dominate. The desire that enters the marriage relationship after the fall is the desire to dominate and control. The response is rule—the man ruling over the woman.

This is not creation order. This is the disorder that sin introduces.

What the text is saying is this: Because of sin, relationships that were meant to be partnerships become hierarchies. The corresponding strength becomes subordination. The mutual submission becomes power imbalance. The man will rule the woman not because this is how God wanted it but because this is what the fall produces.

The curse describes what will happen. It does not prescribe what should happen. There is a difference between description and prescription. A description tells you what is. A prescription tells you what should be. The Church has spent centuries treating Genesis 3:16 as a prescription—as if God is saying, "This is how I want your relationships to be structured"—when it is actually a description of what brokenness will produce.

If someone punches you in the face and breaks your nose, and a doctor says "Your nose will be crooked and you will have trouble breathing," the doctor is describing what the injury produces. The doctor is not prescribing a broken nose. The doctor is not saying your nose should be broken. The doctor is telling you what to expect from the damage.

Genesis 3:16 is a description of the damage that sin produces in gender relations. It is telling us what to expect when the fall enters the world. It is not God's vision for marriage. It is not God's design for leadership. It is what happens when creatures made for partnership choose rebellion instead.

And here is the thing that should stop us cold: If the entire narrative arc of Scripture is redemption—if God came in Christ to reverse the curse, to undo what sin broke, to restore what the fall shattered—then patriarchy is part of what is being reversed.

This is not extreme. This is basic gospel logic. Christ came to reverse the curse. Genesis 3 introduced the curse. If patriarchy is part of the curse, then it should not be defended by people who claim to believe in redemption.

But that is exactly what has happened. The conservative church has read Genesis 3:16 as if it were Genesis 1:27. It has taken what sin introduced and called it what God intended. It has taken a description of brokenness and made it a prescription for order. And in doing so, it has actively resisted what Christ came to accomplish—the reversal of curse.

Augustine read Genesis 3:16 and used it to justify male authority in marriage. He was writing in a patriarchal society. He inherited patriarchal assumptions. He read them into the text and declared them biblical.

The medieval church read the same verse and used it to argue that women should be silent in church because their "rule" under men was God's design. They were wrong.

The Protestant Reformers—Calvin, Luther, Knox—read Genesis 3:16 and assumed it meant women were created for subordination. They were wrong.

The Reformed tradition, working from the Reformers' hermeneutic, built entire systems of theology around the permanence of male authority. They were wrong.

The Southern Baptist Convention, in 1998, issued a statement saying that while "both men and women are equal before God," women should submit to their husbands, and husbands should not be deceived into egalitarianism because it contradicts "the clear teaching of Scripture." They cited Genesis 3:16. They read a description of curse as a prescription for order. They were wrong.

And this pattern has repeated for centuries. Every generation of male interpreters has read the fall and found in it permission to maintain their own authority. Every generation has found in Genesis 3:16 justification for the status quo. And every generation of women has been told that their limitation is not cultural oppression but biblical design.

Here is what I did not understand as a man benefiting from this system: Every time we preach that Genesis 3:16 prescribes male authority, we are preaching that the fall is permanent and that Christ's redemption is incomplete. We are saying that the curse cannot be reversed in gender relations—that men will rule and women will bear the burden forever.

That is not the gospel. That is not redemption. That is spiritual abuse dressed in theological language.

The gospel says something entirely different. It says that what sin broke, Christ is fixing. It says that the powers of the age to come are breaking into this age and healing what the fall damaged. It says that in Christ, there is neither male nor female—that the deepest divisions are being healed, that the barriers are falling, that the old order is passing away and the new order is breaking through.

When Paul writes in Galatians 3:28 that in Christ there is neither male nor female, he is not talking about spiritual equality while accepting practical hierarchy. He is announcing a reality—that the gospel is breaking through the divisions that sin introduced. That the curse is being reversed. That the hierarchy that sin produced is being undone by the gospel.

This does not mean gender differences disappear. Ezer still means ezer. Complementary strengths are still complementary. But the power imbalance that sin introduced—the rule, the dominion, the hierarchy—is part of what Christ came to reverse.

The problem with how the conservative church reads this is that it reads salvation history backward. It treats the fall as the true revelation of God's intent. It treats curse as creation order. It mistakes the damage for the design.

What we have to do is read Scripture forward. We have to start with creation. We have to understand that partnership is God's design. Then we have to understand that sin breaks partnership into hierarchy. Then we have to understand that Christ is in the business of restoring what sin broke.

The entire arc moves in one direction: from partnership to hierarchy to the restoration of partnership through gospel redemption.

If women in leadership upsets you because you believe it violates God's design, you are reading the wrong part of Scripture as your starting point. You are starting with the curse instead of with creation. You are treating the damage as if it were the design.

I was taught this my entire life. I preached it without questioning it. I read the text as confirming what I had been told, not challenging what I had been told. I made women apologize for wanting to lead because I assumed the Bible required their silence.

I was wrong. The Church was wrong. And we owe an apology not just to women but to God for spiritualizing injustice, for calling curse creation order, for resisting the work of redemption Christ came to accomplish.

Genesis 3 describes the damage. It does not prescribe it. And the gospel is the good news that the damage is being healed.

The question that haunts every conversation about women and Scripture is this: if the Bible itself contains restrictions on female leadership and authority, by what right do we override those restrictions in the name of biblical faithfulness? If Paul writes that women should not teach men, and we say that women should teach men, are we not simply imposing our cultural preferences on the text? Are we not reading our modern values into ancient words?

This is a genuine hermeneutical problem, and every honest approach to Scripture has to sit with it. But there is a structure that helps us think clearly about it. It comes from William Webb, and it is called the redemptive-movement hermeneutic. The basic insight is this: Scripture itself contains a trajectory toward liberation that the church is meant to follow beyond the text's historical context.

The clearest example is slavery. The Bible does not forbid slavery. The Bible regulates slavery. Exodus 21 provides laws about Hebrew slaves, their treatment, their eventual release. Leviticus 25 establishes the jubilee, a year of general release. Paul writes to Philemon about the enslaved man Onesimus and in effect asks Philemon to release him or at least treat him as a brother rather than property. But Paul does not say slavery is wrong. He does not call for the abolition of the institution. Yet every Christian today—every single one—believes that slavery is a grave moral evil that violates the image of God and cannot be tolerated. We hold that conviction based on Scripture, but not based on any verse that says "slavery is wrong." We hold it because we follow the trajectory.

The trajectory is clear. In the ancient near Eastern world, slavery was absolute. The enslaved person was property with no rights, no dignity, no possibility of redemption. The Bible comes into that world and restricts slavery. It protects the enslaved person. It mandates rest on the Sabbath. It forbids permanent slavery for Hebrews. It establishes release at jubilee. The Bible is already moving toward liberation, even if it has not arrived at complete abolition. The church recognized this trajectory and followed it beyond the text to its logical conclusion. Slavery cannot be tolerated because the Bible itself is moving toward its elimination.

But here is the part that makes Christian leaders deeply uncomfortable: the same Bible used to justify slavery is the same Bible used to justify the subordination of women. And the hermeneutical move that freed the enslaved is the same move that must free women.

In the ancient near Eastern world, women were property. They belonged to their fathers until they belonged to their husbands. They had limited legal standing, limited economic independence, limited voice in community decisions. The Bible comes into that world and restricts patriarchy. It protects women. Proverbs celebrates the woman of valor who buys land and conducts commerce. The law protects women from arbitrary divorce. Jesus treats women as full persons—he teaches them, he listens to them, he commissions them. Paul teaches mutual self-giving in marriage: husbands, love your wives as Christ loved the church. The Bible is already moving toward liberation from patriarchal absolute power, even if it has not arrived at complete equality.

But here is where most churches have stopped. We stopped moving. We took the restrictions that were meant to be temporary—instructions to women in a specific cultural moment—and froze them as eternal truth. We treated the scaffold that held up the renovation as if it were the building itself. And we did this so carefully, so theologically, that we convinced ourselves we were being faithful.

The household codes of Ephesians 5, Colossians 3, and 1 Peter 3 are the crucial texts. "Wives, submit to your husbands as to the Lord." In the Greco-Roman world, this was subversive language. A wife submitting to her husband was not new. That was the law. That was the culture. That was the absolute power structure. What was subversive was what came next: "Husbands, love your wives, just as Christ loved the church and gave himself up for her." In a world where a man owned his wife like he owned his slave, the instruction to die for her was revolution. The wife was not to be property. She was to be loved at the cost of the husband's own life. That is what was subversive about those texts.

But the church read those texts in a moment when patriarchal authority was no longer the default, when women had begun to claim equality, and suddenly we read them as if the wife's submission was the point rather than the husband's sacrificial love. We emphasized the part that protected patriarchal power and ignored the part that was subverting it. We used the scaffold text to resist the trajectory.

Consider the 1 Corinthians 11 passage about women's heads being covered, long hair being a woman's glory, a woman needing a man's authority to pray. This passage is notoriously confusing, and commentators have spent centuries trying to make sense of it. But one thing is clear: Paul was addressing a specific cultural problem in a specific church. He was not establishing universal law about gender. He was addressing a situation in Corinth where women's behavior in worship was causing disruption, and he was adapting to the cultural moment to preserve peace. Yet the church has taken this text and universalized it, made it into doctrine, used it to restrict women across centuries and cultures where that original situation does not exist.

The redemptive-movement hermeneutic asks a different question: what is the direction the Bible is moving? And the answer is unmistakable. The direction is toward women having full standing as prophets, judges, leaders, teachers, and apostles. The direction is toward women's gifts being recognized and deployed. The direction is toward women's voices being heard. When we see a restriction on women in a particular text in a particular moment, the hermeneutical question is not "does this apply forever?" The question is "why is this restriction here in the first place?" And almost always the answer is: because the culture was not ready for full equality, and the church was working within constraints it could not immediately overthrow.

But constraints are meant to be overcome. The trajectory is meant to be followed. And the church that stops moving, that takes the scaffolding and calls it the building, has betrayed the very logic of Scripture that freed the enslaved.

This is not an easy move. It requires us to admit that we have been wrong. It requires us to see that restrictions we preached as eternal were actually accommodations to a fallen world. It requires us to acknowledge that the culture we blamed for women's liberation was actually the culture that finally made visible what Scripture had always been moving toward. And it requires us to repent of the damage we did in the name of faithfulness while we were actually resisting it.

I have had to make this move personally. I have had to see that the complementarianism I preached was not fidelity to Scripture but fidelity to a cultural moment. The moment passed. The constraints are gone. The trajectory continues. And the church's job is not to hold women back from what Scripture is moving them toward. The church's job is to help them get there.

Genesis 3:16 reads like this: "To the woman he said, 'I will greatly increase your pains in childbearing; with pain you will give birth to children. Your desire will be for your husband, and he will rule over you.'"

For centuries, the church has read this sentence as prescription. As God's design for womanhood. Your pain in childbearing is part of the created order. Your desire for your husband is your nature. His rule over you is the pattern that should shape every marriage and every church and every woman's life.

That reading creates a theology that benefits from the fall. It treats the curse—the consequence of sin—as if it were the intention of the Creator. It takes the word "curse" and baptizes it as "design." And in doing so, it makes God responsible for the very thing God was speaking against.

Think about what this reading implies. When God says to the woman, "he will rule over you," God is not describing the structure of the world as it was meant to be. God is describing the structure of the world as it has become because of sin. Patriarchy is presented as a consequence of the fall, not a creation of God. When a man uses his power to dominate his wife, that is not the fulfillment of God's will for his life. It is the manifestation of the curse. It is what happens when sin gets power.

But the church took that curse—that consequence of human rebellion—and turned it into a command. We told women: this domination you experience is not sin. This is God's will for your life. Your husband's rule over you is not something to resist or mourn. It is something to accept as the revealed intention of God. We took the fall and made it into the faith.

The implications are staggering once you see them. If patriarchy is God's design, then God wants men to dominate. Not to serve. Not to lay down their power. To use it. To exercise authority. God prefers a world in which half the human race is under the power of the other half. That is what the theology says if you take it seriously. And most pastors do not take it seriously enough to admit it. They hedge it with "loving" leadership and "mutual" submission (a phrase that has no meaning; submission cannot be mutual). But the fundamental claim is clear: God prefers hierarchy to mutuality.

That is an incoherent theology. It contradicts the narrative arc of Scripture itself. God's redemptive history is the story of the restoration of broken things. The fall breaks the world. Christ's resurrection begins to mend it. The new creation perfects it. Every broken thing in Genesis 3 is being healed by the gospel. Except women's subjugation. Except that one. For women, the curse is repackaged as calling.

Look at what happens in the garden before the fall. Adam and Eve are made in the image of God. Both of them. The text is emphatic: "So God created mankind in his own image, in the image of God he created them; male and female he created them." The image of God is not male. It is not gendered. It is a human thing. Both carry it equally. Both image God equally. The dominion they are given—to have authority over creation, to name things, to order the world—is given to both of them.

That is the creation order. That is what God actually made. A woman as the full image-bearer of God, with the full call to exercise dominion alongside the man. Not under him. Alongside him. Doing what he does. Bearing what he bears.

Then comes the fall. The woman is deceived. The man abandons his headship of the garden—his calling to guard and keep it—and instead eats fruit his wife offers him. Both fail. Both sin. Both break the covenant they were made to keep. And the consequence is that the harmony between them shatters.

"Your desire will be for your husband, and he will rule over you."

That is not a description of intimacy. That is a description of conflict. The woman's desire—her longing, her yearning—will be for her husband. She will want to be close to him. And he will use that desire against her. He will rule. She will lose the mutuality she was made for. The partnership becomes a hierarchy. The communion becomes domination.

That is the curse. That is what sin brings into the world. That is not what God wants for women. It is what God says will happen to women as a result of sin, unless and until that sin is redeemed. And the church took that description of brokenness and made it into a command. We said: this is how things should be. This is how God made the world. This is your calling.

When you do that, you are saying something very specific: the fall improved something. The fall made relationships more as God intended them. Before sin, there was mutuality, but that was less good than the hierarchy that sin brings. That is what the theology says, even though no pastor would actually say it out loud.

But the moment you accept that Genesis 3:16 is prescriptive—that it is describing how God wants women to live—you have committed yourself to that claim. You have said: patriarchy is not a problem. Patriarchy is a solution. The solution God provides to the disorder of equality. The woman's submission is not a tragedy. It is a gift.

I have taught this. I have defended this. I have read Genesis 3:16 from the pulpit and made it sound like it was good news for women to hear. "God made you this way. God designed submission for you. God is restoring order through your acceptance of your place."

I have done that work. And I have watched it work. I have watched women internalize it. Watched them stop asking for things. Watched them explain their husbands' anger as strength. Watched them interpret their own needs as rebellion against God's order. Watched them teach their daughters the same thing I taught them.

And I have watched it break them.

Not immediately. Not in a way that made anyone's faith shatter overnight. But slowly, in the way that teachings work when they are taught by people you trust, in the name of God, and contradicted by what you know about yourself. The contradiction works its way into the soul. The woman knows she is smart. The theology says she should not teach. The woman knows she has gifts for leadership. The theology says those gifts must stay private. The woman knows her voice matters. The theology says it should be quiet.

That is not a minor dissonance. That is the structure of spiritual abuse. You are telling a human being that the most painful contradiction in their life is actually the will of God. You are making God responsible for telling her that her wholeness is rebellion. Her selfhood is sin.

The church did not invent this. We inherited it. But we perpetuated it. We took a description of sin's consequences and packaged it as Scripture's intention. We read a curse as if it were a command. And we did it with such theological confidence that women who questioned it had to wonder if they were the ones who lacked faith.

That is why this verse matters so much. Genesis 3:16 is not a minor exegetical debate. It is the foundation of an entire system of thought that tells women that the best outcome of their lives is to accept the worst outcome of the fall. It tells them that redemption in Christ does not actually apply to this. That when Jesus died to undo the curse, He somehow left this one curse in place. This one broken thing—the domination of women—is still supposed to stand.

That is not the gospel. That is not what redemption means. That is not what Christ won on the cross.

The gospel says the curse is being undone. All of it. The pain of childbearing will not last forever. The domination will not stand. The alienation between men and women will be healed. We are not waiting for heaven for that healing to begin. It begins now, in the church, in the gospel, in the resurrection power that is available to us right now.

But the church has been telling women to wait. To accept the curse. To call it God's will. To raise their daughters to do the same. And the cost of that teaching is immeasurable. It is in the marriages that are miserable because one person has been told to stay and the other has been told he has the authority to make her stay. It is in the talents that are buried because a woman was taught that using them publicly would be rebellion. It is in the girls who learned to make themselves smaller so they could be what their theology said they should be.

This needs to be named clearly: if you are reading Genesis 3:16 as a prescription for how women should live, you are teaching something that is devastating the women in your congregation. You are teaching them that God prefers their pain. That God designed their domination. That their wholeness is a threat to God's order. And you are doing this in the name of faithfulness to Scripture.

But you are not faithful to Scripture. You are faithful to a system. And that system is harming women. Slowly. Persistently. With all the weight of sacred authority behind it.

I know because I have been the one doing the harm.

CHAPTER THREE

The Women Jesus Saw

How Jesus Treated Women and Challenged Cultural Norms

If you want to know what God's vision for women actually is, stop reading the patriarchal interpretations of the Old Testament and the disputed epistles. Stop listening to what the institutional church has said women should be. Go watch what Jesus did. How Jesus treated women is the clearest window into what God actually values and intends for women.

Jesus's treatment of women was not incidental to His mission. It was not a cultural accommodation. It was a scandal. And His scandal against the status quo of women in first-century Palestinian Judaism was so consistent, so deliberate, and so culturally offensive that the religious establishment could never quite figure out how to handle it.

In Second Temple Judaism, the status of women was secure in its subordination. A Jewish man thanked God daily that he had not been born a woman. The prayer is explicit. It appears in the morning liturgy. A man began his day by expressing gratitude that his maleness placed him above female status. That was the religious culture Jesus entered. That was the default assumption—that women were less than, limited to, created for support roles, and primarily defined by their relationship to men.

Jesus did not accommodate this. Jesus attacked it every chance He got.

The Samaritan woman at the well appears in John 4. She is a Samaritan, which means she is ethnically and religiously beyond the bounds of Jewish society. She is also a woman engaging with a man publicly, which violated the norms of interaction between genders. And she is a woman with a sexual history—five husbands and living with a man who is not her husband. By every measure of first-century respectability, she is someone a rabbi should avoid.

Instead, Jesus sits down at the well and asks her for a drink. He initiates conversation with her. And when she points out that He is doing something socially inappropriate—a Jew asking a Samaritan for water—He ignores the objection and keeps talking.

But here is what is crucial: He engages her in a theological conversation. He talks about worship. He talks about truth. He talks about the Messiah. He does not speak to her about her domestic role or her sexual history or her value as a support to a male relative. He speaks to her as a person capable of understanding theology, of grappling with spiritual truth, of comprehending the deepest claims of God.

And when she says, "I know that Messiah is coming," Jesus responds: "I, the one speaking to you—I am he."

He tells her first. Not His disciples. Not the male religious authorities. Not the men who would be considered the proper recipients of this revelation. A woman. A Samaritan woman. A woman with a past. She is the first person to whom Jesus explicitly reveals His identity as the Messiah.

In a culture that thanked God for not being female, Jesus gave a woman the supreme revelation of His identity and purpose.

Then there is Mary of Bethany, who appears in Luke 10. Jesus comes to the home where Mary and Martha live. Martha busies herself with serving. Mary sits at Jesus's feet. This is the posture of a student in the presence of a rabbi. This is what male disciples do. This is not what women do. The roles are too clear. The boundaries are too fixed. Women serve. Men learn.

Martha complains. She says to Jesus, "Lord, don't you care that my sister has left me to do the work by myself? Tell her to help me." It is a reasonable request within the cultural norms. The woman should be serving, not sitting. The woman should be supporting the work, not engaging in the privileged position of learning.

Jesus's response is stark: "Martha, Martha, you are worried and upset about many things, but few things are needed—or indeed only one. Mary has chosen what is better, and it will not be taken away from her."

He places the learning above the serving. He affirms her choice to sit at His feet and absorb His teaching. He rebukes Martha for insisting on the traditional female role of service. In the presence of Jesus, being taught is more valuable than serving. And Mary gets to be a student.

This is not incidental. This is a direct challenge to the gender norms of first-century Judaism. Jesus is saying that women have the right to learn, to sit as disciples, to engage with the deepest teachings of God.

Then there is the woman caught in adultery, in John 8. She is brought to Jesus by the Pharisees and teachers of the law. She was caught in adultery, they say. The law of Moses says such a woman should be stoned. They ask Jesus what He says.

There is a problem with this setup that nobody mentions because patriarchy makes it invisible: Where is the man? Adultery requires two people. If she was caught in the act of adultery, he was there. But he is not here. The law of Moses prescribes that both the man and the woman should be put to death for adultery. But the man is absent.

This is what the fall produces. This is what patriarchy does. It protects men while judging women. It applies the law inconsistently. It creates a system where women's bodies are regulated and men's actions are overlooked.

Jesus does not follow the logic of patriarchal justice. He does not say, "Well, the law is clear, so stone her." He bends down and writes on the ground. He offers a way out: "Let any one of you who is without sin be the first to throw a stone at her." And the accusers leave, because they understand they have been caught in their own injustice.

To the woman, Jesus says: "Neither do I condemn you. Go now and leave your life of sin." He does not excuse her. He addresses her directly as a person accountable to God. But He refuses to participate in the patriarchal system that says her life is more disposable than the man's life, that her body is more subject to regulation, that her worth is more dependent on her sexual status.

Then there is the resurrection. The last narrative detail the gospels give us before the cosmic reordering is this: Jesus appears first to women. In a culture where women's testimony was not legally admissible in court, where women were not considered reliable witnesses, where women were excluded from public truth-telling, Jesus appears first to women and tells them to tell others that He is risen.

In Matthew, it is two women at the tomb. In Mark, it is three women. In Luke, it is multiple women who discover the empty tomb and are told by angels to tell the disciples. In John, it is Mary Magdalene alone who encounters the risen Jesus. Across all four gospels, with varying details, the pattern is consistent: Women are the first witnesses to the resurrection. Women are the first to encounter the risen Christ. Women are the first to be commissioned to tell the story.

Think about what this meant culturally. You are going to go tell people about the most significant event in history—the resurrection of God in human form—and your testimony carries no legal weight because you are a woman. Your word is not considered reliable. Your account is not considered credible. And yet Jesus insists. He appears to women first. He sends women to tell the story.

This is not an awkward cultural limitation that Jesus is forced to work around. This is a direct confrontation with the patriarchal assumptions of the culture. Jesus is saying—by His actions, not by His words—that women's witness matters, that women's voice carries the deepest truth, that the male establishment's assessment of women's credibility is wrong.

Across the gospel narratives, Jesus's pattern with women is consistent: He engages them in theological conversation. He elevates them to the position of student. He refuses to judge them by the standards of patriarchal virtue. He centers them in the most important moments. He sends them to be witnesses to the most crucial truths.

And every bit of it was culturally scandalous.

The religious establishment did not know what to do with this. They could not argue that Jesus was not elevating women—the evidence is too clear. They could not argue that He was not breaking cultural norms—He was doing it deliberately. So instead, the institutional church has spent two thousand years trying to domesticate what Jesus did, trying to explain it away, trying to make His defiant affirmation of women fit within patriarchal structures.

But you cannot domesticate it. You cannot fit "women sit at my feet as disciples" into a system where women are not allowed to teach. You cannot fit "women witness the resurrection" into a system where women's testimony is unreliable. You cannot fit "I reveal my identity first to a woman" into a system where women's spiritual understanding is limited.

The Jesus of the gospels saw women. He saw their dignity. He saw their capacity for understanding. He saw their calling. And He called them up and out from the place where the culture had pressed them down.

If we want to know what a biblical vision of womanhood looks like, we do not get there by reading patriarchal commentary on Old Testament curse language. We get there by watching what Jesus did. We get there by seeing how He treated women, how He commissioned them, how He elevated them, how He scandalized the religious establishment in order to honor them.

And then we have to ask ourselves: Are we following the Jesus of the gospels? Or are we defending the system that His actions opposed?

The women Jesus encountered in the Gospels are not afterthoughts or supporting characters in a male narrative. They are the ones who recognize him, trust him, stand with him when everyone else scatters, and bear witness to his resurrection. If you want to understand what Jesus believed about women, do not look at his formal teaching. Look at whom he elevated, to what positions he elevated them, and what he trusted them with.

Start with Mary of Bethany. In Luke 10, Jesus arrives at a home where Mary and her sister Martha are hosting him. Martha is doing what was culturally expected—performing the service role, preparing food, maintaining hospitality. Mary sits at Jesus's feet and listens to him teach. This was the posture of a disciple. Rabbis taught students who sat at their feet. Mary was not a servant in that moment; she was a student. When Martha complains that Mary should help her serve, Jesus does something extraordinary. He corrects Martha. He affirms Mary's choice. "Mary has chosen what is better," he says. Jesus is not dismissing service. He is saying that sitting with him as a learner, as someone being formed in his kingdom, is better than performing expected roles, even in his presence. He is elevating the woman who chose learning over labor.

In John 11, the same Mary appears again. Lazarus, their brother, has died. Martha makes a theological confession: "I believe that you are the Messiah, the Son of God, who is to come into the world." It is a deep statement of faith. But then Mary comes to Jesus, and she falls at his feet and says to him, "Lord, if you had been here, my brother would not have died." Jesus is deeply moved. He weeps. The shortest verse in Scripture is his response: "Jesus wept." But then something happens that Matthew, Mark, and John all record as significant. Jesus raises Lazarus. Jesus performs his greatest miracle. And the Gospel writers emphasize that Jesus did this not primarily to convert the crowds. Jesus did this because he loved these women. He did this in response to the faith and grief of Mary and Martha. Two women are positioned at the center of what should be a story about Jesus's power. They are positioned there because their faith warranted it.

But the medieval church could not leave Mary of Bethany as she was. There was another Mary in Luke's Gospel—the sinful woman who anoints Jesus's feet with her tears and expensive perfume in Luke 7. In the church's imagination, these two women became one. Mary of Bethany became a prostitute. A woman of moral failure. This conflation was given papal weight in 591 AD when Pope Gregory I preached a sermon declaring that Mary of Bethany was the same as the sinful woman, that she had been a prostitute, that she had been forgiven and redeemed. It became doctrine. For over 1,400 years, Mary of Bethany was known as a reformed prostitute. Artwork depicted her as a sensual woman in silks and jewels. Her story was moralized into a narrative about female sexuality and redemption. And the actual Mary—the faithful disciple, the woman who chose learning, the woman whose faith in Jesus was so deep that he wept—disappeared beneath the mythology.

This is what the church does to women. It cannot leave them as they are, full and particular and centered in their own stories. The church brings them into the narrative and then rewrites them to fit preexisting categories about what women are supposed to be. The sinful woman in Luke 7 is her own person. The image of her falling at Jesus's feet in gratitude is deep and particular to her story. But she does not need to be conflated with Mary of Bethany. And Mary of Bethany does not need to be redeemed into someone she was not. Mary was a disciple. That was enough. That is still not enough for the church.

Consider the woman at the well. John 4 presents her without euphemism: she has had five husbands and the man she is living with is not her husband. She is a Samaritan, and Jews did not deal with Samaritans. She is a woman speaking with a man in public, which violated cultural expectations. She is a woman alone at the well at midday, which suggests she was isolated from the community. Yet Jesus sees her. He asks her for a drink. He engages her in the deepest theological conversation in any Gospel—a discussion about the nature of worship, about the coming messiah, about the God who knows all things and loves anyway. She recognizes him. "Could this be the Messiah?" She becomes a missionary. She goes back to the city and tells people about Jesus. "Come, see a man who told me everything I ever did. Could this be the Messiah?" And her testimony brings people to faith.

A woman marked by relational failure. A woman from an enemy people. A woman alone because of her circumstances. A woman who has probably been scorned and excluded. Jesus treats her as a theologian. Jesus treats her as a missionary. Jesus elevates her to the status of apostle—she is sent to testify and gather people to him. And the Gospel preserves her story not as a redemption narrative where her past is erased, but as a story where her past is irrelevant to what she can see and testify and become.

The hemorrhaging woman in Mark 5 was unclean. Not morally unclean, but ritually unclean. The Mosaic law said she could not be touched. She could not worship in the temple. She could not be part of community in the fullness of how the law defined it. For twelve years, she has been bleeding. For twelve years, she has been cut off. She has spent all her money on physicians. Nothing has helped. The law says she is untouchable. The law says she is separated from community. And yet she pushes through a crowd to touch the hem of Jesus's robe. She breaks the law. She violates the purity code. She risks making Jesus unclean by touching him. Jesus feels it. He turns around. "Daughter, your faith has healed you. Go in peace and be freed from your suffering."

Jesus does not shame her for touching him. He does not worry about the transmission of her uncleanness. He calls her "daughter"—a term of affection and inclusion. He affirms her faith. He restores her to community. He does this publicly, in front of everyone, so that everyone knows she is no longer unclean, no longer separated. This is not a private healing. This is a public restoration. A woman who was cut off is brought back in.

Then there is the Syrophoenician woman in Mark 7. This is the only story in the Gospels where someone argues with Jesus and wins. A woman comes to him asking that he heal her daughter. Jesus's response is harsh: "It is not right to take the children's bread and toss it to the dogs." He is saying, effectively, that she is not one of his people. She is a Gentile. She is not included. She is outside. What she does next is striking. She says to him, "Even the dogs under the table eat the children's crumbs." She does not retreat. She does not accept his boundary. She argues. She pushes back. She finds a loophole in his logic and exposes it with wit and insight. Jesus's response is not anger. It is delight. "For such a reply, you may go; the demon has left your daughter." He honors her. He affirms her willingness to argue. He grants her request. A woman is positioned as the one who teaches Jesus something, who challenges him, who wins.

Finally, there is Mary Magdalene. The last person to see Jesus alive. The first person to encounter him after the resurrection. The one to whom the resurrection is revealed first. The one sent by Jesus to tell the disciples that he has risen. The first apostle. Yet the church could not leave her as she was—faithful, present, first—without needing to explain why she was there. So the church made her a prostitute. Not because the Gospel says she was, but because a woman who was that close to Jesus, that trusted by Jesus, that central to the resurrection narrative, required explanation. A woman who had seven demons cast out of her in Mark 16:9, Luke 8:2. But in the church's retelling, that casting out became a euphemism for prostitution. For centuries, Mary Magdalene was known as a reformed prostitute, a repentant sinner redeemed by Jesus's love. She was painted in Renaissance art as sensual, weeping, the image of sexual redemption.

But that is not who she was. She was a woman healed by Jesus. She was a woman devoted to Jesus. She was a woman present at his crucifixion when the male disciples had fled. She was a woman who went to the tomb when it was still dark. She was a woman to whom the risen Jesus appeared first. She was a woman commissioned by Jesus to tell the disciples that he had risen. That was her role. That was her standing. And the church could not leave it at that. The church needed to make her a prostitute so that her proximity to Jesus could be explained by sexual redemption rather than by the simple reality that Jesus trusted her.

What all of these women have in common is that Jesus encountered them in their full particularity. He did not flatten them. He did not ask them to step into male roles to be acceptable. He elevated them in the roles they occupied. A disciple. A missionary. A woman ritually separated who is publicly restored. A woman who argues and wins. A woman who is faithful unto death and chosen to bear the first testimony of resurrection. These are not women who are acceptable because they transcend their womanhood. These are women whose womanhood is the context in which their power becomes visible.

And the church, consistently, has tried to rewrite these stories into something safer. The church has made them into narratives of redemption where the woman's previous status (sinful, sick, unclean, demon-possessed, foreign) is the plot point rather than her faith and her elevation. The church has conflated women who should remain distinct. The church has imposed categories that the Gospel does not impose. And in doing so, the church has hidden what Jesus actually did with women—which was to see them, trust them, commission them, and elevate them beyond anything the culture expected or permitted.

CHAPTER FOUR

Proverbs 31 Is Not a Checklist

The Proverbs 31 Woman: Entrepreneur, Leader, and What Scripture Actually Says

One of the most destructive things the Church has done to women is to turn Proverbs 31 into a performance metric. That woman—the one described in those verses—has become the standard against which women measure themselves and are measured by others. Can you cook? Can you clean? Can you manage the household? Are you attractive? Do you speak wisely? Can you do all of this while also being a homeschooling mother and a worship leader and a perfect wife?

The Proverbs 31 woman has become the modern female aspirational myth. But this biblical portrait of womanhood is not what we have been taught. She is held up as the biblical model of womanhood. And in the process, she has been thoroughly misread.

Let me tell you what I watched happen at First Baptist Fenton.

A few years ago, one of our female leaders proposed a women's Bible study focused on Proverbs 31. It sounded reasonable. A whole chapter in Scripture about a woman of virtue. What could be wrong with studying that? We approved it. The women signed up. They were excited.

I did not attend the study. I was leading other things. But toward the end of the series, women began to come talk to me. Not enthusiastically. They came to me looking exhausted, guilty, overwhelmed. One woman—a physician, a mother of three, a person of genuine competence—told me she had spent ten weeks reading about the Proverbs 31 woman and come away convinced that she was failing at being a woman. She could not homeschool her children and maintain a full-time medical practice and keep her house perfect and look beautiful and speak wisdom in the gates. So something was wrong with her. Either she was lazy, or she was disobedient, or she did not love her family enough.

That study did not draw women closer to Scripture. It did not deepen their faith. It convinced them they were failing.

That is what we have done with Proverbs 31. We have taken a passage about a particular woman doing particular work in a particular historical context and transformed it into a universal checklist that no woman can possibly complete. And then we blame women for the failure instead of blaming our interpretation.

To understand what Proverbs 31 actually is, you have to know its form. It is an acrostic poem. Each verse begins with the next letter of the Hebrew alphabet. Aleph, bet, gimel, and so on, all the way through the alphabet. This is a specific literary form in Hebrew Scripture—a way of structuring wisdom that emphasizes completeness, the fullness of a subject from A to Z. It is also a way of saying: This is wisdom literature. This is poetry. Do not read it as systematic instruction about how every woman should live her life.

Who wrote it? Proverbs 31:1 identifies the author: "The sayings of King Lemuel—an inspired utterance his mother taught him." This is a queen mother teaching her son. This is not God speaking. This is not timeless prescription. This is a mother passing wisdom to her child in a specific cultural context—ancient Near Eastern monarchy.

What is she describing? A woman of honor. A woman worthy of respect. And here is what is striking: She is not described primarily through her domestic work. Yes, she tends to her household. But she is described as an investor, a merchant, a producer, an entrepreneur.

> *"She considers a field and buys it; out of her earnings she plants a vineyard." She is not waiting for her husband to provide for her. She is assessing land, making purchases, taking entrepreneurial risk. In the ancient world, this would have been shocking. A woman managing her own capital? Making her own investments? This was not the norm.*
>
> *"She makes linen garments and sells them, and supplies the merchants with sashes." She has a textile business. She manufactures products. She sells them. She supplies other merchants. This is not a woman tending her home. This is a woman running an enterprise. She is creating wealth. She is providing jobs. She is an economic force in her community.*
>
> *"She sets about her work vigorously; her arms are strong for her tasks." She is not frail. She is not delicate. She is strong. She works hard. She gets things done.*
>
> *"She sees that her trading is profitable, and her lamp does not go out at night." She monitors her investments. She understands profit margins. She works into the night to manage her growing enterprise.*
>
> *"She opens her arms to the poor and extends her hands to the needy." She has enough margin in her life, enough wealth from her enterprises, enough time in her day to serve others. She is generous. She gives to those with less.*
>
> *"When it snows, she has no fear for her household; for all of them are clothed in scarlet." Her family is secure because of the wealth she has built. This is not a woman whose security depends on her husband's provision. This is a woman whose financial management ensures her family's wellbeing.*
>
> *"She is clothed in fine linen and purple." She wears the clothes of nobility and prosperity. She is beautiful, yes, but her beauty is not her primary characteristic. Her accomplishment is.*
>
> *"Her husband is respected at the city gate, where he takes his seat among the elders of the land." This is interesting. Her husband benefits from her success. His position in the community is enhanced by her accomplishments. But she is not there to make him look good. She is succeeding in her own right, and he benefits. That is not the modern complementarian vision of womanhood—where the woman exists to support the man's calling. That is something closer to the opposite.*
>
> *"She speaks with wisdom, and faithful instruction is on her tongue." She teaches. She has authority to speak. She offers instruction. This is not the silent, submissive woman the Church has sometimes claimed to find in Scripture.*
>
> *"She watches over the affairs of her household and does not eat the bread of idleness." Yes, she manages her home. But not because that is her only role. It is one of many responsibilities. And the text does not suggest this requires her constant attention or that it prevents her from other work.*
>
> *"All her children arise and call her blessed; her husband also, and he praises her." Her children respect her. Her husband honors her. But again—this is not because she sacrificed herself for them. This is because she accomplished things. This is because she built something. This is because she is worthy of honor.*
>
> *"Many women do noble things, but you surpass them all." The final words: She is exceptional. She is not the norm. She is held up as an ideal, but not as an achievable standard for all women. She is the woman of excellence. She is the woman who does it all—and she is extraordinary for it.*

When I first actually read this passage—not as commentary had explained it but as the text presents it—I realized what had happened. The Church had taken a poem about a woman of unusual accomplishment and entrepreneurial vision and flattened it into a domestic checklist. We had taken a woman who was running enterprises and investing capital and speaking with authority and transformed her into a wife whose primary virtue was keeping her home in order.

And then we told women they should aspire to be like her.

But here is the thing: The Proverbs 31 woman is not primarily defined by her relationship to her husband and children. She is defined by what she accomplishes. She has her own work. She has her own calling. She makes her own decisions. She builds wealth. She speaks wisdom. She is honored for her accomplishments, not for her support of someone else's accomplishments.

If the Church actually believed in the Proverbs 31 woman as the model of biblical womanhood, we would be releasing women to:

Run businesses. Manage capital. Take entrepreneurial risk. Make financial decisions. Speak with authority. Teach others. Build things. Create wealth. Work hard. Be strong. Accomplish great things. Be honored for those accomplishments.

Instead, we have reduced her to someone who cooks and cleans and looks beautiful while supporting her husband's vision.

We have lied about what Proverbs 31 says, and in doing so, we have lied to women about what Scripture commends.

I watched those women leave my church's Bible study on Proverbs 31 feeling like failures because they could not accomplish what the Proverbs 31 woman accomplishes. They did not understand that she is exceptional. They did not understand that she is described as one who surpasses all others. They understood only that they were not her, and therefore they were failing.

The Church did that. We created an impossible standard. We misrepresented Scripture to defend that standard. And then we wondered why women felt exhausted and guilty.

What if we read Proverbs 31 as a vision of women at full strength? As a vision of women with permission to build, to lead, to speak, to accomplish, to be ambitious, to be strong, to be entrepreneurial, to be honored for what they create? What if we stopped flattening her into a domestic ideal and instead expanded our vision of what women could do?

What if we released women to be the Proverbs 31 woman instead of asking them to be an impossible version of her that our patriarchal theology has invented?

The damage of misreading Proverbs 31 is concrete. It is in the woman who left our church because her gifts were not being used. It is in the wife who minimized herself to support her husband's calling because the Church told her that was biblical womanhood. It is in every woman who has been told that her ambition is a character flaw, that her desire to lead is rebellion, that her intellectual strength is something to be managed rather than released.

We did that by reading Scripture through a patriarchal lens and then defending the lens by saying Scripture demanded it.

Proverbs 31 is not a checklist for wives. It is a vision of a woman of strength, accomplishment, vision, and honor. It is a woman who does what she sets her mind to. It is a woman who is not diminished by supporting her family but elevated by her own work. It is a woman worth honoring because of what she accomplishes, not because of what she sacrifices.

If that is the biblical woman, then the question is not: How can women fit into the roles we have assigned them? The question is: How have we been preventing women from becoming the women Scripture commends?

Proverbs 31 is often preached as the portrait of the ideal wife. Women hear it and see something impossible—a woman who wakes before dawn, manages a household perfectly, serves her family with endless grace, keeps her appearance flawless, and somehow generates contentment in all of it. The sermon concludes with something like, "This is what biblical womanhood looks like. This is what God calls you to." The woman listening often feels the weight of it. She has a career and three kids and is exhausted by 7 PM. She is not Proverbs 31. She wonders what is wrong with her.

But this reading deeply misunderstands what the text is actually saying. It mistakes the portrait for the principle. It confuses the historical context with eternal instruction. And it happens because English translators and English preachers have domesticated a text that is deeply about economic power, not domestic achievement.

The Hebrew term for the woman in Proverbs 31 is eshet chayil. This phrase appears several times in the Old Testament, and every time it appears, it refers to a woman of military valor, strength, and power. Ruth is called eshet chayil. In that moment in the text, Ruth has been working the fields, has been strategically positioning herself to catch Boaz's attention, and is being recognized for her ingenuity and her strength. Naomi calls her this because Ruth has acted with the strategic intelligence of a general.

Chayil means strength, valor, army, power, military force. It does not mean domestic virtuousness. It means capacity for action in the world. An eshet chayil is not a good wife. She is a woman of strength. A woman of valor. A woman of power.

This is the woman Proverbs 31 is describing. But because English translators rendered her as a "wife of noble character" or "excellent woman" or "virtuous woman," the text has been read as domestic aspiration rather than economic reality. The portrait shifts entirely when you read what the text actually says she is doing.

> *"She considers a field and buys it." She is engaging in real estate. In the ancient world, land was wealth. The ability to buy land required capital, credit, and the legal standing to do so. Women had limited legal standing. Yet this woman is doing it. She is not asking permission. She is not consulting anyone. She considers. She buys. She makes a unilateral economic decision.*
>
> *"With the fruit of her hands she plants a vineyard." She is in agriculture. She has assessed what can be grown and where. She has invested in it. She is managing an agricultural operation. This is not domestic gardening. This is commercial agriculture.*
>
> *"She girds herself with strength and makes her arms strong." The language of girding and strengthening is the language of preparation for labor or battle. She is a woman of physical labor, not ornamental presence. She works.*
>
> *"She perceives that her trading is profitable." She is engaging in commerce. She is buying and selling. She is making profits. She is not simply generating goods for her household; she is conducting business that generates surplus.*
>
> *"She opens her arms to the poor and extends her hands to the needy." She is a woman of significant means and influence. She has enough that she can distribute to those in need. Her economic power allows for philanthropy.*
>
> *"Strength and honor are her clothing." This is not saying she is well-dressed for appearance's sake. In Hebrew thought, what clothes you determines what you can do. A warrior is clothed in strength. A king is clothed in royalty. She is clothed in strength and honor. She is equipped for action.*
>
> *"She laughs at the time to come." She has the security of knowing that her economic decisions have positioned her for the future. She does not fear what is coming. She has secured her position.*
>
> *"She opens her mouth with wisdom and kindness is on her tongue." She speaks with authority. She offers counsel. She is not silent. She uses her voice.*
>
> *"She watches over the affairs of her household." Only after all of the economic power is described does the text mention household management. And note: she watches over it. She does not serve it. She manages it. She has authority over it.*
>
> *"Her children rise up and call her blessed; her husband also, and he praises her." Her family recognizes her. But note the order: her children first, then her husband. Her primary loyalty and her primary impact is on her children. Her husband "praises her"—he recognizes her achievement.*

This is not a domestic portrait. This is a portrait of a woman who has consolidated economic power through astute business decisions, who manages significant assets, who has the standing to conduct commerce, who has the capital to give to the poor, and who exercises authority in her household. She is not the archetypal wife and mother. She is the archetypal woman of valor, strength, and economic independence.

The church has read this text and concluded that the woman of Proverbs 31 is the ideal, and the ideal is to find fulfillment in serving others. But the text itself describes a woman finding fulfillment in creating wealth, making strategic decisions, exercising influence, and securing her future. The text celebrates her competence and her autonomy, not her self-sacrifice.

When the church preaches this text to women and emphasizes the "she provides food for her household" part while ignoring the "she buys a field" part, we are preaching a domesticated version of Scripture. We are reading our cultural values into the text and calling it exegesis. We are telling women that this text, which actually celebrates female economic power, is instead a call to contentment with domestic limitation.

I have preached this way. I have stood before women and told them that Proverbs 31 is what they should aspire to, and I meant it as a portrait of domestic virtue. I meant it as an encouragement that their work in the home was noble and valued. I did not notice that I was systematically excluding from my reading every verse that had to do with economic activity, business acumen, land ownership, and wealth creation. I was not reading the text. I was reading what I had already decided women should be, and I was selecting the verses that supported that prior decision.

The text itself is much stranger and much more subversive. It is a text about a woman who has used her intelligence, her labor, and her strategic thinking to build something. She is not serving a man's vision. She is executing her own. She is not fulfilling a role assigned to her. She is defining her own role through her choices and her work.

Here is what makes this even more difficult: we cannot completely separate the ancient context from the text. Proverbs 31 is not universal prescription. It is not saying that every woman must buy a field or plant a vineyard. It is a portrait of what a strong woman can accomplish in her particular historical moment. The specific activities are contextual to an ancient agrarian economy. But the principle is clear and non-contextual: the ideal woman, in Scripture's view, is a woman who exercises agency, makes decisions, creates wealth, takes responsibility, and builds something of lasting value. She does this not by stepping into male roles but by bringing her own capacity to bear on the world she inhabits.

The church's error is not in recognizing that Proverbs 31 is contextual. The church's error is in deciding that because the specific activities are contextual, the principle of female agency and economic power is also contextual—something that applied in the ancient world but is not meant to apply now. But the principle is exactly what should translate. If the ideal woman in Scripture is one who considers, buys, plants, perceives, extends, opens, watches, and builds—then the ideal woman now should do the same in her context. She should consider options and make decisions. She should invest and plan for the future. She should work and build value. She should create something that has weight and duration. She should not be contained by a role that diminishes her capacity.

The woman of Proverbs 31 is a woman of complete autonomy within her social structure. She is not asking permission. She is not waiting for leadership. She is making decisions and building something. And the text celebrates her for it. When we preach this text to women in the modern world and emphasize their domestic role, we are actually preaching against what the text itself is celebrating. We are diminishing women in the name of Scripture while Scripture is doing the opposite.

**CHAPTER 5**

The Submission Question

Understanding Ephesians 5: Mutual Submission, Marriage, and What the Bible Really Teaches

I was standing in a couple's kitchen on a Tuesday night when it happened. Mark and Jennifer had asked me to come over—not for counseling, though it became that. They were redoing their wedding vows, Jennifer told me, and they wanted the ceremony to reflect their actual marriage, not the wedding they'd had twenty years ago. She said this while looking at her hands.

Mark shifted. He said they'd been working through some things in marriage counseling, and their therapist had suggested they revisit their covenant together. He didn't mention submission. But I could feel it in the room. They were wrestling with what Ephesians 5 actually says about submission and marriage, and what most churches teach it to mean.

I asked them what they wanted to change. Jennifer went first. She said when they married, they'd done the traditional evangelical thing—she wore white, he slipped the ring on her finger, and the pastor had said something about her submission that made her feel like a daughter being handed to a new father. She'd been twenty-two. She'd been told her whole life that submission was the goal of Christian womanhood. And she'd believed it.

"For twenty years," she said, "I thought that meant I was supposed to make myself smaller."

Mark's face was working. He said, "I never wanted that."

I asked him, "What did you think submission meant?"

He couldn't answer.

This is the moment where everything comes apart and nothing gets fixed with a proof text. Ephesians 5 had been opened in that living room a hundred times—verses 22 and 24 especially, the commands for wives to submit. But Mark and Jennifer had never opened it at verse 21, which changes everything. Or rather, it explains everything, if you're willing to let it.

---

"Submit to one another out of reverence for Christ."

That is the foundation. Not hierarchy. Mutuality. Out of reverence for Christ—because Christ is the one who reframes what submission means. And then Paul speaks to wives and husbands separately, but never outside of that mutual submission frame. The wife is to submit to the husband. But the wife is not alone in submission. The husband is called to something harder.

"Husbands, love your wives, just as Christ loved the church and gave himself up for her."

That is not love as preference. That is not love as feeling. That is not love as partnership where both people look out for their own interests. That is love as death. Christ gave himself up for the church. He died. And Paul calls a husband to die for his wife.

The passage does not say wives should obey husbands. It says wives should submit to husbands as the church submits to Christ. And the church does not obey Christ as a subject obeys a king. The church submits to Christ the way a body submits to its head. The head and the body are not separate entities; they are one living thing. The head cannot function without the body. The body cannot function without the head. Submission is not hierarchy. It is integration.

And the husband is not told to have authority over his wife. He is told to love her as Christ loved the church. Christ did not wield authority over the church. He emptied himself. He became vulnerable. He washed feet and ate with sinners and faced accusation and died on a cross. His love was costly. And Paul says: husbands, that is your model.

But that is not how the passage has been read.

For fifteen centuries, the church extracted Ephesians 5:22-24 from its context, placed it under a microscope, and preached it as the foundation for male headship. Not mutual submission. Male headship. The submission language became the pillar of a theology of authority, and wives were built into a structure designed to diminish them. Not always consciously. Not always with malice. But systematically. The passage was pried open, and verse 21 was allowed to fade. The beginning was forgotten.

And this reading has its sources. Augustine said women were created from man's rib and are therefore subject to man. Aquinas said women are naturally subject to men because men have more reason. Calvin said the woman is under the man because she was created after him and from him. These were the theological giants, and they built a house where wives live in rooms designed by men, with windows that face inward, looking always toward the head of the household.

The Southern Baptist Convention codified it in 1998. The Baptist Faith and Message stated that "wives are to submit graciously to the servant leadership of their husbands." That phrase—servant leadership—was supposed to redeem the power imbalance, but it did not. It just gave it a gentler name. The husband still has authority. He is just supposed to wield it kindly. And the wife's virtue was measured by how well she accepted that arrangement.

I know this world. I was shaped by it. I grew up hearing that male headship was God's design, and I internalized it the way you internalize the air you breathe. I did not question it because I did not have to. It was written into every structure I belonged to. And when I married Susanna, I brought that theology with me into the bedroom.

The confession needs to be specific: I used Ephesians 5 to win arguments. I used submission language to suggest that Susanna's hesitation about a decision was her struggle with obedience. I preached the passage to thousands while treating my wife in ways that contradicted its actual content. I was doing what the church had done for centuries—extracting the verses that gave me authority and ignoring the verse that demanded my death.

Susanna never said it this way. She said it like this: "You talk about servanthood and then you act like your opinion matters more than mine."

She was right. And I was wrong.

---

The recovery of Ephesians 5 begins with a question: What if we read the passage the way Paul wrote it?

Then wives are not told to submit to authority. They are invited into mutual self-giving. And that is not weakness. That is not subordination. That is costly love.

And husbands are not told to rule. They are told to die. They are told to love as Christ loved—which means pouring out, emptying, making yourself the servant of the other's good. The head serves the body. Jesus washed feet. He broke bread. He bore a cross. And a husband is called to that model.

This terrifies men. Or it terrified me. Because if your whole identity has been built on the idea that you are the head and your wife is the body, the invitation to mutual submission, mutual self-giving, mutual laying-down of your life, feels like a threat to your manhood. It is not. It is the only thing that has ever made you human.

The passage does not work if both people are not all in. If the wife is submitting and the husband is not dying, the passage is broken. If the husband is dying and the wife is withholding herself, the passage is broken. Both have to be true. Simultaneously. With no score kept.

That is harder than hierarchy. Hierarchy is easy. Hierarchy says, I have authority, you follow, and if something goes wrong, it is someone else's fault. It says my burden is to decide. Mutual submission says my burden is to lay down my life for you, and you are doing the same, and we are building something together where neither of us gets to be safe.

This is what happened in Mark and Jennifer's kitchen when they decided to renew their vows. They said no to the version where Mark was the head and Jennifer was the body following orders. They said yes to the version where they were both willing to die for each other. Where Mark had to own that his love had been too small, that he'd been using authority language when he needed to be using sacrifice language. And Jennifer had to say, I'm not going to make myself smaller anymore. I'm going to live with the power God gave me.

The new vows he wrote for her—he gave up the language of headship. He wrote her a promise that he would lay down his life for her good, not because he was in charge, but because she was the image of God and she was worth dying for.

The new vows she wrote for him—she named his sacrifice and said she would meet it. That she would give herself to him the same way. And she would not apologize for being his equal.

---

The cost of this is not paid in the wedding ceremony. It is paid every day in the small deaths of marriage. It is paid when you give up being right in order to listen. When you give up your preference so your wife's joy matters more than your comfort. When you look at her and realize you do not own her, you never did, and you are invited to love her not as a possession but as a person.

And for wives, the cost is the terrifying freedom of not being told what to do. Of not having a man's head to follow, which means you cannot blame a man's head when your life goes wrong. You are accountable. You have power. You have to choose.

This is what Ephesians 5 actually promises: not hierarchy but mutuality. Not the wife's submission but mutual submission. Not the husband's authority but the husband's death. And both of these things required. Both of these things difficult.

The passage has been preached as a woman's problem to solve through obedience. But it is a man's problem to solve through sacrifice. And the church has spent centuries helping men avoid that problem.

My own work has been to name it. To say: I was wrong. To say to Susanna: you were not the problem, the theology was. And then to live it out in the thousand small surrenders that make a marriage real.

That is the reframe Ephesians 5 offers. Not hierarchy that diminishes women. But mutuality that transforms men into the image of Christ—sacrificial, empty, willing to lay down everything for the other's good.

And that is costly hope. Because it means you cannot be the head of your wife. You can only be the one who dies for her. And she will only be the one who does the same.

The passage that has done more damage to women's leadership than perhaps any other is Ephesians 5:22-24. "Wives, submit to your husbands as to the Lord. For the husband is the head of the wife as Christ is the head of the church, his body, of which he is the Savior. Now as the church submits to Christ, so also wives should submit to their husbands in everything." This text has been used to establish a permanent hierarchy. Wives submit. Husbands lead. It is presented as the created order, transcultural and eternal. And based on this one text, the church has restricted women's leadership, limited their voices in decision-making, and told them that their submission is their greatest calling.

But this text cannot be understood in isolation. It sits within a corpus of household codes that Paul and other New Testament writers were deploying in a specific historical context. And understanding that context completely changes how we read the text.

The household codes appear in three places: Ephesians 5-6, Colossians 3, and 1 Peter 2-3. These were not unique to early Christianity. Similar household codes appeared throughout Greco-Roman philosophical literature. They were tools for organizing social life in ways that would be recognizable and acceptable to the broader culture. In an empire where Christianity was a new and potentially dangerous movement, these codes served a practical function: they helped early Christians fit into the social fabric so that the church could exist and grow without being completely destroyed by Roman suspicion.

But—and this is crucial—the early Christian household codes were subversive of the cultural assumption in particular ways. In a Greco-Roman household, the paterfamilias (the male head of the household) had absolute authority. He could beat his wife. He could divorce her unilaterally. He owned everything, including her body and her will. A wife submitted to her husband because she was his property under the law. This was not a theological claim; it was a legal reality. The wife had no power, no voice, no recourse.

When Paul writes to this context, he writes: "Wives, submit to your husbands." In the cultural moment, this sounds conventional. But then he writes: "Husbands, love your wives, just as Christ loved the church and gave himself up for her." In the cultural moment, this is revolutionary. A husband is not supposed to die for his wife. A husband is supposed to use his wife. In a world where a man owned his wife like he owned his slave, the instruction to die for her is a complete reorientation of the relationship. It is not the wife's submission that is subversive here. It is the husband's sacrificial love.

The same pattern appears in Colossians. "Wives, submit to your husbands, as is fitting in the Lord. Husbands, love your wives and do not be harsh with them." Again: wives submitting is expected in the cultural context. Husbands being commanded not to be harsh is the countercultural move. In a world where a husband could be harsh, could dominate, could demand absolute obedience, Paul is saying: no. You will love her. You will not be harsh with her. You will treat her with dignity.

The household codes are not establishing a new hierarchy. They are regulating the existing hierarchy in ways that protect the subordinated person. They are a scaffold. They are scaffolding that the church was using to work within the cultural moment while still moving toward liberation.

But here is what happened: Christianity gained political power. The scaffold became permanent. Women's submission, which was meant to be a temporary accommodation to a fallen world, became eternal doctrine. And the subversive elements—husbands' sacrificial love, the protection of women's dignity—got overwhelmed by the submission language. The church read these texts as if the wife's submission was the point rather than the husband's transformation.

The word "head" (kephale in Greek) has been the hinge on which everything turns. The husband is the head; therefore he leads. The wife is the body; therefore she follows. But there is serious debate about what "head" actually means in this context. Some scholars argue it means "leader" or "authority." Others argue it means "source" or "origin." In classical Greek, kephale primarily refers to literal head—the physical head of a body. In a metaphorical sense, it often means source or beginning, not authority. The source of a river is the headwaters, not the authority over it.

Moreover, Paul uses other language in Ephesians that complicates the hierarchy claim. He calls the church "the body of Christ, the fullness of him who fills everything in every way." The church is the fullness of Christ. The church is not subordinate; the church completes Christ. By this logic, the wife, as the body, would complete the husband, not be completed by him. The logic of the text is more complex than a simple hierarchy allows.

But the most decisive problem with reading Ephesians 5 as establishing male authority is the verse that comes immediately before the household code section. In Ephesians 5:21, Paul writes: "Submit to one another out of reverence for Christ." He calls for mutual submission. Everyone submits to everyone else. Wives to husbands, yes—but in the context of everyone submitting to everyone else out of reverence for Christ. This is not a hierarchy. This is a complete leveling where the only authority is Christ and the only posture is mutual deference.

Then the specific instructions: wives submit to husbands, children obey parents, slaves obey masters. But every specific submission is framed by the mutual submission and by specific charges to the one in power. Husbands love your wives sacrificially. Fathers do not embitter your children. Masters treat your slaves justly. The one with power is charged with restraint, sacrifice, and love. The one with less power is asked to submit. But the entire structure is held within mutual submission to Christ.

What Paul is doing is providing guidance for relationships within the fallen world, knowing that complete equality is not yet possible, but channeling power in directions that move toward justice. He is not establishing eternal hierarchy. He is providing temporary scaffolding while the church is embedded in an oppressive empire.

But the church, when it gained political power, did not need the scaffold anymore. The church became the power structure. And suddenly the language of wives' submission became permanent doctrine. It became the way God wanted relationships to be. It became the way God designed women to be—subsidiary, obedient, supportive. All of the scaffolding language about cultural context and temporary accommodation disappeared. What remained was wives submit.

In my own marriage, Susanna and I have had to reckon with what mutual submission actually looks like. We were taught—both of us—that I was supposed to be the leader and she was supposed to submit. We bought it. We tried to execute it. But what we discovered is that mutual submission is a completely different reality. It means that when we disagree, neither of us is deciding unilaterally. It means that we are both bringing our full selves to the conversation. It means that her wisdom is not supplemental; it is essential. It means that my authority is not mine to exercise; it is shared.

The shift has been jarring and necessary. When we operated under the model where I was the head and she was submissive, I experienced it as having certain authority and certain responsibility. I thought that was what leadership meant. But what actually happened is that I prevented her voice from having the weight it deserved. I made decisions because that was my role, not because my decision was better. She accommodated herself to my preference not because she agreed but because she was supposed to. We had a relationship structured by hierarchy, not by truth. We had a relationship shaped by roles, not by reality.

Mutual submission has meant learning that her perspective on our finances might be more careful than mine. That her reading of a situation involving our kids might be sharper than my instinct. That her assessment of a decision we are facing might carry more weight because she has information or intuition that I lack. It has meant learning that submission is not the woman's job. Submission is what we both do—we listen to each other, we defer to each other's strengths, we make decisions together.

This is what the household codes were moving toward, even if they could not get there completely in the cultural moment. This is the trajectory Paul was following. The church stopped moving. The church took the scaffold and said it was the building. And we have paid the price for it. Women have paid the price for it.

The question is not whether wives should submit to husbands. Mutual submission is the answer to that question—yes, but also husbands should submit to wives, in the context of everyone submitting to everyone else out of reverence for Christ. The question the church needs to answer now is this: why did we stop moving? Why did we take the temporary accommodation and make it eternal doctrine? Why did we read a text that was meant to regulate oppressive power structures as if it were meant to establish them forever?

The answer is that it served us. Men benefited from permanent hierarchy. Women's voices could be overruled by the assertion that they were meant to submit. Male authority could be maintained without having to earn it. The church kept the scaffold up because the power it provided was too useful to dismantle.

Ephesians 5 calls for mutual submission. Not submission of wives to husbands alone, but submission of all to one another in the fear of Christ. When I finally read that text on its own terms—without the commentaries that had already told me what it meant—I realized I had been reading someone else's interpretation my entire ministry. The text says something much more revolutionary.

But theory is different from Tuesday night at the dinner table.

Here is what mutual submission looks like in a real marriage, the one I share with Susanna. It looks like both of us speaking. Both of us being heard. Both of us having authority over the decisions that shape our lives. Neither of us has unilateral power. Not because I am generous. Not because Susanna demanded it. But because that is what the covenant between us actually requires.

In financial decisions, it is clean. We have one bank account. Both of our names are on it. Both of us can spend without permission. Both of us have a voice in large purchases. We have disagreed about money more times than I can count. Early in our marriage, my instinct was to pull rank. I earned it. I should decide. But Susanna is not a dependent. She is a partner. Her voice in how our money works is not a privilege I grant her. It is a right that belongs to her because we are bound together.

So we argue. Sometimes for hours. I want to invest. She wants to save. I want to take a risk on a ministry project. She wants stability. And we resolve it not through my authority but through dialogue. Through me having to actually convince her. Through her not having to convince me—she does, but the point is that the outcome is not decided by my preference. It is decided by our mutual agreement, even when that agreement takes time.

That matters because it means I cannot just decide. I cannot just use the fact that I bring home the paycheck to override her wisdom. And I have had to develop the capacity to hear her say no and actually listen instead of figuring out how to get my way anyway. That is not the theology I was taught. The theology I was taught said: love your wife as Christ loves the church (which actually means give everything for her), but also lead. The leading part was supposed to mean I got final say.

But if I give everything for her—if I really mean that—then her wellbeing becomes more important to me than my preferences. And that means I have to hear her when she says something is wrong with a financial decision. I have to care about her judgment. I have to actually defer to her wisdom when she has it.

Parenting is where this becomes even more visible.

Susanna and I disagree about how to raise our boys. Constantly. About rules. About consequences. About when to push and when to give grace. About how much risk is healthy and how much is reckless. I am more rigid. She is more flexible. My instinct is toward structure and accountability. Her instinct is toward possibility and second chances. Neither of us is right all the time. But the old theology said: I decide. The wife follows. And if the wife disagreed, she followed anyway.

But Susanna is not following. She is bringing her voice to this. And I have learned—sometimes slowly, always reluctantly—that her voice makes our parenting better. There are moments when one of our boys has done something that, by my standard, deserves punishment. By my old theology, I would have just decided it and implemented it. My authority. My judgment. My house.

Instead, I say to Susanna: "Here is what I think we should do." And she says: "I think that is too harsh." Or I hear her say: "I want to give him another chance." And I have to actually weigh her judgment against my judgment. Not dismiss it. Not override it. Actually hear it.

The truth is, she is often right. Not always. Neither of us is always right. But her voice brings wisdom I do not have. Her instinct toward grace has saved me from decisions I would have made in anger. Her willingness to see our boys' hearts instead of just their behavior has shaped how they understand the gospel.

That only happens if I do not have final authority. That only happens if we are actually submitting to one another.

In sexuality, the teaching is usually so wrapped up in marital hierarchies that people do not even see the problem. The theology says the husband leads and the wife follows. Even there. Even in intimacy. Especially there. The wife's role is availability. Her body is for the husband. His desire is the primary consideration.

That is not what mutual submission means. Mutual submission in sexuality means that both of us matter. Both of our desires matter. Both of our bodies matter. Both of our consent is required. Not just hers—his too. He does not just have authority to take. He has a responsibility to ask. To listen. To care about her pleasure as much as his own. To understand that when they come together, it is not the acting out of a hierarchy. It is the physical reality of a covenant between equals.

There have been times in our marriage when I wanted something sexually and Susanna did not. And the old theology would have said: she is your wife. Your right to her body is part of the covenant. The new theology says something completely different. It says: you do not own her body. You can ask. She can say no. And when she says no, that ends the conversation. Not because she is being difficult. Not because she is being unsubmissive. Because her no is her authority. And her authority is sacred.

I have learned the hard way what that means. The first time Susanna said no and I felt the familiar pull to override that, to use my strength or my persuasion or my spiritual authority to get what I wanted—that is when I realized how deep the problem goes. That is when I realized how thoroughly I had been taught to see myself as having authority over her body. That is when I had to repent.

That repentance changed how I understand the entire relationship. If I do not have the authority to override her no in bed, then I do not have the authority to override her no anywhere else either. If her body is hers, then her voice is hers. If her body is sacred, then her judgment is too.

These moments—the financial arguments, the parenting disagreements, the moments when Susanna said no and I had to learn to accept it—these are the actual content of mutual submission. Not theory. Not poetry. The lived reality of two people who are both learning to give up the fantasy of control and actually build something together.

Susanna has had to do the same work. She came into this marriage with her own pathologies. Her tendency to disappear. Her willingness to say yes to things she did not want because she was taught that her job was to keep me happy. Her assumption that her needs were less important than mine. She has had to learn to speak. To insist. To say: I matter too. My voice matters too. My desires are not less valid than yours.

That work is still happening. We have been married nearly two decades and we are still learning how to be partners instead of hierarchical actors playing out roles. But the thing I have learned is this: the theology matters. If you teach people that one of them is supposed to lead and the other is supposed to follow, they will enact that. They will create that reality. Even if both of them want something different, the teaching will shape them.

Conversely, if you teach them that they are bound to each other as equals, that both of their voices matter, that they are meant to submit to one another—that teaching shapes them too. It makes them want something different. It gives them language for what they are feeling. It makes them believe that mutuality is possible.

That belief is what allows the work to happen. That is what gives them permission to have the hard conversations. To argue about money without one of them being able to just decide it. To disagree about parenting without one of them being able to just overrule it. To say no to each other in bed and have that no be sacred.

This is not effortless. It is harder than hierarchy. Hierarchy is simple. One person decides. The other obeys. Done. Mutuality is complex. It requires real conversation. It requires actual dialogue. It requires the humility to admit you might be wrong. It requires the willingness to change your mind when the other person makes a better argument.

But this is what the covenant actually calls for. Not one person leading and the other following. Both people giving everything for the other. Both people serving. Both people having authority. Both people having voice. Both people having power that they can use for the good of the partnership or the destruction of it, and both choosing to use it for good.

That is what Susanna and I are building. Not perfectly. Not without cost. But real. And the cost is worth it because the alternative is one of us disappearing. And I cannot ask my wife to disappear in the name of God.

**CHAPTER 6**

When the Church Silences Women

Women in Ministry, Preaching, Teaching, and the Misuse of Scripture

The Bible passage most responsible for silencing women in church history is not very long. It fits in a single breath. But what it has done across two thousand years is immeasurable. Women in ministry, women preaching, women leading—all of it has been shut down by a few verses taken out of context.

"I do not permit a woman to teach or to assume authority over a man; she must be quiet." (1 Timothy 2:12)

These seventeen words have closed doors, shut down voices, and convinced countless women that their gifts—their intelligence, their theological depth, their prophetic clarity—were not welcome in the rooms where the church gathered. The words are brief. The damage has been infinite.

But they are not the only ones. Across 1 Corinthians 14, Paul writes: "Women should remain silent in the churches. They are not allowed to speak, but must be in submission, as the law says. If they want to inquire about something, they should ask their own husbands at home; for it is disgraceful for a woman to speak in the church." (1 Corinthians 14:34-35)

Two passages. Two cultures. Two centuries of interpretation that extracted these texts from their context, treated them as universal prescriptions, and built the entire scaffolding of female restriction around them.

I know the cost of this because I have watched it happen. I have watched the most gifted Bible teacher in my congregation—a woman named Catherine—sit in the pew while less-gifted men stood in the pulpit. I have watched her teach Sunday School to children and women and seen her intelligence go untapped for the wider church. I have watched her bite her tongue in leadership meetings when her theological insight would have changed the conversation.

I have also been the man who told her to bite it.

---

Let us establish what these passages actually say by reading them in context.

The church in Ephesus was struggling. Paul is writing to Timothy, the pastor there, and the problem he names is false teaching. He says women have been drawn into this false teaching and have become "gossips and busybodies, saying things they ought not to." (1 Timothy 5:13) The women of Ephesus—specifically, some women—had become vectors of heresy. So Paul tells Timothy to instruct these women in sound doctrine, to have them learn quietly. Not because women cannot learn. Because they had learned bad teaching and were spreading it.

The command is not universal. It is pastoral. It is addressed to women who are spreading false doctrine, and it is designed to interrupt that spread until they can be properly taught.

Then in Corinth, the problem is different. The Corinthian church is chaotic. Women are disrupting worship by asking questions from their seats. Paul's solution is not that women cannot ask questions ever. His solution is that they ask their husbands at home, not in the middle of the service. He is regulating the timing and the method of the questions, not forbidding questions altogether. He wants order in worship. That is the problem he is solving.

But this is not how these passages have been read. They have been ripped from their specific contexts and universalized. They have been treated as if Paul is revealing an eternal principle about women: they should not teach, they should be silent, they should be in submission. This becomes the template for excluding women from all positions of authority and teaching in the church.

The cost of this extraction is that we miss what Paul says everywhere else.

In Romans 16, Paul commends Phoebe as a deacon of the church. Not a helper. Not a servant. A deacon. A formal office. In the early church, deacons were not simply administrative. They were leaders. Phoebe was a leader. And she was a woman.

In Acts 18, Priscilla—a woman—takes Apollos, an eloquent and learned man, "and explained to him the way of God more accurately." She taught him. She was his theologian. And Paul commends her for it in his letters.

In Romans 16:7, Paul writes about Junia, whom he calls "outstanding among the apostles." Junia was a woman. An apostle. One of the foundational leaders of the church.

Throughout Paul's letters, women prophesy (1 Corinthians 11:5), pray publicly (1 Corinthians 11:4), and serve as leaders (Philippians 4:2-3, where Euodia and Syntyche are described as having "fought at my side in the cause of the gospel"). Paul's actual practice shows women teaching, leading, preaching, and exercising authority.

So we have a contradiction. Either Paul believed women should be silent and never teach, or something more complicated is happening in 1 Timothy and 1 Corinthians.

The answer is not that Paul changed his mind. The answer is that these passages are addressing specific problems in specific churches, and we have mistaken particular solutions for universal principles.

---

Here is what happened in church history: the exceptions became the rules.

The medieval church did this consciously. Commentators like Augustine and Jerome took these restrictive passages and universalized them as doctrine. They reasoned—quite illogically—that if women should not teach in Corinth, women should not teach anywhere. If women should be silent in Ephesus, women should be silent in perpetuity. They took pastoral guidance for a specific crisis and made it the permanent architecture of the church.

The Reformation did not fix this. Calvin and Luther, for all their biblical recovery, never questioned the exclusion of women from teaching. It was too embedded in the culture. The society was patriarchal, and the church reflected it. By the time Protestant denominations formalized, the silence of women had been written into the very structure of ordained ministry.

And then the question became encoded in seminaries, licensing boards, ordination standards. Women were not invited to pastoral ministry. Not because of some exegetical breakthrough, but because fifteen centuries of habit had calcified into doctrine. The particular became universal. The contextual became eternal.

But even this did not contain the principle. If women should not teach men in the church, should not women also not teach men in the home? The logical conclusion was complementarianism—the theology that women are equal in worth but different in function, created to be helpers but not leaders, capable but subordinate. It sounds more generous than outright prohibition. It is actually more insidious, because it offers the appearance of equality while maintaining the architecture of restriction.

I believed this for decades. I preached it. I built my early pastoral ministry on it. And I never questioned it because I benefited from it. Women's gifts being restricted meant more room for me. Their voices being quieted meant mine could be louder. I had not constructed the system, but I had inherited it and accepted it without examination.

The cost of this—and this is what broke open my conviction—was Catherine.

---

Catherine was a theology student when she came to First Baptist. She had taken a year off from seminary. She wanted to see if pastoral ministry was actually her calling, or if she was following someone else's path. She joined a small group I was leading, and I watched her unfold. She had read everything. She had wrestled with texts in their original languages. She had thought through the hard questions. She was, quite honestly, a better theologian than I was at that stage of my ministry.

After a few months, she asked to meet with me. She said she wanted to be part of our teaching team. She said she felt the Lord calling her to preach and teach in the congregation. She said she did not want to be a pastor, necessarily, but she wanted to use her gifts.

And I told her no.

I do not remember my exact words, but I remember the theology I used. I said the church was called to male leadership. I said 1 Timothy was clear. I said she could teach women and children, but not men. I said this was not about her gifts or her intelligence—it was about God's order.

She asked me if God's order looked different in Junia and Priscilla and Phoebe.

I did not have an answer that held.

She left the church three months later. She finished seminary and is now a pastor in a denomination that does not restrict her based on her sex. And the cost to our congregation—the intellectual poverty, the limited vision, the half of our prophetic voice that we silenced—that is ongoing.

I mention her specifically because I think the church needs to name the actual women we have silenced. Not in the abstract, but in their particularity. Catherine. How many Catherines have we lost?

---

This is where 1 Timothy and 1 Corinthians become a different reading.

They are not permissions to silence women. They are permissions to interrupt specific harms—false teaching in Ephesus, chaos in Corinth. Paul is not establishing a universal principle. He is solving a local problem. And the moment we universalize his solution, we betray his actual principle, which is that women are image-bearers of God, gifted by the Holy Spirit, and called to participate fully in the body of Christ.

Phoebe did not stop being a deacon when 1 Timothy was written. Priscilla did not stop teaching Apollos. Junia did not stop being an apostle. The restrictions in 1 Timothy and 1 Corinthians were local and temporary. The inclusion of women in leadership was the actual pattern of Paul's ministry.

But the church chose to remember the restrictions and forget the inclusion. We built our architecture on the exceptions and called them the rules.

The recovery of women in the church requires reading all of Paul, not just two passages. It requires asking what would happen if we took his actual practice seriously. It requires looking at history and asking ourselves: What if we have been wrong? What if the cost we have accepted—the silenced voices, the unused gifts, the theological poverty we have normalized—is not the price of fidelity but the price of unfaithfulness?

There is a cost to including women. The cost is that men like me have to surrender power we never should have had. The cost is that the architecture we have built around restriction has to come down. The cost is admitting that we have been wrong and have perpetuated that wrongness in the name of Scripture.

But the cost of not including women is also clear: we keep half the body of Christ's gifts locked away. We keep the Catherines in the pew while we preach about the Holy Spirit's gifting. We read about Phoebe and Priscilla and Junia and then act like the church has never had female leaders.

That is costly. And it is wrong.

The reframe is this: when we read the full Paul—not just the restrictions but the practice—we see a church that was actually subversive in its inclusion of women. Not because Paul decided in advance that women should lead. But because the gospel made no distinction. There is neither male nor female. The Spirit gives gifts without consulting anyone's sex. The church in Ephesus and Corinth was learning to live with that, sometimes badly, sometimes well. And the restrictions in 1 Timothy and 1 Corinthians are not the final word—they are interruptions. Pastoral interventions. Not the principle.

The principle is that women are invited into the full life of the church. Teaching, leading, prophesying, serving as deacons and preachers and theologians. Not because women demanded it or culture evolved to include it. But because Christ is the head of the church and the Spirit moves where the Spirit will, regardless of the sex of the vessel.

The costly hope is that men will have to repent. Not for a day or a sermon but for the structures we have built and the voices we have silenced. And women will have to step into leadership knowing that the path has been made hard by history and that stepping into it is an act of both obedience and resistance.

That is what it costs to unmute the church.

The early church was not a patriarchal institution in the way that later Christianity became. Women prophesied. Women led house churches. Women taught. Women were recognized as apostles. The texts themselves make this plain if you read them carefully. But the texts have been misread for so long that the reality has become invisible to most Christians. We have inherited a church history that is half-told, a narrative where women's leadership has been systematically erased and then selectively reintroduced as if it were a modern innovation rather than a return to the original pattern.

In Romans 16, Paul greets the members of the church at Rome. He takes his time. He names people individually and assigns them roles and recognitions. "Greet Phoebe, our sister, who is a deacon of the church at Cenchreae." Phoebe is a diakonos. This is the word used for male deacons. It is the word Paul uses for himself as a deacon. Phoebe is not a deaconess (a separate, lower position). She is a deacon, full stop. She has the same title and presumably the same function as any male deacon. But the church has largely forgotten this or read it in ways that minimize it.

Then: "Greet Priscilla and Aquila, my co-workers in Christ Jesus." Priscilla and Aquila are a couple, but notice the order. Priscilla is named first. In an ancient document, naming order indicated prominence. Priscilla is the primary figure. When the same couple is mentioned elsewhere in Acts, sometimes the order is reversed, but across the texts, Priscilla appears as the prominent partner. Acts 18:26 describes her teaching: "When Aquila and Priscilla heard him, they invited him to their home and explained to him the way of God more accurately." Priscilla is teaching Apollos, a learned man who knew Scripture. She is instructing him. She is not assisting her husband while he instructs. She is instructing him herself.

Then there is the most striking name in the entire list: "Greet Andronicus and Junia, my fellow Jews who have been in prison with me. They are outstanding among the apostles." Junia is a female name. She is an apostle. She is "outstanding among the apostles." But later manuscripts of Romans changed her name to "Junias," a masculine form, because the church could not reconcile the fact of a female apostle with its theology. The name was literally changed by scribes who could not accept what the text was saying. It was not until recent scholarship that the truth became clear again: Junia was a woman, and she was an apostle.

These are not isolated examples. Throughout Paul's letters, women appear in positions of leadership and authority. Lydia is the first convert in Europe and becomes the host of a house church. Euodia and Syntyche are women whom Paul recognizes as having "contended at my side in the cause of the gospel." Nympha hosts a church in her home. These are not women in supportive roles. These are women as primary leaders and gatekeepers of the gospel community.

But the church's historical narrative gradually erased this. As Christianity became institutionalized, as it gained power in the Roman Empire, as the office of bishop became formalized, women were systematically excluded from official positions. The sources for this exclusion are relatively late. The Pastoral Letters (1 Timothy, 2 Timothy, Titus), which are among the New Testament texts most directly opposing women's leadership, are also among the most disputed in terms of authorship and dating. Many scholars argue they were written decades after Paul and do not represent his views but instead represent a second-generation attempt to make the church more respectable to the surrounding culture. "I do not permit a woman to teach or to assume authority over a man; she must be quiet" (1 Timothy 2:12). This text appears in a letter that may not have been written by Paul, that addresses a specific situation in a specific church, but which the later church took as universal and eternal.

The result was a systematic narrowing of women's roles. Where the early church had women prophets, the later church had women saints. Where the early church had women teaching, the later church had women submitting. Where the early church had women apostles, the later church had women supporting the apostles' work from behind the scenes.

But there is more to the story. In the second century, there was a movement called Montanism that explicitly elevated prophetic experience above institutional authority. And this movement was led by prophets—some of them women. Montanus himself was a convert to Christianity who believed he was the instrument of the Paraclete, the Holy Spirit. His ministry was accompanied by women prophets, most notably Priscilla and Maximilla. These women prophesied. They were recognized as bearers of divine truth. They received spiritual authority based on their encounter with the Holy Spirit, not based on institutional office.

The institutional church—the bishops and presbyters who were consolidating power—saw Montanism as a threat. And it was a threat, not primarily because it was heretical, but because it was charismatic and decentralized and gave authority to people (especially women) who had not been officially ordained. The church's response was to suppress Montanism, to declare it heretical, to discredit its prophets. And in the process, women's prophetic authority was categorically rejected. The church established that true authority came through institutional office, which was male. Prophecy could still happen, but it was not authoritative. The official voice was the voice of the institutional leadership.

This is a pattern that repeats: women lead, the male institutional church systematizes and formalizes authority, women get excluded from the formal structure, women's authority is reinterpreted as subordinate or supplemental. It happened to Phoebe—she was a deacon, then she became a "servant" or "helper." It happened to Priscilla—she was a co-worker, then she became a wife supporting her husband's ministry. It happened to Junia—she was an apostle, then she was not even recognized as a woman. It happened to the Montanist prophets—they were voices of the Spirit, then they were heretics whose voices could be dismissed.

The early church had not solved the problem of gender and authority. Women's leadership existed, but it was not without tension. The Pastoral Letters themselves are evidence that by the late first century, there was already pushback against women in leadership roles. But the original impulse of the movement was toward inclusion. Women prophesied in the Spirit. Women led house churches. Women were recognized by the apostles themselves. The gospel, in its original power, did not ask women to wait for male permission. The gospel called them directly.

The institutionalization of the church turned it into something different. It turned a movement into a hierarchy. It turned the Spirit's work into official doctrine. It turned the Holy Spirit's direct call into something that had to be mediated through institutional structures that, by that time, were exclusively male. The women did not disappear. But their leadership was reinterpreted. Their authority was questioned. Their voices were placed under male oversight. The church took the movement toward women's full participation and slowed it, then stopped it, then reversed it.

I have become convinced that the church made a grave error at that junction. Not all errors made in history are recoverable. Some doors, once closed, are hard to open again. But this one is. The early church pattern is there in the text. Women apostles. Women deacons. Women prophets. Women teachers. Women church planters. The question for the modern church is whether we will continue to resist the trajectory of our own Scripture or whether we will follow it to its actual destination.

The damage of this long reversal is immense. Generations of women have grown up in a church that tells them their spiritual gifts are real but that those gifts cannot be exercised in certain ways because of their gender. A woman feels called to preach. The church says, "That is good. God has gifted you. We will find a way for you to use that gift—just not from the pulpit." A woman has the capacity to lead. The church says, "Your leadership is valuable. We just need a man in the official position." The message is consistent: your gifts are real, but they are not quite powerful enough to be trusted with full authority. You are welcome here, but only in certain rooms. You are valued, but conditionally.

I preached this message for years. I told women in my congregation that they could lead in children's ministry, women's ministry, specific teaching contexts. I believed I was honoring their gifts while maintaining biblical truth. But what I was actually doing is what the church has always done: finding a space for women's gifts that does not threaten male authority. I was not being faithful to Scripture. I was being faithful to a tradition that had systematically misread Scripture to protect patriarchal power.

The early church did not have all the answers about gender. But it had something the later church lost: it was still operating under the assumption that the Holy Spirit calls whom the Holy Spirit calls, and that call does not check someone's gender first. It was still operating under the reality that women's voices carried authority because the Spirit gave them authority. It took institutional power to silence those voices. And the church has been living with the consequences ever since.

**CHAPTER 7**

Beauty, Bodies, and the Female Soul

Purity Culture, Body Image, Christian Womanhood, and Freedom from Shame

The rhetoric came from two directions, and it trapped women between them. Purity culture and secular body obsession met on one devastating point: a woman's value is tied to her appearance.

The church said: Your body is dangerous. It is a stumbling block. Boys and men cannot be trusted alone with it. You must be modest, covered, careful. Your body is not yours—it is a test that other people are taking. How you clothe yourself determines whether you lead men into sin. Your body is the problem. Manage it.

The culture said: Your body is your currency. It is your power. You are nothing without it. Be beautiful, be sexy, be desirable. Your body is your identity. Use it. Sell it if you have to. Your body is the answer. Show it.

The church preached purity. The culture preached performance. But they agreed on one thing: a woman's worth is measured by her body.

Evangelical girls in the 1980s and 1990s grew up inside this contradiction. They got the purity pledges and the modesty lectures in youth group. They learned that their bodies were temples and that covering was virtue. They learned to see their own bodies as potential sources of male temptation. They learned shame.

And then they turned on MTV and they saw bodies celebrated. They saw girls their age who were thin and blonde and flawless, and the world told them that was the goal. They learned that the right body meant acceptance, power, worth. They learned hunger.

The church tried to manage the problem by policing the visible body. Modesty became the metric. How short were your shorts? What happened to your collarbone? Could your underarms see daylight? The church turned teenage girls into objects to be monitored, and then told them this was protection.

The culture sold the problem. Buy this cream, this diet, this workout program, this clothing brand. Fix your body and you will fix your life. Fix your body and you will be loved. Fix your body and you will matter.

Both were lies. But they were lies that worked together. The church managed the shame of the body. The culture managed the hunger for worth that the body promised. And women—girls, really—were trapped between the two with no language for the person underneath the body at all.

---

There is a scene in medieval hagiography that shows how deep this goes.

The desert mothers and fathers—the fourth and fifth century ascetics—would fast, pray, and deliberately deprive their bodies of comfort. They did this to mortify the flesh and draw closer to God. In the lives of the male ascetics, this was treated as spiritual heroism. These men were called saints.

But the female ascetics faced a different pressure. The theologians of the time were obsessed with women's bodies as sources of sexual temptation. Jerome, that great scholar of Scripture, spent enormous energy worrying about women's bodies and the men they tempted. So when women ascetics chose poverty and nakedness and denial, the medieval interpreters had to explain why this did not make them sexually attractive. They wrote that God made these women ugly. Scarred them. Deformed them. Made them repulsive so that men would not be tempted by their asceticism.

The female ascetic was praised not for her spiritual discipline but for her ruined body. She was holy because she was no longer beautiful.

This is not ancient history. This is the template that shaped modern evangelical discourse about women's bodies.

The message was: your body is dangerous because it is beautiful. Your beauty is a threat. Therefore, either hide it or destroy it. There is no middle ground where your body is simply your body—a temple not for others to evaluate but for you to inhabit.

I grew up in this world. I preached to young women about modesty. I did not say it was about my purity or my struggle. I said it was about yours. I said you need to be careful what you wear because boys cannot be trusted with what they see. I was, without meaning to be, telling girls they were responsible for boys' sin. That their bodies were the problem. That covering was virtue and exposure was transgression.

I was, without knowing it, echoing Jerome. I was saying your body is dangerous. And if your body is dangerous, then your body cannot be trusted. Then you must control it. Then shame is the appropriate response to your own flesh.

---

But the Scripture says something different.

> *"Do you not know that your bodies are temples of the Holy Spirit, who is in you, whom you have received from God? You are not your own; you were bought at a price. Therefore honor God with your bodies." (1 Corinthians 6:19-20)*

A temple.

Not an object for others to manage. Not a test for others to take. Not a currency to sell. A temple. A sacred space. A place where God dwells. A place you inhabit.

The word "temple" is deeply relational. A temple is a place of presence. It is a place where the holy dwells. It is not primarily about how it looks. It is not about whether it is covered or exposed. It is about what it houses. It is about who lives there.

When Paul says your body is a temple, he is saying something to women that the medieval church did not want to say. He is saying your body is not the problem. The Spirit of God dwells in your body. Your body is where the sacred and the human meet. Your body is not a stumbling block. Your body is a sanctuary.

This changes everything.

If your body is a temple, then you are not responsible for managing other people's lust. You are responsible for honoring God in the space you occupy. That is a completely different ethic. That is an ethic of self-respect, not self-denial. That is an ethic of presence, not performance.

It means you can wear what you choose because your body is not the problem. It means you can move through the world in your own skin without shame because you are housing the Holy Spirit, not testing anyone's virtue. It means the conversation is not about how covered you are or how beautiful you appear. The conversation is about whether you are honoring the sacred space your body is.

For some women, that means dressing modestly because modesty feels like presence. For other women, it means wearing what makes them feel alive in their own skin. For others still, it means something in between. The point is not to find the right outfit. The point is to know that your body is not the problem.

---

The cost of this reframe is that women have to reclaim their bodies from both the church and the culture.

You have to reject the church's shame about your body being a stumbling block. You have to reject the culture's promise that your body is your worth. You have to stand in the middle and say: this is my body. It is sacred. And its sacredness has nothing to do with how you see me.

This is why eating disorders are so prevalent among evangelical women. The church told them their bodies were dangerous and needed to be controlled. The culture told them their bodies were their identity and needed to be perfect. The only way to obey both was to control your body into perfection. And if you could not achieve perfect control, then you were failing God and the culture simultaneously. The body becomes a battleground.

There is a version of Christian teaching about women's bodies that sounds gentle but is actually violent. It says: love yourself, but not too much. Honor your body, but manage it carefully. Do not be vain, but do not let yourself go. Be beautiful for your husband, but not so beautiful that you tempt other men. Be confident, but not prideful. The message is: your body is not trustworthy, so constant vigilance is required.

The recovery begins with a different message: your body is trustworthy. Your body knows when it is hungry. Your body knows when it is tired. Your body knows when it is in pain. Your body knows when it is being violated. Your body is telling you truth about the world and about yourself. Listen to it.

This sounds simple. It is subversive. Because listening to your body—really listening, not overriding—means you cannot starve yourself trying to be perfect. You cannot wear clothes that hurt you trying to look right. You cannot be silent when your body is telling you that something is wrong.

Women have been taught not to trust their own bodies. They have been taught that their bodies lie. That their hunger is gluttony. That their desire is lust. That their pain is weakness. That their no is not enough. That other people's comfort with their bodies matters more than their own.

The Scripture says something different. The temple is yours. Your body houses the Holy Spirit. That body is sacred. You are not responsible for how others see you. You are responsible for honoring the space you occupy.

---

The problem with both the church's shame and the culture's hunger is that they both locate your worth in your body.

The church says your body is dangerous, so your worth depends on controlling it. The culture says your body is your currency, so your worth depends on maintaining it. Both systems require that you are constantly evaluating yourself through someone else's eyes.

The recovery requires a different question altogether: What if my worth is not located in my body at all?

What if I am not my body? What if I am a person who has a body? What if that person is made in the image of God and that image is not diminished by aging, sickness, disability, weight gain, scars, or any of the things that the culture says makes a body less beautiful?

Then the conversation about your body changes completely. Your body is no longer a project to perfect. It is no longer a test you are taking for others. It is not your identity. It is the vehicle through which you move through the world. And you can honor it—feed it well, move it, rest it, enjoy it—without believing that it determines your worth.

This is costly because it means you have to stop measuring yourself against every image you see. You have to stop believing that if you just fixed this one thing, your life would be different. You have to accept that you might be seen as less beautiful by the culture's standards, and your worth does not diminish by a millimeter.

The hope is that you will know yourself as a temple. That you will inhabit your body without shame. That you will listen to it when it speaks. That you will reject both the church's fear of your body and the culture's reduction of you to it. That you will know you are more than how you look, more than how you are desired, more than how you are evaluated.

That your body is sacred space. And you do not owe it to anyone but the God who dwells there.

THE DEEPER CORRUPTION: PURITY CULTURE AND THE GIRL WHO BELIEVED SHE WAS RUINED

I stood in the youth group room on a Wednesday night in 1998. Seventeen girls sat in a circle. We were doing what every evangelical church did in the late nineties—we were talking about purity.

I held up a chewed piece of gum. I asked the girls: who wants this? No one raised their hand. Then I said: "This is what you are if you lose your virginity before marriage." The metaphor was meant to be instructive. What I taught that night was that a girl's worth was consumed by her sexuality, and once that sexuality was "given away," she was trash.

I have thought about that moment a thousand times since, and it never gets less repulsive.

The chewed gum metaphor was everywhere in evangelical youth ministry in the eighties and nineties. Variations appeared in countless churches. A cup everyone drank from—if you had sex, you were that cup, and no one would want you. A flower with its petals plucked—beautiful once, ruined now. A new car with the odometer turned back—used goods. The consistency was sickening: your body is valuable because it is untouched, and once it is touched by anyone other than your future husband, it is worthless.

I was not inventing this theology. I was repeating what I had been taught. The purity culture movement was not some fringe phenomenon—it was the dominant narrative in American evangelicalism from the mid-1980s through the early 2000s. Joshua Harris's book "I Kissed Dating Goodbye" sold over a million copies. True Love Waits, the Southern Baptist campaign that encouraged teenagers to sign purity pledges, was a national movement. Evangelical conferences sent thousands of girls through programming designed to make them terrified of their own sexuality.

The theology was simple and consistent: a girl's sexuality is dangerous. It is a threat to boys and men. Therefore, a girl must control her body, her appearance, her desires, her very presence. She must be modest so she does not tempt. She must be careful so she does not lead anyone astray. She must guard her virginity like it is the most precious thing she possesses because it is. That is where her value lives. That is the only thing that makes her worthy of a good man's love.

For girls who bought this narrative—and millions did—the result was catastrophic.

I have counseled women in their thirties and forties who were still carrying the shame of being sexually abused as children. They believed they were complicit. A man had violated them when they were five, eight, twelve years old, and they believed they had done something to cause it. The purity culture had taught them that their bodies were potential sources of male temptation, and somehow they had tempted this man into a crime. The theology had made them responsible for their own abuse.

I have talked to women who were raped as teenagers and whose first thought was not that they had been violated—it was that they had been ruined. That they were now damaged goods. That they had forfeited their worth. That if they told anyone, they would be known as the girl who was not pure. The purity culture had created a scenario where the victim of violence feared exposure more than she reported the violence.

I have sat with women whose eating disorders were tied directly to the messages they received about their bodies in the church. The body must be controlled. The body must be managed. The body must not be attractive in ways that tempt men. So starve it. Punish it. Make it smaller. Make it disappear. If you can erase your body, you can erase the threat it poses.

The purity culture told girls that their bodies were temples. But what it actually taught them was that their bodies were the enemy. Temples are sacred. Temples are honored. What we taught girls was that their bodies were obstacles to overcome, temptations to resist, liabilities to manage. We told them that honoring their bodies meant controlling them, hiding them, denying their hungers and desires.

And we did this to girls. Young girls. Children who did not have the cognitive development to resist the narrative, who accepted it as truth because the adults they trusted said it was God's design.

I need to be specific about my own complicity. I did not just repeat this theology. I amplified it. I made it vivid. I used the metaphors that stuck in girls' minds. The chewed gum. The used goods. The cup everyone drank from. I was trying to scare them into obedience, and what I was actually doing was teaching them to fear themselves.

When I look back at sermon transcripts from that period, I see how deliberately I crafted the rhetoric to create shame. I talked about a girl's body as if it were a separate entity from her, something that needed to be managed and controlled. I used language that turned her flesh into a problem to be solved. I spoke to the boys about their desires and struggles, naming that as real and valid, and then I spoke to the girls about their responsibility to not cause those struggles. I created a structure where male desire was natural and female responsibility was absolute.

The implicit message to boys was: your sexuality is powerful and you cannot be blamed for it. The implicit message to girls was: your sexuality is dangerous and you are entirely responsible for it. And then I acted confused when girls grew up with eating disorders, sexual dysfunction, an inability to trust their own judgment, a deep and abiding shame about their bodies.

Here is what needs to be said clearly: the purity culture was not just bad theology. It was abusive theology. It traumatized girls who internalized it. It created the conditions for predators to operate because it taught girls that their bodies belonged to others' moral frameworks, not to themselves. It made victims complicit in their own violation. And it came from pulpits. It came from people who were trusted. It came from God, we said.

The reframe begins with a return to what Scripture actually says about the body.

Paul writes about the body as temple, and the context matters. He is writing to the Corinthian church, a congregation embedded in a pagan culture where sexual immorality was normal and accepted. The issue is not that a girl has allowed her body to be seen. The issue is that people are participating in sexual sin, literal prostitution, in temples dedicated to false gods. That is the corruption he is addressing.

He says: "Do you not know that your bodies are temples of the Holy Spirit, who is in you, whom you have received from God? You are not your own; you were bought at a price. Therefore honor God with your bodies." The body is a temple. A sacred space. The Holy Spirit dwells there.

Here is what this actually means: your body is not the problem. Your body is not dangerous. Your body is not a threat. Your body is a sanctuary where God lives. And you are not responsible for how other people respond to your body. You are responsible for honoring the sacred space your body is.

If you wear a revealing dress, your body is still a temple. If you are sexually attractive, your body is still a temple. If you have desires and hungers and a sexuality that is alive, your body is still a temple. The presence of the Holy Spirit does not depend on how much skin you are showing or whether you have had sex outside of marriage. It depends on whether you have been bought by the blood of Christ. That is the condition. That is the only condition.

This means that the girl in the youth group who had already been sexually abused is not less of a temple. She is not damaged goods. She is not used. The Holy Spirit does not leave temples when violence is committed against them. The abuse does not change her worth. The violation does not change her identity. She is still a person in whom God dwells.

It means that the girl who chose to have sex, who made a decision about her own body—she is not ruined. She is not a chewed piece of gum. She is not less valuable. She is a human being who made a choice about her own body, and that choice does not determine her worth before God. The presence of God is not contingent on her virginity.

The cost of this reframe is enormous. It requires women to reject what they have been taught about themselves. It requires them to reclaim their bodies from the shame the church installed. It requires them to stand in front of the mirror and say: this is my body. It is sacred. And its sacredness has nothing to do with my virginity, my appearance, or how men respond to me.

For women who grew up in purity culture, this is not a small shift. This is a complete reorientation. This is untangling years of messages that said your body is not yours. This is learning to trust your body when you have been taught that your body lies. This is learning to listen to your hunger when you have been taught to deny it. This is learning to honor your own boundaries when you have been taught that your body belongs to a larger moral narrative that has nothing to do with you.

And it requires the church to repent. Not just to say we got this wrong. But to grieve what we did. To acknowledge the eating disorders we created. To acknowledge the sexual dysfunction we created. To acknowledge the women who believed they were trash because we taught them the metaphor of trash. To sit with the fact that we did this systematically, institutionally, in God's name.

When Joshua Harris finally recanted his own book twenty years later, he said something that mattered: "I realize that I want to publicly reconsider the ideas I promulgated in that book." But by that point, the damage was done. A generation of women had grown up believing their bodies were their shame.

The hope is not that we will undo the past. We cannot. But we can tell the next generation something different. We can tell girls: your body is sacred space. The Holy Spirit dwells in it. You are not responsible for how men respond to you. You are not the problem. Your sexuality is not dangerous. Your hunger is not something to be ashamed of. You are free to honor your body however you choose. You are free to listen to it. You are free to make your own decisions about it. And your worth, your eternal worth, is not tied to your virginity. It is tied to the fact that you are made in God's image.

That is the reversal. That is the hope that costs something. It costs the comfort of simple stories about sexuality. It costs the false security of a narrative that makes boys and men the victims of girls' power. It costs the easy answer that purity equals worth.

But what we gain is women who are not ashamed of their bodies. Women who know their worth is intrinsic. Women who can listen to their own bodies and trust what they hear. Women who are free from the cage of shame the church built around them.

And that is worth the cost.

The body is not the problem. This is the foundational theological claim the church needs to recover, and it will overturn more teaching than you expect.

For centuries, Christian theology has positioned the female body as the primary threat to male righteousness. The early church fathers spoke of women's beauty as a snare. Medieval theology treated the body as inherently sinful, with women's bodies being more sinful because they were more tempting. This fear has shaped pastoral teaching, dress codes, purity culture, the entire architecture of how churches teach young women to relate to themselves.

The consequence is that girls grow up believing their bodies are dangerous. Not dangerous because they can hurt themselves or others through recklessness. Dangerous because they cause men to stumble. Dangerous because desire is awakened in the presence of women's bodies, and that desire is treated as if it were the woman's fault. Your body is responsible for what happens in other people's minds. Your body is the occasion for sin. Your body is the thing that needs to be managed, controlled, hidden.

That is not theology. That is a system designed to make women responsible for men's moral choices. It places the burden of male sexuality on female bodies. It says: if a man struggles with lust, it is because you were not modest enough. If a man is drawn to you, it is because you were not careful enough. If a man acts on his desire, it is because you gave him cause.

That is a lie constructed to protect men from accountability for their own choices. And it has been one of the most damaging lies the church has told women about their bodies.

The incarnation speaks a different truth. God took on a body. Jesus was born of a woman. His birth was not a transaction that bypassed embodiment or sexuality. Mary was a woman. Her body was the site where God entered the world. And that body was blessed, not because it was asexual, but because it was the means of redemption.

Think about what that means. The body is not the problem. The body is the place where God meets a person. The body is where the infinite touched the finite. The body is good.

That is the starting point, and it changes everything. If the body is good, then the female body is good. Not in a way that invites male consumption. In a way that is inherent and independent of how men respond to it. A woman's body is good because she inhabits it. Because she is in it. Because through it, she relates to the world, to other people, to God.

When you teach that, everything about how girls relate to themselves shifts. Instead of teaching girls to monitor their bodies, to wonder if they are too visible, too attractive, too provocative, you teach them: your body is the vessel of your will. Your body is how you move through the world. Your body is good.

That does not mean the body is not capable of desire. It means the body's capacity for desire is not sinful. It means a woman's sexuality is not borrowed from a man's. It is hers. It belongs to her. It is part of her good creation.

The church has been terrified of female sexuality. I do not think this is an accident. Female sexuality, unlike male sexuality, has been written about primarily by men. And the narrative male theologians created was simple: female sexuality exists to serve male need. The female body arouses. The male body responds. That is the order of things. Treat female sexuality as a thing to be managed in service of male desire, and you have a system where women's bodies are always in the service of someone else's need.

But Scripture itself does not teach this. The Song of Songs is erotic poetry written from a woman's perspective. She desires her beloved. Her body is her own possession. She speaks of his body with the same kind of appetite he shows toward hers. It is mutual. It is reciprocal. It is good.

When the church reads that text, we have to contend with the fact that Scripture celebrates female desire. Not female availability. Female desire. A woman's wanting. Her body aroused and delighting in arousal. Her sexuality as the expression of her own will, her own pleasure, her own experience.

That is so far from what most women have been taught that it almost requires translation. A girl raised in the church has been told: your body is a temple. Keep it pure. Your sexuality is something that will be given to your husband on your wedding night. Until then, your job is to not stir up lust in him. To not be the cause of his falling. To understand that your sexual feeling is secondary to the need to protect his purity.

Then she gets married and the same body that she was told to suppress, control, hide—suddenly that body is supposed to spring alive sexually for the benefit of her husband. The suppression is supposed to vanish. The shame is supposed to evaporate. The voice that told her: your body is dangerous—that voice is supposed to go quiet.

It does not work that way. Shame does not vanish because a pastor said a prayer over you in a white dress. The message sinks deep. The girl learns her body is the problem. She learns to disconnect from her desire. She learns to perform sexuality rather than experience it. She learns to check the box—sex is now allowed—while her actual feeling, her actual want, remains as suppressed as it ever was.

This is why so many marriages have a sexual problem that is actually a spiritual problem. The woman was taught to kill her desire. Now the man is hurt that she does not have any. The church taught her shame. Now the church is confused why she has shame.

But the cure is not a new theology of submission that makes sex another arena where the wife's job is to be available. The cure is to go back to the beginning. The body is good. Your body is good. The desire in your body is good. You do not have to wait for a man's permission to feel it. You do not have to perform it for anyone. Your pleasure is not borrowed from his. It is yours.

That is what needs to be taught. Not from a stage, using clinical language, in forty-five minutes. In homes. In trusted relationships. In conversations between mothers and daughters. A girl needs to hear from the women in her life: your body is good. It will change. It will develop. Hair will grow where it didn't before. You will feel things that might confuse you. None of that is sin. None of that is dangerous. That is you. That is what it means to be a living, embodied human being.

She needs to hear: your body is not responsible for what others do with their eyes. If a man looks at you with lust, that is his choice. His eyes are his responsibility. Your body is just your body. It is not an invitation. It is not a temptation. It is not a thing to be apologized for. It is yours.

She needs to hear: your body will age. It will change. You will get cellulite. You will get stretch marks. You will look less like a magazine in a few years. And none of that diminishes what your body is. Your body is the place where you live your life. Your body is the location of your will. Your body is the site of your work, your play, your rest, your love. As long as you are alive, your body is valuable because you inhabit it.

She needs to hear: pleasure is not sin. Wanting is not sin. Desire is not sin. If you feel your body respond to something beautiful, to something sensual, to something that moves you, that is not corruption. That is wholeness. That is the body working as it was designed to work.

And eventually, in the right context, with the right person, she needs to hear: your body is good in pleasure too. The pleasure you feel is not shameful. The wanting you experience in sexuality is not borrowed from a man's wanting. It is yours. And when you come together with someone you love, both of your pleasures matter equally. Both of your bodies are good. Both of your wants are valid. The goal is not for one of you to give and the other to receive. The goal is for both of you to give, both of you to receive, both of you to experience pleasure as a language of love.

That is subversive in a church that has spent centuries telling women their bodies are the problem. But it is not extreme in Scripture. It is basic. The body is good. God said so in Genesis. Christ proved it in the incarnation. And the church needs to start believing it.

What keeps me up at night, as a pastor, is all the women who have sat in my congregation while I was teaching something different. While I was protecting the purity narrative. While I was making their bodies the problem. While I was teaching them to fear what they were created to be.

There is a woman in our church who told me, years after hearing a series I did on purity, that she spent her teenage years wearing clothes two sizes too big because she did not want her body to be an occasion for sin. She was a girl. She was eleven, twelve, thirteen years old. And she was trying to disappear inside larger clothes because the church had made her body the site of moral failure.

When I heard that, I wept. And I repented. Because I had been the one teaching the message that put her in that position. I had been the one suggesting that her job was to manage her body for the benefit of others' purity.

The course correction is not complicated. It is this: teach girls that their bodies are good. Not in the future tense. Not after they are married. Not in a way that is always mediated by how their bodies will serve others. Good. Right now. As they are.

Teach them that their bodies are capable of pleasure and that pleasure is a good gift from God. Not in a way that is shameful or secretive. In a way that is matter-of-fact. Your body responds to beauty. Your body enjoys sensation. Your body has hunger and thirst and the capacity for rest. All of that is good.

Teach them that their bodies belong to them. Not to their fathers. Not to their future husbands. To them. And decisions about what happens to their bodies are their decisions. Decisions about who touches them, how they dress, what they do with their own physical will—those are hers to make.

Teach them that when they become women, their sexuality is not something that appeared to serve a man's need. It appeared because they are whole people. And whole people have desire.

Do that teaching, and you will have transformed what the church offers to girls. You will have given them a foundation that allows them to grow into women who know they are good. Not conditionally. Not in service to others. Simply good, because they are, because their bodies are, because they image God and that image is not male only.

That is the theology that will set women free.

**CHAPTER 8**

Motherhood Is Not Your Identity

Christian Motherhood, Calling, Worth Beyond Mothering, and a Woman's Value

The stage lighting was soft, the music was atmospheric, and the speaker was telling the women in the auditorium that motherhood was their highest calling. This was a women's conference, the kind that evangelical churches have sent their women to for decades—a day of inspiration and affirmation, the message from every angle and every session designed to tell women that their identity and worth came from Christian motherhood.

I watched the faces. Some lit up. Some went hard. Some simply looked away.

Because not everyone there was a mother. Some had miscarried repeatedly and sat in the pews with phantom children they could not hold. Some had chosen not to have children and were being told, implicitly, that their obedience to God was incomplete. Some were infertile and were suffering in a room that spoke only of the joy and calling of motherhood. Some were single and could not be mothers because they did not have the family structure that made it possible. Some were past childbearing age and were being told, by the whole architecture of the event, that their best years and their best calling were behind them.

The speaker did not intend harm. But the structure was harmful. Because the message—repeated across the women's conference circuit, from church platforms, in books and Bible studies designed specifically for women—was that motherhood is the ultimate expression of feminine virtue, that it is where a woman finds her deepest meaning, that it is the proof of faithfulness.

I know this messaging because I have preached it. Not directly. But I have stood beside people who preached it, and I did not object. I have been to women's events and said nothing while the assumption nested into every sentence: that women's fulfillment comes through their children, that their bodies' primary purpose is reproduction, that their identity is tied to their capacity to nurture.

---

The evangelical obsession with motherhood is not primarily theological. It is cultural.

The cult of domesticity—the idealization of the home, the mother, the wife who finds her complete identity in nurturing her family—came out of Victorian England and Victorian America. It was a response to industrialization. As men left the home to work in factories and offices, women were constructed as the keepers of the domestic sphere. Motherhood became sacred. The home became a sanctuary. The woman became the priestess of that sanctuary.

This was not biblical. This was a cultural response to a particular historical moment. But the evangelical church took it and baptized it as God's eternal design for women.

The great irony is that the church did this at the exact moment when motherhood was becoming less central to women's identity in the broader culture. As women began working outside the home, as education opened to women, as contraception made it possible to choose if and when to have children, the evangelical church dug in harder. Motherhood became not just one expression of femininity but the only expression. The woman who did not mother was seen as incomplete. The woman who mothered and did something else was seen as divided. The only complete woman was the one who made her children her entire world.

This created an architecture of shame that is still standing.

The structure says: motherhood is your highest calling. Therefore, if you do not have children, something is wrong with you. If you have children and do not make them your entire focus, something is wrong with you. If you are past mothering age, something is past. Your best years are gone. Your highest purpose has expired.

I have watched this destroy women. I have watched women's conferences where childless women sat isolated, treated as incomplete. I have watched mothers apologize for having jobs while their husbands never apologized for theirs. I have watched women stay in jobs they hated or leave jobs they loved because the message was that their children needed them at home, not that their children needed them to be alive.

And I have watched the church quote Mary.

---

"I am the Lord's servant," Mary said to the angel. "May it be to me as you have said." (Luke 1:38)

She said yes. She said yes to an announcement that would mark her with scandal, cost her reputation, put her in poverty, and—though she did not know it then—would require her to watch her son be murdered by the state.

She said yes. And the church has spent two thousand years treating that yes as the totality of who Mary was.

She became the symbol of passive obedience. The virgin mother. The gentle woman who said yes and then receded into the background. The feminine ideal: silent, compliant, entirely devoted to nurturing her son. The church reduced the most significant yes that any woman in Scripture has ever said—a yes that required everything—down to a symbol of maternal meekness.

But Mary was not just a mother. She was a disciple. She was a woman who wrestled with things she did not understand. When she did not understand Jesus—and Scripture tells us there were moments when she did not—she stayed with him. She was present at his death. She was present in the upper room after his resurrection. She did not recede into the background. She was part of the founding community of the church.

She was more than a mother. She was a person. She was a follower. She was a woman.

But the cult of motherhood had no need for that complexity. It needed Mary the symbol. Mary the mother. Mary the woman who said yes and was therefore complete.

So the church took her and made her a stamp. Pressed her image onto the foreheads of every woman and said: this is who you are supposed to be. Maternal. Obedient. Devoted. And the stamp did not fit most women. So they tried to stretch themselves to fit it. They tried to become something smaller so they could be something more.

---

The reframe begins with a question: What if women are more than mothers?

Of course they are. Any woman who has existed outside of motherhood knows this. The woman who writes, or teaches, or heals, or builds, or creates, or leads, or thinks—these are not secondary expressions of her humanity that she pursues when she is not mothering. These are expressions of the image of God in her. These are her calling.

And yes—this is crucial—for some women, motherhood is the primary expression of their calling. There are women for whom nurturing children is the deepest yes they have to give. That is real. That is not lesser. And it is also not the only way to be a woman in the kingdom of God.

The cost of the obsession with motherhood is that we have lost sight of the women who mother differently. The woman who is a teacher—she is mothering in a classroom. The woman who is a doctor—she is mothering in a practice. The woman who writes or thinks or builds or leads—she is participating in the work of the kingdom. Not despite her womanhood. Because of it.

And the woman who does not mother at all—the single woman, the infertile woman, the woman who has chosen not to have children—she has the same worth before God as every other image-bearer. Her calling is not lesser. Her identity is not incomplete. She is not waiting for motherhood to make her whole.

I think about the single women in my congregation who have heard, explicitly and implicitly, that their lives are incomplete until they have a husband and children. I think about the women who have tried desperately to have children and have sat in front of women's conferences where the speaker assumed all the women in the room were mothers, spoke only to mothers, treated non-mothers as an afterthought or a problem to be fixed.

I think about the women in their sixties and seventies whose children have grown and whose whole identity was built on the identity of mother, and now they are untethered. They have no language for who they are. They have no sense of their own worth apart from the work of raising someone else.

And I think about what would be different if the church had told them: you are made in the image of God. That image is not dependent on whether you have children. That image is not dependent on whether you are married. That image is not dependent on whether you are young or beautiful or fertile. You are valued because you are.

---

The recovery requires saying no to motherhood-as-identity and yes to something harder.

It requires saying that women can mother and work and create and lead. That these are not competing callings. That you can be devoted to your children and devoted to something else without being divided.

It requires saying that women who do not mother are not incomplete. That their gifts matter. That their calling is real.

It requires saying that motherhood is one expression of femininity—a deep and important one, for those who experience it—but not the only expression. The woman who heals is feminine. The woman who teaches is feminine. The woman who leads is feminine. The woman who thinks is feminine. The woman who creates is feminine.

And it requires that the church repent of making motherhood a test of faithfulness.

The cost is real. It means women have to think harder about what they actually want versus what they have been told to want. It means men have to accept women in spaces other than the home. It means the church has to build systems that support women in their full humanity, not just in their capacity to nurture children.

But the hope is worth it. The hope is that a woman will know herself as more than a mother. That she will own her gifts and her calling without apology. That she will not measure her worth by whether she has children. That she will not apologize for working or creating or leading. That she will know that being made in the image of God is enough.

Mary said yes to an impossible calling. And then she said yes to a thousand other things. She was a woman. She was a mother. She was a disciple. She was a follower. She was a person. All of that was true simultaneously.

That is what it looks like when a woman is made for more than motherhood. That is what it looks like when the church tells her the truth: you are valued because you are. Not because of what you produce or who you nurture or whether you have children. You are valued because you are made in the image of the God who looked at creation and said it was good.

That is where your worth lives. Not in your womb. Not in your capacity to nurture. In the image of God that is stamped into your being from the beginning.

And that image is not diminished if you never have children. It is not completed if you do. It simply is. And it is enough.

WOMEN WITHOUT CHILDREN: THE BIBLE'S FORGOTTEN GALLERY OF POWER

The women's conference speaker asked a question I have heard a thousand times: "Mother, what is your greatest blessing?" Every mother in the room raised their hand. Then she looked at the women without children and asked: "And what about you?" The silence was loud.

A woman in that room had chosen not to have children. She was intelligent, successful, fulfilled in her work, and she sat in that silence knowing she was incomplete. Another woman had desperately wanted children and had miscarried six times. She sat in that silence carrying phantom babies she could not hold, and in that moment, the church had made her grief public. A woman who was single at age forty, who had never had the opportunity to mother, sat in that silence knowing she was running out of time. A woman in her sixties whose children had grown sat in that silence mourning the identity she no longer had.

The speaker did not mean harm. She was reflecting what the evangelical church has taught for seventy years: motherhood is your highest calling. If you are a woman, that is where your glory lives. Not in your work. Not in your intellect. Not in your leadership or creativity or strength. In your womb. In your capacity to bear and raise children.

I have stood in pulpits and reinforced this narrative. I have preached about women's roles using motherhood as the apex, the ultimate expression of femininity, the place where a woman reaches her full humanity. I have sat on panels where it was assumed that all the women present were mothers, and I did not object to the structure that suggested that only mothers had a voice in the conversation. I have benefited from a theology that elevated women's domestic role while narrowing their horizons to a single expression of their worth.

This is not biblical. It is cultural. And the Bible is full of women whose primary identity was not motherhood.

Deborah was a judge and a prophet and a military commander. She led Israel. "Deborah, a prophet, the wife of Lappidoth, was leading Israel at that time." That is the introduction. Wife of Lappidoth is mentioned in passing. It provides context. But it is not her identity. Her identity is judge. Her identity is prophet. Her identity is the woman leading a nation in a time of crisis.

She called Barak and said: "Go! This is the day the Lord has given Sisera into your hands. Has not the Lord gone ahead of you?" And then—this is crucial—the text says she went with him. She did not stay home and pray. She went with him into battle. She was present. She was leading. She was risking her life. She was not mothering. She was prophesying and commanding and shaping the future of her nation.

Does Scripture ever mention that Deborah had children? No. Does Scripture ever suggest that her lack of a public identity as a mother made her incomplete? No. Scripture presents her as a full human being whose calling was leadership. Period.

Esther was a queen and a political strategist. She hid her identity to survive in a foreign court. She navigated power structures with brilliance. She fasted and prayed and then she acted. She went before the king uninvited, which was a capital offense, to plead for the lives of her people. She was not trying to mother anyone. She was trying to save a nation. And her story is celebrated as a triumph of courage and wisdom.

Does Scripture mention her children? Does Scripture suggest that her failure to be a mother diminished her? No. Her identity is queen. Her calling is to use her power to protect her people. That is what makes her significant. That is what earns her a book in the Bible.

Huldah was a prophet. A woman whose primary identity was hearing from God and speaking God's word. "Huldah the prophetess, the wife of Shallum" is how she is introduced in 2 Kings 22. The same formula—wife mentioned in passing. But she is consulted about matters of national importance. When the high priest discovers the Book of the Law, he does not go to a male prophet. He goes to Huldah. He trusts her judgment. He listens to her interpretation. She is an authority on Scripture. She is an authority on God's will.

The fact that this surprises us says something about how far we have fallen from what Scripture actually teaches.

The daughters of Zelophehad challenged the law of inheritance. Their father died with no sons, and the law said his inheritance went to his brothers. The daughters came before Moses and made their case: "Why should our father's name disappear from his clan because he had no son? Give us a property among our father's relatives." And here is the stunning part: Moses brought their case before the Lord. And God sided with them. "What Zelophehad's daughters are saying is right." God changed the law because women challenged it.

These women were not mothering. They were advocating for themselves. They were using their voices and their intelligence to change an unjust system. They were acting with agency and courage. And God sided with them. God affirmed that their claim on justice was legitimate.

Ruth was a foreigner and a widow and a worker. She worked in the fields, gleaning after the harvesters, because she and her mother-in-law had nothing. She was not waiting for a man to rescue her. She was providing. She was making a way. She was intelligent and strategic—she asked Naomi for advice on how to approach Boaz. She was not passive. She was not waiting. She was solving problems.

When she eventually married Boaz, that is presented as a blessing. But her identity is not "wife." Her identity is the woman who worked, who provided, who had agency and used it. Yes, she eventually became a mother. But that is not what her story is primarily about.

Anna was a prophet. An old woman who spent her days in the temple, fasting and praying. When Jesus was presented as an infant, she recognized him. She proclaimed him to everyone. Her identity was prophecy. Her identity was faith. The text does not tell us she was a mother. The text tells us she was a person devoted to God and given the gift of recognizing the Messiah.

Lydia was a businesswoman. A dealer in purple cloth—high-end goods, significant capital, business acumen. When she heard the gospel, she believed and was baptized. Then she opened her home and her resources to Paul and his team. She was not mothering. She was providing leadership and hospitality and financial support to the new movement. She was using her wealth and her position to advance the gospel.

Mary of Bethany was a learner. She sat at Jesus's feet while he taught—a position that was restricted to male disciples in that culture. When Martha wanted her to go do domestic work, Jesus affirmed her choice. "Mary has chosen what is better." She chose to learn. She chose to be a student. She chose to sit in a position of authority and receive teaching. And Jesus affirmed it.

The list goes on. Miriam leading the women in celebration after the Red Sea crossing. Huldah interpreted Scripture. Jezebel was a woman of power—wrong power, but power. Bathsheba was eventually honored as the mother of Solomon and a woman of influence. Esther saved her people. Ruth was a woman of character. Deborah was a judge.

Where in this gallery of women is the narrative that says your highest calling is motherhood? Where is the woman whose entire identity is defined by her children? The Bible does not have one. The Bible has women who were judges, prophets, queens, workers, learners, leaders. Women whose primary identity was not their relationship to children.

The cult of motherhood is not biblical. It is Victorian. It is American. It emerged in the nineteenth century as a response to industrialization and the separation of work and home. The ideology of motherhood became sacred precisely at the moment when women were being excluded from the public sphere. If your purpose is to nurture children in the home, then you do not need to be educated for work in the world. Then you do not need political representation. Then your entire existence can be oriented toward service to the family. It was an ideology designed to keep women in their place—and it worked.

The evangelical church took this nineteenth-century cultural ideology and baptized it as God's eternal design. We read it into Scripture. We reinterpreted the women of the Bible so that they fit the story we wanted to tell. We saw Deborah as a judge but emphasized her femaleness as an exception. We saw Proverbs 31 as the ideal woman but read her through a Victorian lens—an industrious homemaker—rather than as a businesswoman making her own money.

I have done this. I have preached about women through the lens of motherhood. I have implied that a woman's work outside the home was secondary to her "real" calling. I have used Scripture to reinforce an ideology that had nothing to do with Scripture and everything to do with American cultural assumptions about gender.

The reframe is simple but destabilizing: women are not mothers first. Women are people first. For some women, motherhood is the primary expression of their calling. For others, it is one expression among many. For others still, it is not their calling at all.

A woman who is a teacher is not mothering in a classroom—though there is something nurturing about teaching. She is teaching. Her calling is education. Her identity is educator. That is complete and whole and needs no justification.

A woman who is a doctor is not some maternal expression of caregiving. She is a physician. Her calling is medicine. Her identity is healer. That is complete and whole.

A woman who is a businesswoman is not somehow performing motherhood in the marketplace. She is a businesswoman. Her calling is commerce and leadership. Her identity is entrepreneur. That is complete and whole.

A woman who is single and has no children is not incomplete. She is a person. Her calling is whatever God has given her to do. Her identity is not "waiting for marriage." Her identity is who she is. That is complete and whole.

This is costly because it requires us to let go of the simplicity of the motherhood narrative. It requires us to accept that women are complex, complex beings with various callings. It requires us to accept that a woman can be devoted to her children and devoted to her work without being divided. It requires us to accept that a woman who chooses not to have children is not missing something essential.

And it requires men to give up the comfort of thinking we understand what women should be. We do not. Women should be what God calls them to be. Some of them will be mothers. Some will not. Some will be teachers and writers and leaders and healers and prophets and judges. And the church's job is not to fit them into a predetermined mold. The church's job is to recognize the gifts God has given them and make space for those gifts to flourish.

The hope is that we will recover what Scripture has always shown us: that women are full human beings. That their worth is not tied to whether they have children. That their calling is not predetermined by their gender. That they are free to be who God has made them to be.

Mary—the one who said yes to becoming the mother of Jesus—was not defined by motherhood. She was a disciple. She was a follower. She was a woman who wrestled with the strange calling she was given. She was more than a mother. And the church needs to stop reducing her to that single identity.

Every woman in every church is more than a mother. She is a thinking, feeling, powerful image-bearer of God. And if the church cannot make space for her full humanity, then we are failing her. We are committing a form of violence against her, reducing her to a single acceptable expression of her existence.

That is the costly hope. It costs the simplicity of a single story. But what we gain is the liberation of half God's people.

**CHAPTER 9**

The Working Woman and the Guilt Machine

Working Moms, Career, Christian Guilt, and Freedom from False Theology

It was Tuesday at 2 p.m. when Sarah walked into my office and wept before she sat down.

Her hands trembled as she held the coffee I offered. She had been coming to FBC for three years. Her husband, Mike, was in my small group. They had two children — Emma, six, and Thomas, four. She worked as an operations manager for a manufacturing company thirty minutes from our church. She was good at it. The company had recently promoted her. This should have been a moment of celebration.

Instead, she was sitting in my office, guilt-stricken, asking if she was failing her family. She was carrying the weight of what the church calls the "working mom guilt"—a burden made explicitly spiritual by bad theology.

The narrative was predictable. Her mother-in-law had mentioned that "the kids really need their mom home." A women's Bible study had discussed Titus 2:4-5 with emphasis on the phrase "keepers of the home." Someone at church had gently noted that she seemed "busy." The implication crystallized into certainty: her ambition was killing her children. Her career was a rebellion against God's design.

What made this moment unbearable was that Mike had lost his job four months earlier. Sarah's salary was keeping the mortgage paid. Her benefits were the only health insurance the family had. She had become the provider not through personal ambition but through necessity. And the church had made her feel guilty about it.

This is the guilt machine. It runs on bad theology and cultural assumption masquerading as Scripture.

The American evangelical church has spent the last seventy years building a theology of domesticity. Women belong in the home. Their primary calling is motherhood and housekeeping. Work outside the home is permissible only when absolutely necessary, and even then, only with reluctance and apology. A woman who pursues a career, who builds something, who takes satisfaction in her professional accomplishment, is being seduced by worldly ambition. She is neglecting her true calling. She is — the whispered conclusion — not being biblical.

I have watched this theology destroy women. Not through explicit commands but through the slow, suffocating pressure of constant implication. Women who work do so under the weight of unspoken judgment. They apologize for their hours. They feel guilty on school days when they cannot volunteer in the classroom. They rush to prove they are still "putting family first," which means their professional success must always be subordinate to the emotional needs of others. They are caught between two impossibilities: be a good mother and career woman, and somehow hold both without the tension.

Meanwhile, the same church quoting Titus 2 has never seriously reckoned with Proverbs 31.

Read it without the sentimentality that usually surrounds it. "A wife of noble character who can find? She is worth far more than rubies." The passage describes a woman who plants a vineyard, considers a field and buys it, works with her hands with vigor. She is a merchant — she deals in fabric and sells garments. She profits. She sees that her trading is profitable. She does not apologize for it. She does not explain it as a temporary necessity until her husband finds work. She is building something. She is entrepreneurial. She is successful. And Scripture presents her as the model of feminine virtue.

The passage ends: "Charm is deceptive and beauty is fleeting, but a woman who fears the Lord is to be praised." Her worth is measured not against her husband's career or the neatness of her home. It is measured against her character, her industry, her wisdom, and her faith. The Bible has no problem with the working woman. The American church does.

Lydia appears in Acts 16 for a brief moment, and that moment tells everything. "On the Sabbath we went outside the city gate to the river, where we expected to find a place of prayer. We sat down and began to speak to the women who had gathered there. A woman named Lydia, a dealer in purple cloth from the city of Thyatira, was listening. The Lord opened her heart to respond to Paul's message. When she and the members of her household were baptized, she invited us to her home and said, 'If you consider me a believer in the Lord, come and stay at my home.' And she persuaded us." Lydia was a businesswoman. Dealing in purple cloth was not subsistence work — it was a high-end trade that required capital, supplier relationships, and market savvy. She was successful enough to host Paul and his missionary team in her home. The first European convert to Christianity was a woman who owned a business and used her home as a base for ministry.

There is no apology in the text for her work. There is no narrative suggesting she felt conflicted about balancing business and faith. She simply heard the gospel, believed, and opened her home and her resources to the new movement. Her work was never presented as something to transcend or apologize for. It was the structure of her life, and she lived it faithfully.

Or consider Deborah. "Deborah, a prophet, the wife of Lappidoth, was leading Israel at that time. She held court under the Palm of Deborah between Ramah and Bethel, and the Israelites went up to her to have their disputes decided." She was a judge. She was a prophet. She was a military commander — she called Barak and told him, "Go! This is the day the Lord has given Sisera into your hands. Has not the Lord gone ahead of you?" And then the text says she went with him. She did not stay home and pray. She went with him. She was present. She was leading. She was risking. And no one in Scripture suggested this was a violation of feminine virtue.

The pattern is clear through Scripture. Women owned property. Women ran businesses. Women prophesied and judged and taught. Women were not confined to domestic space. The Bible has no unified prohibition against women working outside the home. What it has is a pattern of women working, contributing, leading, and being valued for their contribution.

The guilt machine does not come from Scripture. It comes from American culture married to evangelical theology in a way that has corrupted both.

In the 1950s, after World War II, the American economy decided women should leave the factories and return home. This was not theological reflection. It was economic policy. There were factories full of women who had proven they could do the work. When the soldiers came home, those jobs were reserved for them, and women were told that their true fulfillment was in the domestic sphere. The American church did not resist this narrative. It baptized it. It found theological support for it. It built complementarian frameworks around it. It made women's confinement to the home a spiritual virtue.

It is worth asking: if the American church had been shaped by 1950s Japanese culture instead of American capitalism, would it have developed the same theology of domesticity? Of course not. The theology followed the economic arrangement, not the other way around.

I have been complicit in this. I have stood in pulpits and quoted Titus 2 in ways that pressured women to choose between ambition and faithfulness. I have counseled couples with the assumption that the man's career took priority. I have offered pastoral care to women that implicitly affirmed the guilt machine's narrative. I have done the thing I am now critiquing. That matters. That needs to be said directly.

Sarah did not leave my office that day with new theology. She left it with permission. Permission that should never have needed to be given — permission to work, to provide, to build something, to find satisfaction in professional excellence, without apology to the church or God. She left knowing that I believed her family would be fine. Better than fine. Her children would grow up knowing that their mother was capable, that she solved problems, that she provided security for her family in a time of crisis. Her example would teach them more than any sermon about what it means to be faithful when circumstances demand it.

The guilt machine runs on the assumption that women working outside the home is a concession to necessity, a compromise with worldliness, a failure to prioritize what matters. This assumption is not biblical. It is cultural. It is time we said so clearly.

Women work. They have always worked. They work because they are called to image God's creative agency in the world. They work because their gifts and intelligence are resources given by God, not borrowed from their identity as wife or mother. They work because building something, solving a problem, creating value, moving toward excellence — these are not secondary to the domestic sphere. They are expressions of being made in God's image.

The church's job is not to manage women's guilt about working. The church's job is to free women from guilt that was never biblically grounded in the first place. To tell them clearly: your work matters. Your gifts matter. Your ambition to do something well is not a rebellion against God. Your success does not diminish your worth as a mother or wife. You are not betraying your family by providing for them or by finding satisfaction in your work.

This is what Sarah needed to hear. It is what countless women in the church need to hear. Not a theology that makes room for working women as an exception. A theology that recognizes women working, building, and leading as entirely consistent with being made in God's image.

That is not an extreme position. It is returning to what Scripture actually says.

THE MANDATE BELONGS TO BOTH: WORK IS NOT A CONSEQUENCE OF SIN FOR WOMEN

I want to begin with something Genesis tells us that we have almost completely missed.

> *"So God created mankind in his own image, in the image of God he created them; male and female he created them. God blessed them and said to them, 'Be fruitful and increase in number; fill the earth and subdue it. Rule over the fish in the sea and the birds in the sky and over every living creature that moves on the ground.'" This is Genesis 1:27-28. God gives the mandate to both. Male and female. The cultural mandate—the calling to work and create and build and govern—is given to both equally.*

Then Genesis 2 provides more detail: "The Lord God took the man and put him in the Garden of Eden to work it and take care of it." Work is part of God's original design. Work is not punishment. Work is not a consequence of sin. Work is created goodness. And in Genesis 2, before the Fall, before sin entered the world, both the man and the woman are in the garden. Both are given the mandate to work.

Yet the evangelical church has taught for generations that work outside the home is a concession for women, a necessary evil when circumstances demand it, but never the place where a woman's heart should be. A woman's true calling is the home. Her highest virtue is motherhood and housekeeping. If she must work, she should do so reluctantly, with guilt, understanding that she is compromising her calling.

This theology is not biblical. It is cultural captivity dressed up as Scripture.

The American church pulled this theology from nineteenth-century Victorian ideology, reinforced it with twentieth-century economics, and then found ways to make it seem biblical. We took Titus 2:4-5—"to be self-controlled and pure, to be busy at home, to be kind, and to be subject to their husbands, so that no one will malign the word of God"—and we made it the foundation of a theology of domesticity. But read it in context. Paul is addressing a specific problem in the Cretan church: "For there are many rebellious people, full of meaningless talk and deception, especially those of the circumcision group." The issue is not work outside the home. The issue is women being deceived by false teachers. Paul is telling Timothy that he needs to teach women sound doctrine so they will be grounded and not vulnerable to deception.

But we pulled Titus 2:4-5 out of that context and turned it into a universal law: women belong in the home. End of story.

Except Scripture does not say that. Scripture shows women working. Lydia was a businesswoman. Proverbs 31 describes a woman who owned property, ran a business, sold goods, and made profit. There is no embarrassment in that passage about her work. There is no suggestion that she should have stayed home. She is presented as a model of virtue.

I sat with Sarah in my office, and her hands were shaking, and she was convinced she was failing her family. But the reality was different. Her husband had lost his job. Her salary was paying the mortgage. Her work was providing stability for her family at a moment when everything was uncertain. And the church had made her feel guilty about it.

This is what the guilt machine does. It makes women feel like they are choosing ambition over family when they are actually choosing survival. It makes them feel like they are being selfish when they are providing. It makes them feel like they are disobeying God when they are actually being faithful to their calling as a provider.

I told Sarah something that should not have needed to be said, but in this moment, desperately needed to be heard: your work matters. Your job is not a failure of your femininity. Your salary is not a compromise of your calling. You are imaging God. You are using the gifts God has given you. You are solving problems and leading people and creating value. This is not secondary. This is not temporary. This is not something you should apologize for.

But here is what broke my heart: Sarah's relief when she heard this was so deep that it suggested how much she had internalized the alternative message. The church had convinced her that she was failing. She needed permission to work. Permission that should have come automatically from Scripture, but which the church had conditioned her not to have.

This is not an accident. The theology of female domesticity is not a mistake. It is a system designed to keep women contained. And the evangelical church has been its most effective enforcer.

In the 1950s, after World War II, American women came home from factories and offices where they had proven themselves completely capable of doing serious work. The American economy decided—because women now represented a threat to the jobs the returning soldiers needed—that women should be ideologically confined to the home. Women were told that their place was with their children. That the most important work they could do was domestic. That leaving the home meant abandoning their families.

This was not universal. It was a specific economic decision made to protect male jobs and male wages. But it was presented as if it were eternal truth.

The evangelical church did not invent this narrative, but we weaponized it. We made it biblical. We created theological frameworks around it. We preached it from pulpits with the authority of Scripture. We taught women that this was what God wanted. We taught men that this was the order God intended.

And now, seventy years later, we are still living with the consequences.

Women apologize for working. They feel guilty about their ambition. They sacrifice their careers—not because they want to, but because the church has convinced them that sacrificing is what God demands. They leave jobs they love because a sermon about Titus 2:4-5 landed in a way that made them feel unfaithful. They make less money than they are capable of making because they cannot justify being away from their children the way the church has taught them to feel they must justify it.

Meanwhile, we have no problem with men working. Men's work is their calling. Men's ambition is them imaging God. Men's provision is their greatest virtue. We do not tell men they should feel guilty about missing school events because they are at work. We do not tell men that their family would be better off if they stayed home. We tell them that their work is important and they are right to pursue excellence in it.

The double standard is the point. It is designed to keep women contained while appearing to value them. It is designed to make women's work secondary while presenting male work as primary. It is designed to keep the power structures intact while using Scripture as justification.

I have been the one doing this. I have preached about women's roles in ways that made work outside the home seem like a less spiritual option than staying home. I have counseled couples in ways that assumed the husband's career mattered more. I have failed to challenge the cultural assumptions that I inherited and thought were biblical.

Here is what needs to be said clearly: work is not less spiritual than motherhood. Building something is not less important than raising children. Using your gifts in the world is not a compromise of your calling. If God gave women the cultural mandate in Genesis 1—and He did—then work is part of what women are called to do.

Genesis 3 tells us that work became more difficult after the Fall. "By the sweat of your brow you will eat your food." But that does not change that work itself is good. Work is part of God's design for humanity. And if women are fully human, then work is part of God's design for women.

Martin Luther understood this. He had a concept called vocation—the idea that all work can be sacred if it is done in service to God and neighbor. A woman working as a baker is imaging God and serving her community. A woman working as a physician is imaging God and serving her community. A woman working as a businesswoman is imaging God and serving her community. Luther preached vocation for everyone—though, to be honest, his actual application was selective. But the concept is right.

The evangelical church has taken Luther's concept of vocation and applied it primarily to men. Men have vocations. Women have roles. The difference is enormous. A vocation is a calling from God. A role is a function you are assigned. A vocation implies agency and choice. A role implies duty and submission.

We have robbed women of vocation. We have confined them to roles. And we have called this God's design.

The reframe begins with a return to Genesis: work is God's design. It is pre-fall. It is part of the original goodness of creation. And it belongs to both men and women equally.

This does not mean that all women should work outside the home. Some women will choose to focus on domestic work and raising children. That is a valid choice, made freely. But it is a choice. Not a mandate. Not a requirement of faithfulness. Not the only expression of virtue for women.

It means that a woman who works outside the home is not compromising her calling. She is fulfilling it. She is using the gifts God gave her. She is contributing to her family and her community. She is imaging God's creative agency in the world.

It means that a woman can be ambitious without being faithless. That she can pursue excellence in her work without being accused of selfishness. That she can find satisfaction in her professional accomplishment without being told she is neglecting what matters.

It means that the church stops using Scripture to manage women's guilt about working, and instead affirms that their work has value. That their gifts matter. That their contribution to the world is not secondary to their relationship to their family.

The cost is that men have to accept women as competitors. As equals in the workforce. As people whose professional advancement is as important as men's. It costs the comfort of an unquestioned hierarchy where men advance and women support. It costs the simplicity of a single story about what women should do.

But the hope is that women will be free. Free to choose how they spend their time and energy. Free to develop their gifts. Free to build something. Free to provide for their families. Free to work alongside men without apologizing for it.

Sarah left my office knowing that her family was going to be fine. More than fine. Her children would grow up knowing that their mother was capable. That she solved problems. That she provided security for them. That she was not broken by the necessity of working. That being a woman did not mean being confined to a single sphere.

That is what the gospel looks like for women and work. Not permission that should have been unnecessary. But clarity that their work matters. That their gifts matter. That they are free to be who God has made them to be.

**CHAPTER 10**

When Men Fail Women

Church Abuse, Protecting Women, Accountability, and Theology That Enables Violence

This is the hardest chapter I have written.

Not because it requires careful theology. Not because it demands historical nuance. It is hard because I must tell the truth about things I have seen in pastoral ministry and things I have failed to address. The church's catastrophic failure to protect women from abuse. The theology we built to justify keeping women trapped. The cost of our silence.

I will start with specifics because abstraction lets us off the hook.

Five years ago, a woman in our congregation came to me. She had been married for twelve years. Her husband was a prominent man in our church — a deacon, a volunteer, someone with authority. She came to my office with bruises. She came with a story of control, of emotional devastation, of physical violence. She came to the shepherd and asked: what do I do?

I listened. I was compassionate. I did not minimize her pain. But I told her something I believed was true, something I had been taught was biblical, something I had preached from this pulpit. I told her that marriage is a covenant, and covenants are not broken lightly. I told her that God hates divorce more than He hates the circumstances that create it. I told her to go home, to pray, to be the wife he needed her to be. I told her — these words still haunt me — that "submission covers a multitude of sins."

She left my office and went home. Six months later, her seven-year-old son called 911 when his father put her in the hospital.

I want to be clear about what happened: I did not cause that violence. Her husband did. But I used Scripture to lock her in a room with him. I cited theology as justification for my cowardice. I chose the "stability" of marriage over the safety of a human being. I chose the comfort of doctrine over the cry of the wounded.

That moment broke something open in me. Not immediately — I clung to my theology for another two years, counseling two more women in similar situations with similar counsel. But eventually, the weight of what I had done forced a reckoning.

Here is what the church has done for centuries: we have built a theology of marriage that elevates the institution over the people in it. We have quoted Ephesians 5 on submission while ignoring Ephesians 5 on headship meaning laying down your life for her. We have quoted 1 Corinthians 7 on indissolubility while ignoring the historical reality that women were property in the ancient world and Paul was actually expanding their rights. We have used Scripture as a cudgel to keep women trapped with men who hurt them.

Martin Luther wrote letters about wife-beating in the sixteenth century. He accepted it as inevitable. A man should beat his wife if necessary for her own spiritual good. The wife should accept it as God's will. This was not fringe theology. This was the theology of the Protestant Reformation. For centuries, the church accepted domestic violence as part of the normal order of Christian marriage. A man could discipline his wife just as he disciplined his children or slaves. This was taught from pulpits. This was defended by theologians. This was Scripture, properly understood.

The Victorian church — the church of propriety and purity — was silent on domestic violence. The doctrine of wifely submission was preached with absolute clarity. The reality of what happened in homes was never examined. Good Christian women endured abuse in silence because the church had taught them that their suffering was redemptive. That their submission was holy. That divorce was a greater sin than the violence they experienced. Women died. Children grew up in terror. And the church maintained its pastoral distance and its doctrinal clarity.

The modern church has not reckoned honestly with this history. We have softened the language. We say headship means "servant leadership." We say submission is "mutual." But the functional structure remains: women are told to stay, to pray harder, to be better wives. Men are told they have authority. The church reinforces the power imbalance even as it pretends to have become more enlightened.

And when a woman finally comes to the pastor with bruises, she comes with a thousand sermons in her head telling her that leaving is sin.

The passage that appears again and again in church teaching on divorce is Malachi 2:16. "I hate divorce," says the Lord, the God of Israel." This verse is wielded like a weapon to keep women in violent marriages. But read the whole passage. "The man who hates and divorces his wife does violence to the one he should protect. So be on guard, and do not be unfaithful." The passage is not condemning women who flee violence. It is condemning men who commit violence while claiming they are still serving God. It is saying: the man who beats his wife while calling it Christian discipline is doing violence. And the God of Israel hates that violence.

The Greek word for "hates" is miseo. It means to hate intensely, to abhor, to regard with contempt. God abhors violence toward women. God abhors the man who covers his garment with violence. God abhors the theology that locks women in with abusers.

But the church has read Malachi 2:16 backwards. We have made it about the woman's escape rather than the man's violence. We have turned God's abhorrence of violence into God's abhorrence of divorce. We have created a theological structure where a woman's only spiritual options are to stay and suffer or to leave and sin.

This is idolatry disguised as theology.

I have counseled women in situations where the only way to describe their experience is abuse. Emotional abuse that isolated them from friends and family. Financial abuse that left them without agency or resources. Sexual coercion. Physical violence. And I have watched the church surround them with a theology that held them in place.

I have also watched the church protect abusers. I have been in elder meetings where a man with a reputation for integrity, a man who gave money and volunteered and seemed like a good Christian, was accused by his wife of abuse. And I have heard elder boards rationalize why she might be exaggerating. Why both sides need to be heard. Why the church should not get involved in "private marital matters." Why the man's position in the church should not be jeopardized by an accusation. I have watched the church choose the man's reputation over the woman's safety.

I have counseled women who reported abuse and were told they were not submitting enough. I have known pastors who told abused women they could not receive communion because they were considering divorce. I have heard ministers equate an abused woman's protection of herself with sin.

This is the \#MeToo moment the church still has not had. We are not having a serious reckoning with abuse in our institutions. We are not protecting women. We are not changing the theology that enables abuse. We are making gestures toward reform while maintaining the structures that create the conditions for violence to thrive.

The problem runs deeper than bad interpretation. It runs to the question of power. The theology of male headship is not incidental to the problem of abuse. It is central. When a man is taught that he is the head of his household, that a woman's role is to follow, that his interpretation of Scripture is authoritative, that her submission is a spiritual virtue — you have created the perfect conditions for abuse. You have given him the theology to justify his control. You have given her the theology to accept it.

I am not saying that all men who hold complementarian theology abuse women. I am saying that complementarian theology creates a power structure that makes abuse easier and makes escape harder.

The church's response should not be to argue about whether complementarianism enables abuse. The response should be to take abuse seriously enough to change the theology that protects it.

Let me be specific about what this means.

First: the church must stop using Scripture to keep women in violent situations. Full stop. If a woman comes to the pastor with evidence of abuse, the response is not theological debate about divorce. The response is: your safety matters. God does not want you to suffer violence. We will help you get safe.

Second: the church must stop protecting abusers. When a man is credibly accused of abuse, the response is investigation, accountability, and the protection of the victim. Not rationalization, not the assumption that both sides have a point, not the prioritization of the man's reputation or ministry position.

Third: the church must tell men explicitly: if you harm a woman, you forfeit your place of authority in this community. This is non-negotiable. Not "you will go through a restoration process." Restoration with the abuser should never be the goal. The goal is the safety and healing of the person who was harmed.

Fourth: the church must change the theology that creates the conditions for abuse. This does not mean eliminating the concept of headship. It means understanding headship not as authority over but as responsibility for. A man who claims to be the head of his household must be understood as bearing the weight of ensuring the safety, dignity, and flourishing of the woman in his life. Any headship that involves control, intimidation, or violence is not headship — it is tyranny.

I failed that woman. I used Scripture to hold her in a cage with a man who beat her. The church failed her. The theology we preached failed her. Her son had to call 911 to get her out.

That is not abstract failure. That is not theological error without cost. That is a woman's body in the hospital and a child traumatized by witnessing his father's violence.

The church's response to this kind of failure should not be to double down on the theology that created it. The response should be to interrogate that theology with brutal honesty and ask: what have we done? What are we still doing? What needs to change?

I cannot undo what I counseled that woman to do. I cannot undo the months she suffered because I told her that her suffering was redemptive. I cannot undo the moment her son had to be the one to protect her. But I can tell the truth about it. And I can refuse to give similar counsel to the next woman who comes to my office with fear in her eyes.

The church must do the same. We must tell the truth about what we have done. We must grieve the lives we have harmed. We must change the theology that enabled it. We must make it clear that a woman's safety is not negotiable, that her voice will be believed, that the church will stand with her — even if standing with her means dismantling theology we thought was untouchable.

This is not compassionate. This is not even pastoral in the traditional sense. This is what repentance looks like when the thing you must repent of is structural, when it is built into the theology you have taught.

Women deserve better from the church. They deserve a community where safety is more important than doctrine. Where their voice is heard and believed. Where the protection of the vulnerable takes priority over the reputation of the powerful.

They deserve a God who abhors violence toward them. They already have that God. The church needs to stop hiding that God behind theology that protects abusers.

THE STRUCTURES THAT ENABLE VIOLENCE: COMPLIMENTARIANISM AND THE THEOLOGY OF CONTROL

I need to be precise about something because abstraction lets churches off the hook.

Complementarian theology—the teaching that men and women are equal in worth but different in role, with men given headship—creates a power structure that makes abuse easier and makes escape harder. This is not a slippery slope. This is a direct line.

When you teach a man that he is the head of his household, you have given him a category that places him above his wife. You have given him a theological structure for leadership that inherently involves authority. You have made his interpretation of Scripture the final word. You have made his will the directive for the family. And when you then teach a woman that her calling is to submit, you have created the perfect conditions for control.

I am not saying that all men who hold complementarian theology will abuse their wives. That is obviously not true. But I am saying that the theology makes abuse easier, and the victim's escape harder, and the church's response weaker.

A man who has been taught that he is the head of his household and a woman who has been taught that she should submit have been shaped by a power dynamic that is already established before any violence occurs. When the violence comes—and it often does—it does not feel like violence to the victim. It feels like what headship is supposed to look like. It feels like what she is supposed to accept.

I saw this in my pastoral work over and over. A woman would come to me with bruises and tell me about her husband's control. He tells her what to wear. He monitors her phone. He prevents her from seeing friends. He controls the finances. He makes decisions about the children without consulting her. And I listened to this catalog of control and never said: this is abuse. I listened to it as marital conflict. As a problem to be solved through better communication or submission. I did not recognize—because I had not been trained to recognize—that this was a man using theology as a tool to control another person.

The theology said: he is the head. The reality was: he is using that theology to justify treating her as property.

Here is what needs to be said clearly: if a man interprets his headship as control, if he uses that theological category to dominate his wife, if he prevents her from having agency or autonomy or voice—that is abuse. Full stop. It does not matter if there are no bruises. It does not matter if he is a deacon or a pastor or a respected man in the community. If he is using the theology of complementarianism to control another person, he is committing abuse.

And the church has enabled it. For centuries. By teaching the theology, by not challenging men who misuse it, by surrounding women who are being controlled with messages that tell them submission is their calling, by treating the headship-submission relationship as the basis of Christian marriage.

I have to be honest about what happened when that woman came to me with bruises. I did not cause the violence. Her husband did. But I amplified it. I told her that covenants are unbreakable. I told her that God hates divorce more than He hates the circumstances that create it. I told her that submission covers a multitude of sins. I was using Scripture to tell her that her suffering was something she should endure, not escape.

She left my office with those words in her ears. She went home to a man who was beating her. She tried to be the wife he needed her to be. She submitted. She stayed. Six months later, her seven-year-old son had to call 911 because his father had put her in the hospital.

I want to sit with that moment because I cannot undo it, but I need to understand it.

That woman believed what I told her. She believed that divorce was worse than violence. She believed that her suffering was redemptive. She believed that if she just submitted more, if she just prayed harder, if she just loved her husband better, he would change. She had internalized the theology that I had reinforced. And it kept her in a room with a man who was destroying her.

That is what complimentarian theology does when it is combined with a man who is violent. It becomes a weapon. It becomes a justification. It becomes a lock on the cage.

Now I need to talk about what comes next, because the failure is not just in counseling one woman. The failure is in what the church did when the violence became public.

When the ambulance came and everyone knew, when it was clear that this man had broken his wife's bones, when there was no more hiding—the church still protected him. Not explicitly. But functionally. An elder meeting was called. The man's reputation was discussed. His standing in the church was weighed. There were considerations about whether his wife might be exaggerating. Whether both sides should be heard. Whether this was really a church matter or a private family matter.

I was in that room. And I listened as good men—men who were not abusive, who cared about their families, who had theology they believed was biblical—discussed whether we should really be taking the side of a woman against a man who had been a loyal member of the church for years.

That is the moment I want to focus on because it shows how complimentarian theology enables the protection of abusers.

When you have a theology that positions men as leaders and decision-makers, when you have a church structure where men hold most positions of authority, when you have a narrative that tells you that a woman's role is to follow male leadership—then when that woman accuses a man of abuse, you have built-in reasons to doubt her. She is not accustomed to making important decisions. She is accustomed to deferring to male authority. Maybe she is misinterpreting his leadership as abuse. Maybe she is being emotional. Maybe she is not being submissive enough.

The theology itself becomes a tool for silencing her.

And I participated in that. I sat in that elder meeting and did not fight hard enough for her. I accepted the logic that this was complicated. I did not insist that a woman's safety took priority over a man's reputation. I did not stand up and say: this man beat his wife. That is not a marriage problem. That is a crime. And our response should not be to debate the nuances. Our response should be to protect the victim and hold the perpetrator accountable.

I want to be clear about what I think should happen when a credible accusation of abuse is made.

First: believe the woman. This is where complementarian theology makes things harder because it has taught women that their voice matters less than men's. But the voice of a woman who says she is being abused should be believed. Not because women do not lie. But because abusers are skilled at appearing trustworthy, and victims are skilled at hiding their abuse because they have been taught that their abuse is shameful. The presumption should be that if a woman risks the consequences of speaking up, she is telling the truth.

Second: investigate. Gather evidence. Talk to the man. But do not do the thing we did, which is to wait for the victim to prove her case while the man sits in his authority. The church should be actively investigating, not passively hoping more information comes out.

Third: if the abuse is substantiated, the man loses his position. Not temporarily. Not pending a restoration process. Permanently. If a man has used his position or his authority to abuse another person, he forfeits the right to hold authority in the community. This is not cruelty. This is basic protection.

Fourth: the victim gets full support. Counseling. Financial assistance. Legal help. Housing if she needs it. The church stands with her. This is not even controversial. This is basic Christianity.

Fifth: the man is held accountable not just by the church but by law. If it is criminal abuse, it should be reported to authorities. The church does not get to handle this internally. That is not mercy. That is covering up a crime.

Now, here is what will happen if churches do this. Some men will leave because they feel persecuted. Some men will say that the church has become too feminist. Some will argue that we are being unfair to men by believing women. Some will claim that we are destroying marriages.

But what will also happen is that women will know they are safe. Women will know that if they are being abused, the church will protect them, not protect the abuser. Women will know that their voice will be heard. Women will know that the church is serious about their safety.

And yes, it will require changing the theology. It will require moving away from complementarianism toward something that actually reflects what Paul says about mutual submission and equality in Christ. It will require men to have less unquestioned authority. It will require women to have more agency and voice.

For churches that have built their entire structure on complementarian theology, this will feel threatening. But the alternative is that we continue enabling abuse while calling it biblical.

I know what it is like to walk into a room and feel the weight of what I did not do. I know what it is like to stand behind the theology that kept a woman trapped with a man who was destroying her. I know what it is like to be complicit in her suffering because I believed that doctrine mattered more than her safety.

That woman did not die, but she could have. Her son was traumatized, but he could have been killed. And the church—my church, the institution I represent, the theology I taught—did not protect them. The theology we held more dear than her safety actually enabled the abuse.

Here is what needs to change.

First: the church must stop using Scripture to keep women in abusive situations. This is non-negotiable. The passages that are used to justify women's submission—Ephesians 5:22-24, 1 Corinthians 11:3, 1 Peter 3:1—are not permission for men to control women. They are calls for mutual love and respect. But for decades we have preached them as permission for men to rule and women to obey. That has to end.

Second: the church must teach men explicitly what headship actually means. If you hold a complementarian position, then teach it right: the head is the one who lays down his life for the other. The head is the one who is responsible for the welfare of the other. The head is not the one who controls or dominates or demands obedience. A man who thinks headship means authority over his wife has not understood the model. Jesus did not lord authority over the church. He laid down His life for it.

Third: the church must have zero tolerance for abuse. Not zero tolerance for divorce or zero tolerance for women reporting abuse or zero tolerance for disrupting the peace. Zero tolerance for abuse. If a man is beating his wife, if he is controlling her, if he is sexually coercing her, if he is emotionally devastating her—that is not something the church mediates or heals with marriage counseling. That is something the church responds to with protection of the victim and accountability for the perpetrator.

Fourth: the church must support women in leaving. If a woman is being abused, the church should not be asking her to pray harder or submit more or try to make it work. The church should be helping her get safe. That means housing, that means legal support, that means counseling, that means financial help.

Fifth: the church must change its theology if its theology is enabling abuse. If complementarianism is being used by men to control women, then the church has to decide what matters more: the theology or the people. And I believe the answer has to be: the people.

The woman whose son called 911 is alive. She is healing. She left her husband. She found her voice. But she lost twelve years to a theology that told her to stay. She lost the ability to trust her own judgment. She carries trauma. Her son carries trauma. And the church—my church, my theology—is partly responsible.

That is not abstract. That is not theoretical. That is real. And every church that holds complementarian theology while being silent about abuse is participating in that reality.

The hope is that churches will take abuse seriously enough to change. The hope is that women will know they are safe. The hope is that the church will be the first place that gender equality is actually lived. Not preached. Lived. Practiced. Modeled. Protected.

The church's instinct to handle abuse internally is the same instinct that protected predators for decades in Catholic dioceses. Institutional self-protection always looks like pastoral care from the inside.

I write that sentence knowing it will cost me. Pastors in my network will see it as an attack on pastoral authority. Denominational leaders will see it as an embrace of external judgment. Church members will worry that I am saying their church cannot be trusted to do the right thing. But the sentence is true, and the cost of naming it is smaller than the cost of remaining silent.

The internal handling model works like this: a woman comes forward about abuse. She tells a pastor, a deacon, someone in the pastoral team. The church tells her they will pray about it. They will work to help reconciliation. They will talk to the accused. They will seek wisdom. They will keep it quiet. The reasoning is theological: the body of Christ should heal its own wounds. Going to the authorities would be bringing the church into disrepute. The family's privacy should be protected. The church has the authority to deal with internal matters.

Every single part of that structure prioritizes institutional protection over victim safety. Not intentionally. The pastors involved usually believe they are being pastoral. But that is what happens. The victim is asked to keep quiet while the church "handles it." The accused is often given a chance to stay. If he leaves the church, he leaves quietly, goes to another congregation, starts over, and eventually has access to new victims.

I have watched this happen. Not in detail. Not the worst versions. But I have watched the smaller versions. I have watched good men—men I respected—be confronted with credible reports of their abuse, and the church's response was not to report, not to remove, but to counsel and accommodate. To give the man grace while the victim is told that bringing in the authorities would destroy a family, would split a church, would damage Christ's reputation.

That is evil clothed in pastoral language. And I have participated in it, in lesser forms, through my inaction. I have not seen a clear case of abuse, but I have seen inappropriate behavior, boundary violations, men in authority who used their position for power over women, and I told myself it was not my place to escalate, not my role to bring in external judgment.

I was wrong. My inaction was action. My silence was a form of consent.

Here is what needs to happen instead. Any credible report of abuse—sexual, physical, emotional, spiritual—must be reported to law enforcement. Not investigated internally first. Not prayed over before reporting. Reported. Immediately. The church is not a law enforcement agency. The church does not have the training, the accountability, the expertise to determine if a crime has been committed. The church's job is to protect the vulnerable and the innocent. That job requires reporting.

I say that knowing what it costs. A pastor's first instinct is to protect his church. To protect his reputation. To protect the reputation of his members. To keep the body intact. But integrity requires that you protect the innocent over the reputation of the institution. And reporting abuse damages the institution's reputation only because the institution has been complicit in abuse.

If you report when you should have reported—when there is a credible allegation—the damage to the institution is deserved. The church has not been hurt by the report. The church has been hurt by the abuse and by the cover-up. The report is not the wound. The report is the exposure of the wound. And exposure, though painful, is the only way to heal.

But that is only the beginning. Reporting to law enforcement is not the same as actually protecting people. You have to have systems in place before abuse ever happens. You have to train every person in leadership—not in one workshop, in ongoing training—on how to recognize signs of abuse. You have to know what grooming looks like. You have to know what boundary violations look like. You have to be able to identify the moment someone starts exerting inappropriate power.

Most churches do not have this training. Most pastors would not recognize grooming if they saw it. Most leadership teams would rationalize boundary violations as not that serious. That is what I would have done. That is what I did do, in smaller instances, before I had to confront the reality of what those smaller violations become if they are not stopped.

I have implemented policies at FBC Fenton that I wish I had implemented ten years ago. Background checks for anyone who will be working with children or vulnerable populations. Not optional. Required. Training for all children's ministry workers on appropriate boundaries, on how to report concerns, on what the church will do if there is an allegation. Clear consequences for boundary violations—and I mean actual consequences, not another chance, not moving the person to a different ministry where they are away from children but still in the church. Out. If you violate the boundaries we have set, if you use your position in the church for inappropriate power over someone vulnerable, you will be removed.

I have created a process where any concern can be reported confidentially, and that concern will be taken seriously. Not investigated internally. Reported to the authorities. The church will support both the person making the report and the process of justice, even if that process is painful for the institution.

I wish I had done this sooner. I wish I had not needed external pressure to create these policies. I wish I had cared enough about prevention to implement them preemptively. I did not. And there is no way to undo that. There is only the commitment to be different going forward.

But I also know that policies are not enough. Policies are a floor, not a ceiling. A church with good policies can still fail to protect people if the culture does not support it. If pastors do not actually believe that a woman's report of abuse is more important than protecting a man's reputation. If church members do not actually believe that bringing in law enforcement is the right thing to do. If the institution still prioritizes itself over the victim.

That is cultural work. It is slow. It is the work of pastors preaching about it. Of church leadership talking about it in meetings. Of making it clear, over and over, that if you choose to stay in this church, you are choosing to be part of a community that will protect the vulnerable and will hold the powerful accountable, even when that is costly.

I have done some of that work. I have preached about abuse. I have stood with victims. I have removed men from positions of authority. But I have done it late. And I have done it incompletely. There are systems at FBC Fenton that are still not what they need to be. There are areas where we still prioritize the comfort of the church over the safety of the vulnerable.

What I can do, now, is name this: that is the work that matters. That is the theological priority that needs to override all other institutional concerns. If you are a pastor and you have not implemented clear reporting policies, you are failing in your basic pastoral duty. If your church has not trained leaders on recognizing abuse, you are creating an environment where abuse can flourish. If you have been complicit in keeping abuse quiet, you need to repent of that and change course.

The cost of that change is real. You might lose members who believe the church should handle things internally. You might face legal liability as you acknowledge past failures. You might have to sit with the pain of knowing that you moved too slowly. But the alternative is being the kind of pastor who, when a woman comes forward, protects the institution instead of her. And I cannot be that man anymore.

**CHAPTER 11**

Raising Daughters in a Confused World

Christian Daughters, Strong Girls, Voice, Leadership, and Confidence Without Apology

I am a father of five sons. Not daughters.

This matters because it means I am writing this chapter from the outside of an experience I do not have. I have never raised a Christian daughter in a church that has no idea what to do with girls who are strong. I have never watched a daughter internalize the message that her strength and voice are dangerous. I have never had to counsel my daughter to make herself smaller so that men would be comfortable.

But I am a pastor. I have counseled families raising girls. I have baptized them. I have performed weddings for women I watched grow up in our congregation. I have sat with mothers who are terrified about the messages their daughters are hearing — from the culture, from other churches, sometimes from their own church.

And I know what it means to raise boys without a father. That shapes what I need to say here.

The crisis for girls in the American church is not primarily theological. It is that we have no idea what we are doing with them. We want them to be women of virtue, which is good. But we are not sure what virtue means for a girl who is smart and strong. We are not sure if she should lead or defer. We are not sure if her power is a gift or a threat.

So we hedge. We teach girls to be "modest" in ways that make them responsible for boys' temptation. We teach them to be "submissive" in ways that undermine their confidence in their own judgment. We encourage them to develop their gifts while simultaneously making it clear that those gifts must always be secondary to their eventual role as wife and mother. We tell them to be strong but not too strong. Bold but not bossy. Ambitious but not threatening.

Is it any wonder that girls internalize confusion?

The fundamental problem is this: the church has not reckoned with the reality that girls are human beings with the same imago Dei as boys. Not supplementary humans. Not partial humans. Not humans whose primary value comes from their relationships to men. Humans. Full and complete, worthy of the same respect, the same voice, the same agency.

When we teach a girl that her identity is rooted in her appearance, we have already lost her. We have told her that what matters about her is not her mind or her character or her strength, but how she looks to others. In a world that measures female value in beauty and sex appeal, the church should be the counter-cultural voice saying: that is a lie. Your worth is not tied to your appearance. You are not the sum of your body. Your value is intrinsic, rooted in being made in God's image.

Instead, the church often reinforces the cultural message. We teach "modesty" in ways that focus on girls' bodies as objects that boys must not be tempted by. We teach purity culture in ways that measure a girl's worth by her virginity. We create prom dress codes that are stricter for girls than for boys. We send the message — loud and clear — that girls' bodies are the problem.

Then we act shocked when girls develop eating disorders. When they internalize shame about their sexuality. When they become disconnected from their bodies as sources of strength and instead experience them as liabilities.

The church needs to say something different. You are not an object. You are not responsible for managing boys' attraction. Your body is not the problem. Your body is yours. It is strong. It is capable. It is worthy of respect. And your value has nothing to do with how you look in a dress.

Then there is the question of strength itself. We tell girls to be strong, but we panic when they exercise that strength in ways that challenge traditional gender roles. We tell them they can do anything, but when a girl shows ambition or leadership, we find ways to make her fit the shape we have decided she should be.

I have watched this in our congregation. A high school girl with clear leadership gifts asked to lead a small group. The response was that she should wait until she had more life experience — even as boys her age were leading without question. A woman in her twenties showed intellectual capability and wanted to teach a Sunday school class. The feedback was that perhaps she should start as an assistant, supporting a man's teaching.

The message is consistent: your strength is acceptable only as long as it does not challenge the authority structure. Your gifts are valuable only as long as they remain in service to someone else's vision. Your voice is fine as long as you are not actually leading.

This is not raising daughters. This is raising daughters to doubt themselves.

The alternative is subversive. It is telling a girl: your strength is not a problem. It is a gift. If you are intelligent, use that intelligence. If you are bold, be bold. If you are called to lead, lead. If you are talented, develop your talent. You do not need to shrink to make room for others. You do not need to apologize for being capable. You do not need to wait for a man to give you permission to be who God has made you to be.

This will frighten some men. Good. If a man's sense of manhood is threatened by a strong woman, that says something about his sense of manhood, not about her strength.

There is also the question of voice. Girls are taught early that being heard is not as important as being nice. That challenging an idea is the same as being disrespectful. That speaking your mind is unfeminine. We tell girls to be confident, but we reward them for being compliant. We tell them their opinion matters, but we teach them to wait for someone else to speak first.

The church has historically been one of the institutions that reinforces this silencing of girls' voices. Scripture is interpreted to justify women's silence. Authority is structured to exclude women's leadership. Even in spaces where women have the most presence and participation — children's ministry, women's groups, worship teams — the decision-making power rests with men.

What we are teaching girls through this structure is that their thinking does not matter as much as the thinking of men. That their ideas, even if good, are less authoritative than men's ideas. That the primary value of their intelligence is in how it serves others' leadership.

Then we act surprised when women leave the church, when they go to college and discover other women doing things women in the church are not allowed to do, when they become alienated from a community that spent their entire childhood teaching them their voice did not count as much.

What girls need from the church is permission. Permission to be strong without apology. Permission to be intelligent without having to hide it or pretend deference. Permission to have ambitions that are their own, not derivative of a future husband's calling. Permission to speak, to question, to lead, to fail and get back up and try again.

They need to see women in positions of actual authority and decision-making. Not assistant positions. Not supportive roles. Not spaces where women lead but in the end answer to a man. Real authority. Real leadership. Real voice.

And they need fathers — or the men in their lives — to model respect for women.

This is where my sons come in. I have five of them, and they are growing up in a congregation where some women teach them, where some women make decisions about the ministry they are in. I am trying to teach them something that the broader culture is not: that women's equality is not a loss for them. It is a gain.

When a man lives in relationship with women who are his intellectual equals, his moral equals, his equals in every meaningful way, his life is richer. His thinking is sharper. His vision is broader. His leadership is better.

But this only happens if he respects women. And respect is not something you acquire by listening to sermons. You acquire it by watching the men in your life model it. You acquire it by seeing your father listen to your mother. By seeing him defer to her judgment. By seeing him acknowledge when she is right and he is wrong. By watching him treat the women in his church with the same respect he gives to the men.

I have failed at this in ways I cannot undo. I have spoken about women in pulpits in ways that diminished them. I have taught theology that reduced them. I have benefited from systems that silenced their voices. I have a lot of ground to make up with my own sons.

But I can start. I can tell them clearly: the way you treat women is the clearest test of your theology. If you believe women are made in God's image, then every woman you encounter is sacred. Not because she is someone's daughter or future wife. Because she is a human being made in the image of God.

Girls deserve to grow up in a church where that is not a controversial statement. Where a girl can be strong and be celebrated. Where she can ask questions and be taken seriously. Where she can have ambitions and not be pathologized for them. Where her voice counts and her thinking matters and her leadership is not contingent on a man's permission.

This requires change from the church. It requires acknowledging that we have gotten this wrong. That we have silenced girls who needed to be heard. That we have taught them to doubt themselves in ways we never taught boys.

And it requires men — fathers, pastors, leaders — to be willing to have less power so that girls can have more agency. Not token power. Not carefully limited power that makes male leaders comfortable. Real power.

This is not extreme theology. It is basic Christianity. It is taking seriously the idea that women are made in God's image. Actually taking it seriously, not just saying it while structuring everything else to undermine it.

Girls are watching. They are listening. They are learning what the church believes about them from how the church treats them. If we want them to believe they matter, we need to show them.

WHAT GIRLS NEED: PERMISSION, VOICES, AND WOMEN WHO ARE MORE THAN HELPERS

I have five sons. I have watched them grow. And I have also watched them grow up in relationship with women who lead them—teachers, pastors, directors. I have seen what happens when a boy has to listen to a woman in authority. He has to reckon with the reality that women are his intellectual equals, his moral equals, his equals in every meaningful way.

But more importantly, I have watched what happened when a girl in our church showed strength and the response was not celebration but management. The response was: we need to help her understand that her strength should be channeled in ways that support men's leadership.

That is what we do to girls. We do not celebrate their strength. We manage it. We tell them their power is welcome as long as it does not threaten the existing power structure.

I want to talk about what girls actually need. Not what we think they need. Not what our theology tells us they should need. What they actually need.

First, they need to see women in positions of real authority. Not assistant positions. Not supportive roles where a woman leads but in the end reports to a man. Real authority. Decision-making authority. Prophetic authority. Teaching authority. Pastoral authority. Authority that is not contingent on a man's permission.

In our church, we have women teaching Sunday school to children and to adults. We have women leading worship. We have women counseling. But the final authority—the positions that make actual decisions about the direction and priorities of the congregation—those positions are held by men.

What does a girl learn from that? She learns that women can do important work as long as that work supports men's leadership. She learns that women's voice matters as long as it does not challenge male authority. She learns that there is a glass ceiling—not explicit, not dramatic, just present—and it is there for her.

I want her to learn something different. I want her to see a woman stand up and preach the Word. I want her to see a woman sit on the board and make decisions about the church's budget and direction. I want her to see a woman voted in as a pastor, as an elder, as someone with real authority.

I have heard the theological objections. I know them well because I used to believe them. Paul says women should not teach men. Paul says women should not have authority over men. But Paul also says there is neither male nor female in Christ. Paul also sends Priscilla and Aquila out as missionaries—and Priscilla's name comes first, which in the ancient world indicated the more prominent person. Paul also greets Junia as "outstanding among the apostles." The fuller picture of Scripture does not support a blanket prohibition against women leading.

But even if it did, I would still say: what is the cost of that theology to girls? The cost is that they grow up believing that their gifts have a ceiling. That their authority is contingent. That they cannot be fully trusted to lead.

Second, girls need permission to be angry. The church has taught girls that niceness is a virtue, that being agreeable is the path to acceptance, that strong emotions should be managed and controlled. Especially anger. Girls are taught that their anger is unattractive, unfeminine, a sign that they are being difficult.

But anger is not a sin. Anger at injustice is holy. Anger at being treated as less-than is appropriate. Anger at abuse is necessary.

We teach girls to suppress their anger, and then we act surprised when they internalize injustice. When a girl learns that her anger is not welcome, she learns that her sense of justice does not matter. She learns that other people's comfort with her is more important than her own sense that something is wrong. She learns to override her own instincts for the sake of peace.

Jesus was angry. He overturned tables in the temple. He called the Pharisees hypocrites and whitewashed sepulchers. He was not nice. He was righteous. And His anger was directed at injustice.

Girls need to learn that their anger can be righteous. That their sense that something is wrong can be trusted. That they do not have to smile and be agreeable when they are being treated unfairly. That their voice in naming injustice matters.

Third, girls need their intelligence celebrated. Not just in the abstract. Not just in contexts where their intelligence supports male leadership. But celebrated as a good in itself.

We have a tendency to channel girls' intelligence toward service. The smart girl becomes the one who helps the boy with his homework. The intelligent girl becomes the assistant to the male leader. The bright girl becomes the one whose thinking supports someone else's vision.

But what if we told her: your thinking matters. Your ideas have value. Your insights are important. You do not need a man to validate your intelligence. You do not need to put your thinking in service to someone else's goal. You can set the goals. You can lead the thinking. Your intellect is not a tool for someone else's ambition. It is your own power.

I sat in on a high school Bible study where a girl asked a sharp theological question. It was a good question. It showed real thinking. And the young man leading the study—a good young man, well-meaning—answered her and then immediately said something like, "I really admire how you think, \[name\]," in a tone that suggested: women do not usually ask smart theological questions. The compliment was a subtle way of saying: you are exceptional for a girl.

I pulled him aside afterward and we talked about it. But the damage was already done. The girl had heard: your intelligence is noteworthy because it is unexpected for your gender. And she will carry that message forward. She will assume that her thinking is less valuable than a man's.

What girls need is for their intelligence to be celebrated without surprise. To be told: you are smart, therefore you can lead. You can teach. You can make decisions. You can trust your judgment.

Fourth, girls need women leaders who are fully human. Not sanctified women who have transcended their struggles. Not women who have sacrificed themselves on the altar of service. But women who are intelligent, powerful, sometimes difficult, sometimes ambitious, sometimes wrong, sometimes right. Women who are complete people.

The church tends to celebrate women who are in supporting roles. The pastor's wife who is a helpmeet. The missionary's wife who sacrificed her career. The woman who gave up everything for her children. We celebrate women when they are in service to something larger than themselves.

But what does this teach girls? It teaches them that their value comes from what they do for others. It teaches them that being strong is only acceptable if they are strong in service to someone else's vision. It teaches them that ambition for themselves is selfish, but ambition for others' goals is holy.

I want girls to see women who have ambition for their own goals. Women who have built something for themselves. Women who have chosen their own path. Women who are not defined by what they have given up but by what they have created.

Fifth, girls need men who respect women. And this is on us. I say "us" because I am one of the men who shaped how girls understand men. If a girl grows up seeing her father respect her mother—really respect her, defer to her judgment, acknowledge when she is right and he is wrong—then she will expect that from men. She will not tolerate disrespect because she has seen that respect is possible.

But if a girl grows up seeing men who listen to women only when it suits them, who dismiss women's concerns, who explain things to women as if they cannot understand, who take credit for women's ideas, who speak over women in meetings—then she will internalize a narrative that this is normal. This is how men treat women. And she will accept less than she deserves.

I have failed at this with my sons. I have modeled some things I am not proud of. I have not always listened to Susanna the way I should have. I have made decisions that affected her without fully seeking her input. I have been the man in the room with something to say, and I have not always deferred to the women's voices.

But I am learning. And I am trying to model something different for my boys. I am trying to show them what it looks like for a man to actually respect a woman. To listen when she speaks. To value her judgment. To acknowledge when she is right and he is wrong. To not use his position to override her voice.

If every man did this—if every father modeled respect for the women in his life, if every pastor modeled respect for women in his congregation, if every leader modeled respect for women on his team—then girls would grow up knowing that they deserve respect. That their voice matters. That their judgment is as valuable as any man's.

Finally, girls need the church to stop organizing around their beauty and their sexuality, and start organizing around their humanity. The purity culture did damage that runs deep. But it is not just the explicit purity culture. It is the constant focus on whether girls look right, whether they are appropriately dressed, whether they are attractive in the right ways or unattractive in the wrong ways.

The message is: your body is the most important thing about you. We are not sure if we think your body is good or bad, but it is definitely the most important thing. So manage it. Control it. Present it in ways that make us comfortable.

What if the church organized around something different? What if the message was: you are a mind. You are a voice. You are a person. Your body is yours and has nothing to do with your worth. Your appearance is not our concern. Your intelligence is. Your character is. Your calling is.

What would change if girls grew up believing that?

The cost of all of this is significant. It costs the comfort of familiar structures. It costs the sense that men understand their role in the world and women understand theirs. It costs the false security of knowing everyone is in their place. It costs men the unquestioned authority they have always had.

But the hope is worth it. The hope is that girls will grow up knowing they are full human beings. That they can be strong and be celebrated. That they can ask hard questions and be taken seriously. That they can have ambitions and not be pathologized. That their voice counts. That their thinking matters. That their leadership is not contingent on a man's permission. That they are made in the image of God, and that image is not diminished by their gender.

This is what daughters deserve. This is what the church owes them. Not management of their strength, but celebration of it. Not guidance of their ambitions, but support of them. Not permission, but freedom.

**CHAPTER 12**

The Woman God Is Building

Galatians 3:28, Equality in Christ, and the Church's Vision for Women

> *"There is neither Jew nor Gentile, neither slave nor free, nor is there male and female, for you are all one in Christ Jesus."*

Paul wrote this to the Galatians—Galatians 3:28. He was addressing a church being pressed by false teachers to accept the requirements of Jewish law, including circumcision. The church was tempted to create a hierarchy of Christian identity: those who followed the Law more fully were more Christian. Those outside Jewish tradition were less fully Christian.

Paul rejected this categorically. He used three categories that represented the deepest divisions in the ancient world: ethnicity (Jew vs. Gentile), class (slave vs. free), and gender (male and female). And he made a sweeping claim about gender equality in Christ. In the culture surrounding the Galatian church, all three created rigid hierarchies. A free person had rights a slave did not have. A Jew had a different status than a Gentile. A man had legal, social, and spiritual authority a woman did not have.

And Paul said: that is over. In Christ, those divisions are obliterated. They do not disappear in some spiritual, metaphorical way. They become functionally irrelevant to your identity and status in the community of faith.

This is not Paul saying that gender ceases to exist. It is Paul saying that gender ceases to be a basis for hierarchy, for limited access, for reduced authority or voice.

But Christianity has spent two thousand years trying to find ways to make Galatians 3:28 not mean what it says.

The logic has been creative. Some traditions have claimed the verse applies only to spiritual status, not to practical matters like authority or teaching. Some have reframed submission as equality. Some have created elaborate theological structures to maintain male authority while claiming to honor women. Some have simply ignored the verse and continued operating as if the categories Paul said were obliterated still matter.

What we have not done is the harder thing: actually structure the church according to what Paul claimed had already happened in Christ.

The eschatological vision here is subversive. The new creation has already begun. The future that God is building — where injustice is overturned, where the last are first, where the meek inherit the earth — that future is breaking into the present through the church. The church is not a waiting room. It is not a holding pattern until Jesus comes back and fixes everything. The church is where the future is already becoming real.

This means the church should look like a place where the old orders are coming undone. Where the hierarchies that the fallen world takes for granted are being dismantled. Where women, who have been excluded from authority throughout history, are now full participants in the leadership and mission of God's people.

This is not a liberal political value that we are importing into Scripture. This is what the text claims has already happened.

But implementing this requires something most churches are not willing to do: it requires admitting that we have been wrong. For decades, for centuries, the church has structured itself in ways that contradict what we claim to believe about who women are in Christ.

We have preached that women are equal while organizing church government to exclude them from final decision-making. We have taught that women are full members of the body of Christ while creating complicated theological frameworks for why they cannot teach certain things or lead certain people. We have claimed to believe Galatians 3:28 while functioning as if it said something it does not say.

The cognitive dissonance is enormous. And rather than resolve it by actually making women equal, we have simply lived with the contradiction. We have told ourselves stories that make the contradiction feel consistent.

The woman God is building is not a complementarian ideal. She is not a woman who submits to male headship but does so with "equal value," whatever that means. She is not a woman with a "different but equal" role that happens to have no authority. She is not a woman who is taught that her primary calling is motherhood and wifedom, with work and leadership as secondary expressions of her gifts.

The woman God is building is a person being conformed to the image of Christ. That is her primary identity. Not her gender. Not her marital status. Not her reproductive capacity. Her becoming more like Christ.

What does that person look like? She is intelligent and encouraged to develop her mind. She is strong and taught that her strength is not threatening but necessary. She is capable of leadership and given opportunities to lead. She is listened to in decision-making spaces. She is believed when she speaks about her own experience and her own needs.

She may be married or single. She may be a mother or childless. She may work outside the home or within it or both. None of those things define her or limit her. They are expressions of her agency and calling, not boundaries placed upon her.

The church that is becoming what God is building is a church where women preach. Where women teach theology to men. Where women sit on boards that make decisions. Where women do not have to apologize for having ambitions or using their gifts. Where a woman's voice in a meeting carries the same weight as a man's voice.

This is not extremism. This is basic Christianity. This is what it means to take seriously that in Christ, the hierarchy based on gender has been undone.

The hardest part of this shift, for many churches, is that it requires men to have less power. Not men as individuals — some men will discover that actually, they were invested in power structures in ways that limited them too. But men as a group, in systems we have built and benefited from, will have to give up privilege.

This feels like loss. But it is not loss. It is gain. A church where women are fully heard, fully valued, fully empowered is not a church where men suffer. It is a church where the whole body is stronger.

The woman God is building knows her own mind. She knows her own worth. She does not need a man's validation to trust her own judgment. She does not interpret rejection of her ideas as confirmation that she should have kept quiet. She does not shrink when she is right and a man is wrong.

This terrifies some churches. They fear that if women have real authority, everything will fall apart. That women will take over. That men will be emasculated.

But this is the voice of fear, not faith. If you believe God created both male and female in His image, if you believe the Spirit distributes gifts according to His will and not according to gender, if you believe that leadership should go to those most qualified and called rather than those born with a certain anatomy — then you cannot believe that giving women authority will bring chaos. You can only believe it will bring better leadership.

The church is already experiencing the consequences of excluding women's gifts and voices from leadership. We are leading with less wisdom, less discernment, less of the image of God. We are excluding half of what God is offering us.

The eschatological reality Paul is naming in Galatians 3:28 is this: God is building something new. The old orders are passing away. In the Kingdom that is coming, there is no male and female hierarchy. There is only the body of Christ, unified, whole, using all of the gifts God has given it.

The church's job is not to wait for that future. The church's job is to embody it now. To live as if the future is already breaking in. To structure ourselves according to the reality that gender hierarchies have no place in the people of God.

This will cost something. It will cost the security that comes from familiar structures. It will cost some men the unquestioned authority they have enjoyed. It will cost the church some members who are invested in the old order.

But it will gain something more valuable. It will gain the integrity of actually believing what we claim to believe. It will gain the strength that comes from using all of the gifts God has distributed. It will gain the trust of women who have been excluded, when they see that the church is finally serious about their equality.

The woman God is building is not waiting for permission. She is already emerging in churches that are brave enough to let her. She is preaching, teaching, leading, prophesying, pastoring, building, creating. She is using her gifts without apology. And the churches that are supporting her are experiencing resurrection.

That is what the future looks like. Not some distant, theoretical future. The future that is already happening in churches that have decided to take Galatians 3:28 seriously.

The question facing every church is whether we will move toward that future or cling to the dying order. Whether we will trust that God knows what He is doing when He gifts and calls women to lead. Whether we will have the courage to dismantle the hierarchies we have built and accepted.

God is building something. A renewed creation. A redeemed people. A church that looks like the image of Christ. In that Kingdom, women are not second-class. They are not limited. They are not constrained by gender to a particular sphere. They are free. They are heard. They are leading.

That is not something we need to create. That is something we need to align ourselves with. To let the future reshape us rather than trying to preserve the past.

THE ALREADY/NOT-YET KINGDOM: WHAT THE CHURCH SHOULD LOOK LIKE NOW

There is a fundamental misunderstanding in how the church relates to the future kingdom of God. We treat it like a promise. Something that will happen one day, so we do not need to worry about it now. We will be equal in heaven. For now, let's maintain the structures that work.

But the New Testament does not present the kingdom of God as something that is only coming. It presents it as something that is already here, breaking in, transforming the world, calling us to align ourselves with what God is already doing.

Jesus taught us to pray: "Your kingdom come, your will be done, on earth as it is in heaven." Not in heaven. On earth. Now. The kingdom that is fully realized in heaven is supposed to be breaking into and reshaping the earth right now. And the church is supposed to be the preview of that kingdom. The place where the future is already becoming real.

If that is true—and I believe Scripture demands that we take it seriously—then the church should be the first place where the hierarchies that the fallen world takes for granted are being dismantled. The first place where women are not second-class. The first place where gender hierarchies have no power. The first place where the old orders are passing away.

But we have made the church a place where we preserve the old orders while claiming to believe in the new kingdom. We teach that women are equal in Christ while structuring everything so that men hold most of the power. We quote Galatians 3:28 in our baptism liturgies while functioning as if that verse said something it does not say. We claim to believe that in Christ there is neither male nor female, and then we create complicated theological frameworks for why women still cannot lead.

The cognitive dissonance is enormous. And rather than resolve it by actually making women equal, we have lived with the contradiction. We have told ourselves stories that make the contradiction feel consistent. We have said: women are equal but different. Women are equal in value but different in role. Women are equal but not in positions of authority.

What we have not done is the harder thing: actually restructure ourselves according to what we claim to believe.

I want to be clear about what the already/not-yet kingdom means for women in the church.

It means that the church should be the place where a woman's voice carries the same weight as a man's voice. Not eventually. Now. In the meetings where decisions are made. In the pulpits where teaching happens. In the positions where authority is held. Her voice should count.

It means that the church should be the place where a woman does not have to apologize for being smart, or ambitious, or powerful, or right. These things are celebrated in the kingdom of God. Therefore, they should be celebrated now in the church.

It means that the church should be the place where a woman does not have to justify her calling. Whether her calling is to be a mother, or a teacher, or a pastor, or a businesswoman, or all of these things, the church should affirm it without qualification.

It means that the church should be the place where a woman does not have to perform humility or defer to men to be acceptable. She can be confident in her gifts because those gifts are given by God. She can advocate for herself. She can lead. She can challenge ideas. She can take up space.

It means that the church should be the place where a woman is safe. Where her voice will be heard and believed. Where abuse is not tolerated. Where her safety takes priority over the reputation of the powerful. Where she knows that if she is harmed, the community will stand with her.

All of these things are true about what the kingdom of God will look like. All of them. If we are to take seriously the idea that the church is the preview of that kingdom, then all of these things should be true about the church now.

This will require change. Real change. Not token change. Not gestures toward equality while maintaining male authority. Real structural change.

For many churches, this means giving women real authority. Voting women into positions of leadership. Having women preach from the pulpit as pastors, not just guest speakers. Having women sit on boards that make decisions. Having women teach theology to men. Having women prophesy. Having women lead.

For some churches, especially those in complementarian traditions, this will require changing theology. It will require acknowledging that the theological frameworks that have limited women's authority are not as biblically grounded as we thought. It will require humility about the fact that we got this wrong. It will require repentance.

The hardest part of this for many churches will be that it requires men to have less unquestioned power. Not that men will suffer. Not that men's calling or gifts are diminished. But the structures that have given men authority because they are men will have to be dismantled. And for men who have benefited from those structures, this will feel like loss.

But it is not loss. It is gain. A man who works alongside a woman as an equal, who listens to her voice as an authority, who defers to her judgment when she is right, who supports her leadership—that man is not diminished. He is strengthened. He is learning what it means to live in the kingdom of God.

The woman that the church is building is already emerging. I see her in churches that are brave enough to let her. She is preaching. She is teaching theology. She is sitting on boards. She is prophesying. She is pastoring. She is building. She is creating. She is using her gifts without apology.

And the churches that are supporting her are experiencing resurrection. They are not just surviving. They are alive. They are using all of the resources God has given them. They are not wasting the gifts of half their people. They are embodying what the kingdom of God looks like.

The question facing every church is whether we will move toward that future or cling to the dying order. Whether we will trust that God knows what He is doing when He gifts and calls women to lead. Whether we will have the courage to dismantle the hierarchies we have built and accepted.

The already/not-yet kingdom says: do not wait for perfection to begin moving toward justice. Begin now. Align yourselves now with what God is doing. Take seriously what you claim to believe about the equality of women in Christ, and structure yourselves accordingly.

It is not extreme theology. It is basic Christianity. But it will require the courage to change structures that have been in place for centuries. It will require men to be willing to have less power so that women can have more agency. It will require the humility to admit we got this wrong. It will require the faith to believe that God's kingdom is stronger than the kingdoms we have built.

That is what the hope looks like. Not some distant, theoretical hope. The hope that is already breaking in for the churches that have the courage to embody it now.

Galatians 3:28 reads: "There is neither Jew nor Gentile, neither slave nor free, nor is there male and female, for you are all one in Christ Jesus."

That is not a metaphor. That is not a spiritual reality that does not apply to actual church structures. That is a statement about who belongs and how they belong and what kind of community Christ's death created. And the church has spent two thousand years figuring out how to honor that verse while still maintaining structures that contradict it.

The standard move is to say: yes, spiritually we are equal, but practically we need order. Yes, in Christ there is no male and female, but God has ordained leadership structures. Yes, we are one body, but some members have different functions. All of it true. All of it designed to preserve a hierarchy that the text explicitly denies.

But what if we took Galatians 3:28 seriously? Not as a spiritual abstraction. As a practical reality. What would a church look like?

Women would teach in mixed settings. Not because the culture demanded it. Because the theological claim of the gospel demands it. If you are one in Christ, then the woman's voice belongs in the theological formation of the church. Her insights into Scripture matter. Her understanding of the gospel informs the whole body. You do not get that voice if women are teaching only women, only children, only in private settings. You need women teaching the whole congregation. You need women's voices shaping the understanding of Scripture for everyone.

I can envision what that looks like at FBC Fenton. A woman teaching from the pulpit. Not as a special event. Not as Women's Ministry week. As a regular part of the teaching calendar. A woman standing where I stand, interpreting Scripture, making the case for what the text demands, shaping the congregation's understanding of God. That is not a loss to the church. That is the recovery of what we have been missing.

There would be women on the elder board. Not as tokens. As equal voice in the governance of the church. When we make decisions about budget, about ministry priorities, about pastoral care, about church discipline, women would be in the room. Women would have vote. Women would help set the direction. The congregation would see women in places of authority and would understand, without anyone having to say it, that their voice matters.

I think about what that would mean in a church like mine. We would make different decisions about how we spend money if we had women's wisdom in the room. We would prioritize differently if women had authority. We would care about different things if women were not just advising the men who decide but actually deciding themselves. And I believe—I am convinced—that those different priorities would be closer to what the gospel actually demands.

There is a woman in our congregation who has more theological training than some of our elders. She has read the Bible more carefully. She understands Greek. She thinks about Scripture seriously. She could lead this church. She could shape its theology. She could stand in the pulpit and the congregation would be better for it. Instead, we have her in women's ministry, which is valuable work, but it is not the place where she belongs.

That is our loss. Not hers. Our loss. We are making decisions as if the voice that would most sharpen our thinking is not available to us. We are organizing the church as if the person who could best help us understand Scripture is not in the room. We are acting as if God did not give her gifts of leadership and theological insight that belong in places of authority.

What would change if we actually made space for her voice? Everything. Different decisions. Different priorities. Different way of understanding what the gospel demands of us. And that is the point. The gospel demands it. Galatians 3:28 demands it. Not because the culture is pushing. Because Christ's death created a community where that hierarchy is no longer supposed to exist.

It would change how we understand worship. Women would shape what we sing, how we pray, what we emphasize. The experience of worship would reflect the fullness of the congregation, not just the half that is typically in charge. There would be songs that women chose. Prayers that women wrote. Readings that centered women's experience of Scripture. The whole congregation would feel more known because all the people would be represented in what we actually do.

It would change how we care for the grieving. A woman who has lost a child needs to be ministered to by women who understand that loss. Yes, the pastor can care. But the care of women, the presence of women who have walked that road, is irreplaceable. And in a church where women are full participants in pastoral care, not just assistants to the pastor, that care is available in a different way.

It would change how we think about sexuality and gender. If women are teaching Scripture, if women are interpreting what the gospel says about the body, about desire, about sexuality, you get a different message than if men alone are speaking about these things. Women would teach what they actually know, from their actual experience, instead of what men imagine women's experience to be.

It would change how we think about justice. Women would bring concerns that would not otherwise be named. Women who have been marginalized by systems, by theology, by the church itself—their voices would shape how the church understands what justice actually means. The church would not be able to ignore injustice that affects the congregation's women because those women would be in the room where decisions get made.

I can envision a specific future for FBC Fenton. Ten years from now, I want us to have women teaching regularly from the pulpit. I want us to have at least one woman on the elder board. I want the theological leadership of the church to reflect the fullness of the congregation. I want a young woman growing up in this church to look around and see herself reflected in the places where authority lives.

That is not utopian. That is not unrealistic. That is what taking Galatians 3:28 seriously looks like. And it is what the church is called to be.

What I want most is for the young women we are raising to grow up knowing that the church believes they belong everywhere. That their gifts matter in every setting. That the gospel is not a message that requires them to make themselves smaller. That becoming a Christian woman does not mean learning to quiet their voice, limit their gifts, or accept a secondary role.

I want them to grow up in a church where women teach them. Where women pastor them. Where women make decisions about their lives alongside men. Where women's leadership is not a controversy but a baseline assumption. Where if someone asked, "Can women teach?" the answer would be obvious: "Of course. Why would we silence that voice?"

That is the church I want to build. That is the vision that drives me to keep working through this theology. That is what I want the book to make possible—a different way of reading Scripture. A different way of understanding what the gospel demands. A different kind of church.

Galatians 3:28 is not a nice sentiment. It is a revolutionary claim. It says that the gospel breaks down every barrier—ethnic, economic, gender. It says that in Christ, we have become one thing. Not that we are spiritually one while practically remaining separate. Actually one. Functionally one. The kind of oneness where you do not have to wonder if your voice belongs. You just belong. And that belonging shapes everything about what the community becomes.

That is the vision. That is what we are aiming at. And as I work to move FBC Fenton toward it, I am aware that I am asking the congregation to give up something—a certain kind of order, a certain kind of clarity about who leads. But what we get in return is something much more valuable: a community that actually looks like what the gospel promises. A church where women are not waiting for permission to use their gifts. A church where the fullness of the congregation is reflected in the fullness of what we do together. A church where Galatians 3:28 is not a verse we believe in our heads and contradict in our practice.

That is the church I want to leave behind. That is the legacy I want the women who grow up under my ministry to inherit.

What I Want My Sons to Know About Women

Teaching Boys to Respect Women, Honoring Female Leadership, and What Real Partnership Looks Like

Titus, Joseph, Samuel, Benjamin, and David.

I am writing this for you. Not for the larger audience that might read this book. For you.

I want you to know something clearly and directly: how you treat women—your respect for their voice, your honoring of female leadership, your view of partnership—is the clearest test of your theology. It is more important than what you believe about the Trinity. It is more important than your eschatology or your soteriology or any of the doctrines we discuss in Sunday school.

Because theology that does not change how you treat people is not theology. It is just words.

If you believe women are made in God's image, then every single woman you encounter is sacred. Not because she is pretty. Not because she is useful to you. Not because she is someone's daughter or wife or mother. Because she is a human being, stamped with the image of God, worthy of the same respect and dignity you demand for yourself.

This means something specific.

It means that when a woman speaks, you listen. Actually listen. Not listen while waiting for your turn to correct her. Not listen while evaluating whether her point is valid. Listen because what she is saying matters and she matters.

It means that when a woman is right, you say so. Clearly. Without hedging. Without minimizing it with "yeah, but." You do not get to protect your ego by finding ways to claim partial credit or subtle disagreement. You were wrong. She was right. That is what integrity looks like.

It means that when a woman sets a boundary, you respect it. Immediately. Without debate. Without explanation of why you think the boundary is unnecessary. She is the expert on what she needs. You are not. Respect what she tells you.

It means that you do not make decisions about her life without her voice. Not in a marriage, not in a church, not in any context where her life is being affected. You ask her. You listen to her answer. You take it seriously. This is not negotiable.

It means that you do not use your physical strength or authority to intimidate or control. Ever. If you have ever thought about using your size or your position to make a woman comply with what you want, that thought is poison. Root it out. Confess it. Change.

It means that you are honest about your mistakes. When you have gotten this wrong — and you will — you own it. You apologize. You make amends. You do not make excuses. You do not blame her for misinterpreting your intention. You take responsibility for the impact of your actions, regardless of your intention.

It means that you do not reduce women to their bodies or their sexuality. This is the hardest one in a world that does almost nothing but that. But you are called to something different. When you look at a woman, you see a person. Not an object. Not a temptation. A person with thoughts and dreams and ambitions and a right to be safe in her own skin.

Let me tell you something about your mother that you might not understand yet.

Your mother is the strongest person I have ever known. Not physically — though she is tougher than she looks. I mean that she has more integrity, more courage, more clarity about what matters than anyone else I know. She has disagreed with me in ways that have made me change my mind. She has pushed back on decisions I made that were wrong. She has done this not because she was trying to be difficult, but because she loves me and the family enough to risk my anger to protect what matters.

I have not always received that well. There have been times when I would have preferred that she simply agree with me. When her challenge to my thinking felt threatening instead of strengthening. I have not always listened the way I should have listened.

But our marriage got better the more I learned to actually hear her. The more I learned that her disagreement was not rejection of me. The more I learned that her strength was not a threat to mine. Our marriage became stronger when I stopped treating her as someone who should follow my lead and started treating her as someone I am leading alongside.

Your mother is also generous in ways I am not. She is more naturally merciful. She is more aware of other people's pain. She is more attuned to what people need and when they need it. These are not weaknesses. These are strengths that have shaped our family. I have learned more about what it means to follow Christ from watching her than from any theology book.

What I want you to know is this: in a woman who is strong and intelligent and wise, do not fear her. Do not try to diminish her so you feel bigger. Do not compete with her. Learn from her. Make space for her strength alongside yours. The two of you together will be more than either of you apart.

This is not about being weak or passive. It is not about abandoning your calling or your gifts. It is about understanding that partnership is stronger than dominance. That a woman who is fully herself, fully heard, fully empowered, makes you better.

I have gotten this wrong. I have preached things I now believe were distortions of Scripture. I have used theology to justify structures that reduced women and elevated men. I have stood in pulpits and spoken about women in ways that were less than full. I have benefited from systems that silenced women's voices and then convinced myself that silence was what God wanted.

I am telling you this not to make you feel bad about your heritage. But so you know: this is how it happens. Good men, trying to follow Scripture, build theological systems that are wrong. Not because we are evil. Because we are blind. Because we are embedded in a culture that has one set of assumptions, and we mistake those assumptions for Scripture.

You have a chance to see more clearly than I did. You have women in your life — teachers, pastors, leaders — who are using their gifts without apology. You are growing up in a moment when it is harder to hide the cost of excluding women. You can learn faster than I did what it means to actually believe women are made in God's image.

Do not waste that opportunity. Do not accept the easy theology that makes you comfortable. Do not build systems that give you power at the cost of silencing others. Do not convince yourself that what you want is the same as what Scripture says.

Instead, do this: when you are unsure, ask a woman. When you make a decision that affects women, listen to women's voices. When a woman tells you that something is hurting her, believe her. When a woman tells you she is called to something, support her calling. When a woman leads, follow. Not because you are trying to be politically correct. Because she is a human being, made in God's image, and she deserves the same respect you demand for yourself.

The hope of the church is not that men will finally figure out how to lead women well. The hope of the church is that men and women will lead together, fully, without hierarchy, without reserve. The hope is that we will stop wasting the gifts of half our people because we are afraid of what might happen if women had the same authority men have always had.

I believe you can be part of that hope. I believe you can build relationships and communities where women are not constrained by gender to a particular sphere. Where their voice carries weight. Where their leadership is celebrated. Where their strength is not threatening but necessary.

This is not extreme. This is basic Christianity. But it will require you to be willing to have less so that others can have more. It will require you to trust that God knows what He is doing when He gifts women and calls them to lead.

It will require you to love women — not as a category, but as people. To see them as fully human. To respect their judgment. To honor their calling. To make space for their strength.

This is my prayer for you. Not that you will be strong or successful or impressive. Those things do not matter. My prayer is that you will be men of integrity. That you will treat women the way you want to be treated. That you will see in every woman you encounter the image of God. That you will use whatever power and influence you have to make space for women's voices and gifts.

That is what matters. That is what will last.

The woman God is building does not need men to fix her or lead her or make her acceptable. She needs men who will see her fully, hear her clearly, and get out of her way while standing beside her.

Be that man. Be the presence for women that I did not know I needed to be. Be the voice that says: your strength does not threaten me, it strengthens me. Your voice matters. Your leadership is needed. Your place at the table is not something you have to earn or apologize for. It is yours.

This is the gospel. Not just for women. For all of us.

I love you.

Dad

I have not always received that well. There have been times when I would have preferred that she simply agree with me. When her challenge to my thinking felt threatening instead of strengthening. When her strength looked like competition instead of partnership. I have not always listened the way I should have listened.

But our marriage got better the more I learned to actually hear her. The more I learned that her disagreement was not rejection of me. The more I learned that her strength was not a threat to mine. Our marriage became stronger—deeper, richer, more true—when I stopped treating her as someone who should follow my lead and started treating her as someone I am leading alongside.

Your mother is also generous in ways I am not. She is more naturally merciful. She is more aware of other people's pain. She is more attuned to what people need and when they need it. These are not weaknesses. These are strengths. Strengths that have shaped our family. I have learned more about what it means to follow Christ from watching her than from any theology book or any sermon I have preached.

She has taught me that strength without mercy is tyranny. That authority without love is oppression. That leading means making the space for others' gifts, not controlling the space for your own security.

What I want you to know is this: in a woman who is strong and intelligent and wise, do not fear her. Do not try to diminish her so you feel bigger. Do not compete with her. Learn from her. Make space for her strength alongside yours. The two of you together will be more than either of you apart. A partnership is stronger than dominance.

This is not about being weak or passive. It is not about abandoning your calling or your gifts. It is about understanding that you do not have to be the only strong one in the room. You do not have to be the loudest voice. You do not have to make all the decisions. A woman who is fully herself, fully heard, fully empowered makes you better.

I have gotten this wrong. I need to be clear about that. I have preached things I now believe were distortions of Scripture. I have used theology to justify structures that reduced women and elevated men. I have stood in pulpits and spoken about women in ways that were less than full. I have benefited from systems that silenced women's voices and then convinced myself that silence was what God wanted.

I am telling you this not to make you feel bad about your heritage. Not to make you ashamed of being men. But so you know: this is how it happens. Good men, trying to follow Scripture, build theological systems that are wrong. Not because we are evil. Because we are blind. Because we are embedded in a culture that has one set of assumptions, and we mistake those assumptions for Scripture.

You have a chance to see more clearly than I did. You are growing up in a moment when it is harder to hide the cost of excluding women. You have women in your life—teachers, pastors, leaders, mentors—who are using their gifts without apology. You can learn faster than I did what it means to actually believe women are made in God's image.

Do not waste that opportunity. Do not accept the easy theology that makes you comfortable. Do not build systems that give you power at the cost of silencing others. Do not convince yourself that what you want is the same as what Scripture says. Do not mistake cultural assumptions for God's truth.

Instead, do this: when you are unsure, ask a woman. When you make a decision that affects women, listen to women's voices. When a woman tells you that something is hurting her, believe her. Do not explain why she might be wrong. Do not suggest that she is being emotional. Believe her.

When a woman tells you she is called to something—to preach, to lead, to build, to teach—support her calling. Do not make her earn it by proving she is capable. Do not hedge your support with suggestions that she should develop her skills before really stepping into it. Support her. Be the man who says: your calling is real and I am with you.

When a woman leads, follow. Not because you are trying to be politically correct. Because she is a human being made in God's image, and she deserves the same respect you demand for yourself. Because her gifts are gifts. Because her calling is real. Because the kingdom of God is stronger when women lead.

The hope of the church is not that men will finally figure out how to lead women well. The hope of the church is that men and women will lead together. Fully. Without hierarchy. Without reserve. Without the constant negotiation of who gets to speak and who has to defer. The hope is that we will stop wasting the gifts of half our people because we are afraid of what might happen if women had the same authority men have always had.

I believe you can be part of that hope. I believe you can build relationships where women are not constrained by gender to a particular sphere. Where their voice carries weight. Where their leadership is celebrated. Where their strength is not threatening but necessary.

This is not extreme. This is basic Christianity. But it will require you to be willing to have less so that others can have more. It will require you to trust that God knows what He is doing when He gifts women and calls them to lead. It will require you to love women—not as a category, but as people. To see them as fully human. To respect their judgment. To honor their calling. To make space for their strength.

This is my prayer for you. Not that you will be strong or successful or impressive. Those things do not matter. My prayer is that you will be men of integrity. That you will treat women the way you want to be treated. That you will see in every woman you encounter the image of God. That you will use whatever power and influence you have to make space for women's voices and gifts.

That is what matters. That is what will last.

The woman God is building does not need men to fix her or lead her or make her acceptable. She needs men who will see her fully, hear her clearly, and get out of her way while standing beside her.

Be that man. Be the presence for women that I did not know I needed to be. Be the voice that says: your strength does not threaten me, it strengthens me. Your voice matters. Your leadership is needed. Your place at the table is not something you have to earn or apologize for. It is yours.

This is the gospel. Not just for women. For all of us.
