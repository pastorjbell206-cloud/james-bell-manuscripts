**INTRODUCTION**

The Question Nobody Answered

I was a boy with no father in a house full of questions.

Not the theological kind. The real kind. The kind that shape how you see yourself and every man you will ever meet. What makes a man a man. What should a man want. What should a man do when he wants something he shouldn't. Where does a man find his worth. Why does my father not want to know me.

I was raised by a single mother who was fiercely smart and could fix anything that broke around the house. She worked, managed money, made decisions. She did not have answers to my questions about manhood because she had never been a man. She had only ever had to survive without one.

The culture offered me two options.

Option one was the man who dominates. The athlete. The guy who wins. The one who takes what he wants because he is strong enough. I found this man everywhere. He was in sports. He was in movies. He was in the pulpit on Sunday mornings, though he was dressed up and talking about Jesus. The message was the same: a man is measured by what he conquers. A man is defined by his output, his status, his ability to make other people do what he wants them to do.

I pursued this version with the fullness a teenage boy can bring to a wrong thing. I played football. I worked my body into something that could hit hard and move fast. I developed a will that refused to break. I learned that if you were strong enough and angry enough, people would not ask you about the hole inside you. They would get out of your way.

Option two was the man who defers. The one who is emotionally available and relationally present and has learned that his strength is actually the problem. He had been taught to apologize for his masculinity rather than understand it. He was kind by rebuke. His presence was an indictment. He had absorbed the culture's message that traditional manhood was a disease and he was the cure. He was soft in the way that makes other people comfortable, which is a different thing than being gentle.

I found this man in progressive spaces. He was presented as enlightened. He had rejected the aggression of the first option and replaced it with constant self-examination and emotional confession. He used therapy language like a religious rite. He was afraid of power the way other men were addicted to it. Both orientations were pathological.

The church tried to split the difference. It took the domination model and baptized it in Scripture. Men were the leaders and decision-makers. Men were the providers and protectors. All true. But the way it was preached made it sound like leadership was the same as control, protection was the same as paternalism, and provision meant your presence in the home was optional as long as you made money. I watched men use these verses to justify checking out of their families' emotional lives. I watched them use authority as an excuse for absence. I watched them confuse headship with domination and call it theology.

None of these options was right. All three were wrong. But I did not know that when I was young. I only knew something was broken.

I did not find an answer to my question in the culture. I did not find it in the church. I found it in a single moment when a Christian man—a stranger, really, someone who came to my school—answered the question I had been asking silently for years.

He did not answer it with a formula or a list or a sermon. He answered it by being present. He asked me actual questions. He listened like the answer mattered. He did not flinch when I told him I did not believe in God. He did not try to win an argument. He treated my disbelief like it might be honest instead of stupid.

That man showed me something I had never seen. A man could be strong and gentle at the same time. He could be certain about what he believed and humble about what he didn't know. He could lead without dominating. He could provide without abandoning his family's emotional life. He could be authority without being authoritarian.

He was the first picture I ever saw of biblical manhood.

That moment shaped the trajectory of my life. It led to faith. It led to seminary. It led to pastoring. And it led me to understand that what happened to me—a boy who had no father and found a man who became a presence in the void—was supposed to happen all the time in the church but almost never does.

I founded the Pastors Connection Network because I have spent my adult life trying to be for other men what that man was for me. Not because I am particularly good at it, but because I know what it costs when no one shows up in that role. I know what a boy's soul does when there is no man to show him what manhood looks like.

Now I am a father of five sons. I have watched my oldest boy become a teenager in a world that offers him the same broken options I had. The culture still says a man conquers or he defers. The church still says a man leads or he fails. The world is still broken on the question of what a man is for.

This book is written because that question still needs answering.

It is written for the man who suspects all the options are wrong but has no replacement. For the father raising sons in a world that cannot define manhood. For the woman trying to understand the man she married when she has been told that manhood itself is the problem. For the teenager who knows the cultural script is broken but has never heard an alternative. For the pastor trying to call men to something biblical when the culture has already written the script for what a man should be.

It is written from a specific place. Not from academic distance. From the room where I grew up without a father and spent decades in the hole that left. From the room where I watched a man be present and it changed everything. From the room where I am now raising sons and trying to give them what I did not have. From the room where I sit with pastors and men who are drowning in the confusion the culture and the church have created about what they are supposed to be.

This book does not offer a formula. It does not offer a program. It offers something harder and more essential. A return to what the Bible actually says about manhood when you strip away the cultural noise. An answer to the question: What is a man for.

The question that nobody answered for me. The question I am spending my life answering for others.

This is where we begin.

My mother raised five sons without a man in the room. That fact is incomplete. It was not absence alone that shaped us. It was her specific fierceness in the face of that absence.

She did not pretend to be what she was not. She did not try to teach us masculinity by performing it. What she did was fiercer than that. She worked in a factory. She came home exhausted. She made sure we had what we needed, and when we did not have it, she made sure we knew why and that it was not our fault. She taught us that manhood is not about the absence of need. It is about taking responsibility for what you can carry and not pretending to carry what will crush you.

I remember a moment, I was maybe nine. One of my brothers had done something wrong. Not catastrophic, but wrong. He was waiting for punishment the way we all waited for punishment in those days—the belt, the anger, the shame. Our mother made him sit at the table with her and explain what happened. Not once. Three times. Each time he had to look at her and say what he had done. By the third time, he was crying. By the end, he understood not what he had done wrong, but why it mattered. That is different. That is the kind of clarity a man needs.

She had no theology degree. She had no man to model. What she had was a refusal to let us become numb. In a way she never articulated, she was teaching us that masculinity is not invulnerability. It is the willingness to feel the weight of your choices and carry that weight forward into change. It is not hardness. It is honesty.

When I was sixteen, I tore my ACL in football. The dream that had organized every decision since I was twelve years old ended in a collision that lasted three seconds. The injury was real. The loss was real. But what I remember most is not the pain. It is the moment after surgery when I realized I did not know who I was without football.

A man's identity has to be located somewhere. For me, it had been located entirely in the sport—the position, the statistics, the belonging to a team, the narrative of being somebody because I could do something exceptional. When that was taken, I was empty. And the emptiness was terrifying.

My mother did not try to fix it. She did not say "you will play again" or "this will make you stronger" or any of the comfort language we use to try to make loss smaller. What she did was let me sit in the loss. And then she said something I have never forgotten: "You were always more than that game. Now you get to find out who."

She could not have known how much I would need that permission to fail, that understanding that a man is not the sum of his accomplishments. That message became the foundation of everything else. Years later, when faith came, it would be built on that same foundation: I am not who I am because of what I achieve. I am who I am because of who I am known by.

My atheism in high school was not rebellion. It was intellectual. I had questions the church could not answer. I did not doubt the existence of God. I doubted the existence of anything good. I had seen what men were capable of. I had seen the silence of institutions. I had built a worldview where the Christian narrative was not false. It was naive. That was worse.

The Christian who asked me about my doubt did not give me apologetics. He gave me the permission to ask the question without first proving I was not evil for asking it. He said, "You are right to be angry about those things. Most Christians I know are angry about them too. We are just angry in a different way." That sentence changed the architecture of everything. It suggested that faith was not the opposite of rigorous thinking. It was the completion of it.

When my first son was born, I realized I was terrified. Not of the baby. Of myself. Of being the man who leaves. Of becoming the story I knew. The decision to stay, to be present, to show up—that was not a virtue I discovered. It was a resistance against a narrative that was written before I was born. Every day I was a father was a day I was rewriting what a man could be.

My sons have taught me things I did not expect to learn. My oldest taught me that strength without tenderness is just brutality dressed up. He is a big man, and he uses his size to protect smaller people without ever making them feel small. That did not come from me telling him to be that way. It came from him watching what the gospel actually looks like when a strong person loves a weak person.

My second son taught me that a man can ask for help and not lose his manhood. He struggled in school in ways that required him to admit he could not do it alone. A lot of boys translate that admission into shame. He translated it into wisdom. He learned to ask, to listen, to adjust. That kind of man is rarer than we have admitted.

My third son taught me about grief. When our dog died, most of the family moved on in a few weeks. He did not. He wrote the dog letters. He drew pictures. He carried the weight of the loss like a man carries a child—not pretending it is not heavy, but walking forward anyway. I realized I had never seen a man mourn without hiding it. I was watching my son become a man who knows how to carry loss.

My fourth son taught me about conviction. He is the one who will not bend. Not out of pride, but out of principle. There are a thousand ways a man can compromise without anyone noticing. My son is the kind of man who notices. He holds the line when it costs him something. That is a kind of courage I had not modeled well.

My fifth son is still becoming. But he is teaching me that a man can be different from his brothers and not be less. That there is not one way to be a man. That diversity in how we carry ourselves and make decisions and love people is not weakness. It is richness.

Being a father did not teach me that I had figured out manhood. It taught me that I am still in the middle of figuring it out. The difference is that now there are young men watching. That changes everything.

I have five sons, and each one has taught me something different about what it means to be a man.

The oldest showed up in the world already knowing things nobody taught him. Leadership is not something you install into a firstborn son. It emerges from some deep place in him, wired in before we ever said a word about responsibility or example. By the time he was twelve, other kids were following his lead without his asking them to. He had a kind of natural authority that had nothing to do with aggression and everything to do with clarity. He knew where he was going, and people sensed it. My job was not to create that. My job was to shape it, point it, keep it from becoming arrogance. That took a different kind of parenting than what the youngest needed.

The middle ones came into the world as a unit, or so it seemed. They competed with each other in ways that the oldest never did, and they cooperated in ways that only siblings locked in the same age bracket understand. They were Genesis brothers—Cain and Abel dynamics, Jacob and Esau dynamics, Joseph and his brothers dynamics. They loved each other and they despised each other, sometimes in the same afternoon. Watching them taught me that rivalry is not a disease to be cured. It is part of how brothers become men. They were learning to stand their ground with each other, to fight without destroying the relationship, to want something the other had and still choose the other. That is training for every other relationship they will ever have.

Titus is fourteen, and he is asking questions that I wish someone had let me ask at his age. Not rebellious questions—genuine ones. What does it actually mean that God is good when the world has this much suffering? How do I know what I believe about sexuality in a world that is screaming a thousand different answers? Am I supposed to care what other people think of me or not care at all? If my faith is supposed to change me, why does it feel like I am the same person I was before I believed? These are not the questions that fit neatly into a youth group curriculum. They are the questions that matter. And I realized that the worst thing I could do was answer them too quickly. He does not need my answers. He needs to see his father sitting with hard questions without panic, without needing to resolve everything by Sunday.

The youngest is still forming. Still watching. Still absorbing everything. He has not yet decided who he is going to be, and he is watching all of us to figure it out. He is the one most likely to catch the inconsistencies between what I say and what I do. He is the one most likely to believe that whatever I do is the right way to be a man, for better or for worse.

And here is what I have learned that no parenting book told me: you cannot mass-produce men.

The contemporary church operates as if manhood is a standardized product. Same sermon to every man. Same expectations. Same three-step action plan. Same outcome. Produce more godly men. Get all the men to the conference. Make sure the men are serving in the church. But manhood is not manufactured this way. Manhood emerges in relationship with a father who sees you—not the version of you that fits the template, but the actual you.

The oldest needed me to be present but not hovering. He needed room to lead without constant affirmation, which would have insulted his intelligence. He needed me to challenge him, to point out where leadership was becoming tyranny, to show him that authority without mercy is just violence wearing a better suit.

The middle ones needed me to let them fight each other, to not resolve their conflicts by imposing peace, to let them discover that they could be angry at each other and still be brothers. They needed to learn that love is not the absence of conflict. Love is conflict that does not end the relationship.

Titus needs me to sit with his questions. Not to have all the answers. Not to shut down the questioning by citing Scripture. But to say: I have asked these too. I am still asking them. And faith is not the absence of questions. Faith is moving forward in the presence of questions.

The youngest needs to watch his father fail and repent. Needs to see that being a man is not perfection. It is admission. It is showing up the next day after you have gotten it wrong.

The father who gives the same speech to every son is not discipling anyone. He is broadcasting. He is treating his sons like they are an audience rather than persons. And the result is that his sons learn to perform for him rather than be known by him. They learn that being a man means fitting the category rather than becoming a person.

This is what the church has missed. Real male discipleship is not scalable. It cannot be done at a conference. It cannot be done through a curriculum. It can only be done in the presence of a man who sees you, who knows you are different from every other man he knows, who does not need you to be someone you are not, and who is willing to reveal his own becoming.

That is the kind of man I am trying to be. Not the man who has arrived. The man who is becoming. The man who is far enough along to help others see the path, but honest enough to say: I am still walking it. I get lost. I turn back. I pick the wrong direction sometimes. But I keep moving, and if you want to learn to move like that, come with me.

My five sons are teaching me that this is what their generation is hungry for. Not another man performing confidence he does not feel. Not another authority figure telling them what to do. One man telling them what happened to him when he did the thing, and another man telling them what is happening to him right now in his own becoming.

That is real male discipleship. And it starts not with a sermon. It starts with a father looking his son in the eye and saying: I see you. And you are going to be okay.

**CHAPTER 1**

The Crisis Nobody Names

There is a crisis of manhood in America. Both sides agree on that much.

But they disagree completely on the diagnosis.

The progressive left looks at men and sees a problem that is fundamental to maleness itself. Masculinity, in this telling, is inherently toxic. The male drive to lead, to compete, to overcome obstacles is pathological. The solution is for men to reject these impulses and embrace emotional interiority, collaborative decision-making, and the kind of vulnerability that looks indistinguishable from helplessness. Men who succeed in this project are celebrated as enlightened. Men who struggle are labeled as resistant, stuck in old patterns, unable to evolve.

The conservative right looks at the same culture and reaches the opposite conclusion. Masculinity is not the problem. The problem is that men are not masculine enough. Men have become soft. They have ceded their authority. They have been feminized by culture. The solution is for men to reassert themselves, to remember that leadership is their birthright, to take back what has been lost. Men who succeed in this project are celebrated as strong. Men who struggle are told to stop being weak and start being men.

Both diagnoses sound authoritative. Both marshal evidence. Both claim to defend something essential. And both are wrong.

The crisis is not too much masculinity. The crisis is not too little masculinity. The crisis is that we have lost the biblical vision of what masculinity is for. We have abandoned the theological definition and replaced it with a cultural one. And now the entire apparatus of meaning-making—the left trying to dismantle what they see as toxic, the right trying to resurrect what they see as lost—is building on sand.

The problem runs deeper than anyone on either side is willing to say.

Start with the left's diagnosis. The critique of toxic masculinity comes from the observation of real damage. Men do commit most acts of violence. Men do use power to exploit. Men do check out of families and abdicate emotional responsibility. The critique is correct about the symptoms. What it gets wrong is the root cause.

The left looks at a man who dominates his wife and concludes that the problem is that he is too much of a man. The real problem is that he is not enough of a man. A man who is genuinely secure in his identity does not need to dominate anyone. A man who understands what he is for does not use power to exploit. A man who has been shaped by the gospel of grace does not require constant affirmation from those beneath him.

The toxic version is not an excess of biblical masculinity. It is a counterfeit. It is strength severed from gentleness. It is authority without accountability. It is male dominion without male service. The left is right that this version is toxic. But it is toxic not because it is too masculine. It is toxic because it is not masculine enough. It is a lie about what a man is supposed to be.

But the right's diagnosis is equally confused, just in the opposite direction.

The conservative response takes the left's attack on traditional manhood and concludes that the left wants to destroy manhood itself. So the response is to double down on the cultural stereotype that the left is attacking. Men should be strong, decisive, authoritative, unconcerned with emotional nuance. Men should lead their families with a firm hand. Men should compete and win. Men should not waste energy on feelings.

What the right has done is baptize the very counterfeit that the left is critiquing. It has taken the domination model, dressed it in Scripture verses, and called it biblical masculinity. And now men are caught between two false options: either you reject traditional manhood entirely (the left), or you embrace the domination model that has been labeled traditional (the right). Neither is biblical. Both are cultural.

The real crisis is that we have stopped reading Scripture on its own terms.

When you open the Bible and actually read what it says about men, what you find is something altogether different from both of these options. The first man was made from dust. That is not an accident. The first thing God tells us about the male is his material origin—he is not transcendent, he is not primarily rational, he is not above the earth. He is clay. The imago Dei is stamped onto someone who is fundamentally physical and mortal. Adam's strength comes not from transcendence but from his intimate connection to the earth from which he came.

That detail matters. It shapes everything that follows.

The work God gave the male is significant. He was told to tend and keep the garden. The Hebrew word shamar, usually translated "keep," is the same word used throughout the Old Testament for priestly service. Guarding the sanctuary. Protecting what is holy. A male exists to serve something beyond himself. His purpose is derivative. He is made for the garden, not for his own glory.

And then comes the moment when manhood is first tested, and manhood fails.

The serpent speaks to the woman. The woman listens. The woman takes fruit. The woman gives fruit to the man. And the man eats. That sequence is important. But what is more important is what the man does not do. He does not speak. He does not act. He stands silent while the serpent speaks a lie about God. He watches while the woman makes a decision that will echo across all of human history. He neither corrects the serpent nor stops the woman. He is present but absent. He is there but not there.

The first failure of manhood was not aggression. The first failure of manhood was passivity.

This is the pattern Scripture highlights again and again. The male crisis is not about being too much. It is about being too little. It is about silence when truth needs to be spoken. It is about absence when presence is required. It is about safety when faithfulness would cost something.

Cain killed Abel. The text does not frame Cain's failure as a problem of having too much male aggression. It frames it as a problem of man's refusal to govern the sin that is crouching at his door. God asks: "Why are you angry? If you do what is right, will you not be accepted? But if you do not do what is right, sin is crouching at your door; it desires to have you, but you must rule over it."

The male responsibility is to rule over sin, not to rule over people. The male vocation is to govern the darkness within, not to dominate those around him.

Samson is given strength beyond measure. And he uses it for self-gratification, for revenge, for private glory. The text shows a man who has physical power but no internal governance. His eyes betray him. His sexuality betrays him. His pride betrays him. He is a cautionary tale of what strength looks like when it is divorced from the inner integration that the Bible calls righteousness.

David is a man after God's own heart. But he is also a man who commits adultery and murder. The text does not hide this. It does not present David as someone who became too masculine. It presents him as someone who became too comfortable with his own power. He had become accustomed to getting what he wanted, and when desire rose up, he did not rule over it. He had abdicated his responsibility to govern himself.

The pattern is consistent throughout Scripture. Male failure is not a failure of having too much strength or too much will. It is a failure of internal governance. It is a failure to shape oneself according to God's design. It is a failure to submit one's own desire to the good of others. It is a failure of the male vocation to serve something beyond himself.

This is what the Bible means by the word meekness. The Greek word is praus. And what it means is not weakness. It means strength under discipline. It was used to describe a war horse—powerful, capable of great force, but trained to submit to the rider's will. The strength is still there. But it has been brought under the governance of wisdom and law.

Jesus Christ called himself meek. He said, "Come to me, all you who are weary and burdened, and I will give you rest. Take my yoke upon you and learn from me, for I am gentle and humble in heart, and you will find rest for your souls." The word translated "gentle" is the same word as meek. The strongest man who ever lived described himself with a word the modern church associates with weakness.

That is the revolution we need to understand.

Biblical strength is not the absence of gentleness. Biblical strength is the integration of power with restraint. It is authority placed under governance. It is power submitted to love. When Ephesians 5 calls husbands to love their wives as Christ loved the church, it is calling them to the highest display of strength—the strength to lay down power for another's good. When 1 Peter 3 says husbands should live considerately with their wives, showing honor to the weaker vessel, it is not calling men to abdicate strength. It is calling them to exercise strength in service.

The crisis of manhood is that we have been offered two false options, and the church has largely embraced one of them while the culture fights for the other. What we have lost is the third way. The way of a man who is fully alive. Fully present. Fully responsible. Fully integrated.

A man who cannot be strong has no power to protect anyone. He is just a large child who cannot govern himself. But a man who cannot be gentle has no strength either. He only has aggression. And aggression is the weak man's substitute for strength.

This book is an attempt to recover what we have lost. To name the crisis with precision. And to show what the Scripture actually teaches about what a man is supposed to be. Not what the left says he should be. Not what the right says he should be. But what God says he should be. When you read Scripture not through the lens of the culture's categories but through the categories Scripture provides for itself.

The crisis is real. But the solution is not what either side is offering. The solution is older than the culture wars. The solution is Jesus.

The progressive diagnosis of masculinity is not false. It is incomplete. When young men are three times more likely to die by suicide than young women. When 80 percent of male college students have experienced sexual assault trauma (most of it from other men). When the vast majority of violence in this country is perpetrated by men. When entire families are destroyed because a man could not manage his anger. When the statistics show what we are refusing to look at directly—then something is broken. The progressive voice is right to say it. The mistake is in the diagnosis of what is broken.

Toxic masculinity says the problem is masculinity itself. That the world would be better if men were less like men and more like the sanitized version of femininity that certain progressive voices celebrate. This diagnosis gets the observation correct and the solution backward. The observation: men are breaking things and people. True. The solution: eliminate the thing that makes men men. This is like saying because cars have accidents, we should eliminate the accelerator.

The real problem is not masculinity. It is masculinity without formation. A man is a dangerous creature because he is built for something. His strength, his aggression, his capacity to focus on a single objective for years—these are not the problem. They are design features. Remove them and you do not have a better man. You have a confused creature who has no idea what his capacities are for.

The conservative response says "man up." Return to strength, to discipline, to the traditional codes. There is something right in this. Men do need to be formed. They do need initiation. They do need the old stories of sacrifice and service and the warrior who lays down his weapons. Wild at Heart captured something true: men are dying inside from the message that their strength is shameful. But the conservative answer often baptizes the wrong thing. It takes the aggressive, competitive, dominating parts of a man and calls that strength when strength is actually something larger.

A man can be strong and gentle. A man can be courageous and kind. A man can lead and listen. But the conservative world often treats listening like it is a concession a man has to make to women, not a virtue that makes a man more fully himself. So you get men who embrace traditional masculine codes without ever learning to integrate the deeper parts of themselves that the codes do not permit. The result: men who are strong but isolated. Men who can build empires but cannot build relationships. Men who know how to win but do not know how to surrender.

Andrew Tate has over 200 million social media followers. Young men follow him by the millions. He sells dominance. He sells the narrative that a man's value is his ability to control, to win, to refuse softness, to treat women as property and other men as competitors. He is not popular because young men are evil. He is popular because he is answering a question with absolute clarity.

The question is: What is a man for?

The progressive voice answers: A man is for nothing specific. He is a person who happens to have a male body. Congratulations. Now go deconstruct your assumptions.

The conservative voice answers: A man is for leadership, provision, protection. He is the head of the household.

Andrew Tate answers: A man is for conquest. For dominance. For the accumulation of wealth and women and status. For the refusal to be weak.

Millions of young men are listening to Tate not because they do not know better. They are listening because in a world that tells them manhood is shameful, he is the only one speaking with absolute conviction about manhood being something. He is wrong about what manhood is for. But he is right that it is for something. That clarity is seductive when the alternative is a silence that sounds like shame.

The church is largely absent from this conversation. Some churches embrace the progressive deconstruction and offer young men nothing but apology for existing. Some churches embrace the conservative codes and offer young men permission to dominate without permission to feel. Almost no church is offering young men what they actually need: a vision of strength that is inseparable from sacrificial love. A vision of leadership that is defined by serving the vulnerable, not controlling them. A vision of masculinity that is tethered to the gospel of a God who came weak.

This is not a problem you solve with better marketing or more enthusiasm in worship. This is a problem you solve by going back to the foundation. By asking what the Scripture actually says a man is for. By being willing to stand apart from both the progressive world that wants to unmake men and the conservative world that wants to remake them into conquerors. By offering something the culture cannot offer because the culture does not have the vocabulary for it.

The young man does not need to be told that traditional masculinity is toxic. He knows the stats. He has seen the harm. What he needs is to be told that there is a vision of manhood so countercultural, so costly, so filled with actual power that anything Tate is selling looks like a counterfeit by comparison. That vision is a man who lays down his life for others. Not metaphorically. Actually. That he does not need to be ashamed of his strength but he also does not get to use it however he wants. That the measure of his manhood is not the women he conquers but the people he serves. That his body is not his own to do with as he pleases. That he is built for something larger than himself.

This is not an easy sell in a world that worships autonomy. But it is the only message that has ever actually freed a man to become who he was built to be. Every other message—whether it comes from the progressive left or the conservative right or the manosphere—is a version of the same lie. That a man's freedom is his ability to do whatever he wants. The gospel says something entirely different. That a man's freedom is his ability to lay down his freedom for something larger than himself.

Young men are hungry for this. They are not looking for permission to stay comfortable. They are looking for a reason to be uncomfortable on purpose. For something worth the sacrifice. For a man to believe in who is different from the men they have seen and willing to say it out loud. When the church becomes that, we stop losing young men to Andrew Tate and start calling them to something real.

The church has failed men on the question of what it means to be a man.

Not in one way. In almost every way that matters.

Walk into the typical men's ministry in the American evangelical church and here is what you find: a pancake breakfast on Saturday morning. A hunting trip in the fall. A book study that meets once a month and reads some variation of "What Every Man Needs" or "How to Be the Leader of Your Home." A conference once a year where a famous pastor comes and gives a motivational speech and everyone feels fired up for about three weeks. Then it is back to Sunday morning, where the men sit in the pew, and nobody says anything real.

Meanwhile, in the pews and the small groups, men are falling apart.

The marriages are collapsing. The man sitting next to you in row seven has not been intimate with his wife in months, and he has no idea how to tell her. The man three rows back is waking up at three in the morning with pornography addiction that he confessed once, years ago, and has never mentioned since because there is nowhere in the church to actually talk about it. The man serving as a deacon is drowning in debt he has been hiding from his family. The man leading the youth group is having thoughts about another man that terrify him, and he has no one to tell. The man who appears to have it all together is one bad month away from losing his family because his children do not know him and his wife has stopped believing he loves her.

And the church is serving him pancakes.

The deepest conversation most men have in a church men's group is a book study that skims the surface of life and calls it depth. These studies are designed by people who have never had to sit in a room and tell another man that his marriage is failing. They are designed by people who believe that the problem with men is that they do not have enough information. But information is not what broken men need. Confession is what they need. Witness is what they need. Another man who has walked through the same valley saying: you are not the only one, and God is not done with you.

The contemporary men's ministry has made an idol out of scalability. How many men can we reach? What program can we mass-produce? But the gospel was never mass-produced. It was always incarnational. It was always: a man sitting with another man, looking him in the eye, and saying: come follow me.

Here is what a real men's ministry would look like, and why most churches are not willing to build it.

It would be small. Not five hundred men on Saturday morning. Eight men. Maybe twelve. Not a lecture hall. A living room. Not a formal agenda. Honest conversation that sometimes goes nowhere because it has to be real.

It would be recurring. Not once a month. Every week. Because confession and witness require rhythm. You cannot tell a man something that might save his life and then not see him for thirty days. The muscle of honesty atrophies quickly.

It would be led by men who are willing to go first. Not the most polished man. The most honest one. The man who says: here is where I am failing. Here is what I am learning. Here is what I do not know. When the leader goes first with his own failure, every other man in the room can breathe. Permission has been granted to be human.

It would include confession. Not the kind that is performed for the group. The kind that requires vulnerability that costs something. Not casual. Not the performance of being open. The actual saying of the thing that you have been hiding from everyone, and the actual staying in the room while other men hear it.

It would include accountability that is not surveillance. Not tracking whether you prayed today or stayed off a website. Witness. Someone who knows your story, knows where you are weak, and checks in not to police you but to ask: how is your soul? What is the Holy Spirit doing in you? What are you learning about yourself? These are different questions entirely.

It would require a man to say no to everything else for that hour. Not his phone. Not his side conversation. Not his performance. All of him in one room with other men who are doing the same thing.

This is threatening to churches because it cannot be measured. You cannot say: we have eight men in a small group. It is not impressive on a report. It will not grow the church numerically in any predictable way. It will not sell books or get social media engagement. But it will transform men. It will transform marriages. It will transform the way a man shows up in his family and his community and his faith.

The reason most churches are not willing to build this is because it requires a man to be vulnerable, and vulnerability is contagious. If one man admits his marriage is struggling, suddenly everyone is thinking about their marriage. If one man confesses addiction, suddenly everyone is aware of their own hidden behaviors. If one man says he does not know if he believes anymore, the whole room has to reckon with doubt. Most church leaders do not want that. They want certainty. They want the appearance of things being fine. They want men who show up, volunteer, give money, and do not create complications.

But that is not discipleship. That is management.

Real discipleship is always disruptive because real discipleship requires men to tell the truth about themselves. And the truth about men in the American church is that most of them are drowning. Most of them are lonely. Most of them have no place where they can say: I am failing. Most of them have built a life where admitting weakness is not an option. Most of them are so busy performing manhood that they have forgotten what it means to be a man.

The church could be the place where that changes. It could be the place where a man walks in carrying a secret that is destroying him, and he walks out carrying it with someone else. But that requires the church to be willing to fail at scale in order to succeed at depth.

The pancake breakfast is fine. But it is not discipleship. And if that is the extent of what a church offers men, then the church has abandoned its responsibility to help men become men.

**CHAPTER 2**

What the Bible Actually Says About Men

Strip away every cultural overlay and read Genesis 1 through 3 fresh. Not fresh as if the texts have no history. Fresh as if the culture's debates about gender have not already written the interpretation for you. Fresh as if you are willing to be surprised by what the text actually says rather than what you assumed it said.

Begin with the creation account. Genesis 1:27 says, "So God created mankind in his own image, in the image of God he created them; male and female he created them."

There is something crucial happening here. The imago Dei—the image of God—is attributed equally to male and to female. The text does not say the man bears God's image and the woman is derivative. It does not say the man is the full expression and the woman is a support. It says both are equally image-bearers. This is the foundation of everything else that follows. Before any discussion of roles, before any mention of difference, the first theological claim is equality in image. Both are representations of God. Both carry intrinsic dignity. Both matter infinitely.

That foundation will shape everything we understand about male identity.

Then comes Genesis 2, and the text slows down. It gives us the story of the man's creation in detail. Genesis 2:7 says, "the Lord God formed the man from the dust of the ground and breathed into his nostrils the breath of life, and the man became a living being."

Stop there for a moment. This is the first thing we are told about the man: he is made from dust. Not spirit. Not mind. Not will. Dust. The Psalmist will later write, "Remember that we are dust, and to dust we will return." The human creature—specifically the male human creature—begins with a confrontation with his own mortality, his own materiality, his own complete dependence.

This is not an accident. This is the beginning of true strength, and most men never arrive here.

A man who has not confronted his own dustiness will spend his entire life trying to prove he is not mortal. He will pursue permanence through accumulation. He will try to write his name on something that lasts. He will use other people to validate his significance. He will dominate because he is unconsciously terrified of the void. He is running from the fact of his own dust.

But a man who has accepted that he is dust—who has looked at his own mortality and not looked away—is free. He no longer needs to prove anything. He no longer needs the world to validate him. He can serve something beyond himself because he is not trying to make himself matter. He already knows he does not matter in the cosmic sense. And that knowledge is liberating.

The text continues. Genesis 2:15 says, "The Lord God took the man and put him in the Garden of Eden to work it and take care of it."

This is the vocation. Before companionship, before sexuality, before anything relational, the man is given work. He is given purpose. His identity is not found in relationship. His identity is found in calling.

This matters because the modern church has largely inverted this. We tell men that their identity is found in being a husband, being a father, being the provider. And those roles matter. But they are not your identity. Your identity is found in what you are called to do. You exist to steward something. You exist to care for what has been entrusted to you.

The Hebrew word for "take care of" is shamar. It appears elsewhere in the text to describe the role of the priest—to guard what is holy, to maintain what has been set apart, to protect what is sacred. This is the male calling. Not to rule. Not to command. To guard. To tend. To keep.

Then comes the woman. Genesis 2:22 says, "Then the Lord God made a woman from the rib he had taken out of the man, and he brought her to the man."

And the man says, "This is now bone of my bones and flesh of my flesh; she shall be called 'woman,' for she was taken out of man."

The text highlights that woman comes from within the man. She is not created separately from the dust. She is created from the man's own substance. But notice what the man does not do. He does not say, "Now I will teach her what I know." He does not say, "Now I will lead and she will follow." He does not establish a hierarchy. He recognizes her as flesh of his flesh. He recognizes her as fundamentally one with him.

The role that comes later—in Ephesians 5, headship—will have to be understood in the context of this recognition. A man leads the woman he has recognized as bone of his own bones. He leads someone who is his equal in image, his companion in purpose, his partner in the vocation of stewardship.

Genesis 2 ends with a summary: "That is why a man leaves his father and mother and is united to his wife, and they become one flesh."

The text is very clear. The male vocation is not private. The male purpose is not solitary. The man is made for relationship. He leaves his family of origin and enters a covenant union. The work of tending the garden now happens in partnership. The stewardship now involves another person in a way that is total and exclusive.

And then comes Genesis 3. This is the moment when manhood is tested.

The serpent addresses the woman. "Did God really say, 'You must not eat from any tree in the garden'?"

The woman responds. She clarifies. She defends God's word. But she also adds to it—"and you must not touch it"—and she does not notice that she has added. The serpent sees the opening. And the serpent exploits it.

> *"You will not surely die," the serpent says. "For God knows that when you eat from it your eyes will be opened, and you will be like God, knowing good and evil."*

This is the first temptation. The promise of divine knowledge. The promise of autonomy. The promise that you can determine good and evil for yourself rather than trusting God's definition.

The text continues: "When the woman saw that the fruit of the tree was good for food and pleasing to the eye, and also desirable for gaining wisdom, she took some and ate it. She also gave some to her husband, who was with her, and he ate it."

Stop on that phrase. "Who was with her." The man is present. He is there. He has witnessed the entire exchange. And he says nothing.

This is the first male failure in Scripture. It is not aggression. It is passivity. The man does not correct the serpent's lie. He does not stop the woman's hand. He does not speak the truth. He simply eats what has been offered.

The question God asks afterward is directed at the man. Not the woman. God asks the man: "Have you eaten from the tree that I commanded you not to eat from?" The man's response shifts blame. "The woman you gave me—she gave me some fruit from the tree, and I ate it." He blames the woman. He blames God for giving him the woman. He abdicates responsibility.

This is the pattern. The male failure is not strength misused. The male failure is strength abdicated. The male failure is the man who is present but not present. Who witnesses but does not speak. Who chooses safety over truth. Who abdicates the responsibility he has been given.

And the consequences are catastrophic. The entire human drama flows from this moment. Death enters the world. Pain enters childbirth. The ground becomes cursed. The man and woman are expelled from the garden. The vocation is disrupted. The relationship is broken. All of this flows from the moment when the man failed to govern—not the woman, not creation, but himself. He failed to rule over the desire to eat what he should not eat. He failed to speak the truth he knew to be true. He failed to fulfill his calling to guard what was holy.

This is the biblical diagnosis of male failure. It is not the diagnosis the culture offers. But it is the one that Scripture provides.

The theology here is crucial. The man was made with authority—authority to name, authority to steward, authority to guard. But authority in Scripture is always authority to serve. It is authority to protect what has been entrusted to you. It is authority to maintain what is good. It is authority to stand against what would destroy.

When a man abdicates this authority—when he is silent, when he looks away, when he lets evil speak unopposed—he has failed at the most fundamental level. He is not a man. He is a creature who has refused his calling.

But there is another side to the male failure. And this is what the modern church often misses.

The male is not simply called to inner governance. The male is called to presence. He is called to participate. He is called to lead in the sense that a shepherd leads sheep—not from domination but from service. The shepherd is present. The shepherd knows each sheep. The shepherd stands between the sheep and danger.

When the text describes the man's failure in Genesis 3, it is not only that he did not govern himself. It is that he was present but not present. He did not engage. He did not speak. He did not stand for what he knew to be true.

This is the male responsibility. Not domination. Not abdication. Presence. Engagement. Faithfulness.

A man is to be present in his home. Present in his marriage. Present in his children's lives. Present in his community. Present when truth needs to be spoken. Present when weakness needs to be protected. Present when evil needs to be resisted.

This kind of presence requires strength. It requires the strength to speak unpopular truth. It requires the strength to admit failure. It requires the strength to grieve. It requires the strength to be vulnerable with another person. It requires the strength to sacrifice his own comfort for someone else's good.

This is the revolution. Biblical manhood is not about power over others. It is about presence with others. It is about a man who has governed himself so thoroughly that he is free to serve beyond himself. It is about a man who has looked at his own dust and accepted his own mortality, and who therefore can live for something that will outlast him.

When you read Scripture on its own terms, you find a man who is strong and present. A man who speaks truth and protects the vulnerable. A man who knows he is dust and therefore does not need the world to validate him. A man who serves the calling he has been given. A man who leads by example rather than by coercion.

This is what Genesis 1 and 2 set up. This is what Genesis 3 violates. And this is what the rest of Scripture will keep circling back to, again and again, calling men to something they keep abandoning.

The crisis of manhood is not that we have too much of this. The crisis is that we have lost it entirely.

It is easy to miss the disruptive thing Moses is saying in Genesis 2. He is not describing a system. He is describing a crisis. God creates the man. God places him in the garden. God gives him work to do. And then God looks at the creation and speaks. "It is not good for the man to be alone."

This is the only moment in Genesis 1 and 2 where God looks at creation and says something is not good. Light is good. Land is good. Vegetation is good. Animals are good. The man is good. But the man alone is not good. Not because he is a failure. Not because he has done something wrong. But because he was built for something that cannot happen in isolation.

God is not describing a problem that can be solved by productivity. The man is working. He is naming the animals. He is fulfilling his purpose. But the purpose itself is incomplete without relationship. A man can spend his entire life achieving, building, producing, creating, and still be profoundly alone. And God says that aloneness is not good.

This is the theological foundation for a revolution in how men think about friendship. The culture tells a man that he is supposed to be self-sufficient. That needing other people is a weakness. That vulnerability is shameful. That the strong man stands alone and the weak man asks for help. The Scripture says the opposite. That a man was built for companionship. That aloneness is a corrupted state. That the man who refuses friendship is the man who is refusing the design of his own creation.

There is a particular kind of loneliness that has become the default state for a lot of men. It is not the loneliness of being alone. It is the loneliness of being surrounded. A man can have colleagues, acquaintances, family, a church community, and still be utterly alone because there is no one who knows him. No one who has given him permission to be something other than what is expected. No one who holds him accountable to anything other than productivity and image.

Breaking this loneliness is not a therapy project. It is a spiritual formation project. It begins with the recognition that aloneness is not good. Not because it feels bad. Because it violates the design. Then it requires a man to actually do the terrifying thing: to tell another man the truth about himself. Not the version of himself he has constructed for public consumption. The real version. The one with the doubts and the failures and the shame and the places where he is not enough.

Most men die having never done this with another man. They have colleagues. They have acquaintances. They have buddies they watch football with. But they have never had another man see them and stay. That is a kind of death. And the culture does not even name it as death because the culture has normalized it as normal.

The shamar that the man does in the garden is not what most English translations make it sound like. To keep is not the right word. The word is shamar. It means to guard, to watch over, to tend with care. It is the word used for priests in the temple. The man was not a laborer in the garden. He was a priest. His work was not just to produce. His work was to steward, to care for, to maintain the sacred trust of the place he inhabited.

This is a revolution in how a man thinks about his daily work. His job is not just to provide income. His work is worship. His presence in the garden—tending it, guarding it, caring for it with attention—this is priestly work. It is liturgy. It is the way he participates in God's ongoing act of creation. The man who shows up to work and moves through his day with the assumption that it is just a job, just a paycheck, just a necessary evil—that man has been robbed of something essential. He has been robbed of the understanding that his work is the way he serves something larger than himself.

This is why a man can have a six-figure salary and still feel empty. Because he has never understood that his work is priestly. He has never grasped that the way he shows up to his job—with attention, with care, with the assumption that what he does matters—is the way he participates in the redemption of the world.

When a man understands that his work is shamar work, everything changes. He is not working to accumulate. He is working to steward. He is not working to prove something to himself or to the world. He is working as a priest, tending something that has been entrusted to him. When he goes to the job site and lays the brick, he is not just laying brick. He is stewarding the materials and the craft and the building that will shelter people for years. When he shows up to the office and makes the decision and manages the team and builds the system, he is not just competing for advancement. He is stewarding something that affects how people spend their days and whether they can feed their families.

A man does not need to be in professional ministry to have priestly work. A man is a priest in his work whenever he understands that what he does matters not because it makes him money but because it serves something he has been entrusted to steward. A man is a priest when he shows up with attention. When he refuses to do his work halfway. When he understands that carelessness is a form of irreverence. When he knows that the measure of his work is not his profit margin but his faithfulness to what has been entrusted to him.

This is why the separation of work and worship has been so catastrophic for men. A man spends forty hours a week at work and two hours on Sunday at church and assumes the Sunday hours are the ones that matter spiritually. But the Scripture says the opposite. The work is priestly. The work is worship. If a man cannot see his work as priestly, he will spend his entire life bifurcated—one person at work and another person on Sunday and never knowing that he was built to be whole.

Genesis 2:15. The man was placed in the garden "to tend and keep it."

This verse is almost always bypassed in the sermon that flows straight from Genesis 2 into Genesis 3. We are in such a hurry to get to the fall, to explain where the curse came from, that we skip over something central: work was part of the original design. Work is not a punishment. The curse was that the work became toilsome. The work itself was the point.

This changes everything about how a man should think about his career, his job, his daily labor.

For the man who has built his identity around his work, who has tied his worth to his title and his paycheck and his position, who wakes up at four in the morning thinking about his next promotion, who goes to sleep counting the ways his company does not recognize him: your work matters. The world needs what you are building. But your work is not you. You can be fired and still be a man. You can lose the title and still be of infinite worth. You can fail at work and still be loved by God. The capacity of work to define you is the capacity of work to destroy you. And most men learn this only through devastation.

For the man who has lost his job and is sitting at home and feels worthless: the value was never in the position. You were not valuable because of what was on your business card. You are valuable because you are made in the image of God. The job was a vehicle for your work, but it was not the work itself. Your capacity to be a provider does not disappear because one source of income did. What disappears is the narrative that you have been telling yourself about where your worth comes from.

For the man who hates his job, who dreads Monday, who feels like his life is being drained by something he hates: the call is not necessarily to a different job. The call is to a different relationship with the job you have. Brother Lawrence was a lay brother in the 1600s. He spent most of his life in the kitchen of a monastery, washing dishes and peeling potatoes. And he wrote: "The time of business does not differ from the time of prayer, and in the noise and clatter of my kitchen, while several persons are calling for different things at the same time, I possess God in as great tranquility as if I were upon my knees."

This is not resignation. This is transfiguration. It is the capacity to see the sacred in the ordinary. The potato you are peeling is the Lord's potato. The dishes you are washing are the Lord's dishes. The email you are sending is the Lord's email. When you are not waiting for your real life to start after work, when you stop treating work as the obstacle to meaning and start seeing work as the place where meaning lives, everything changes.

This does not mean your job is the perfect expression of your calling. Maybe you are working in a place that is destructive or dishonest. Maybe you are in a season where you are transitioning to something else. But while you are there, the call is to bring the same presence, the same integrity, the same care that you would bring if you were doing something you loved. Not for the paycheck. Not for the promotion. Because the work is worth doing well, and you are the kind of man who does work well.

The contemporary culture has created a false binary. Either your work is your passion, your dream, your calling, or it is meaningless. Either you are doing what you love or you are wasting your life. But this is a recipe for despair. Because most men will not land in a job that is their burning passion. Most men will have work that is ordinary. And if the only meaningful work is passionate work, then most men are leading meaningless lives.

That is a lie.

The meaningful work is the work that provides for your family. The work that you show up to even when you do not feel like it. The work where you do the thing with integrity even though nobody is watching. The work that you offer to God as an act of worship. This is what Brother Lawrence knew. The potatoes matter not because they are your passion but because they are what is in front of you and they deserve to be treated like they matter.

For the man who is building something and loves what he builds, there is a different temptation. The temptation is to let the work become the ultimate thing. To sacrifice family for the project. To tell yourself that you are building something that will outlast you, so the cost to your immediate relationships is worth it. The man who builds an empire and loses his family has not won. He has made a terrible trade.

The work is good. The work is part of how God made you. But the work is not the thing. The people are the thing. The marriage is the thing. The children are the thing. The work is what sustains them and what shapes you in the process. When the work starts to demand the sacrifice of the people, you have inverted the order. And the cost is everything.

This is what Genesis is trying to teach us. The man was given work before he was given a wife. Work is not a consolation prize for not having someone to love. Work and relationship are both part of the original design. And the man who tends his work well but neglects his wife and children is not obedient. He is selfish. He is taking the command to work and using it to justify the abandonment of the command to love.

So the man who works must work like a man who sees that his work is part of his worship. But he must also work like a man who knows that when the work is done, he goes home. And what waits for him there is the actual substance of his life.

**CHAPTER 3**

The Fatherless Generation

I was a boy without a father. Not because he died. Because he left.

I do not remember him. I was very young when he chose absence over presence. My mother raised me alone. And she did it with a fierceness that shaped everything I became.

She worked. She managed the money. She made decisions. She fixed things around the house. She did not cry in front of me. She was a single mother living in a single-story brick house in rural Michigan, and she made it all work. She did what needed to be done.

But she could not tell me what a man was supposed to be. She had never been one. She could tell me what a man should not do. She could tell me the ways my father had failed her. But she could not show me what success looked like. She could not show me what strength that also cares. She could not show me what authority that also listens. She could not show me what a man looked like when he was fully alive.

So I looked for him elsewhere. And what I found were images. The athlete who dominated. The tough guy who did not let anything break him. The man who had money and power and people who deferred to him. These were the closest thing I had to a model. And I pursued them with the intensity a boy can bring when he is trying to fill an absence with an image.

I became a football player. I built my body into something that could hurt people. I developed a will that refused to break. I learned that if you were strong enough and angry enough, nobody could ask you about the hole inside you. They would just get out of your way.

But the hole was there. It was always there.

The hole was in the room when I was studying. It was in the room when I was at parties. It was in the room when I was lying in bed at night asking questions about who I was supposed to be. The silence where a father should have been was deafening. And I filled it with aggression and performance and the kind of achievement that looks like success from the outside and feels like drowning from the inside.

I was an atheist through high school. This is not separate from the fatherlessness. It is connected. A boy who has no model of a man who is strong and also trustworthy will not trust strength. And if strength is what the culture associates with manhood, and strength is dangerous, then manhood itself becomes suspect. If the only man who was supposed to be present was not present, then perhaps the whole thing is a lie. Perhaps there is no such thing as a trustworthy man. Perhaps the only honest thing is to reject the whole system.

I rejected God. I rejected the church. I rejected the culture's version of manhood. I had nothing to replace it with, so I just rejected. I was angry. I was lost. I was a teenager with a hole inside him and no way to talk about it.

And then a man showed up.

He came to my school. He was a Christian. He came to answer questions. And he did something that changed everything. He treated my atheism like it might be honest. He did not treat me like I was stupid or rebellious. He treated me like I had real questions that deserved real answers.

I have told this story before. I will probably tell it the rest of my life. Because that moment—when a man who was strong and also kind and also willing to listen—showed me something I had never seen. That moment opened a door I had slammed shut.

He became a presence in my absence. Not because he was trying to. Not because he knew the hole that was inside me. Just because he showed up. He paid attention. He asked follow-up questions. He demonstrated what a man could be when he was not performing and not dominating and not running from his own doubt.

And I wanted that. I wanted to understand what he had understood. I wanted to know the God he knew. I wanted to become the kind of man he was becoming.

That is when everything changed.

But I did not change alone. And this is what the statistics do not tell you.

The statistics on fatherlessness are devastating. Seventy percent of incarcerated youth grew up without a father in the home. Sixty percent of men in prison grew up without a father. Boys raised without a father are five times more likely to commit suicide. Three times more likely to use drugs. Three times more likely to end up in prison. More likely to fail in school. More likely to be unemployed as adults. More likely to end up on the streets.

The data is clear. There is a crisis of fatherlessness in America. Millions of boys are growing up without a man in the home. Millions of boys are experiencing what I experienced—the hole, the absence, the silence where presence should be.

But the data does not tell the complete story.

The data does not tell you what happens in the soul of a boy when no one is there. The statistics cannot measure the moment when a boy realizes that the man who made him did not want to know him. The statistics cannot quantify the shame that attaches itself to a boy when he believes, at some deep level, that there is something about him that is unworthy of presence.

The statistics cannot measure what it does to a boy's developing sense of manhood to have no model. To have only images. To have only the cultural stereotypes and the broken versions of strength. To have no living example of what a man could be when he is whole.

I grew up surrounded by other boys like me. Boys without fathers. Boys who were angry. Boys who were performing. Boys who had made some version of the same choice I had made—either to become dominating or to become empty. Either to pursue the tough version or to give up entirely.

And in the room where I found faith, I found something else. I found a circle of men who were not like the image. They were not dominating. They were not broken. They were present. They were honest. They were shaped by something larger than themselves. And they seemed to know something about what a man was supposed to be that nobody else seemed to know.

That is when I understood the purpose of presence. It is not only that a man can talk you out of atheism or into faith. A man's presence itself is a form of teaching. A man who is whole—who is honest about his struggle and faithful despite his doubt—is a sermon that cannot be delivered any other way.

This is the crisis that the statistics only partially capture.

We have a generation of boys growing up without a father. But we also have a generation of boys growing up without any man who is present. Without any man who is paying attention. Without any man who is demonstrating what strength looks like when it is integrated with gentleness. Without any man who is showing them that you can be competent and also vulnerable. That you can be authoritative and also accountable. That you can be fully alive.

The statistics on fatherlessness are one crisis. But the deeper crisis is the absence of manly presence in general. We have emptied the spaces where men used to inhabit. The church. The neighborhood. The mentor relationship. The initiation that takes a boy and transforms him into a man.

That the very system that is supposed to create male presence has become a system of male absence. Pastors are overwhelmed. They are underpaid. They are expected to be superhuman. And they are doing it alone.

Not a savior. Not a hero. Just a man who shows up and pays attention and demonstrates that there is another way.

This is why the statistics on fatherlessness matter. But it is also why they are not the complete story.

Every boy who grows up without a father needs a man to show up in his absence. Not to replace the father. But to demonstrate that presence is possible. That strength does not require domination. That a man can be honest about his struggle and still be worth following.

I have raised five sons. And I have been present for every moment I could be present. I have been in the room. I have listened when they needed listening. I have held firm when they needed firmness. I have admitted my mistakes when I made them. Not because I am a perfect father. But because I know what the absence of a father does. I know the hole it carves. I know the questions it creates. I know the images it leaves you grasping for.

And I have tried to be for other men what that man was for me. A presence. A demonstration. An example of what a man could become when he stops performing and starts serving.

This is the work. This is the calling. This is the reason the crisis of fatherlessness matters so much to me. Not as data. Not as a social problem to be solved with programs. But as a reality that shapes the soul of a human being at the deepest level.

A boy without a father needs to see a man. A young man confused about who he is supposed to be needs a model. A pastor drowning in isolation needs a brother. A husband struggling with what it means to lead needs someone to show him it is possible.

The fatherlessness is real. The statistics are accurate. But the solution is not a program. The solution is presence. The solution is a man who shows up and pays attention and demonstrates—not through words but through his life—that there is another way.

This is the gospel applied to the crisis of manhood. Not a formula. Not a system. Just a man being present to another man. Just a strength that is also gentle. Just a presence that opens a door in someone's soul that they thought was locked forever.

I know because it happened to me. And I have spent my life trying to make sure it happens to others.

I was in second grade when I realized my father was not coming back. Not because anyone told me. But because I understood for the first time what the absence meant. Other children had fathers. Mine did not live in our house. And the thing I did not have was not an abstract concept. It was a person. A man. Someone I did not know.

There was a Little League game when I was nine. I played shortstop. I was good. I remember standing in the field in the first inning and looking toward the bleachers to see if he had come. He had not. By the third inning I had stopped looking. By the end of the game I did not even remember whether I had gotten a hit. All I remember is the emptiness of the bleachers where a man should have been.

I did not cry about it. I did not tell my mother. I incorporated it. I added it to the architecture of my anger. The anger was not sharp then. It was dull. It was the kind of anger that does not announce itself. It is the kind that builds. Brick by brick.

My high school graduation was on a Saturday. My mother was there. My brothers were there. I wore the cap and gown and walked across the stage and shook the principal's hand and felt nothing. Because somewhere in the crowd where a father should have been proud, there was only absence. The anger was sharper then. It was the first time I understood that the feeling I carried was not about being angry at the world. It was about being angry at one man who had made a choice.

The face I looked for was not the face of someone I knew. It was the face of someone I had invented. In my head, in my fantasies, in the story I told myself about what could have been, I had built a father out of the negative space his absence created. He was everything I was not. He was confident where I was uncertain. He was brave where I was afraid. He was present where I was lonely. He was the man I was supposed to become, except the blueprint had walked out when I was three.

This is what fatherlessness does. It does not just take away a parent. It takes away the template. It leaves a young man with the job of inventing himself from scratch while simultaneously carrying the rage at the man who should have done the inventing.

When I was twelve, a man named Coach Willis started coaching my baseball team. He was not my father. He did not try to be. But he was present in a way that filled a particular kind of space. He noticed when I swung the bat wrong and showed me how to correct it. He benched me when I had a bad attitude and made me sit on the sideline until I understood that my behavior affected the team. He told me once, after a game where I had made an error and then given up, "One mistake does not define you. How you respond to it does." That sentence lodged somewhere in me. It became part of how I thought about failure. It became part of how I thought about myself.

He did not call me son. He did not tell me he loved me. He did not show up to my house and become a father figure. What he did was something simpler and more powerful. He was consistent. He was honest. He paid attention. He held me to a standard. He did not make excuses for me and he did not shame me. He expected something of me and he believed I could do it.

There was a neighbor named Mr. Hutchins who let me work in his garage. I was maybe fourteen. He fixed cars and he let me help. I did not know anything about cars and I was terrified I would break something. He put a wrench in my hand and said, "You will not break anything. And if you do, we will fix it. You learn by doing." I spent every afternoon that summer in that garage. And what he taught me was not just about cars. It was that a man does something with his hands. That a man can figure things out. That there is a particular kind of thinking that happens when you are looking at a problem and trying to solve it. That work itself can be a form of thinking.

There was a youth pastor at the church named Robert. When I was angry—and I was always angry—he did not try to fix it. He did not tell me that anger was sinful and I needed to get over it. What he did was ask me what the anger was about. And when I told him, he did not dismiss it. He did not minimize it. He said, "That is a real thing to be angry about. The question is not whether your anger is justified. The question is what you are going to do with it." He gave me permission to be angry. And then he gave me the harder thing. He gave me a vision of manhood where anger could be channeled into something. That I could be angry at injustice without being destroyed by anger. That my rage could become fuel instead of poison.

None of these men replaced my father. That is important to say. There was a hole that had the shape of a father and none of these men fit that shape perfectly. But what they did was different. They gave me pieces. They showed me what a man looks like who is not consumed by bitterness. They showed me what a man looks like who keeps his word. They showed me what a man looks like who tells the truth even when it costs him. They showed me what a man looks like who believes in someone even when that someone is not sure he is worth believing in.

The absence of my father never went away. I made a decision when my first son was born that I understood what that absence meant because I had lived inside it. And that understanding came with a responsibility. Every day I showed up for my sons was a day I was not just being their father. I was rewriting a story that had been written into me before I was born. I was saying to the absence: You do not get to define what comes next. I do.

That is not virtue. That is resistance. And the resistance is harder than anyone who has a father in the room will ever understand. Because the default narrative is silence. The default is the choice my father made. Every other choice requires consciousness. It requires deciding every single day that you are going to be different. That you are going to stay. That you are going to show up. That you are going to be the presence you did not have.

There is a moment in every fatherless boy's life when he realizes that the other boys have something he does not. It is not a dramatic moment. It is quiet. It is the moment when everyone else's dad shows up to the game and yours does not. It is the moment when a friend says "my dad taught me" and you realize that nobody taught you. It is the moment when someone asks "what does your dad do?" and you have to decide whether to lie, explain, or change the subject.

I chose to change the subject. For years.

The absence of a father does not announce itself. It seeps. It shapes the boy without his knowing. He does not grow up thinking "I lack a father figure and therefore I will struggle with identity formation." He grows up thinking something is wrong with him. He grows up watching other boys receive something he cannot name, and he assumes the reason he does not receive it is because he does not deserve it.

That assumption, planted before a boy has the language to challenge it, becomes the root system for everything that follows. It becomes the reason he cannot accept a compliment without suspicion. The reason he works harder than everyone else but never feels it is enough. The reason he pushes people away the moment they get close, because closeness is the setup for abandonment, and he has already been abandoned once.

The church often speaks about fatherlessness in statistical terms. Twenty-three million children growing up without a father in the home. Children from fatherless homes are four times more likely to live in poverty. Seven times more likely to experience teen pregnancy. More likely to drop out of school, abuse substances, end up incarcerated. The numbers are staggering. But numbers do not bleed.

What bleeds is the boy sitting in the back row of a youth group watching the youth pastor talk about God as Father while his own father is a ghost. What bleeds is the teenager who cannot form a sentence about what he is feeling because nobody ever modeled emotional articulation for him. What bleeds is the young man who gets married and realizes on his wedding night that he has no idea what a husband actually does because he never saw one.

The fatherless boy does not just lack a parent. He lacks a template. He lacks a man who shows him by living it what it looks like to be angry without being destructive. What it looks like to be tired without being absent. What it looks like to fail and stay in the room. What it looks like to love a woman over decades, not months. What it looks like to hold authority without abusing it.

And so the fatherless boy improvises. He pieces together a version of manhood from whatever is available. From coaches who gave him attention. From older boys who gave him an identity. From the internet, which gave him everything except wisdom. From the culture, which sold him competing visions that all had one thing in common: none of them required him to be known.

This is the deepest wound of fatherlessness. Not the absence of provision or protection. The absence of being known. A father who is present knows his son. Knows the way his son walks into a room. Knows the face his son makes when he is pretending to be fine. Knows the specific kind of encouragement his son needs that is different from what every other boy needs. That knowing is irreplaceable. And when it is missing, the boy spends the rest of his life trying to be known by people who do not have the capacity to know him the way a father would have.

I found Christ because a man saw me. Not my father. A man in a small group who paid attention. Who asked questions I did not expect. Who did not try to become my father but who gave me something a father would have given me: the experience of being seen and not rejected. That man did not save me. God saved me. But God used that man's eyes and that man's presence and that man's willingness to stay in the room.

The church has an opportunity here that it has largely squandered. Every congregation has fatherless men sitting in the pews. Men who came to faith without a father's guidance. Men who are raising children without a template. Men who love God but have no idea how to be the kind of man God is calling them to be because they never saw it modeled.

These men do not need a conference. They need a man. One man. A man who will sit across from them and say: I see you. I know you did not have a father. I know you are figuring this out as you go. And I am going to be here. Not as a replacement. As a witness. As a man who has walked this road far enough to know where the ditches are.

That is what changed my life. Not a theology of fatherhood. A man who showed up.

**CHAPTER 4**

Strength and Gentleness Are Not Opposites

I learned the word as a teenager, and I hated it.

Meekness. It sounded like weakness. It sounded like a man who could not stand up for himself. A man who let people walk over him. A man who apologized too much and asserted himself too little. The cultural definition was clear: meekness meant you were soft. Meekness meant you were not really a man.

I wanted nothing to do with meekness. I wanted to be strong. I wanted to be the kind of man who did not need to apologize. I wanted to be the kind of man who took what he wanted and did not worry about other people's feelings. I wanted the opposite of meekness.

What I did not know was that I had the word completely wrong. And that my ignorance of the true meaning was costing me everything.

The Greek word used in the New Testament for meekness is praus. And it does not mean weakness. It means something much more specific and much more powerful.

The word was used in ancient Greek to describe a war horse that had been trained. A war horse was one of the most powerful creatures on a battlefield. It could kill with its hooves. It could kill with its weight and strength. But a trained war horse—a meek horse—was a horse that had learned to submit its massive power to the will of its rider.

The horse had not lost its strength. It had not become weak. It had become strong under discipline. Its strength had been brought under governance. Its power had been placed under control.

That is what meekness means. Not the absence of strength. The integration of strength with submission. Not weakness. Disciplined power.

This is the definition that changes everything.

Jesus Christ called himself meek. He said to the disciples, "Come to me, all you who are weary and burdened, and I will give you rest. Take my yoke upon you and learn from me, for I am gentle and humble in heart, and you will find rest for your souls."

The word translated "gentle" in that passage is the same word—praus. Meek. Jesus, the man who walked on water and cast out demons and raised the dead and spoke with absolute authority over Scripture, described himself as meek.

He was not being modest. He was not being self-deprecating. He was claiming to be a man whose power had been brought under the governance of love. He was the strongest man in history, and his strength was expressed through service rather than domination.

This is the revolution that the culture misses. This is what biblical manhood actually is.

I grew up in a culture that had two options for power. Option one was the dominating kind. The kind that takes what it wants. The kind that asserts itself. The kind that makes other people do what you want them to do. This was power, or so I thought.

Option two was to give up power entirely. To become nice. To apologize. To defer. To let other people have what they wanted. This was the soft option. This was the meek option, or so I thought.

What I did not understand is that both of these options are failures. Both of these options are forms of strength without discipline. The dominating man is strong, but his strength is not governed. It is expressing itself through violence—whether physical or emotional or psychological. The man who defers is strong, but his strength is being withheld. He is choosing safety over faithfulness. He is choosing comfort over cost.

Biblical meekness is neither of these. Biblical meekness is strength that is integrated. Strength that is governed. Strength that knows what it is capable of and chooses to express it in service rather than in domination.

This requires a different kind of strength altogether. It is easy to dominate. Any man with size or aggression or social position can dominate. Domination is the lazy form of strength. It is strength that does not have to think about anyone else. It does not have to consider. It does not have to listen. It just takes.

But meekness is hard. Meekness requires that a man know what he is capable of and not do it. A man has to be strong enough to dominate in order to be meek. A man who could not dominate if he wanted to is not meek. He is just weak. He just has no choice.

Meekness is strength making a choice.

This shows up in Jesus's life in ways the Gospels do not always highlight but are there if you are looking for them.

In the Garden of Gethsemane, Jesus has been arrested. He is being tried before the religious authorities. He is being mocked. He is being spat upon. Men are hitting him in the face and demanding he prophesy about who did it.

And Jesus does not fight back. He does not call down angels. He does not use his power.

But this is not because he could not. It is because he chose not to. Matthew 26 gives us the detail: "Then one of those with Jesus reached for his sword, drew it out and struck the servant of the high priest, cutting off his ear. 'Put your sword back in its place,' Jesus said to him, 'for all who draw the sword will die by the sword. Do you think I cannot call on my father, and he will at once put at my disposal more than twelve legions of angels?'"

Jesus was completely capable of defending himself with force. He could have called down twelve legions of angels. He could have destroyed everyone in that courtroom. Instead, he made a choice. He put his power under restraint. He allowed himself to be beaten and mocked and condemned.

That is meekness. That is the integration of infinite power with infinite restraint.

This is what transforms the cross from an act of submission into an act of strength. The crucifixion is not weakness. It is the most powerful display of strength in history. It is the strength to lay down power. It is the strength to absorb violence without returning it. It is the strength to love someone who is destroying you.

Most men do not have this kind of strength. Most men do not have the discipline to let go of the things they could take. Most men do not have the restraint to absorb pain without lashing out. Most men do not have the love to serve someone who is harming them.

But this is what the Bible calls a man to.

When Ephesians 5 calls husbands to love their wives as Christ loved the church, it is not asking for a soft kind of love. It is asking for the strongest kind of love there is. The love that lays down power. The love that absorbs cost. The love that serves even when it is not reciprocated.

I understand why men resist this. I have felt the resistance in my own heart. It feels like loss. It feels like abdication. It feels like giving up the power that makes you a man.

But what you are actually giving up is the counterfeit. What you are actually refusing is the dominance model that was never biblical in the first place.

A man who dominates his wife by virtue of physical strength or social position or financial control is not a biblical man. He is a tyrant. He is a man who has refused to integrate his strength. He is a man who has chosen aggression over meekness.

But a man who loves his wife as Christ loved the church. A man who lays down his authority for her good. A man who serves her in the way Jesus served his disciples by washing their feet. That man is displaying meekness. That man is displaying the integration of strength with restraint.

This is what changes everything in a marriage.

My wife is significantly smaller than I am. I could physically dominate her. I could make decisions without her input. I could use my position in the church to establish authority in the home. I could use my income to make it clear that I have power.

Or I can do something harder. I can listen to her. I can ask her opinion. I can admit when I am wrong. I can let her lead in areas where she is stronger. I can serve her in the way that costs me something.

The first option feels strong in the moment. It feels like power. But it is not strength. It is cowardice. It is a man who is afraid of partnership so he establishes hierarchy. It is a man who is insecure about his worth so he asserts his authority.

The second option is actually hard. It requires that I be strong enough to not use all my strength. It requires discipline. It requires meekness.

And it is the stronger option. In every marriage I know where the man has integrated his strength with service, the marriage is strong. The woman is honored. The man is respected. The partnership functions as God designed it to function.

But in every marriage where the man has chosen domination, I see the erosion. I see the resentment. I see the withdrawal. I see a woman who is learning that her husband cannot be trusted with power because he will use it against her.

This is what the culture gets wrong about biblical manhood. It assumes that if men are not dominating, they are weak. But that is a false choice. There is a third way. The way of meekness. The way of integrated strength.

A man can be firm and also kind. A man can be decisive and also humble. A man can be an authority and also accountable. These are not opposites. They are the same quality expressed in different contexts.

When I am with my children, I am their father. I have authority. I make decisions that affect them. I discipline them. I lead them. But I also listen to them. I admit when I have made a mistake. I ask for forgiveness. I am the strongest person in the room, and I use that strength to protect them and serve them, not to dominate them or shame them.

When I am in my church as a pastor, I have authority. I preach with conviction. I make decisions about the direction of the congregation. I set boundaries. I hold people accountable. But I also listen to dissent. I change my mind when I am wrong. I am vulnerable about my struggle. I do not hide behind the authority.

This is meekness. This is strength under discipline. This is what the biblical man looks like.

The culture will tell you that this is weakness. The culture will tell you that a man who admits he is wrong is not really a man. The culture will tell you that gentleness is the opposite of strength.

But Jesus was the strongest man in history. And Jesus was the gentlest. He could condemn the Pharisees with absolute clarity, and then he could look at a woman caught in adultery and say, "Neither do I condemn you. Go now and leave your life of sin."

He could be judgment and grace at the same time because his strength was integrated. He was not pretending to be gentle. He was not hiding his power. He was expressing power in the way power is supposed to be expressed. In service. In restraint. In love.

This is what a man is called to be. Not soft. Not dominating. Meek. Integrated. Strong in the way that actually matters.

And the only way to get there is to do the hardest work a man can do. The work of integration. The work of bringing your strength under the governance of love. The work of allowing yourself to be shaped by something larger than yourself.

This takes more strength than domination ever requires. This is the work of a lifetime. And it is the only work that makes you actually a man.

Praus is a word that has been destroyed by misunderstanding. It is often translated as meekness, which English turns into weakness. A meek man is a weak man in our language. A man without backbone. A man who cannot stand up for himself. That translation has cost us. It has taught a generation of men that strength and gentleness are opposites. That a man has to choose between power and kindness. The Scripture says something entirely different.

Praus means controlled strength. It is the word used to describe a wild horse that has been broken and trained. The horse has lost none of its power. It has all of its strength. But that strength is now directed. It is under authority. It is channeled toward something other than its own impulse.

When the Scripture uses the word praus for a man, it is describing a man who possesses power and chooses what to do with it. Not a man who is powerless and therefore has no choice. The difference is enormous. A weak man is gentle because he cannot hurt you. A man of praus is gentle because he could hurt you and has chosen not to.

Dietrich Bonhoeffer is one of the most important examples of this kind of man. We know him as a theologian. We know him as a pastor. We know him as a writer whose words changed how entire generations think about faith in a hostile world. What we often do not know is that he was also a conspirator. He was part of a plot to assassinate Adolf Hitler. He had decided that the point had come where the only Christian response was to take a life to save millions.

He was not a man without power. He was not a gentle man because he was weak. He was a gentle man because he had counted the cost of his own convictions and decided that he could be ruthless—actually ruthless, not metaphorically—and still be Christian if that ruthlessness was in service of something larger than himself. He was willing to do the hard thing. He was willing to sin (as he understood it), to take the life of another human being, because the refusal to act was itself a form of betrayal.

In the end, the plot failed. And rather than flee when he could have, he stayed. He was arrested. He was tortured. And from his cell, he wrote letters that still carry more weight than most theology books. He lived as a man of praus under the most hostile circumstances imaginable. He was a man with power—the power of his intellect, the power of his moral authority, the power of his physical person—and he chose at every moment what to do with that power.

He was gentle not because he was weak. He was gentle because he was faithful. And those two things are not the same.

William Wilberforce spent decades in Parliament fighting against slavery. He was a man of means. He was eloquent. He was positioned to influence policy. He had power. What he did with that power was the slow, meticulous work of persuasion. He introduced the same anti-slavery bill year after year. He was defeated. He came back. He was mocked. He persisted. He was gentle in the way he spoke, but he was absolutely ruthless in the goal. He would not stop. He would not compromise on the fundamental principle. He would not accept the incrementalism that would have given him a partial victory and political capital.

That kind of gentleness is not the opposite of strength. It is strength disciplined by purpose. It is power harnessed toward something that will not bend. Wilberforce could have been aggressive. He could have been harsh. He could have used the power he had to shame his opponents into submission. Instead, he chose the harder path. He chose to remain gentle while being absolutely unrelenting.

This is what praus looks like in the public sphere. It is what it looks like in the personal sphere as well.

A father who disciplines his son with gentleness is a man of praus. He is not a man without power. He is a man who possesses the power to strike, to wound with words, to shame into submission. He has all the power he needs to make his son obey through fear. And he chooses not to. Instead, he speaks with firmness. He sets a boundary that his son understands cannot be crossed. He follows through with consequence. But every action is calibrated. Every response is measured. The power is there. It is just not being used to crush the person he loves.

That kind of father is rare. Most fathers are either men without power who let their children dominate, or men with power who use that power to control through fear. The man of praus is something different. He is strong enough to say no and mean it. He is secure enough to say yes and mean that as well. He is under control enough that his emotions do not determine his actions. He is powerful enough that his boundaries are real, not performative.

When a man yells at his son, he is usually not exercising power. He is losing it. His emotion has hijacked his judgment. His anger is in the driver's seat. A man of praus does not need to yell. He speaks with the weight of his own integrity. His word is heavy because he has proven that his word can be trusted. His son knows that when his father says something, it means something.

This is the kind of man who can also hold his child without any agenda. He can sit on the bed next to his son and just be present. Not to interrogate. Not to teach a lesson. Not to extract information. Just to be with him. That kind of presence is power too. It is the power to make another person feel known and safe. It is rare because it requires a man to lay down the constant drive to achieve, to produce, to extract value. It requires him to simply be.

A man teaches his sons what God is like far more through how he disciplines them than through what he says about God. A son who has been corrected firmly but kindly learns that authority can be trusted. That power does not have to be cruel. That someone can hold you to a standard and still love you. That learning requires pain sometimes, but pain does not mean abandonment. A son who has been shamed and broken by his father learns something else entirely. He learns that power is the ability to make someone else small. That love is conditional on performance. That safety is always temporary.

This is why the discipleship of a father is so consequential. He is not just teaching his son facts. He is teaching his son who to become. He is teaching him what strength looks like. He is teaching him what he is allowed to feel. He is teaching him what he is worth.

A man of praus is a man who understands that everything he does is being studied. His son is learning from how he treats his wife. From how he speaks about his boss. From how he responds when he loses. From how he handles his own fear. The man who wants his son to be strong but gentle has to be strong but gentle. The man who wants his son to tell the truth has to tell the truth even when a lie would be easier. The man who wants his son to forgive others has to forgive. Has to visibly, consistently, sometimes painfully, lay down his right to hold a grudge.

This is not performance. This is the actual work of being a man. This is the thing that nobody sees. This is the slow, painstaking, daily discipline of becoming the kind of man your children deserve to be raised by. This is praus. And it is the strongest thing a man can be.

**CHAPTER 5**

THE PROVIDER TRAP

The man sits across from me in my office on a Tuesday afternoon. His hands are steady. His voice is calm. He tells me he has not been home for dinner with his family in four months. Not once. He leaves before the children wake. He comes back after they sleep. His wife manages the household, manages the children, manages the calendar, manages the expectation that he will not be there.

He is doing this, he tells me, for them. For his family. He has taken a second job. He is pushing for the promotion. He is building the nest. He is securing the future.

This is what providing looks like in the American imagination. It is also what abandonment looks like. He does not see this because the culture has convinced him that the checkbook is the same thing as presence. That money is a substitute for attention. That working yourself into invisibility is the highest form of love.

I ask him a question: "What does your wife need from you?"

He does not pause. He lists the bills he pays. The mortgage. The insurance. The college funds he is building for the children. The vacation he is planning for next year.

I ask again: "What does your wife need from you?"

This time there is a pause. A long one. He is confused by the question because no one has ever asked him this before. The pastor who preaches about male leadership has never asked him this. The culture that celebrates the provider has never asked him this. His father did not model this question. His friends do not ask him this.

What your wife needs from you is not a bigger house. It is you at the table. What your wife needs is not more money in the account. It is a man who knows her name on a Tuesday night. What your wife needs is not security for tomorrow. It is presence today.

The Bible says something real about provision. First Timothy 5:8 is not ambiguous: "Anyone who does not provide for their relatives, and especially for their own household, has denied the faith and is worse than an unbeliever." That is not gentle language. That is indictment. A man who refuses to work. A man who leaves his family to fend for themselves. That man has denied the faith. The Bible takes provision seriously.

But the church has weaponized this verse into an excuse for workaholism. We have turned it into a license for absence. We have made it the permission slip for a man to leave his house at 6 a.m., return at 9 p.m., and call himself faithful because the bills are paid. We have preached a gospel of provision that has created a generation of men who are financially present and emotionally gone.

I know this because I have been this man.

In my early years of marriage, I thought provision meant money. I thought the way to love Susanna was to earn enough that she would never have to worry. I worked late. I took extra opportunities. I filled the house with things. I made sure the mortgage was easy, the cars were reliable, the children's education was secure. I was excellent at the external metrics of provision.

What I was not doing was sitting at the table. I was not in the kitchen. I was not asking her about her day because I was not there when her day was happening. I was not present to her in the way that actually matters. I brought her a paycheck. I did not bring her a husband.

The turning point came in a conversation where Susanna said something I have never forgotten: "I don't need a bigger house. I need a man who is in it."

That sentence broke something open in me because it forced me to face a truth I had been avoiding: I had confused provision with presence. I had allowed the culture to redefine my responsibility as a husband into something that required my absence. And she was naming it clearly.

The Hebrew concept that helps us here is shalom. We often translate it as peace, but that is not precise enough. Shalom is wholeness. It is completeness. It is the state of things being right, integrated, whole. When God speaks shalom into creation, God is speaking a word of coherence and completeness. The opposite of shalom is brokenness. Fragmentation. Things falling apart.

What a man is meant to bring to his household is shalom. Not just resources. Not just money. Wholeness. Direction. Presence. Attention. The word that your wife and children need to hear is not just "I have provided for you." The word they need is "I am here. I am paying attention to you. I know you. I see you. Your life matters to me."

This is what the provider often misses. He thinks his presence is implied by his paycheck. He thinks his wife understands that every hour he works late is an hour he chooses her security over his own ease. But what his wife experiences is a man who is not there. What his children experience is a father who is a visitor in their own home.

The culture that celebrates this is broken. It tells men that ambition is love. That sacrifice is absence. That the man who climbs the highest and earns the most is the most faithful husband. But the man who comes home and is not there is not faithful. He is absent. The walls of your house do not need your success more than they need your presence.

There is also a theological problem here that the church has not adequately named. When we make provision the primary measure of a man's faithfulness, we have implicitly said that women and children need money more than they need fathers. We have said that the measure of love is external. We have evacuated the home of its spiritual significance and made it a place where only economic values matter.

But the home is not an economic unit. It is a spiritual unit. It is the place where children learn whether God can be trusted. It is where they learn whether a man's word means anything. It is where they learn whether a father's presence is a gift or a burden. When the father is absent, these lessons are still being taught. The child is learning that money matters more than they do. That productivity matters more than relationships. That the outside world is more important than the inside family.

I have counseled men who came to faith at forty-five years old and realized that they had missed their children's entire childhood in the service of building an empire that they would eventually have to leave behind. I have sat with widows who told me that their husbands provided everything except himself. I have worked with adult children who could not name a single conversation with their father because the conversations never happened.

This is the cost of the provider trap. Not just a wife who feels abandoned. Not just children who grow up with a father who is functionally absent. But a man himself who trades his actual life for a life that looks impressive from the outside. A man who provides for everyone except himself. A man who gives his children money and takes away his presence.

The way forward requires naming something difficult: you are using provision as an excuse for absence. Not consciously. Not maliciously. But functionally. You are telling yourself a story about responsibility and care and duty, and that story allows you to live in a way that requires no vulnerability, no attention, no actual presence. You are safe in work because work does not require you to know anyone. It only requires you to perform.

The question is not whether provision matters. Of course it does. The question is whether provision is sufficient. It is not.

The father who provides well and is not present is like a man who builds his house on sand. It might look impressive for a while. The structure might be sound. The architecture might be beautiful. But when the wind comes, when the rain falls, when the actual weight of life presses down, the house will not hold because the foundation was never there.

Your wife does not need another paycheck. She needs to know that you have chosen her. Actively. Repeatedly. Even when choosing her costs you something. She needs to see you turn off your phone. She needs to see you sit at the table. She needs to see you ask about her day and then listen long enough to actually hear the answer. She needs to know that you see her not as the manager of your household but as the person you chose to spend your life with.

Your children do not need more money. They need a father. Not a concept of a father. Not a father who provides from a distance. But a father who is present. Who knows them. Who sits with them. Who teaches them not through lectures but through presence. Who shows them that being a man means showing up.

The costly hope is this: it is not too late. Even if you have spent years in the provider trap. Even if you have prioritized work and missed your children's childhoods. Even if your wife has become someone who no longer expects you to be there. You can still choose presence. You can still come home. You can still build something that matters more than the external metrics of success.

But it will cost you. It will cost you promotions. It will cost you money. It might cost you the respect of people who believe that a man's worth is measured by his productivity. It will require you to want something different than what the culture is offering.

The man you are becoming is not the man who provides the most. He is the man who is there. He is the man who knows his wife's heart. He is the man who his children can name. He is the man who comes home and that is enough.

That is what it means to be a man.

There was a basketball game. Titus had been working toward it. He had made the travel team. He had gone to the extra practice. He had put in the work. And on the day of the game, I was scheduled to speak at a conference. It was not a small thing. It was a platform where I could influence hundreds of people. It was the kind of opportunity that, five years earlier, I would have structured my entire life around.

I told the conference organizers I could not come. I told them why. I was available to help them find another speaker. But I was not available that day.

Nobody applauded that decision. Nobody wrote me a note of appreciation. Nobody said, "That is the mark of a truly faithful man, prioritizing his child." My church did not put it in the newsletter as an example of what it means to follow Christ. My name did not get more famous because of my absence. I was not celebrated for the choice I made.

What happened instead was this: My son knew his father came to watch him play. That is all. That is everything.

The economics of presence is the most counterintuitive calculation in a man's life because it makes no sense to the world. The world tells a man that his value is his productivity. That his worth is measured in the impact he has and the platform he builds. That the legacy that matters is the one that reaches the most people. A man who turns down a speaking engagement to watch his son play basketball is, by the world's standards, making a bad business decision. He is choosing the small over the great. He is choosing the local over the large.

The Scripture says he is choosing the only thing that will last.

A man cannot be fully present while part of him is climbing the ladder. He cannot be with his family while he is also climbing the ladder. He cannot be with his congregation while he is also climbing the ladder. He cannot be with his own soul while he is also climbing the ladder. Because climbing requires calculation. It requires the constant assessment of which opportunity will move him forward. It requires the evaluation of what is worth his attention based on what it will do for him.

Presence requires something entirely different. It requires the willingness to be where you are, fully, without the constant calculation of whether you should be somewhere else. A man who brings half of himself to his family because the other half is thinking about the meeting at work is not present. A man who sits at dinner while his mind is already tomorrow is not present. A man who watches his son's game while composing an email in his head is not present.

The culture does not ask for a man to be present. It asks for a man to succeed. It will reward the man who sacrifices his family time for the career. It will promote the man who answers emails at 9 pm. It will celebrate the man who turns the speaking engagement into an opportunity to build his platform. It will never acknowledge the man who turned it down.

But his son will know. And that knowing will become the foundation of how his son understands what a man is. A man who shows up. A man who prioritizes. A man who does not just say he loves his family. A man who actually reorganizes his life around that love.

The economics of presence is complicated because work is real. A man has to provide. A man has to be responsible. A man cannot simply opt out of the world and spend every hour with his family. The balance is not simple. It is not a formula. But there is a point at which the balance tips. There is a point at which a man starts making decisions based on what the career demands rather than what the family needs. And at that point, he has already lost something. He may not know it yet. But something has shifted.

I have known men who achieved great things. Men who built organizations. Men who influenced thousands. Men who reached a platform that most people will never access. And I have watched many of them lose their sons. Not all of them. But many of them. The sons did not become failures or reject the faith or go off the rails in any obvious way. They just did not know their fathers. Because their fathers had made a calculation decades earlier that the work was more important than the presence. And they never revised it.

The Hebrew concept of Shabbat is not a break from productivity. It is a refusal of productivity as the metric of worth. It is a man saying, "On this day, I do not produce. I do not achieve. I do not accomplish. And my value does not decrease." Shabbat is resistance. It is the man who says to a culture obsessed with output, "I am not what I make. I am not what I build. I am not what I accomplish. I am what I am known by."

A man who cannot rest cannot be present. Because presence requires the cessation of the constant drive. It requires the ability to stop calculating. It requires the willingness to simply be with the people in front of you, with no agenda other than being with them.

This is counterculture in a way that is almost scandalous. It is more subversive than any of the social positions the culture debates. To say that you will not sacrifice your family on the altar of your career is to question the fundamental assumption of the age. It is to say that success as the world defines it is not worth what it costs. That the platform is not worth the presence. That the reputation is not worth the relationship.

A man can become famous and still be unknown to his own children. A man can have thousands of followers and still eat dinner alone, literally or metaphorically. A man can achieve what the world calls success and still fail at the only thing that actually matters, which is knowing and being known by the people he loves.

The decision to be present is not a single decision. It is a thousand small decisions. It is the decision not to check email at dinner. It is the decision to turn off the phone. It is the decision to put down the book and listen to your son tell the story nobody else thinks is important. It is the decision to cancel the meeting for the baseball game. It is the decision to say no to the opportunity because you have already said yes to your child.

Each of these decisions costs something. Each one is a loss. Each one is the sacrifice of something the culture tells you to value. And each one is the accumulation of your actual life, the life that will matter when the platform is gone and the book is forgotten and the reputation has faded. What remains is not what you built. It is who you became, and whether the people you love know you.

I have spent the last ten years learning something I should have learned in my first year as a pastor. The lesson came not from a sermon I preached but from a question Susanna asked me in the kitchen on a Tuesday night.

> *"You listen to strangers for a living. When was the last time you listened to me with that same attention?"*

The question was not angry. That would have been easier. It was honest. It was the kind of honesty that does not demand a response but makes you sit with yourself and realize that the answer is not good.

I had become the kind of husband who counsels other men about their marriages while his own wife sits at the dinner table waiting for him to look at her the way he looks at the man in his office who is confessing an affair. I had become the kind of pastor who gives his best emotional energy to the congregation and comes home empty. I had become the kind of father who my boys know is always on call for someone else's crisis but rarely fully present for theirs.

This is the unique trap of pastoral ministry. Nobody warns you about it, or they do and you do not believe it will happen to you. You tell yourself you are being faithful. You tell yourself that your presence with the congregation is a reflection of your presence with Christ, that when you listen to that man pour out his heart you are imaging the love of God. All of that is true. But the truth does not absolve you of the cost.

The cost is that your wife goes to bed before you come home. The cost is that your children learn to entertain themselves rather than be entertained by you. The cost is that the people who love you most see you at your worst—tired, distracted, irritable—while the people who barely know you see your best—patient, wise, attentive.

This is the burden of the pastoral marriage that nobody talks about except in the hollow language of balance. As if balance were possible. As if you could spend two hours counseling someone in crisis and then come home and be fully present to your wife who is asking you how your day was. The emotional bandwidth does not work that way. The pastoral presence depletes something. And if you do not actively and consciously choose to reverse the flow of your presence, your marriage becomes the thing you give your leftovers to.

I began to notice something in my congregation. The men who were thriving in their marriages were not the men who worked less. They were the men who made a deliberate choice about where their presence went. They were the men who said: at five o'clock I stop answering emails. At seven o'clock I put my phone away. At dinner, I listen to my wife the way I listen to people in my office. On Saturday, the congregation does not have access to me. My wife and children do. Some of these men held significant positions. Some of them were under more pressure than I was. But they had made a choice. And the choice had consequences. Some people in their congregation felt it when their presence was not available. But their marriages thrived.

The opposite was also true. The men whose marriages were failing all told the same story. They were too busy being a pastor to be a husband. They were too focused on the congregation to focus on their wife. They were so consumed with shepherding others that they forgot to shepherd their own household. And then one day they looked up and their wife had checked out. Not from bitterness. From loneliness. She had stopped waiting for his presence and started accepting his absence.

This is what I had to confront in my kitchen on that Tuesday night. I was becoming one of those men.

The consequence of that realization was not guilt. Guilt would have been easy—a feeling I could sit with and then move on. The consequence was a choice. I had to decide whether my primary ministry was to my congregation or to my marriage. Not either/or. But which one claimed my first allegiance. Which one would get my best attention, my most alert mind, my most patient listening.

The answer to that question changed everything.

It meant that Tuesday and Thursday nights became non-negotiable with my wife. No late counseling appointments. No church meetings. No emergencies except genuine emergencies. It meant that Saturday morning was protected time with my sons. Not something I fit in around the ministry schedule. The ministry schedule fit around them. It meant that I had to learn to say to a man in crisis: I care about you, and I want to help you, but I cannot meet with you today. It meant that my congregation had to learn that I was not available at all hours, that their pastor had a wife and children who had first claim on his time.

This created resistance. Not hostility, but confusion. Some people in the congregation had become accustomed to my being accessible whenever they needed me. They had to learn that access has limits. Some of them judged me for it. Some of them thought I was being selfish, prioritizing my family over my calling. But they were wrong about that. Prioritizing my family is part of my calling. The man who abandons his wife in the name of serving God is not serving God. He is serving his own avoidance.

What Susanna did not know when she asked that question is that she was not just confronting me about my presence. She was confronting me about the system I had built. The system where the church came first. The system where my wife was the backstop for whatever energy was left. The system where I believed my pastoral presence was so critical that I could not afford to limit it. All of that was true enough. The church does need pastoral presence. But the system was designed in a way that guaranteed the destruction of the one relationship that was supposed to model Christ's love to everyone else.

So I had to change the system. Not by doing less ministry. By doing less of the kind of ministry that required my physical presence at all hours and all days. By training leaders. By setting boundaries. By being the kind of pastor who was available but not omnipresent.

This is where the real work happens. Not in deciding that your marriage matters. In reorganizing your life so that your decisions reflect that priority. It is easy to say your marriage is your first priority. It is hard to say no to a man who needs you because you need to be present to your wife. It is easy to believe you are serving God by serving the church. It is hard to believe you are serving God by serving your marriage.

But every man in my congregation who is struggling in his marriage is watching me. Not to see if I can build a bigger church. To see if I can keep my wife. To see if I can have a family that loves me not because I am their pastor but because I am present. To see if the gospel is true enough to actually work at home, where there is nowhere to hide.

That is the ministry that matters. Not the number of people sitting in the pew. The number of people at the dinner table who know that their father's presence is real.

**CHAPTER 6**

THE ANGER PROBLEM

The culture says anger is toxic. Men should not feel it. They should process it. They should regulate it. They should channel it into productivity or transcend it into forgiveness. Anger is the last stop before violence. Anger is what broken men feel. Anger is a problem to be solved.

The Bible says something different. Ephesians 4:26: "In your anger do not sin. Do not let the sun go down on your anger."

Note what that verse does not say. It does not say "do not be angry." It does not say anger is always wrong or that good men do not feel it. It says be angry. And then it says something harder: do not sin in your anger. Do not let it sit. Do not let it ferment. Do not let it become something it was never meant to be.

The problem is not anger. God experiences anger. Read the Old Testament. God is angry at injustice. God is angry at idolatry. God is angry at the abuse of the vulnerable. The psalms are full of the anger of God. "The Lord is slow to anger, abounding in love, but he will not leave the guilty unpunished" (Numbers 14:18). God's anger and God's justice are not separate things. They are the same thing said two different ways.

Jesus overturned tables in the temple. That is not a calm moment. That is not a man processing his emotions in a healthy way. That is a man in the grip of righteous indignation at something obscene happening in the house of God. John 11 tells us that when Jesus stood before Lazarus's tomb, he wept. But the Greek word there is also connected to anger. He was disturbed. He was moved. He was experiencing something that was both sorrow and fury at the death that sin brings.

So the problem is not anger itself. The problem is unprocessed anger. It is anger that has no language. It is anger that has no outlet. It is anger that men have learned to convert into something else because they were never taught to handle it.

This is where I need to be honest about what I carry.

I grew up without a father. That absence creates a particular kind of anger in a boy. It is not the obvious kind of anger. It is not hot. It is cold. It is a rage at something that cannot be fixed because it happened before you had any ability to prevent it or address it. You cannot confront the man who left. You cannot make him explain. You cannot force him to take responsibility. So the anger becomes something else. It becomes a constant hum beneath everything. It becomes the thing that makes you overachieve. It becomes the thing that makes you fight with people who are not actually your enemy. It becomes the thing that makes you interpret every criticism as proof that you are not enough.

I carried that into marriage. I carried it into ministry. I carried it into fatherhood. I did not call it anger. I called it drive. I called it passion. I called it conviction. But Susanna knew what it was. She could feel it in the way I responded to small things. She could see it in the way I would snap at the children over nothing. She could recognize it in the arguments we had where I was not actually listening to her. I was defending against something she was not attacking.

The anger was coming from below the surface. It was anger at my father for his absence. It was anger at myself for not being enough to make him stay. It was anger at God for letting that happen. None of that anger was anywhere near the surface. I did not have language for it. I had no idea I was carrying it. What I had was a man who could not regulate his emotions because he had never processed the core wound underneath them.

This is the tragedy that the church and culture have created together. Men are not taught to feel. They are taught to convert every emotion that is not anger into anger because anger feels like control. A boy feels afraid. He converts it to anger. A boy feels sad. He converts it to anger. A boy feels ashamed. He converts it to anger. A boy feels lonely. He converts it to anger. Anger is the one emotion he is allowed. So he uses it to manage everything.

The result is men who are walking around with a suitcase full of converted emotions, and it is poisoning everything they touch. They explode at their wives over a dish in the sink because the anger is not really about the dish. The anger is about the feeling that they are not doing enough, not providing enough, not being enough. They snap at their children for asking a normal question because the anger is not really about the question. The anger is about the feeling that they are failing.

Proverbs 29:11: "Fools give full vent to their rage, but the wise bring themselves under control." That is often read as "good men do not get angry." But that is not what it says. It says that the fool gives full vent. The wise bring themselves under control. Control requires something other than suppression. It requires understanding. It requires naming what is actually happening beneath the anger.

James 1:19-20 gives us the path: "My dear brothers and sisters, take note of this: everyone should be quick to listen, slow to speak and slow to become angry, because human anger does not produce the righteousness that God desires."

The instruction is not "do not become angry." The instruction is "slow to become angry." That is different. That is a pause. That is the space between stimulus and response where something other than automatic reaction can happen. That is the moment where you ask yourself: what am I actually angry about right now?

The man who will never become angry is not a man who has achieved mastery. He is a man who is suppressing. He is a man who is converting his anger into something else — maybe depression, maybe passive aggression, maybe alcoholism, maybe workaholism. The man who can be angry and not sin is the man who has learned to slow down enough to understand what is actually happening.

There is also a gender piece here that needs to be named. Men are socialized to believe that anger is power. That anger is strength. That anger is what keeps you from being vulnerable. And the culture celebrates this. The man who is angry is scary. He is feared. He is in control. The man who is sad or scared or lonely or uncertain is weak. So men learn to default to anger because anger feels like power.

But this is a lie. Anger is not power. Anger is pain that has nowhere to go. The man who is actually powerful is the man who can feel his sadness without needing to transform it. The man who can acknowledge his fear without needing to prove that he is not afraid. The man who can admit that he is lonely. That is the man with actual power because he is not spending all his energy managing his image.

I had to learn this the hard way. I had to have my anger named by my wife in a moment where I could not ignore it. We were in a conflict. I was angry. And she said something I have never forgotten: "You are not arguing with me right now. You are arguing with your father."

That sentence stopped me. It broke through the layer where I was convinced that my anger was about the current conflict. It forced me to look down to the layer beneath where the real anger was living. And once I could see it, once I could name it, once I could acknowledge it, the anger I was feeling about her in that moment began to separate from the anger that had been with me since I was a child.

That did not mean the anger disappeared. It meant I could respond to it. It meant I could ask myself: is my wife the one I am actually angry at? No. Am I angry at the circumstance? Yes. Am I angry at myself? Probably. Am I angry at the way this feels like the abandonment I have always feared? Yes.

And once you can answer those questions, you can actually have a conversation. You can actually be with another person.

The work of a man who wants to grow is not to eliminate his anger. It is to get to the bottom of it. What are you actually angry about? And usually when you ask that question, the answer is not what you thought. The anger at your wife for speaking a certain way is anger at yourself for not being the man you thought you would be. The anger at your child for something small is anger at how small your own childhood made you feel. The anger at your pastor for a sermon is anger at God for something you have not said out loud.

Beneath anger at the external is a wound in the internal.

This is why accountability needs to be real between men. Not surveillance. Not the kind of accountability that is about catching someone in sin and exposing them. But the kind of accountability where another man can say: "I see anger in you. What is underneath it?" And you can actually answer.

I have several men in my life who have earned the right to ask me that question. Not because they are perfect. Not because they are older or wiser. But because they have proven that they actually want to know me. Not my public self. The man I am when no one is watching. The man who carries things. The man who sometimes gets angry at the wrong person for the wrong reason because the real wound is below the surface.

Those men have asked me that question many times. What is underneath the anger? And because they have built trust over years, I can answer. I can say: "I am afraid that I am not enough. I am afraid that my boys will grow up and realize that I failed them. I am afraid that Susanna will wake up one day and recognize that she married a man who was broken in a particular way and that my brokenness is now her problem."

Those are the real things. And they are not new. They are old. They are from the wound of the father I did not have. And until I could name them, I was spending my life proving they were not true by being the angriest, strongest, most driven version of myself. Which just exhausted me.

The path forward is not to become a man who does not get angry. That is not the goal. The goal is to become a man who knows what his anger is telling him. The goal is to slow down. To listen. To ask yourself what you are actually feeling beneath the rage.

And then to do something harder. To bring that to someone you trust. To say: "I am carrying something and I do not know how to put it down."

That is where the healing happens. Not in the absence of anger. But in the understanding of it. In the naming of it. In the bringing of it into the light.

Susanna helped me understand that my anger was not a strength to be proud of. It was a signal that something had not been dealt with. And once I could hear that signal, once I could pay attention to what my anger was trying to tell me about myself, I could actually change.

The man you are becoming is not the man who never gets angry. He is the man who understands his anger. He is the man who can feel it and still choose how he responds. He is the man who knows that beneath his fury there is a hurt that deserves attention. And he is the man strong enough to pay attention to it.

That is what it means to be a man.

A man has four emotions. That is the vocabulary. He is fine. He is angry. He is tired. He is good. Everything else gets compressed into those four categories. Grief becomes anger. Fear becomes anger. Loneliness becomes anger. Shame becomes anger. Confusion becomes anger. The nervous system of a man is wired to translate the complex internal states into one output channel. And that channel is aggression.

This is not because men are evil. This is because men have been trained. From boyhood, a man learns that certain emotions are not safe to express. He learns that sadness is weakness. That fear is shameful. That vulnerability is dangerous. He learns that the only acceptable way to be strong is to not feel. So he does not feel. Or more precisely, he trains himself not to notice what he is feeling until it becomes so large that it emerges as anger.

A man who cannot name what he is feeling cannot address what he is feeling. He can only react to it. When grief comes and he does not know how to say "I am grieving," he says "I am angry." When fear comes and he does not know how to say "I am afraid," he says "I am angry." When loneliness comes and he does not know how to say "I am lonely," he says "I am angry." And the people around him learn to fear his anger without understanding that his anger is the symptom, not the disease.

The disease is the inability to feel.

Teaching a man to name his emotions is not making him weak. It is giving him the accurate diagnostic tool to understand himself. It is the difference between a man who throws a chair because he is frustrated and cannot find another way to express the frustration, and a man who sits down and says, "I feel frustrated. I need to figure out why." The first man is ruled by his emotion. The second man has authority over it.

This is not therapeutic language. This is practical. A man who knows what he is feeling has choices. A man who does not know what he is feeling has only reactions. A man who can say "I am afraid" to his wife can actually discuss what he is afraid of. They can work through it together. A man who translates fear into anger will destroy the relationship trying to prove he is not afraid.

Teaching a son to be emotionally literate is one of the most important disciplines of fatherhood. And it is almost never modeled. The boy watches his father meet every difficult emotion with the same response. Something hurts. Dad gets angry. Something goes wrong at work. Dad gets angry. Something reminds him of a failure from his past. Dad gets angry. And the boy learns: This is how you are a man. You do not feel. You react.

The process of learning to name an emotion is not complicated. It is just uncomfortable. A man sits with his son and says, "Something happened today that bothered me. I do not know exactly what I am feeling, but it is not anger. It might be disappointment. It might be fear. Let me sit with it for a minute." The boy watches his father not know what he is feeling and not panic about not knowing. He watches his father wait. He watches his father try to describe it. He watches his father be confused and vulnerable and still be a man.

That is the modeling that changes everything. Not because the father has solved the problem. But because the father has modeled the process of being in touch with himself without being destroyed by it.

There is a particular moment I remember with one of my sons. He was maybe twelve. We were driving somewhere, and I could tell something was wrong. Not angry wrong. Something else. I asked him what was going on. He said, "I do not know. I just feel weird." We drove for a while without talking. And then I said, "Weird can mean a lot of things. Can you describe it?" He thought about it. He said, "It is like everything is moving too fast and I do not know how to slow it down."

That is anxiety. But we did not name it that way. We just named the experience. We described what it felt like. And something shifted. Because now it had a shape. It was not just this undefined thing happening to him. It was a describable experience. And describable experiences can be managed.

We talked about what made it worse and what made it better. We came back to his breathing. We came back to the fact that sometimes your body gets ahead of your mind and you have to slow the body down. We came back to the recognition that this feeling was not dangerous. It was just a feeling. It would pass.

Two weeks later, he came to me and said, "I have that weird feeling again." And he said it with recognition rather than panic. Because he knew what it was. Because he had a word for it. Because he had seen that his father had emotions too and could sit with them.

This is different from teaching your son that emotions are good and should be celebrated. That is a different error. Emotions are information. Some of it is useful. Some of it is misleading. A man has to learn to listen to his emotions and also to think clearly about them. He has to learn to say "I am angry" without acting on the anger immediately. He has to learn to feel his fear and also to step forward anyway. He has to learn to sit with his sadness without becoming paralyzed by it.

The emotional literacy of a man is the difference between a man who destroys relationships because he cannot manage his own internal state, and a man who can stay in a difficult conversation because he knows what he is feeling and knows that his feelings do not require him to blow up the room.

A man learns emotional literacy in one place primarily: from his father. Not from therapy (though therapy can be helpful). Not from a book. Not from a support group. From his father. Because his father is the first man he learns from. The first man whose emotions he sees. The first man whose responses he mirrors.

If a man wants his son to be able to name what he is feeling, he has to be able to name what he is feeling. If a man wants his son to know that sadness is not shameful, he has to let his son see him sad. If a man wants his son to know that fear is survivable, he has to admit that he is afraid sometimes. If a man wants his son to be able to sit with his wife in a difficult conversation and say "I do not know what I am feeling, but I know it is not fine," then that man has to do that with his own wife.

This is not easy. It is the opposite of easy. It is the daily decision to be less defended. To be more honest. To let people see that you are not invulnerable. That you are complicated. That you are still figuring things out. That you need other people.

But this is also the only path to actual strength. Not the invulnerability that the culture celebrates. But the real strength of a man who knows himself well enough to be honest about it. Who is secure enough in who he is to admit what he does not know. Who is man enough to say "I am afraid" and keep walking anyway. That is the man his son will become if he is willing to show him what one looks like.

**CHAPTER 7**

SEX, LUST, AND THE WAR INSIDE

The pastor stands at the pulpit on a Sunday morning in 1998. He has just launched the True Love Waits campaign at his church. Hundreds of youth are signing pledge cards in front of the congregation. They will wait until marriage. They will protect their purity. They will not waste their most precious gift on someone who does not deserve it.

The boys in that room hear something specific. They hear that their desire is dangerous. They hear that their bodies are weapons waiting to be mishandled. They hear that sex outside marriage is not just a sin. It is a betrayal. It is a defilement. It is the thing that will destroy you. The girl next to them hears something different. But for the boy sitting in that pew, the message is clear: your sexuality is a problem. Your desire is your greatest weakness. Your body is your enemy.

That boy is now a man. He is sitting in my office. He is thirty-seven years old, married for eight years, and he cannot maintain an erection with his wife. He does not want to look at pornography, but it is what he can feel. He does not want to be broken, but he is. He has internalized the message that his sexuality is dangerous and now he cannot access it even in the one place where it is supposed to be good.

This is the catastrophic result of the church's approach to male sexuality. We have created a generation of men who associate their desire with shame. Men who fight a private war with no allies. Men who hide rather than confess. Men who think the problem is porn when the actual problem is the theology that made them afraid of their own bodies in the first place.

The purity movement made sex into the primary measure of a man's spiritual health. But it did something more destructive. It made sex the measure of a man's value. Stay pure and you are worthy. Fall and you are damaged. But the problem is that purity is a construct that cannot hold because it is built on a lie. The lie is that sexuality is the enemy of spirituality. That your body is at war with your soul. That the desire God gave you is something you must overcome.

Scripture says something very different. The Song of Solomon is in the Bible. It is not there by accident. It is there because the desire between a man and a woman is not a failure of spirituality. It is an expression of it.

Read the Song: "How beautiful you are, my darling. Oh, how beautiful. Your eyes are doves. Your hair is like a flock of goats descending from Mount Gilead." That is not the language of shame. That is the language of a man overwhelmed by the beauty and sexuality of the woman he loves. The poem goes on: "Your breasts are like two fawns, like twin fawns of a gazelle that browse among the lilies."

This is erotic poetry. It is in Scripture. It is affirming the body. It is celebrating desire. And then the woman speaks: "My beloved is mine and I am his. He browses among the lilies. Before the day breaks and the shadows flee, turn, my beloved, and be like a gazelle or like a young stag on the rugged hills."

That is a woman initiating sex. That is Scripture celebrating a woman's sexual agency. The presence of the Song of Solomon in Scripture is a theological statement: sexual desire is good. The body is good. Pleasure is not a temptation to be resisted. It is a gift to be received.

But the purity movement could not hold this theology. It had to make sexuality dangerous in order to keep teenagers from having it. So it lied. It said that your desire will destroy you. It said that your sexuality outside marriage is poison. It said that the thing you are feeling is evidence of your weakness.

First Corinthians 6:19-20: "Do you not know that your bodies are temples of the Holy Spirit, who is in you, whom you have received from God? You are not your own; you were bought at a price. Therefore honor God with your bodies."

The church preached this verse to mean: your body is holy, so do not touch it. But that is not what the verse says. It says your body is a temple. A temple is not a prison. A temple is the place where the sacred happens. The instruction is not to deny your body. The instruction is to honor your body. To steward it. To recognize that what happens in your body matters.

The tragedy is that this theology of the body as temple could have been liberating. It could have said: your sexuality is sacred. Your desire is created by God. What you do with your body matters because it is holy. That would have created very different sexual ethics. Not a prohibition based on shame. But a stewardship based on reverence.

Instead, the church created shame. And shame is the worst possible foundation for sexual ethics because shame does not create purity. Shame creates hiding. Shame creates the split between public and private self that allows pornography to flourish. Shame creates the man who presents himself as pure on Sunday and is at war with his body on Tuesday.

Pornography is not the cause of this war. It is a symptom. Pornography thrives in the soil of shame. A man who has been taught that his desire is dangerous will eventually find a place where his desire can be satisfied without exposure. Pornography offers anonymity. It offers the ability to access desire without anyone knowing. Without shame being witnessed. Without the need to integrate the sexual self with the spiritual self.

The actual problem is that we have told men that their sexuality is something separate from their spirituality. That their bodies are battlegrounds. That their desire is the enemy they must defeat. And then we act shocked when they find a way to access that desire in secret.

The path forward is not more shame. It is not accountability software or masturbation tracking or the kind of surveillance relationships that have become normal in evangelical Christianity. The path forward is better theology.

Theology that says: your sexuality is good. Theology that says: the desire God put in your body is not a mistake. Theology that says: the question is not how to destroy your desire but how to direct it. How to steward it. How to integrate it into who you are becoming.

This is where the conversation about pornography actually needs to happen. Not: "Do not look at pornography because it is sinful and you are weak." But rather: "What is pornography doing for you? What need is it meeting? What are you not getting that you are trying to get there?"

Usually the answer is not primarily about sexual release. It is about being desired. It is about being seen. It is about the fantasy that someone finds you attractive enough to do anything for. It is about the temporary experience of being powerful in a way that your actual life does not allow. It is about escape. It is about the only place where you can feel desire without shame.

The man who is addicted to pornography is not fundamentally a man with a sexuality problem. He is a man with a loneliness problem. He is a man with a worth problem. He is a man who does not feel seen by anyone in his real life.

The invitation is not to white-knuckle abstinence. The invitation is to ask yourself: what am I actually searching for? And then to bring that search into the light. To your wife. To your friends. To a counselor. To God. To say: "I feel unseen. I feel undesired. I feel like I am not enough. And I am trying to meet that need in a way that is destroying me and destroying my marriage."

That is the conversation that actually matters.

For a married man, the work is to recover the ability to access desire with his wife without shame. This requires his wife's partnership. It requires her understanding that his body is not the enemy. That his desire for her is not something to be suspicious of. That his sexuality is an expression of his love, not a separate thing that competes with it.

The Song of Solomon models this. The man desires the woman. The woman desires the man. They pursue each other. They initiate. They play. They speak openly about what they want. And this is celebrated in Scripture.

But for this to happen, a man has to recover his body. He has to reintegrate the sexual self with the spiritual self. He has to move from the place of shame to the place of stewardship. This is not fast work. This is not the work of a weekend seminar or a book. This is the work of years.

For the man in my office whose body has betrayed him, the work begins with a different conversation. Not with his pastor about his sin. But with a good therapist about his theology. About the messages he internalized as a boy. About the way fear got converted into shame. About the way his body became the enemy instead of the temple.

And then, slowly, the conversation shifts. He learns to talk with his wife about desire. Not as a transaction. But as a conversation between two people who have chosen each other. He learns to ask for what he wants. She learns to ask for what she wants. The body becomes a place of conversation rather than a place of shame.

The way forward also requires us to tell the truth about pornography as a cultural problem, not just an individual sin problem. Pornography exists in a culture that has commodified sexuality. That has turned bodies into objects. That has trained men to see women as things to be consumed. This is not primarily a problem of individual willpower. It is a problem of how we have structured sexuality in this culture.

But that is not the conversation for this chapter. The conversation for this chapter is about the theology of your own body. About the work of integrating your desire into who you are becoming as a man.

The expensive truth is this: you were not made to war with yourself. You were made to steward yourself. Your sexuality is not a failure of your spirituality. It is a dimension of your humanity. The question is not how to kill it. The question is how to direct it.

The man you are becoming is not a man without desire. He is a man whose desire is integrated. He is a man who can feel his sexuality and still choose to honor his wife. He is a man whose body is not at war with his soul. He is a man who knows that the deepest act of worship is to offer his whole self, body and mind and spirit, to the woman he has chosen.

That is what it means to be a man.

The conversation most churches have about pornography is fundamentally broken. It operates from a premise that is backwards: that a man's sexual desire is the problem to be eliminated rather than the thing to be redeemed.

The standard approach is now so familiar it has become invisible. Accountability software on every device. Confession groups where men report their failures. Shame spirals that begin as conviction and end as condemnation. The underlying message, stated or not, is this: your sexuality is dangerous. Your longing is your enemy. Your body is a threat you must manage.

This creates a specific kind of man. A man who learns to compartmentalize. A man who hides what he feels. A man who, when he fails, does not bring the failure into the light where it can actually be addressed. Instead, he hides it deeper, ashamed not just of the act but of the desire itself. The desire becomes unspeakable. And what is unspeakable cannot be healed.

The purity movement of the 1990s and early 2000s crystallized this theology into metaphors that still haunt a generation. A girl is chewed gum. A girl is used tape. A girl is a flower that has already been picked. The message to boys was direct: your desire makes you a predator. The message to girls was equally clear: your body is a prize, and once it is taken, you have no value left.

I was shaped by this teaching. I absorbed it. And I have spent years unlearning it while watching it destroy men I love.

Here is what needs to be said clearly: The desire itself is not the problem.

God made you as a sexual being. That is not an accident. That is not a regrettable aspect of creation that the gospel is meant to transcend. Your longing for sexual intimacy is not a fallen impulse that must be eradicated. It is a created impulse that must be rightly ordered.

There is an enormous difference between these two statements: "Your desire is dangerous, so suppress it" and "Your desire is good, but it is misdirected, so redirect it."

The first statement makes a man hide. The second makes a man examine. And examination is the doorway to change.

When a man is taught that his sexual longing is inherently evil, he does what any of us do with things we are told are evil: he keeps them secret. The desire goes underground. It becomes shameful precisely because he has no language for it except the language of sin. He cannot distinguish between lust and longing. He cannot bring the desire to God because he has been taught that bringing it to God is evidence of his corruption, not his honesty.

But when a man is taught that his sexual longing is a created good that has been misdirected, something different happens. He can look at his desire. He can ask what it is really asking for. He can see that pornography is not fulfilling a sexual need. It is a response to a relational need. The loneliness. The disconnection. The sense of not being known and still being wanted anyway. Pornography promises intimacy while delivering isolation. It promises to know him while requiring that he remain unknown.

When a man sees that clearly, he does not need more shame. He needs an alternative. He needs to know that his longing for intimacy is not his corruption. It is his humanity. And there are ways to honor that longing that do not require him to be alone with a screen and a false version of another person.

This is why the gospel response to sexual failure is so different from the therapeutic response or the shaming response. The gospel does not say your desire is the problem. It says your past is not the final word on your future. You are not your worst moment. Your sexuality is not your enemy. Christ's blood covers more than your past. It redeems your future.

A man who understands this can repent. Not from a place of self-condemnation but from a place of reorientation. I was reaching for intimacy in a way that destroyed intimacy. I was seeking to be known in a way that required me to remain unknown. I was trying to satisfy a real hunger with something that would only increase the hunger.

Now I can turn. Now I can bring my longing into the light. Now I can ask for what I actually need.

This is the conversation the church should be having. Not: How do I eliminate my desire? But: How do I honor the design I have been given? Not: How do I punish myself for my failure? But: What does it mean to bring my whole self, including my sexuality, under the Lordship of Christ?

That is a conversation that leads somewhere. That is a gospel that heals.

**CHAPTER 8**

LEADERSHIP IS NOT WHAT YOU THINK

The word leadership has been corrupted. In the church, we speak of male leadership as if it means authority. The right to make decisions. The final say in matters. The position at the top. We have built entire theologies on the assumption that to lead is to be in charge. That the leader decides. That the followers obey. That structure flows downward from the man to his family.

This is not what the Bible means by leadership. But it is what the American church has taught, and it has created a generation of men who believe that their authority is their identity.

Ephesians 5:25-27: "Husbands, love your wives, just as Christ loved the church and gave himself up for her to make her holy, cleansing her by the washing with water through the word, and to present her to himself as a radiant church, without stain or wrinkle or any other blemish, but holy and blameless."

Read that again. The instruction is not: husbands, lead your wives. The instruction is: husbands, love your wives as Christ loved the church. And what did Christ do? He gave himself up for the church. That is the model. That is the leadership we are called to.

This is what makes the verse so difficult for men who want to be in charge. Because what it is actually saying is that male leadership means going first into sacrifice. Going first into vulnerability. Going first into the place of service. The leader in the Christ model does not stay at the top and make the follower climb. The leader descends.

John 13 is where this model becomes visceral. Jesus takes off his outer garment. He wraps a towel around his waist. He gets down on his knees and washes the feet of his disciples. This is the leader. Not standing above them. Not making decisions for them. But on his knees doing the work that was considered the job of the lowest servant in that culture.

And then Jesus says: "Now that I, your Lord and Teacher, have washed your feet, you also should wash one another's feet. I have set you an example that you should do as I have done for you."

The leadership model is the basin and towel. It is kneeling. It is washing. It is the work that no one else wants to do. If you want to lead, you go first into service. You do not command service from above. You model service from below.

The church has inverted this. We have taken the model of Christ and turned it into a hierarchy where the man is at the top and everyone else is below. We have called it headship. We have called it authority. We have preached it as the God-given order. And what we have created is a form of leadership that is the opposite of the model that Jesus taught.

I know this because I lived it. I led my home the way I led my church. From authority. I made the decisions. My word was final. I did not consult. I did not ask. I determined what was best and I moved forward. And I called it leadership.

What I was actually doing was dominating. What I was actually creating was a home where my wife and children learned that their thoughts did not matter. That their input was not valued. That leadership meant not having to listen to anyone below you.

It took Susanna naming this for me to see it. She said: "You do not lead. You decide. There is a difference."

That sentence broke me open because it was true. I had confused authority with leadership. I had assumed that leadership meant I got to be right. That leadership meant I did not have to be influenced by anyone. That leadership meant having the final word.

But the model that Jesus offered is completely different. Jesus led by influence, not by force. Jesus led by example, not by edict. Jesus led by entering into the world of the people he was leading. He ate with them. He listened to them. He was affected by them. He wept with them. He was changed by them.

When the Canaanite woman came to Jesus asking for healing for her daughter, Jesus initially refused her. But she persisted. She argued. And Jesus was influenced by her. He changed his mind. And then he said: "Woman, you have great faith. Your request is granted." That is a moment where the leader is actually changed by the follower. The leader listens. The leader is influenced. The leader moves from his initial position.

This is what leadership actually looks like. It is not: I have decided and you will follow. It is: I am open to being influenced by what I hear from you. It is: your voice matters. It is: I will be changed by what you teach me.

In a marriage, this means the husband is not the decision-maker. He is the servant-leader. He listens to his wife. He values her perspective. He is influenced by her wisdom. And then, together, you move forward.

The early church modeled this in a way that was countercultural. In the Roman empire, power flowed downward. The emperor decided. The generals obeyed. The soldiers obeyed. The hierarchy was absolute. And then you have the early Christians. They are calling each other brothers and sisters. They are practicing foot-washing. They are saying that the first will be last and the last will be first. They are inverting the entire power structure.

Peter writes: "All of you, clothe yourselves with humility toward one another, because God opposes the proud but shows favor to the humble" (1 Peter 5:5). Not: the leaders should be humble while the followers obey. But: all of you. Every single person. Clothe yourselves with humility.

This is subversive. This is destabilizing. This is the opposite of the leadership model that the culture offers.

The man who wants to be the kind of leader that Jesus modeled does something very difficult. He admits that he does not have all the answers. He admits that his wife might be right about something he was wrong about. He admits that his children see things he cannot see. He admits that being in charge is less important than being connected.

I had to make this shift in my marriage and it cost me. It cost me the security of always being right. It cost me the power of having the final say. It cost me the ability to make decisions unilaterally. But what I gained was infinitely more valuable. I gained a wife who actually wanted to follow me because she was not being forced to follow. I gained children who respected me because they saw that I respected them. I gained a home where decisions were made together rather than imposed from above.

This shift happened slowly. It happened through many conversations where Susanna would point out that I was making a decision without her input. It happened through moments where my children would say something wise and I would have to admit that I had not thought of it that way. It happened through the slow and painful work of learning to listen.

The basin and towel are not metaphorical. There are actual things that the leader does. The father who leads like Jesus is in the kitchen with his wife. He is helping with homework with his children. He is present to the life of his family. He is not delegating the care of his home to his wife while he focus on his work. He is living with them. He is serving them. He is present to them.

There is a practical piece here that needs to be said. Servant leadership does not mean you have no boundaries. It does not mean you say yes to everything. It does not mean you have no voice. Servant leadership means you are willing to sacrifice for those you lead, but you are not a doormat. You have convictions. You have standards. You have things you will not compromise on.

But the way you hold those things is different. You do not impose them. You explain them. You invite people into them. You are open to being wrong about them. You hold them with an open hand rather than a closed fist.

The man in my church who is now stepping into this kind of leadership told me recently: "I used to think leadership meant I had to have it all figured out. Now I understand that leadership means I have to be willing to work it out with the people I love."

That is the shift. From authority to influence. From decision-maker to servant. From having all the answers to being willing to explore the questions together.

Jesus modeled this so consistently. He asked questions. He did not make proclamations and expect obedience. He asked: "What do you think? What do you want? What do you believe?" He was engaged. He was curious. He was affected by the people around him.

The leadership that Jesus offered was not about him staying at the top. It was about everyone being elevated. It was about the disciples eventually doing the same works that he did. It was about them becoming leaders themselves. The measure of his leadership was not his authority. It was whether the people he led became leaders.

In a marriage, this means a husband's leadership is measured not by whether his wife obeys him. It is measured by whether his wife is becoming the woman God made her to be. Whether she is growing. Whether she is confident. Whether she is flourishing.

In a family, it means a father's leadership is measured not by whether his children obey him. It is measured by whether his children are becoming people of integrity, wisdom, and courage. Whether they are developing their own convictions. Whether they are learning to think and decide for themselves.

This is expensive. This is harder than authority. Authority requires no relationship. You simply tell people what to do. But servant leadership requires that you actually know people. That you understand them. That you are affected by them. That you are willing to let them change you.

I had to grieve what I was losing to gain what I was gaining. I had to let go of the identity of the man who was always right. I had to admit that I did not have it all figured out. I had to become smaller so that the people I loved could become larger.

And that is what made me a better leader. Not smaller in influence. But different in kind. My influence came not from authority. It came from trust. It came from the fact that Susanna knew that I was willing to listen to her. That my children knew that I valued their input. That the people I lead know that I am not trying to control them but to serve them.

The costly truth is that leadership like this requires you to be vulnerable. It requires you to admit that you have been wrong. It requires you to change your mind when presented with better information. It requires you to kneel.

The man you are becoming is not a man who is in charge. He is a man who is in relationship. He is a man who leads by example rather than by edict. He is a man who is willing to be changed by the people he loves. He is a man who understands that the greatest authority is the authority that comes from being trusted. And trust is earned not by being right, but by being present.

That is what it means to be a man.

Servant leadership in the home is not a concept. It is a practice. And it requires learning to ask the question that most men are trained never to ask: "What do you need?"

The typical male leadership model operates from assumption. I see the problem. I solve the problem. I inform you of the solution. Leadership is decisive. Leadership is confident. Leadership moves forward. A man who stops to ask what others need looks like he is uncertain. Asking looks weak.

This is a catastrophic misunderstanding of what the Ephesians 5 passage actually says.

Most men know the phrase that comes later in the passage: "Husbands, love your wives as Christ loved the church and gave himself up for her." But the passage begins somewhere else entirely. It begins with this: "Submit to one another out of reverence for Christ."

This is the frame. This is the starting point. Before there is any mention of husbands and wives, there is mutual submission. There is the call for all Christians to defer to one another, to consider one another's needs as seriously as their own, to operate from a posture of service to the body of Christ.

Only inside this frame does the passage specify what that mutual submission looks like in the context of marriage. The husband's call is to sacrificial love. The wife's call is to respect. These are not symmetrical commands because the marriage relationship itself is asymmetrical. The husband carries a particular responsibility for the spiritual leadership of the family.

But here is what that responsibility actually means: it means being the first to ask what your wife needs. It means listening to her perspective not because she might be right, but because she is your partner and her wisdom is part of your wisdom. It means deferring when she knows better. It means leading by taking the hardest parts first, not by claiming authority to avoid the hard parts.

I have watched men who think that servant leadership means passivity. They have abdicated. They make no decisions. They never initiate. They do whatever their wife suggests because they have confused deference with leadership. That is not servant leadership. That is cowardice dressed as humility.

Servant leadership is something else. It is clarity about what needs to be decided and openness about how to decide it. It is taking the lead on the conversation rather than waiting to be led. It is saying, "Here is what I think we should do, and here is why. What do you think?" It is being willing to change your mind when you hear something that makes you change your mind.

The historical moment matters here. In the Roman world where Paul was writing, a wife was property. She had no say in the marriage. She had no voice in decisions. The cultural expectation was that she should submit to her husband's will, period. He was the absolute authority.

When the gospel came into that context, it elevated women by demanding more of men. The gospel said: You no longer own her. You no longer have absolute authority over her. You now serve her as your equal before God. Your call is to lay down your life for her. You no longer get to decide unilaterally. You must consider her needs as seriously as your own.

That was countercultural. It still is. In a culture that tells a man his power is his value, the gospel tells him his power is supposed to be used to serve the person he loves most.

Servant leadership in the home means learning to see your wife's needs before she has to name them. It means being the first to apologize. It means being the first to serve. It means that when you come home from work tired, your wife's exhaustion matters more than your fatigue. It means that when decisions need to be made, you are not asking "What do I want?" but "What does my wife need? What do my children need?"

This is not romantic. It is ordinary and sustained and it will cost you something every single day for the rest of your marriage.

But here is what you get in return: a wife who knows she is protected by the commitment to her good, not the commitment to your comfort. Children who see what it looks like when a man uses his strength not for his own advantage but for the advantage of the people he loves. A home where the strongest person in the room is the most willing to serve.

That is a different kind of power than the culture teaches. That is a different kind of manhood.

**CHAPTER 9**

Friendship and the Epidemic of Male Loneliness

The average American man at forty has fewer close friends than he did at thirty. At thirty fewer than at twenty. At twenty fewer than at fifteen. If you trace the line backward through his life, you will see the pattern clearly: friendship was once abundant and it has become scarce. Not friendship as a concept, but friendship as a lived reality. The kind where someone knows what you actually think about your marriage. The kind where you have said out loud that you are afraid. The kind where you show up broken and are not fixed but are simply known.

I know this pattern because I have lived it. More accurately, I have lived the loneliness first and then had to rebuild what I lost.

When I planted my first church, I was surrounded by people. Two hundred showed up to the launch service. We were building something that mattered. I was the pastor, which meant I was the one who counseled the broken, who prayed for the dying, who sat with people through their worst moments. What it meant that I had nobody I could tell about my own worst moments never occurred to me. I was too busy being the answer to notice I was asking a question that nobody was answering.

I had no close friends. I had acquaintances. I had people who knew my name and my sermon notes but did not know the version of me that existed when nobody was watching. I had colleagues I respected and did not trust. I had a marriage that was functioning and a soul that was coming apart. I was, by every measurable standard, successful. By every real standard, I was alone.

That is what happens to a man when he accepts the culture's definition of friendship. Proximity becomes intimacy. Shared activity replaces shared life. You watch the game together and that counts. You work out together and that counts. You swap stories at a men's breakfast and that counts. It all counts, until you realize you are dying and not one person actually knows you.

The Bible has a different picture. In 1 Samuel 18, David's friendship with Jonathan reaches a depth that the text describes this way: his soul was knit to the soul of David. The Hebrew word is qashar—to bind, to tie, to fasten together. It is the language of covenant. What David and Jonathan had was not two buddies who occasionally hung out. It was two souls that were bound to each other. Jonathan stripped himself of his robe and gave it to David—a sign of renouncing claim to the throne itself. He was willing to be unmade so that his friend could become what God called him to be. That is the biblical picture of male friendship.

We have nothing like that anymore. We have something thinner. Something safer. Something that requires less exposure.

The question is: why did this happen?

The first answer is obvious: men moved away from each other. The industrial revolution scattered families. Men went to work and women stayed home. Work happened in offices, not in the company of community. A man's identity became attached to his job. His friendships became subordinate to his productivity. You did not develop deep friendships because you might move for a better job. You did not invest in community because investment took time. Time was money. The equation was simple and it was lethal.

But that is only part of the story. The deeper reason is that modern culture has redefined what is acceptable for men to want. A man is supposed to want status, money, power, sex. A man is not supposed to want to be known. He is not supposed to want presence. He is not supposed to need anyone. Independence is the supreme virtue. The man who admits loneliness has failed at manhood. He has not learned the rules yet.

So what we have created is a generation of successful men who are profoundly isolated. They are accomplished and empty. They have everything the culture promised and none of it works. The man at the top of his career who realizes nobody actually knows him. The man with a good marriage who has never told his wife he is ashamed. The man leading a church who has never let anyone see him break. The pastor at the conference speaking to thousands about faith while his own faith is a performance.

I was that man. I stood in front of my congregation and preached about vulnerability while being incapable of it. I talked about the grace of God while living like I had to earn God's approval through my performance. I had no space to fall apart because there was no one to fall apart in front of. So I did not fall apart. I just got smaller. I became more careful. More controlled. More alone.

The cost was everything.

I was a pastor, which made me the loneliest man in America. This is not poetic exaggeration. Research bears this out. Seventy percent of pastors struggle with depression. Eighty percent feel isolated and alone. They are trained to care for others and forbidden to admit they need care. The expectation is that you are stronger than your struggles, wiser than your questions, holier than your doubts. So you perform. You become very good at the role. And the role becomes a prison.

This is why I started the Pastors Connection Network. Not because I had the answer, but because I finally understood the question. It was not "How do I lead a bigger church?" It was "Is there another human being on earth who knows the actual version of my life?" The answer was no. And the no was killing me.

What changed was a man named Stan Toler. He was a pastor I knew. He called me one day and asked me to lunch. We sat across from each other and he looked at me and said: "How are you actually doing?" Not the pastor answer. The actual answer.

I could have lied. I could have given the standard response: "Blessed and grateful for what the Lord is doing." I could have maintained the boundary between the pastoral self and the actual self. Instead, I told him. I told him about the loneliness. I told him about the pressure I was putting on my marriage because I needed her to be everything—best friend, accountability partner, counselor, cheerleader, lover, defender. I told him that I was dying in the middle of success. I told him the truth.

He listened. He did not fix it. He did not offer advice. He did not minimize it. He just listened. And then he told me something similar about his own life. And in that moment, two souls began to be knit together in the biblical sense.

That lunch led to another lunch. That led to a small group of pastors. That led to the realization that hundreds of men felt the same thing. Not lonely because they were failures. Lonely because they had believed the lie that manhood means independence.

The path back to biblical friendship is concrete and it is narrow and it requires a man to be willing to be undone.

First, you have to name the loneliness. Not as a weakness. As a diagnosis. A man is not well when he is alone in his actual life. This is not opinion. This is theology. God said, "It is not good that man should be alone." That was the prelapsarian world—before sin, before complications, before the need for strategies. It is not good. The man who accepts isolation as the cost of independence has accepted a lie. He is paying with his soul for something the culture told him to want.

Second, you have to find other men who will tell the truth. Not accountability partners. Not in the sense of "I will monitor your sins and make sure you are performing righteousness." That is surveillance masquerading as community. What a man needs is someone who knows what he actually thinks. What he actually fears. What he actually wants when nobody is listening.

I had lunch with a pastor last year. He told me, "I have been in ministry for twenty years. I have a staff. I have a denomination. I have friends. But I have never told any human being that I sometimes wonder if I believe in God. I have never said that doubt out loud to another person. I am fifty-three years old and I am completely alone."

That is the cost of the lie. A man can have everything and still be completely alone.

The men who show up at PCN gatherings are coming because they are finally willing to say the thing out loud. They are admitting that they need to be known. They are accepting that independence is not virtue. They are looking for other men who will not fix them or judge them or demand that they be stronger than their situation requires.

What happens in those rooms is what happened with David and Jonathan. Souls get knit together. A man can say, "I am terrified," and another man says, "Me too." He can say, "I do not know how to be present to my wife," and another man says, "I am working on that too." He can say, "I wonder if God sees me," and another man says, "That question has been with me for months."

The deepest work of redemption in a man's life often happens in friendship. Not in therapy. Not in a sermon. Not in a book. In friendship. When another man knows what you actually think and stays with you anyway. When he does not try to change you and he also does not accept everything about you. When he is honest and he is kind and those two things somehow coexist.

The biblical model is small. Two or three. Not a men's breakfast with a hundred. Not an accountability group with metrics and check-boxes. Two or three men who decide to know each other. To actually know each other. To ask the questions that matter. To tell the truth about the marriage. To admit fear about the kids. To confess that the faith sometimes feels thin. To say the thing you have never said to another person.

That is expensive. It requires vulnerability. It requires the risk of being known and rejected. It requires the slow work of trust. It requires you to believe that manhood is not about invulnerability but about being strong enough to admit you are weak.

A man in this position will often say: "But if I tell my pastor I am struggling, what if it damages his leadership?" Or: "If I tell my friend I am afraid, what if he tells others?" These are real risks. They are also the price of connection.

The alternative is what we have now: men who are alone in rooms full of people. Men whose marriages are dying because their wives are trying to be everything a man is designed to get from brotherhood. Men whose faith is fragile because it is entirely private and entirely unsupported. Men who are so controlled in public that they cannot recognize themselves anymore.

The man who has two friends who know him is richer than the man with a thousand acquaintances. This is not romantic. It is true. The man who has sat in a room with another man and told him the thing he has never said and is still accepted is beginning to understand grace. Not as doctrine. As lived reality.

This is what I would tell a man who is reading this: Your loneliness is not a personal failure. It is not a character defect. It is a sign that you have believed a lie about what manhood is supposed to be. Manhood is not independence. It is interdependence. It is the willingness to need. It is the capacity to be known. It is the courage to let another man carry part of what you are carrying.

Find another man. Tell him the truth. Let him tell you the truth. Build that friendship slowly. There is no hurry. There is no nine-step plan. Just two souls beginning to be knit together in the biblical sense.

The loneliness will not end by itself. It ends when you finally believe that you are allowed to need someone. And that belief only comes when someone proves it to you by staying when you are honest. By listening when you are afraid. By knowing you and choosing you anyway.

That is friendship. That is what has been lost. That is what must be recovered.

The Pastors Connection Network was not born from a vision statement. It was born from a phone call at midnight.

A pastor I knew called me. He was weeping. He said: I have been in the ministry for fifteen years. I have a congregation. I have staff. I have a board. I have nobody I can talk to. I cannot tell my people what I am struggling with because they look to me for answers. I cannot tell my other pastor friends because we are all competing for the same resources, the same growth, the same respect. I cannot tell my wife because if she thinks I am weakening, the church will sense it and the authority will crumble. So I carry this alone. And tonight I cannot carry it anymore.

That phone call became a network.

What he needed was not a conference. Not a book. Not a podcast with a thousand listeners. He needed one person who would know him fully and still believe in him. He needed someone to say: I see you. I see what this is costing you. I see how hard you are trying and how much you are failing and how you are still getting up. I see you.

That is what PCN is built on. Not accountability in the surveillance sense. Not a group of men watching to make sure no one falls. Friendship in the covenant sense. The willingness to be known. The courage to know another person without trying to fix what you see.

The structure is simple because the need is simple. Small groups. Four to six men. A rotating location. A consistent time. Everything you say stays in the room. Everything you say is spoken without advice unless you ask for it. Just witness. Just presence. Just the knowledge that someone knows the real version of your life and still calls you brother.

I have sat in those groups. I have watched men cry. I have watched men admit things they had never said out loud. I have watched them discover that the thing they thought would destroy their reputation actually freed them. That their struggle was shared. That they were not alone.

David and Jonathan give us a picture of what friendship between men is supposed to be. Jonathan came to David when David was afraid. Jonathan had everything — he was the king's son, he had status, he had security. David had nothing. David was running for his life. And Jonathan did something that would have been seen as madness in that culture. He gave David his robe and his armor. He gave him his identity markers. He essentially said: I am laying down my status for you. I am saying that my friendship with you is worth more to me than my position. That is the kind of friendship between men that the gospel requires.

It is rare. It is costly. It is nearly invisible in our culture because we have replaced friendship with networking and brotherhood with competition. Men come together to accomplish something or to be seen together or to use one another. But to come together for the sake of being known and still being wanted? That is unusual. That is dangerous. That is exactly what we need.

PCN exists because pastor loneliness is an epidemic and because nobody is talking about it loud enough. Pastors are dying by suicide. Pastors are leaving the ministry in despair. Pastors are leading churches while carrying secrets that would destroy the church's confidence if they were known. The isolation is spiritual death happening in slow motion.

When a man has someone who knows him fully, everything changes. His failures do not make him hide. They make him seek repair. His questions do not make him doubt his faith. They make him deepen it. His weakness does not disqualify him from leadership. It qualifies him for leadership because he knows he cannot lead in his own strength.

That is the network. That is the point.

Men do not know how to be friends with each other. We have been taught that friendships are the things you have on the side, the buddies you grab beer with, the guys you hunt with or golf with or work out with. But real friendship, the kind that knows you and stays with you, that is something we do not know how to do with each other.

So when a man finds himself in crisis, he often finds himself alone.

He reaches out to his wife because that is the relationship he knows how to have. He confesses to his pastor because that is a professional relationship with a built-in confidentiality. But he does not tell another man. And not because men would judge him. Because men have not been trained to witness without trying to fix. To listen without trying to solve. To stay in the room when someone is falling apart instead of immediately pivoting to an action plan.

What men actually need is witness.

Not advice. Not solutions. Not accountability in the surveillance sense. A friend who says "I see you, and I am not going anywhere." That is not a small thing. That is everything.

The theology of witness is deeply Christian. God is the ultimate witness. God sees everything about you—the hidden thoughts, the secret shame, the things you have told nobody—and God does not flinch and does not leave. God is with you. God has seen you at your worst and God loves you still. When men practice this kind of witnessing friendship with each other, they are imaging God to each other. They are saying: I see you. I am present to you. I will not abandon you.

I have spent the last ten years building Pastors Connection Network on a very specific conviction: pastors are dying in isolation. Not from overwork. Not from theological error. From loneliness. From the absence of another man who sees them and does not flinch. From the certainty that if they are really honest about what is happening to them, they will be disqualified from ministry.

So PCN exists as a small group experience where pastors can say the thing they have been hiding. A pastor confessed once that his marriage was failing. He had been preaching about covenant faithfulness while his own marriage was disintegrating. The alternative was to keep silent and hope nobody found out. But silence was slowly killing him. So he said it out loud in a room with eight other men who could have condemned him or reported him. Instead, they witnessed him. They said: we see you. We are not leaving. We cannot fix your marriage, but we can stay with you while you do the work of trying to save it.

Another pastor admitted that he had not prayed honestly in months. He was giving spiritual counsel to people all day while his own prayer life had collapsed into habit and performance. He knew the answer. He knew the call. He just could not do it anymore. And in a room with other pastors, he could say that. And instead of being condemned, he was witnessed. Eight men saying: I have been there. You are not the only one. And whatever is happening in your prayer life right now, it does not disqualify you from being a pastor.

Another pastor said: I do not know if I believe anymore. The church had become such a burden, the theology had become so complicated, the expectations had become so impossible, that he had arrived at a place where faith felt like a job he was doing, not something he actually possessed. The temptation was to leave quietly, to find another job, to tell people he got a call somewhere else. But instead he named it. In a small group of other pastors, he said: I am struggling with doubt. I do not know if I still believe what I am preaching.

What happened after in each case was not that the man was fixed. The marriages that were failing sometimes failed. The prayer lives that had collapsed did not immediately resurrect. The doubt did not instantly resolve. But something changed that is more important than any of those things. The man was no longer alone. He had been witnessed. He had been seen by other men who knew him and stayed with him. And that witness changed what was possible for him.

This is what the church has missed. Real male friendship is not about activity. It is not about what you do together. It is about what you know about each other and whether you stay after you know it. It is about whether a man can tell another man something that terrifies him and still be loved. It is about whether there is a place where a man can take off the mask and not be abandoned because his face is different than the mask.

I have seen this change men. I have seen a pastor who thought he was the only one wrestling with pride realize that eight other pastors are wrestling with exactly the same thing. I have seen a man who thought his addiction made him disqualified from ministry realize that recovery is possible and that his brothers are willing to walk with him through it. I have seen a man who was certain that his failure meant the end of his ministry realize that failure can be transformed into something deeper if he does not have to carry it alone.

This is not therapy. This is not emotional support group language. This is men witnessing each other. Men saying: I see what you are going through. I have been there. I am still there sometimes. And I am not leaving. That is the presence that changes men. Not someone who has all the answers. Someone who has sat in the same darkness and knows that the darkness does not have to be permanent.

The temptation is to think this kind of friendship is soft. That real men do not need to be seen and witnessed. That strength is the absence of this kind of vulnerability. But that is exactly backward. The man who can be fully seen by another man and still stand is stronger than the man who is always performing. The man who can admit what is true about him and still be loved is freer than the man who is always hiding. The man who has another man who witnesses his becoming is more equipped to finish the race than the man who is running it alone.

This is what men hunger for and what they do not know how to ask for. Permission to be seen. Permission to be known. Permission to be loved not in spite of what is true about them but in the presence of it. That permission comes from another man who has been given that same permission and who is willing to pass it along. That is witness. And witness is everything.

**CHAPTER 10**

Raising Sons in a Confused World

I have five sons. Titus is fourteen. The older ones are working through their own discoveries about what it means to be a man in a world that does not quite know what manhood is anymore. The youngest is still in the space where father is the primary source of meaning. That is a heavy responsibility. Not because I have to be perfect. Because I have to be honest about what I am building.

There are two dominant scripts in our culture for raising boys. The first is domination. Make them tough. Make them strong. Teach them to compete, to win, to never show weakness. Give them tools—sports, combat training, competitive academics—and let them learn that life is a battle and the strong survive. This produces men who know how to perform power. What it does not produce is men who know themselves.

The second script is deference. Make them sensitive. Make them aware of their power and terrified of it. Teach them that masculinity is the problem and that real strength is found in acquiescence. Make them question every instinct. Make them apologize for taking up space. This produces men who are paralyzed by the awareness of their own capacity. What it does not produce is men who know how to lead anything.

Neither script is biblical. Neither script is true. And both scripts are offered to fathers as the complete menu of options.

What I am trying to do with my sons is something else entirely. I am trying to build men who know that their strength is a gift. That it is meant to be used in service. That tenderness is not weakness. That anger is not sin. That wanting to provide and protect is not oppression. That being honest about failure is not shameful. That authority without humility is tyranny. That submission without conviction is cowardice.

This happens not in lectures. It happens in the actual texture of daily life.

My oldest was maybe ten when he got angry at his brother. Not the normal sibling annoyance. Real anger. The kind that makes you want to hit something. He was in my office when it happened, and I watched him try to control it. His whole body was rigid. His face was turning red. He was trying to compress something that was growing bigger. So I told him: "You are not going to apologize for being angry. Anger is not sin. Come with me."

We went outside. I showed him my heavy bag. I told him to hit it. He did. He hit it hard. Multiple times. All the anger that he was trying to contain went into those hits. When he was done, he was breathing heavily and he was different. The rage had moved through him instead of being dammed up inside.

Then I said: "The anger you felt was real. It was proportional. Your brother had wronged you. That was right. But what you do with the anger matters. You can hit a bag. You can run. You can lift weights. You can channel it into work that matters. What you cannot do is hurt someone with it. What you cannot do is pretend it is not there. And what you cannot do is let it convince you that you are bad."

This is what I want my sons to understand about anger: it is not the enemy. It is a signal. It tells you that something matters to you. That a boundary has been violated. That injustice has happened. The Bible does not tell us never to be angry. It tells us to be angry and sin not. The difference is the distance between feeling and action. A man can feel rage and choose restraint. That is where power lives.

The conversation about sex is harder because we have made it dirty. A boy grows up hearing that his sexuality is dangerous. That desire is shameful. That the body is the problem. So when he becomes a teenager and his body starts screaming at him to notice girls, he thinks something is wrong with him. He thinks his desire is the issue. He tries to control it through shame. And shame does not work. It just goes underground.

I have told my sons: your body is not the problem. Your desire is not shameful. God made you male. That includes sexuality. The question is not whether you want sex. The question is what you are willing to do to get it.

The biblical vision is covenantal. You make a promise. You keep the promise. You build something that lasts because both people are committed. This is not about control. It is about reverence. The man who treats sex as something he can take whenever he wants has missed the point entirely. The man who sees a woman as his to use has committed a violence against her and a violence against his own soul.

I have told Titus: the girl you are interested in is not your possession. She is not your project. She is not your conquest. She is a person God loves. She is someone's daughter. She might be someone's wife. If you are going to know her, you are going to know her with respect. You are going to be honest about your intentions. You are going to ask her what she wants and listen to the answer. And if you are going to be intimate with her, you are going to do it in the context of a covenant where both people have chosen each other.

This will limit your options. Good. That is the point.

The conversation about women is connected. I grew up in a culture that taught boys that women were either mothers or temptresses. Either they were people to be protected because they were weak, or they were objects to be conquered because they were threats. Both frameworks dehumanize women. Both make it impossible to see a woman as a full person.

My sons have a mother. Susanna is not delicate. She is not stupid. She is not there to serve my preferences or manage my emotions. She is a person with her own mind, her own strength, her own authority. She is a leader in our home. She makes major decisions. She disagrees with me. Sometimes she is right and I am wrong. Titus watches this. He sees that respect between his parents is not about dominance or deference. It is about two people who know each other's worth.

I have told my sons: the woman you choose to love will be your equal. Not in the same way. In the equal sense. She will have gifts you do not have. She will see things you cannot see. She will be strong in ways that are not your strength. If you are wise, you will honor that. If you are foolish, you will fight it and spend your life in conflict.

The conversation about work and identity is maybe the most important. A boy growing up in America will learn that his worth is attached to his productivity. What he does. What he achieves. What he makes. So the conversation becomes: who are you if you are not winning? Who are you if you fail?

I have tried to teach my sons something different. Your identity is not your job. It is not your grades. It is not your athletic performance. It is not your bank account. You are a human being made in the image of God. That is your worth. That is your identity. It is fixed. It cannot be added to or taken away by anything you do or do not do.

This does not mean you do not work hard. You work hard because the work matters. You work hard because excellence is a form of worship. You work hard because laziness is a form of contempt—contempt for the thing you are supposed to be doing and contempt for the people affected by your work. But you do not work hard because your worth depends on it.

I have told them: you could fail at everything you try. You could lose every game. You could get terrible grades. You could lose your job. You could make poor financial decisions. You could still be my son. You could still be a man. You could still matter. The work you do is important. But it is not who you are.

The model for all of this comes from Deuteronomy 6. Moses is giving the law to Israel and he says: "These commandments that I give you today are to be on your hearts. Impress them on your children. Talk about them when you sit at home and when you walk along the road, when you lie down and when you get up."

Notice what he is not saying. He is not saying "have one big conversation." He is not saying "send them to a class about values." He is saying that formation happens in the texture of daily life. When you are walking. When you are sitting. When you are lying down. The ordinary moments are where the deepest teaching happens.

This looks like conversations in the truck. This looks like working on something together and talking while you work. This looks like a question from your son at night and you taking the time to actually answer it. This looks like admitting when you are wrong. This looks like your son seeing you fail and seeing you handle it with grace.

Titus asked me a few months ago: "Dad, have you ever felt like you didn't know what you were doing?" I could have given him the safe answer. "Sure, son, but I prayed and God showed me the way." Instead I told him the truth. "Yeah. I feel that way a lot. I still do. I am the pastor of a church and I am still not sure about a lot of things. I have made mistakes that hurt people. I have made decisions that I would make differently now. I am still learning what it means to be a man."

He was quiet for a moment and then he said, "That makes me feel better actually." And I understood. What he needed was not a father who had all the answers. He needed a father who was willing to admit he was figuring it out. He needed permission to be uncertain. He needed to know that the path was long and that being on it meant you were always becoming.

That is what I try to do. Not to be perfect. To be honest.

There is also the question of authority. Boys need to know that authority is real. That there are consequences. That not everything is negotiable. I have tried not to be arbitrary about this, but I have also tried not to be completely permissive.

When one of my sons makes a choice that violates the values of our home, there is a consequence. Not shaming. Not anger. But clarity. You chose this and this is what happens now. The consequence connects to the choice. It is not about punishment. It is about learning that the world is just. That choices have weight. That there is order.

But I have also tried to explain the why. The rule is not "don't stay out late because I said so." The rule is "don't stay out late because I am responsible for your safety and you need sleep to function well and there are actual predators and actual dangers and part of my job is to protect you." When the why is clear, the boy understands that the authority is not about control. It is about care.

The hardest part is modeling what I am asking them to be. A boy will forgive a father a lot of things. But he will not forgive hypocrisy. If I ask them to be honest and they catch me lying. If I ask them to control their anger and they watch me lose mine. If I ask them to treat women with respect and they hear me demean their mother. That is the moment they stop believing me.

So I have had to do the harder work. I have had to actually become the kind of man I am asking them to become. Not perfectly. But genuinely. I have had to admit when I am wrong. I have had to say "I should not have spoken to you that way." I have had to let them see me being corrected by their mother. I have had to let them see me working on my own stuff.

The goal is not to raise men who are like me. The goal is to raise men who are like Christ. Who know their strength and use it in service. Who have conviction without cruelty. Who can be tender and still be strong. Who can fail and repent and keep going. Who can lead and also follow. Who can think for themselves and also be accountable to something bigger than themselves.

This is the work. Not the perfect work. The honest work. The slow work. The work that happens in the truck and around the table and in the quiet conversations at night.

I do not know if I am doing it right. I know I am doing it. I know my sons are becoming men who think. Who question. Who are learning what it means to hold power with humility. I know that when Titus is angry, he does not hit his brother. He comes to me. I know that when he is confused about something, he asks. I know that he is watching what I do and what I say and how I treat his mother and his brothers. I know that the formation is happening.

And I know that one day he will be a father and he will have his own sons and he will do some of this differently than I did. He will take the things that served him and he will change the things that did not. And that is what should happen. That is the work being passed down. Not carbon copies. But a genuine line of men trying to understand what manhood means and trying to live it.

The conversation a father has with his sons about women determines the kind of men they will become. Most fathers avoid it entirely.

I have had the privilege of raising five sons. I have watched other fathers try to raise sons. I have sat with men who destroyed the most important relationships of their lives because they had no language for how to treat a woman as anything other than a conquest or a problem to be managed.

Here is where that language needs to begin: Every woman you meet is someone's daughter. Before she is a romantic interest. Before she is attractive or unattractive. Before she is anything else in your mind, she is an image-bearer of God. That is the non-negotiable starting point.

This changes everything. If I see her first as someone's daughter, as someone God loves with the same fierce love he has for me, then I cannot use her. I cannot treat her as less than. I cannot pursue her for my own gratification. I have to ask: How would I want someone to treat my sister?

The practical conversations flow from this starting point. They are uncomfortable conversations. They should be. A father who is comfortable talking to his son about sexuality has probably not gone deep enough into the reality of it.

On consent: It is not a legal concept. It is a covenantal one. Consent is not just the absence of a no. It is the presence of a genuine yes. It is her freedom to say no without consequence. It is her ability to change her mind. It is the two of you moving forward together, not him pushing and her reluctantly accepting. Every time you are together physically, she should feel free. Not trapped. Not obligated. Not like she has already started so she cannot stop. If she has to convince you that it is okay to stop, you are not in a covenant. You are in a power dynamic.

On pornography: Do not describe it to your son as forbidden. Describe it as false. It is a counterfeit of something real and good. It promises intimacy. It delivers isolation. It promises that you will be known. It requires that you remain unknown. It is a response to loneliness. It increases loneliness. A man who becomes dependent on pornography is a man who is learning to separate sex from relationship, to disconnect his sexuality from his soul, to prefer the image to the actual person. If that becomes his habit, he will bring that habit into marriage. He will prefer the image of his wife to his actual wife. He will struggle to connect sexually with a real person who challenges him and disappoints him and says no.

On dating: The question is not how far can I go? The question is how can I honor this person? How can I protect her? How can I move through this relationship in a way that builds her up rather than tears her down? If you are constantly testing the boundaries of physical affection, you are constantly disrespecting her. You are constantly asking what you can get away with rather than what honors her.

This connects to what the Proverbs 31 woman actually is. She is not a checklist. She is not a picture of what to look for in a wife. She is a picture of what a woman becomes when she is treated like a partner rather than a project. She is industrious not because she is trying to prove her worth. She is industrious because she knows her worth is secure and she can pour herself out. She is strong not because she is independent. She is strong because she is in covenant with a man who is secure enough in himself to let her be strong. She is respected by her community because she is respected by her husband.

A man who reads that passage and thinks "I need to find a wife who is like this" is fundamentally misunderstanding it. The right question is: What kind of man do I need to become so that the woman I love becomes this? What kind of man creates the safety, the stability, the genuine partnership that allows a woman to flourish?

That is the conversation that matters.

The conversation about raising sons cannot avoid the question of technology. Not because technology is the enemy, but because technology has become the default parenting partner in most homes, and most parents have not reckoned with what that means.

A boy with a smartphone has access to more information, more temptation, more ideology, and more distraction than any generation in human history. He has Andrew Tate in one tab and a progressive gender studies lecture in another. He has pornography a click away and Scripture a click away. He has friends he has never met telling him what manhood is and a father in the next room who has never said a word about it.

The average American boy spends seven hours a day on screens outside of school. Seven hours. That is not a statistic about screen time. That is a statement about who is raising him. For seven hours a day, the voices forming his understanding of the world, of women, of power, of sex, of meaning, are voices his parents did not choose and may not know exist.

I am not an alarmist about technology. I do not believe the solution is to eliminate screens or retreat to an Amish lifestyle. But I believe something most parents are not willing to say: you are competing for your son's formation, and right now, you are losing.

The phone is not neutral. The algorithm is not neutral. The feed is designed to capture attention, and it captures attention by appealing to the strongest impulses a teenage boy has: sexual desire, status anxiety, rage, and the hunger to belong to something. Every platform knows this. Every platform exploits it. And every parent who hands a boy a phone without a plan for what that phone will do to him is outsourcing his formation to people who do not love him.

This is not about rules. Rules without relationship produce rebellion. This is about presence. The father who is present enough to know what his son is watching, reading, absorbing. The father who is willing to sit down and watch what his son watches and talk about it without panic. The father who can say: I understand why that video appeals to you. Let me tell you what it is actually selling.

The boys who are most vulnerable to the manosphere, to pornography, to nihilistic ideology, are not the boys with the most access to technology. They are the boys with the least access to their fathers. A boy whose father talks to him about sex does not need the internet to explain it. A boy whose father talks to him about anger does not need Jordan Peterson to give him permission to feel it. A boy whose father models strength and tenderness does not need Andrew Tate to tell him what a man looks like.

The technology question is ultimately a presence question. And the presence question is ultimately a priority question. The father who says he does not have time to monitor what his son is consuming online but has time to watch three hours of television himself has made a choice. It may not be a conscious choice. But it is a choice. And his son is the one who pays for it.

What I tell fathers in my congregation is this: you have approximately eighteen years. In those eighteen years, you are building something in your son that will either hold weight or collapse. The world is building something in him too. Every day you are absent, the world gets another day to pour its version of manhood into your boy. And the world does not love him. The world wants his attention, his money, his loyalty, and his rage. You want his heart. That is the difference. And that difference means you have to fight for the hours.

Not with rules. With presence. With conversation. With the willingness to enter his world and speak into it rather than condemning it from the outside.

**CHAPTER 11**

When Men Fail

Every man will fail. The question is not whether but how. Some men fail and spend the rest of their lives managing the narrative. Controlling the damage. Protecting their reputation. Performing repentance without genuinely changing. The mask gets tighter. The truth gets buried deeper. The man becomes smaller and smaller until he barely recognizes himself.

Other men fail and they tell the truth. They admit what they did. They face the cost of the failure. They actually change. Something in them breaks open and they come out the other side different. Not healed. But honest. Not fixed. But moving.

The difference is repentance. Not confession. Not apology. Repentance. The Greek word is metanoia. A complete turning. A reorientation of the will. You stop doing the thing you were doing and you move in the opposite direction. Not because you got caught. Because you finally saw what you were doing.

I have failed in ways that matter. As a husband. As a father. As a pastor. In each failure, I had the opportunity to repent or to manage the narrative. I have chosen both at different times. The repentance cost something. The narrative management cost more.

The marriage failure came first. In the early years of planting the church, I centered the marriage around my calling. Not consciously. But practically. Every decision was made through the lens of "what does the church need?" Susanna's needs were secondary. Her exhaustion did not matter because the church was growing. Her loneliness did not matter because I was working for God. Her voice was not heard in major decisions because I was the leader and she was supporting my vision.

This is spiritual abuse. I did not hit her. I did not demean her. I did not have an affair. But I treated her as an accessory to my purpose instead of as a partner in a covenant. I made decisions that affected her life without her consent. I spent money we did not have on the church. I was gone more than I was home. I was emotionally unavailable because I was pouring everything into my public role.

When she finally said something, I heard it as criticism of the church. She was not criticizing the church. She was saying I was choosing the church over her. I did not hear her. I heard threat. And I doubled down. I worked harder. I justified it theologically. I told myself that this was the cost of ministry. I told myself that she would understand when the church grew. I told myself that I was doing this for us.

All of that was true on the surface. What was also true was that I was afraid. If I stopped working, if I slowed down, if I made decisions democratically with my wife, I would have to face who I was when I was not performing. I would have to admit that I needed her to be okay for me to be okay. So I kept the machinery running. I kept the church growing. I kept myself safe inside busyness.

One day she told me that she was done. Not done with the marriage. Done with being an afterthought. She said: "I have watched you choose this church for years. I have watched you lie to me about why. I have watched you tell yourself that you are doing this for us when what you are really doing is hiding. I am not doing this anymore."

In that moment, I had a choice. I could have defended myself. I could have reminded her of everything I was doing. I could have managed the narrative: "Look, I have never meant to hurt you, but this is what ministry requires." I could have gotten her into counseling and made the issue about her lack of support instead of my misplaced priorities.

Instead I finally heard her. Not as threat. As truth. And the truth was devastating. I had prioritized my identity as a pastor over my identity as a husband. I had used the church as a hiding place from intimacy. I had made my wife carry the cost of my ambition.

What followed was not a quick fix. It was not a weekend marriage retreat where everything got better. It was a slow, painful reckoning. I had to admit that I had been wrong. Not just in my actions. In my thinking. In my theology. I had twisted Scripture to justify my choices. I had made ministry into a god. I had treated my wife as a supporting character in a story where I was the hero.

The change required me to actually change. I had to stop. The busyness was serving a function. It was protecting me from intimacy. It was keeping me safe. When I stopped, the loneliness came. The fear came. The questions came. I had to sit with all of that instead of running from it.

I cut back at the church. Not because it was good strategy. Because Susanna had told me that either the marriage got my time and attention or it would end. I had to believe her. I had to trust that the church would be okay without me there every night. I had to believe that being a good husband was not less important than being a successful pastor. I had to believe that my marriage was actually what I said it was.

The failure as a father came a different way. It was slower and it was more insidious because I thought I was doing it right.

I was the providing father. I provided very well. The bills were paid. The house was good. The food was on the table. But I was not the present father. I was the father who was always working. Always thinking about the sermon. Always on my phone. Always one foot out the door. I provided for my sons while being largely absent from their lives.

One of my sons asked me one day if I would come to his soccer game. I said yes. And then I was not there. Not because something catastrophic happened. Because something at the church came up and I forgot. Or I deprioritized it. I sent Susanna a text: "I am so sorry, can you take him?" She went. He played. I missed it.

This happened repeatedly. Not constantly. But enough. Enough that my son started to have a quietness around me. A resignation. Like he had learned not to expect me. That recognition in his face is something I will carry forever.

The failure was not that I sometimes had to miss things. That is the reality of ministry. The failure was that I did not fight for the time with my sons. I did not make it a priority the way I made the church a priority. I did not consider their development as important as the church's growth. I acted like I could be a good father while being largely absent.

The reckoning came when I realized that my sons would not stay young. That the window was closing. That if I did not change, I would look back and realize I had missed their lives while I was building something that would be fine without me. That I had not actually learned the lesson from my own father's absence. I had just replicated it with better justification.

The repentance was different this time because it required me to make structural changes. I changed my schedule. I protected certain nights. I blocked time with my family. Not as something to fit in around ministry. As the main thing. Everything else is secondary. I said no to meetings. I said no to events. I reduced my involvement in things that were good but not essential.

My sons had to teach me that presence matters more than provision. That sitting with them is worth more than the money I make. That their questions about life are more important than my email. That I am raising men, not running a program.

The failure as a pastor is harder to talk about because it is still happening. I have preached things with confidence that I should have held more carefully. I have made pronouncements from the pulpit that I would make differently now. I have been more certain than I should have been about things that are genuinely complex. I have used my platform in ways that I did not fully consider the weight of.

I have also used the church as a hiding place. I have let the public role shield the private brokenness. I have been so focused on what people needed from me that I did not stop to ask what they were offering to me. I have been the shepherd but not the sheep. The teacher but not the student. The one who helps but not the one who receives help.

This is the subtle failure of pastoral ministry. You are so busy giving that you forget you have needs. You are so busy being strong for others that you forget you are weak. You are so busy having answers that you forget you have questions. And one day you wake up and you realize that you have built a life where nobody knows the actual version of you. Where you are performing virtue for an audience. Where the faith you preach on Sunday is increasingly disconnected from the faith you live on Monday.

The repentance here is ongoing. I am still working through it. It requires me to be honest with my congregation. Not about everything. But about the fact that I am figuring this out too. That I have gotten things wrong. That my faith is not a finished product. That I am a man in process, not a man who has arrived.

The common thread through all these failures is that I was more concerned with the narrative than with the reality. I wanted to be a certain kind of man and I was willing to hide the parts of me that contradicted that image. The good husband. The good father. The good pastor. All of those are things I want to be. But I wanted them for their own sake—so that I would look good. Not because I actually cared about the people involved.

Real repentance shifts that. It says: I am going to be this kind of man not because it makes me look good. But because the people I love deserve it. Because the covenant I made means something. Because the truth matters more than the image.

The biblical picture is David. King David who had everything and wanted something he could not have. So he took it. He saw Bathsheba and he wanted her and he took her. And then when the consequences started to surface, he tried to hide it. He tried to control the narrative. He had her husband killed to cover up the adultery.

But David eventually stopped managing the narrative. He confessed. Psalm 51 is the song of that confession. "Against you, you only, have I sinned and done what is evil in your sight." Not "I made a mistake." Not "circumstances were complex." But a full acknowledgment of what he had done. "Create in me a pure heart, O God, and renew a steadfast spirit within me."

The cost of that repentance was terrible. The child born from the adultery died. His family was fractured. His authority as a king was compromised. People could never look at him the same way again. The failure was permanent. He would carry it forever.

But something happened in David that did not happen in Saul. When Saul sinned, he said "I have sinned"—but it was performance. It was reputation management. He wanted Samuel and the people to think he still had God's approval. David's "I have sinned" came from somewhere else. It came from actual brokenness. From looking at what he had done and feeling genuine remorse. From deciding that God's approval was more important than the people's approval.

That is the difference. Saul's repentance was about managing the narrative. David's repentance was about actually changing.

The man who fails and repents genuinely is more dangerous in the kingdom sense than the man who has never been tested. He knows what he is capable of. He knows how quickly the good can become corrupted. He is therefore more careful. More humble. More aware of his need for grace. The failure, when it is truly repented of, becomes the foundation of something stronger.

I look at my marriage now and I see a partnership that was forged in my failure and her honesty. If I had managed the narrative, she would have left or stayed bitter. Because I repented, we actually built something. I look at my sons and I see young men who know that their father is not perfect but that he is trying. That matters. I look at my church and I see people who know that their pastor has gotten things wrong and is still learning. That permission to be human makes the gospel more credible, not less.

The men who do this well are the ones who decide early that the narrative does not matter more than the reality. That looking good is less important than being good. That their reputation is less important than their integrity.

This is hard. It costs. It means admitting things that shame you. It means facing consequences you would rather avoid. It means living with the permanent mark of the failure. It means the people around you knowing things about you that you would rather they did not know.

But the alternative is smaller. The alternative is a man who spent his whole life protecting an image. A man who got smaller and tighter because he could never let the walls down. A man who had relationships but no real connection. A man who was safe but profoundly alone.

I choose the harder path. Not because I am strong. Because I am tired of hiding. Because the people I love deserve the actual version of me. Because God's grace is more real than my shame. Because the man I am becoming in the repentance is more true than the man I was performing before.

Every man will fail. The question is whether he will turn or whether he will run. Whether he will tell the truth or whether he will manage the narrative. Whether he will let the failure break him open or whether he will let it harden him further.

That choice is made in small moments. In the instant after the failure when you can either confess or hide. In the day after when you can either start changing or start explaining. In the months after when you can either do the hard work of repentance or you can try to move on like nothing happened.

I have chosen confession. Not perfectly. Not even consistently. But I have chosen it enough times to see that it leads somewhere. It leads to being known. It leads to real relationship. It leads to a marriage that is actual. It leads to sons who see their father as a human being. It leads to a church where the gospel is not just words but lived reality.

The failure is permanent. But it does not have to be final.

Shame is not guilt. The distinction is everything and most men do not know how to tell the difference.

Guilt says: I did something wrong. Guilt is about behavior. I committed an action that violated my values or hurt another person. Guilt is painful but it is also healthy. Guilt makes you want to repair. Guilt says: I need to apologize. I need to change. I need to make this right.

Shame says: I am something wrong. Shame is about identity. I am defective. I am broken. I am the problem. Shame does not make you want to repair. Shame makes you want to hide. Shame makes you want to disappear.

Male shame is particularly destructive because the culture has written a script that says a man should not feel shame. A man should be strong. A man should be competent. A man should have it figured out. A man who admits shame is admitting weakness, and weakness is the one thing the culture will not forgive in a man.

The result is men who carry shame in silence. Men who cannot talk about their failures because talking about failure would mean admitting that they are not who they have presented themselves to be. Men who medicate shame with work or alcohol or sex or anger. Men who cannot distinguish between conviction and condemnation.

Conviction comes from the Holy Spirit and it leads somewhere. It says: You have done something wrong and you need to turn. Conviction is uncomfortable but it is productive. Conviction makes you confess. Conviction makes you change.

Condemnation comes from the Enemy and it leads to hiding. It says: You are wrong and you always will be. You cannot be fixed. Why even try? Condemnation is uncomfortable and it is destructive. Condemnation makes you bury the thing deeper.

A man who cannot tell the difference will bury his sin when he should confess it. He will despair when he should repent. He will believe the lie that because he has failed, he is disqualified from leadership, from manhood, from being loved.

This is where Romans 8:1 becomes the most essential verse in Scripture for a man trapped in shame: "There is therefore now no condemnation for those who are in Christ Jesus."

The gospel does not eliminate shame. The gospel provides a place to bring it. You can bring your shame into the light and you will not be destroyed. You will be forgiven. Your past will not determine your future. The person you were is not the person you are becoming.

I have carried shame. I have done things I am not proud of. I have hurt people I loved. I have failed in ways that felt catastrophic. For years I carried that shame in silence because admitting it would have meant admitting that I was not the man I pretended to be.

When I finally brought the shame into the light, when I finally said it out loud to someone I trusted, something shifted. The thing I was hiding lost its power. It became something that could be addressed. It became something I could bring to Jesus instead of something I had to hide from Jesus.

That is the difference between a man who confesses and a man who condemns himself. One of them heals. One of them hardens.

The culture will not teach a man to confess. The culture will teach him to present. To manage his image. To hide what does not fit the brand. But the gospel teaches him something different. The gospel teaches him that the thing he is most ashamed of is the thing that, when brought into the light, becomes the place where grace is most real.

A man who understands this can live differently. He can admit his weakness. He can ask for help. He can confess his sin. He can be known. And he can still be believed in.

The word repentance is used carelessly in the church. We treat it like a moment instead of a direction. A man confesses something in a prayer meeting and we say he has repented. But repentance is not the moment of admission. It is what happens after the admission. It is the long, difficult work of rebuilding when every instinct tells you to hide.

The aftermath of failure is not dramatic. It is tedious. It is showing up the next morning when you would rather disappear. It is sitting at the dinner table with a wife who does not know if she trusts you. It is looking your children in the eye after they have seen you at your worst. It is going back to work after everyone knows what you have done. It is standing in front of your congregation and facing the people whose respect you have lost.

And most men do not have a map for how to do this.

The church calls men to repentance but then leaves them alone in the process. The call is clear: stop doing the thing. But the how is murky. How do I rebuild a marriage that I have damaged? How do I become trustworthy again after I have proven I cannot be trusted? How do I lead people who are no longer sure I have the moral authority to lead them? The church does not give men the answer because the answer is slow and there is no shortcut.

Peter denied Jesus three times. After the resurrection, Jesus found Peter and made him breakfast. The conversation was direct: "Simon, do you love me?" Three times Jesus asked the question. Three denials met with three affirmations. But the affirmations were not dismissals. They were not "that is okay, forget it happened." They were: I see what happened, and I am still here.

And then Jesus gave Peter a job. "Feed my lambs. Tend my sheep. Feed my sheep." The restoration was not getting Peter back to where he started. It was giving him a way forward that incorporated what he had learned from his failure.

This is the template for rebuilding. Not a single moment of forgiveness that erases the damage. A long process of being present, being known, being given something to do that reflects both what was lost and what can be learned.

The man who has been unfaithful must sit with the consequences. His wife does not trust him because he has given her no reason to trust him. The way to rebuild is not grand gestures. It is small, consistent presence. It is being where he says he will be. It is his phone being where he says it is. It is him answering questions about his day without defensiveness. It is him choosing his wife over and over when the temptation returns. It is him understanding that his wife's doubt is not a failure of her faith. It is a rational response to his actions. And the work of rebuilding is his work, not hers. She does not have to heal. He has to prove himself faithful.

The man who has abandoned his children through his own preoccupation has to rebuild something that may be broken more deeply than he realizes. His children have learned not to count on him. The way he rebuilds is by being consistent and present. Not at the level of grand adventures but at the level of showing up. Being at the dinner table. Asking his child how their day was and actually listening. Not looking at his phone. Not thinking about work. Just being present. This is painful because his child may not immediately respond. The child has learned not to expect the father's presence, so the presence may be received with suspicion at first. But over time, if he keeps showing up, something changes. The child begins to believe that maybe this time the father is really here.

The man who has lost the moral authority to lead must decide what he is doing in a leadership position at all. The immediate answer is that he should step back. Not permanently necessarily, but for a time. He should give the congregation the gift of leadership from someone else while he does the work of rebuilding his own house. This is where the church often fails men. The church says: confess, repent, move on. You can be back in leadership next month. But that is mercy on the cheap. The man who has fallen from a position of leadership needs to taste what it is like to not be in charge. He needs to learn to follow. He needs to see whether his faith is real when nobody is looking to him for spiritual guidance. He needs to let someone else feed the sheep for a while.

The rebuilding that matters is not public. It is private. It is a man sitting with his own conscience and his own shame and asking whether he is going to become someone different. Not someone better on the surface but someone different at the core. This requires him to look at the root. Why did he do what he did? Not the circumstances. The core. The pride that made him think he was above the command. The fear that made him run instead of face. The shame that made him hide instead of confess. The selfishness that made him prioritize his own comfort over the wellbeing of the people he was supposed to protect.

Real repentance means he has to change that. Not fake it. Not manage the behavior. Actually become different at the level of desire. That is not possible without help. That is why the man in repentance needs the presence of other men who have done this work, who have sat with their own shame and come out the other side, who can tell him: this is what I learned about myself. This is how I changed. And this is what it cost.

I have done this work. I have had to repent of arrogance, of taking credit that was not mine, of treating my wife carelessly, of assuming my way was the right way. And each time, the process was not dramatic. It was the long work of showing up and being different and hoping that the people I had hurt would eventually believe I was changing.

The costly hope is that rebuilding is possible. Not recovery of what was before. The broken places have scars. But the man who does the work of repentance can become someone deeper than he was before his failure. He learns humility not as a concept but as a lived experience. He learns grace not as a doctrine but as the presence of someone who stays with him after he has been disqualified. He learns what it means to be loved not because he deserves it but because he is seen, in all his failure and shame, and loved anyway.

That is what Peter discovered. Three denials that disqualified him. Three affirmations that restored him. Not to where he started but to where he needed to be. And he spent the rest of his life carrying the memory of what it felt like to fail and be restored. That memory made him a better shepherd. It made him gentler with the people he was called to lead. It made him more aware of the cost of leadership and the weight of responsibility.

The man who has fallen and rebuilt is different. He has something that the untested man does not have. He knows that failure is not permanent. He knows that grace is not a word. He knows that God is real in the presence of someone who stays with you when you deserve abandonment. And he knows that the Gospel is true not because he has never sinned but because he has sinned and been restored.

That is the hope that is worth having. Not that you will never fail. That when you fail, there will be someone there who sees you and does not flinch, and that God, through that person, will show you the way forward.

**CHAPTER 12**

The Man God Is Building

There is an image in the American church of what a biblical man looks like. He is confident in a way that borders on arrogance. He has a firm handshake and a firm sense of conviction. He provides and protects and makes the decisions. He is not emotional except for righteous anger. He is not vulnerable except in carefully controlled ways. He leads through assertion. He wins.

This image is wrong. It is a caricature built from cultural expectations rather than from Scripture. It is what a patriarchal society needs a man to be. It is not what the gospel produces.

The man God is building looks different. He is strong but not defended. He is certain about some things and genuinely uncertain about others. He provides, yes, but not as a way to control. He protects, yes, but not at the cost of presence. He leads, but he follows first. He wins some things and loses others and knows the difference between the two. He has conviction and humility and somehow they coexist.

This man is not in the movies. He is not in the culture's mythology. But he is in Scripture. He is the result of what happens when a human being lets God actually reshape him.

The first thing such a man knows is where his identity comes from. Not from his job. Not from his net worth. Not from his appearance or his power or his influence. These things may or may not be true about him. But they are not him. He is a human being made in the image of God. That is his identity. That is irreducible.

This knowledge does something to you. It frees you from the performance. You do not have to prove yourself anymore because your worth is not in question. You did not earn it through your achievements. You will not lose it through your failures. You are a man because you are. That is the starting place.

From there, everything else follows differently. Work becomes something you do, not something you are. You work hard because the work matters and because excellence is a form of worship. You work to provide and to serve. But if you lost the job tomorrow, you would still be a man. You would still matter. The identity is intact.

Relationships become something you enter into with open hands instead of clenched fists. You do not need your wife to complete you because you are already complete. She is not your other half. She is a whole person and you are a whole person and together you build something that is bigger than either of you alone. This changes everything. You do not cling to her out of fear. You move toward her out of choice. You are present with her instead of demanding that she fix something in you that only God can fix.

Your children are not your legacy project. They are people. They are people you get to know. You teach them what you know. You give them space to become who God is making them to be. You do not need them to be successful to validate your parenting. You are their father. That role has weight regardless of whether they turn out exactly as you hoped.

Your friends actually become friends. Because you are not trying to be anything other than what you are, you can let them do the same. You can tell them the truth. You can ask for help. You can admit weakness. You can be a brother instead of an authority or a competitor or a project.

The man God is building is strong enough to be gentle. This is not an oxymoron. Real strength can afford gentleness. Only the weak have to be harsh. The man who knows his identity is intact can treat people with tenderness. He does not have to prove anything. He is not threatened by other people's strength. He is not afraid that if he admits uncertainty, the whole structure will collapse.

He is gentle with his wife. Not in a way that is passive. But in a way that is honoring. He leads the family, but leadership looks like asking his wife what she thinks before deciding. It looks like considering her input as seriously as his own. It looks like admitting when she is right. It looks like making decisions together.

He is gentle with his children. He corrects them, but not from a place of ego. He sets boundaries, but not from a place of control. He teaches them, but not in a way that demands they turn out a certain way. He is present and he is firm and he is kind and somehow all three things are true at once.

He is gentle with other men. He does not feel threatened by their strength or confused by their weakness. He can celebrate their gifts without needing to diminish them. He can help them without needing to be superior. He can be helped by them without feeling diminished.

But this man is also honest. Gentleness without honesty is just passivity. He tells the truth even when it costs. He does not pretend to agree when he disagrees. He does not hide his convictions to keep the peace. He stands for something. But he stands for it without cruelty. He holds conviction and humility at the same time.

This is rare. We are more accustomed to men who are either fierce and unforgiving or passive and accommodating. A man who is both honest and kind confuses us. He does not fit the script.

He is honest about his own limitations. He does not pretend to be the answer to every problem. He does not have to know everything. He is genuinely curious about what other people think. He is willing to change his mind when evidence warrants it. He is not insecure about the things he do not know. He is learning and that is enough.

He is honest about his failures. When he sins, he says so. When he gets it wrong, he admits it. When he hurts someone, he tries to repair it. Not eventually. Now. Not when he has managed the narrative. When he has faced what he did.

He is honest about his questions. He believes. But he does not believe without wrestling. He trusts God. But he does not trust without asking sometimes why. He is not afraid of his own doubts because he has learned that doubt and faith can coexist. They are not enemies.

The man God is building knows how to work. He does the thing in front of him with full attention. He does not do it for applause or promotion. He does it because the thing matters and because his name is on it. This changes his whole approach to work. It makes him reliable. It makes him conscientious. It makes him someone people actually want to work with.

But he also knows how to stop working. He does not confuse productivity with worth. He does not believe that the work needs him to justify his existence. He takes a Sabbath. He rests. He spends time with people he loves. He does things that have no productive value. He knows that the kingdom does not rise or fall on his labor.

He provides. This is not negotiable. He takes care of the people under his care. He works. He gives. He makes sure they have shelter and food and safety. But providing is not his only role and it is not the deepest one. He is present too. He listens. He knows his children's names and his wife's hopes and his friends' struggles. He is more than the paycheck. He is the presence.

He protects. Not in a way that is suffocating. But genuinely. He is aware of threats. He is willing to put himself between his family and danger. He teaches his children to be safe without making them afraid. He creates boundaries that keep out what should not come in. But he does not use protection as an excuse to control. He does not keep people small to keep them safe.

He leads. He is not afraid of authority. But his authority looks like Christ's authority. It is servant leadership. It is authority that is willing to be questioned. It is authority that serves the people under it instead of demanding to be served by them. He makes decisions, but he makes them with wisdom. He listens to counsel. He considers the impact on the people affected. He does not need to be right all the time.

But he also follows. He has people in his life who have authority over him. His wife in the covenant they share. Other men who speak truth into his life. The church that holds him accountable. God to whom he answers. A man who never follows is a man who will eventually become tyrannical.

The man God is building has been broken and put back together. He has failed. He has faced the consequences of his failure. He has actually repented. The breaking is visible. There is a scar where the break healed. But the break did not end him. It made him deeper.

He prays. Not as a performance. As the actual practice of talking to God about the real things. His fears. His hopes. His confusion. His gratitude. His anger. His desires. He brings his whole self to prayer. Not to be censored. To be known.

He reads Scripture. Not to prove his point. To be formed by it. He lets it question him instead of only using it to answer his questions. He is willing to have his assumptions challenged. He is willing to be changed by what he reads.

He loves Jesus. Not as an abstract. As the person who died for him and rose again and is with him. This is not sentimental. It is real. It shapes how he lives because he knows what it cost. He knows he was purchased at a price. He knows he is owned. He knows he is free. Both things are true.

He is becoming something. He is not arrived. He is not finished. He is pressing on. Philippians 3: "Not that I have already obtained this or am already perfect, but I press on to take hold of that for which Christ Jesus took hold of me." This is the man God is building. Always pressing. Always reaching. Never satisfied with what he has become because he knows what he could be.

He loses sometimes. A man who only wins has never been tested. But this man has been tested. He has lost his job. He has failed at something he cared about. He has had to admit he was wrong. He has faced hard circumstances. And he is still here. He is still moving. He is still reaching toward Jesus.

He is not wealthy necessarily. He is not famous. He is not powerful in the way the world measures power. He might be. Or he might be a construction worker or a teacher or a pastor with a small church. His station is not the point. His character is.

He loves his wife well. He knows her. Not just her body, though he knows that. But her dreams. Her fears. Her thinking. Her hopes. He is present with her. He leads with her. He builds with her. They fight sometimes and they work through it instead of pretending the conflict did not happen. They are naked—physically and emotionally—and they are not ashamed.

He knows his children. He is there for their failures and their wins and their ordinary days. He teaches them who God is by showing them who he is. He is not perfect with them but he is honest. When he fails them, he apologizes. When he gets it wrong, he corrects course. They trust him because he has shown up.

He has friends who know him. Not acquaintances. Not people he watches games with. But men who know his actual life. Men he has told the truth to. Men who have carried part of what he is carrying. Men he has carried. Souls knit together.

He is different. There is something about him that is not of this world. Not weird. Different. He does not chase what everyone else is chasing. He is not afraid in the way the culture is afraid. He is not angry about the things the culture is angry about. His peace is strange. His joy is strange. His conviction is strange. And strangely, it is compelling.

He does not have it all figured out. But he is honest about what he does not know. He is humble. He is curious. He is willing to be wrong. He is willing to be surprised. He is willing to find that God is bigger than he thought.

He is the answer to a confused generation. Not because he has the right position on everything. But because he is genuinely alive. Because he is not performing. Because he is real. Because he knows who he is and he is comfortable in his own skin. Because he is free.

He is what happens when a man decides that the image matters less than the reality. When he chooses becoming over arriving. When he accepts the love of God not as something he has to earn but as something he has been given. When he lets that love reshape him.

He is not an ideal. He is a reality. He is the man that God is patient enough to build. That takes time. That takes failure and repentance. That takes faith in something bigger than himself. That takes vulnerability. That takes the willingness to be changed.

But he is real. And one day, if a man is willing, he can become that man too.

Biblical manhood is not a series of achievements. It is a rhythm. Not a destination you arrive at. A pattern you return to, day after day, for the rest of your life.

The temptation is to make this complicated. To create a list of ten things a godly man does. To turn manhood into another performance metric where you can succeed or fail depending on how many boxes you check. That is not what this is.

This is ordinary. This is the rhythm of a man's days.

Morning: Begin with prayer that is honest, not performative. Not the prayer you think you should pray. The prayer that is actually happening in your heart. If you are afraid, name the fear. If you are angry, name the anger. If you are doubting, bring the doubt. God already knows what you are feeling anyway. He is not waiting for you to get your emotions cleaned up before you talk to him. He is waiting for you to be honest. That honesty is where the conversation can actually begin.

Work: Bring excellence to whatever you are doing. Not for the sake of being seen. Not for the sake of the promotion or the money or the status. For the sake of the work itself. Do it well because it is worth doing well. Do it with integrity because integrity is how you build your own character. Do not let the work define you though. Your identity is not your title. Your identity is not your productivity. Your identity is that you are loved by God. The work is something you do, not something you are.

Home: Be the first to ask what people need. Be the first to serve. Be the first to notice when someone is hurting. Be the first to apologize when you have been wrong. Not because you are trying to earn goodness points. Because you are the one who set the tone for the home when you walked through the door. Your mood fills the room. Your presence shapes everything. If you walk in carrying frustration, everyone feels it. If you walk in with calm clarity, everyone feels that too. This is your responsibility.

Marriage: Lead from your knees, not from your chair. Leadership is not authority. Leadership is service. It means that when a decision needs to be made, you do the work to make the decision well. You listen. You consider. You think about what is best not just for you but for your wife. And then you decide. But the decision is always made with her good in mind, never against her good.

Fatherhood: Be present when it counts, which is always. Not perfect. Not pretending to have all the answers. Just present. There when your kids wake up. There when they ask the hard question. There when they fail. There when they need to know that someone believes in them even when they do not believe in themselves. The time you do not show up is the time they learn that they cannot count on you. There is no amount of money or achievement that makes up for that.

Community: Have friends who know the real version of your life. Not people you perform for. People who know your questions and your struggles and your failures and still call you brother. These friendships are worth protecting. They are worth the vulnerability they require. They are worth showing up for when it is inconvenient.

Church: Serve without needing to be seen. Do the work that matters because it matters, not because someone will notice. If you find yourself disappointed when your service goes unrecognized, you are serving the wrong master.

Rest: Stop before your body forces you to stop. Most men do not know how to rest. We keep pushing because stopping feels like failure. We wear exhaustion like a badge of honor. But exhaustion makes you sharp with your wife. Exhaustion makes you short with your kids. Exhaustion makes you stupid in your decisions. Rest is not laziness. Rest is wise stewardship of the body and the mind you have been given.

These are not heroic practices. A hero does something once. A faithful man does ordinary things repeatedly. He shows up to his marriage in the tenth year the same way he showed up in the first. He serves his church in the thirtieth year the same way he served in the third. He loves his kids when they are small and when they are grown. He keeps his promises when they are easy and when they are impossibly hard.

This is what is countercultural now. Not the big gestures. The sustained ordinary faithfulness that nobody applauds. The consistency that builds character. The presence that shapes souls.

This is manhood.

**CONCLUSION**

What I Would Tell My Father

I never met you. You left before I knew language. My mother has told me things about you over the years, but they are her stories, not my memories. You are a ghost in my life. Not a haunting. Just an absence. A space where something should have been.

I want to tell you some things. Not with anger anymore. That came and went. Not with bitterness. That burned through years ago. With something harder and more honest. With clarity.

You missed me. That is the first thing I want you to know. Not as a accusation. As a fact. You have no idea who I am. You do not know that I am someone who thinks through writing. That I work through philosophy and theology to understand why things happen. That I love my wife so much that the thought of hurting her makes me sick. That I have five sons and they know me because you did not know me and I decided that stops here.

You do not know that I grew up wanting to understand you. That I asked my mother questions trying to piece together why you left. That I created versions of you in my head. That sometimes you were a victim of circumstance and sometimes you were a coward and sometimes you were just broken. You were whatever I needed you to be so that your absence made sense.

You do not know that I hated you. I do not think I have said that out loud before. But I did. Not all the time. But in moments. When my mother was working double shifts and I was old enough to know what that cost her. When I watched her hands. When I realized that the absence of a man was costing her everything.

I know now that you were a man too. That you probably had your own pain. That you probably had reasons that made sense to you. That you probably thought you were doing the best you could. I can tell you that it was not enough. But I can also tell you that I understand the weight of manhood in ways you probably never did. That maybe if you were here now, we could talk about that.

But you are not here. And I have had to build something without you. I have had to figure out how to be a man from other men. From a Christian who answered my atheist questions. From a pastor who invited me into his life. From friends who showed up. From Scripture. From failure and repentance.

I want to tell you about your grandsons. You have five. The oldest is working through what it means to be a man in a world that wants him to be either weak or brutal. He is thinking about it. He is asking questions. He is learning that strength and gentleness can coexist. He is learning this from me. I am teaching him what I wish you had taught me.

Titus is fourteen. He is becoming a man. He has your height, maybe. I do not know. But he does not have your absence. He knows me. He has sat in my office and asked me hard questions and I have answered him honestly. When I do not know something, I tell him. When I am wrong, I admit it. When I am figuring it out, I say so. He knows that manhood is not about having all the answers. It is about asking the right questions and being willing to be wrong.

Your other grandsons are growing. They know their father. They know that they matter. They know that they are loved. They know that they can fail and still be received. These are not small things. These are the things that a man needs to know to become whole.

I want to tell you about the Pastors Connection Network. It exists because of you. Not because of who you are, but because of who you are not. I started PCN because I finally understood that you were not alone in your failure. That there are thousands of men—pastors especially—who are dying in isolation. Who have no one to talk to. Who have no one who knows their actual life. Who have no one to carry part of what they are carrying.

They come to PCN because they need to be known. They need other men to show up. They need to hear that they are not alone. They need to have their souls knit to the souls of other men. They need what you did not get. What I did not get from you. They need presence.

I am giving them what you could not give me. I am showing up. I am listening. I am not fixing anything. I am just being there. And that is changing their lives. That is saving their marriages. That is keeping them alive.

You see what happened? Your absence created a capacity in me. It made me able to understand what other men were dying from. It made me willing to show up for people that nobody else was showing up for. Your failure became my calling.

I am not telling you this to make you feel guilty. I am telling you this because it is true. The deepest work in my life has come from your absence. Not because the absence was good. But because I decided to fill it instead of carry it.

I want to tell you about your daughter-in-law. Her name is Susanna. She is stronger than you can possibly understand. She stayed with me when I was making the same mistakes you made. She told me I had to choose: her or the work. She gave me the chance you never gave my mother. She did not run. She stayed and she fought and she demanded better.

I am grateful to her every day. Not just for staying. But for her refusal to let me hide. For calling me back to myself. For naming what I was doing wrong. For not accepting the narrative I was telling myself. She is the reason I am not you.

I want to tell you that I have forgiven you. I know that you are not here to hear it. I know that you probably never thought about it the same way I have. I know that you have likely moved on and built another life and maybe you think about my mother and me sometimes but maybe you do not. That is not my concern anymore.

What I have done is release you. I have stopped needing you to make sense. I have stopped waiting for you to show up. I have stopped blaming you for everything that is broken in me. I have taken responsibility for who I have become. And I have become a man in spite of your absence, not because of it.

The grace in this is that I do not have to be you. I do not have to replicate your failure. I do not have to run. I do not have to hide. I do not have to be afraid. I get to choose. Every day I get to choose to be present. To be honest. To stay. To show up.

I think that is what God wanted all along. Not for men to be perfect. But for men to be there. To be honest. To face what they face instead of hiding from it. To let other people matter. To let love require something of them.

You did not get that. But I did. And every time I sit with one of my sons and we have a hard conversation, I am getting it. Every time I admit to my wife that I was wrong, I am getting it. Every time I show up for another pastor who is dying of loneliness, I am getting it.

The fatherless boy became a father. And the cycle breaks. Not because I am strong. Not because I did not carry your absence. But because grace is relentless. Because God does not give up on broken lines. Because He is patient enough to rebuild what men destroy.

I sat with Titus the other night. He asked me if I thought he would be a good father someday. I told him the truth. I told him that I did not know. That nobody knows that. That the only thing he could control is whether he showed up. Whether he was honest. Whether he let people matter. I told him that his job was not to be perfect. His job was to be there.

He nodded. And I realized that I was not just talking to my son. I was talking to the part of me that is still the boy whose father left. I was saying to that boy: This is what manhood is. Not absence. Presence. Not hiding. Honesty. Not performance. Reality.

I think you would be different now if you could see this. If you could see what your absence created. If you could see the strength that grew from it. If you could see the men you did not know you were building.

But I do not need you to see it anymore. I have seen it. And that is enough.

I have become for other men the presence I did not have growing up. I have built a line of men who know what it means to be known. I have broken the cycle. Not perfectly. But genuinely.

And your grandsons will do it differently than I did. They will take what served them and change what did not. And their sons will do the same. And on down the line. The grace moves forward. The brokenness does not have to repeat.

That is the gospel. Not that everything turns out okay. But that God is patient enough to rebuild what men destroy. That grace is relentless. That love comes looking for us even when we run. That a fatherless boy can become a father to many.

You are gone. I accept that now. But what you left behind is not just pain. It is a calling. It is a wound that has become a ministry. It is an absence that taught me the weight of presence.

So I forgive you. Not for your sake. For mine. Not because what you did was okay. But because holding the anger was costing me more than letting it go.

And I release you.

Go in peace.

I will be here. Building what you could not. Staying where you could not. Loving in ways you could not imagine. Showing my sons that manhood is not about running. It is about coming home.

That is what I would tell my father.

I have five sons. Their names are different. Their personalities are different. Their paths will be different. But there is something I want each of them to carry from my life, even if they never read this book.

To Titus and the others: You are not becoming a man. You are receiving manhood. It is not something you achieve. It is something that is given to you by the God who made you, shaped by the men who know you, refined by the failures that humble you, and redeemed by the grace that will not let you go.

You will fail. You will fail in ways that feel catastrophic. You will do things you are not proud of. You will hurt people you love. You will face moments where you do not recognize the man in the mirror because he does not look like the man you thought you were.

That is not the end of the story. That is the place where the story actually begins.

The gospel is not a story about men who got it right. It is a story about men who got it wrong, who brought their wrong to Jesus, and who were loved anyway. David was not a righteous king. He was a man who committed adultery and murder and psalm-writing repentance. Peter was not a bold apostle. He was a man who denied knowing Jesus three times and then became the rock the church was built on. Paul was not a consistent missionary. He was a man who murdered Christians and then spent his life trying to make it right.

Your failure does not disqualify you. It qualifies you. It teaches you that you cannot do this in your own strength. It teaches you that you need grace. It teaches you that the God you worship is not a God who demands that you be perfect. He is a God who demands that you be honest.

When you are honest about your weakness, you become dangerous. Not to other people. To the Enemy. Because you are no longer carrying the burden of maintaining an image. You are no longer alone with your shame. You are no longer trying to fix yourself. You are letting yourself be fixed. And a man who has been broken and repaired by grace is a man who can be trusted with power because he knows his power is not his own.

There is a specific prayer I want you to know. Not the words. The pattern. The stance.

First, ask for what you need. Not what you think you should need. What you actually need. Are you afraid? Ask for courage. Are you discouraged? Ask for hope. Are you lonely? Ask for connection. Do not pray like you are checking a box. Pray like you are talking to your father.

Second, listen for what God is asking of you. Not what the culture is asking. Not what your image is asking. What God is asking. It might be to stay. It might be to go. It might be to speak. It might be to be silent. Listen.

Third, do what he is asking. Not because you will be rewarded. Not because it will work out. Just because he asked. That is the posture of a faithful man. Not a man who calculates whether obedience is worth it. A man who obeys because the one asking is worthy.

The world needs men who are willing to be weak in front of their wives. Men who are willing to admit they do not know the answer. Men who are willing to be servants instead of generals. Men who are willing to lay down their power for the sake of their families and their communities.

The culture will not celebrate you for this. The culture celebrates the opposite. The culture celebrates the man who wins, who dominates, who controls. But the gospel celebrates something different. The gospel celebrates the man who kneels.

I do not know what your life will look like. I do not know what failures you will face or what victories you will celebrate. I do not know if you will be a pastor or a plumber or a father or all of those things. I do not know the specifics of your story.

But I know this: If you build your life on the foundation that you are loved by a God who does not ask you to be someone you are not, if you learn to bring your whole self to him instead of just the parts you have cleaned up, if you learn to serve the people you love more than you serve yourself, if you learn to be honest about your weakness, if you learn to be faithful in small things so that you can be trusted with larger ones, then you will have a life that matters.

Not a life of achievement. A life of faithfulness. Not a life of being seen. A life of seeing others and loving them the way Christ has loved you.

That is the manhood the gospel is calling you toward. Not perfection. Faithfulness. Not strength that dominates. Strength that serves. Not success that builds your name. Success that builds the kingdom.

The prayer I pray for you, every day, is not that you will be perfect. It is that you will stay. It is that you will have the courage to tell the truth about yourself and trust that the truth will not destroy you because the God who made you already knows it. It is that you will have the gentleness to kneel. It is that you will have the strength to stand back up.

It is that you will be the man your family needs and the man the world needs and the man God made you to be.
