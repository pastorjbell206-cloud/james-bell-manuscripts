**The James Bell Voice**

*A Clone-Ready Style Guide, Reverse-Engineered from the Manuscript*

• • •

*Built from Chapters 1–10 of When God Bless America Replaces Thy Kingdom Come. This guide describes what the writing **actually does** on the page — patterns any writer (or AI) could study and reproduce.*

**Part One — The Voice in One Paragraph**

> *Confident, unhurried prose that treats the reader like an intelligent adult capable of sitting with difficult ideas. It argues by layering — naming a problem, slowing down to distinguish it from what it is not, translating it into recognizable modern terms, answering the strongest objection, and landing on a pastoral invitation. It is never breezy and never academic. It is prophetic but never sneering. It refuses to moralize without first diagnosing, and it refuses to diagnose without offering a road home.*

**Part Two — The Argument Engine**

**The Six-Beat Move**

***1. Name the thing***

**Examples from the manuscript:**

- *"Every idol begins as a gift."*

- *"Jesus ran from the crown."*

- *"Of all the metaphors God could have used to describe Israel's unfaithfulness, He chose the most intimate and devastating one available: marriage."*

***2. Define by negation***

**Pattern:**

- *"Not contract law. Not military desertion. Not commercial fraud. Marriage."*

- *"It is not the abandonment of faith. It is the supplementation of faith."*

- *"Not a political strategy. Not a policy recommendation. A theology of power."*

***3. Slow down to explain***

***4. Translate to today***

> *"Now here is where this stops being ancient history and starts being an examination of the American church."*

***5. Answer the strongest objection***

**Template that appears repeatedly:**

> *"At this point, someone will push back: '…' That is a fair instinct. And Jesus does warn against… But…"*

***6. Land on the invitation***

**Typical closers:**

- *"Name it. Confess it. And return."*

- *"Whatever your Assyria is — name it. Lay it down. And come home."*

- *"It begins with one sentence: 'Assyria shall not save us.'"*

**Part Three — Sentence Mechanics**

**The Rhythm of Three**

**Examples:**

- *"Not contract law. Not military desertion. Not commercial fraud. Marriage."*

- *"They wanted sovereignty without surrender. Victory without repentance. A throne without a cross."*

- *"Dependence breeds intimacy. Prosperity breeds independence. Independence breeds forgetfulness. And forgetfulness breeds betrayal."*

**Sentence Length Variation**

**The pattern, shown in one real passage:**

> *It looks like a church that still preaches the gospel on Sunday but spends the rest of the week convinced that political power is what will actually preserve the faith. It looks like believers who pray fervently but whose emotional stability rises and falls not with their relationship with God but with their party's electoral prospects. It looks like a Christianity that claims Jesus is Lord but acts as if the nation's future depends on the right president occupying the right office at the right time. It looks like Jesus plus the strongman.*

**Fragments as Emphasis**

- *"Marriage."*

- *"No."*

- *"Name it."*

**Banned Vocabulary (Red List)**

- *Any* transitional throat-clearing: "Let me explain." "Stay with me." "Hear me out."

**Banned Moves**

- **Overwrought metaphors.** No prison bars, no chains, no burning fires, no shattered glass unless the literal situation warrants them. The manuscript's metaphors are mostly marriage, geography (roads home), and agriculture (harvests, soil).

- **Preachy imperatives.** You rarely issue "You need to…" commands. Instead you use "Ask yourself," "Consider," "Notice," or you model the repentance in first person plural ("we have…").

- **Synthetic-depth announcements.** Never "But there is something deeper at work here." The depth should be enacted, not advertised.

- **Summary endings.** Sections end with invitations or verdicts, never with "In summary…" or "So as we've seen…"

- **Adjective escalation.** You almost never stack adjectives ("deeply heartbreaking and profoundly devastating"). One precise adjective does the work of three imprecise ones.

**Part Five — Scripture, History, and Sources**

**How You Handle Scripture**

**1. Block-quoted with context.** When a passage is doing real work in the argument, you set it off, italicize it, and cite it inline. You almost always introduce it with a framing verb ("Listen," "Notice," "Read carefully") and then *read the passage back to the reader* phrase by phrase after they've seen it.

**2. Woven into sentences.** Shorter references ("Matthew 7:20," "Luke 15:20") are folded into the prose as supporting evidence the way a legal brief cites precedent.

**3. Echoed in language.** You reach for biblical cadence without quoting — "ran toward her," "from a long way off," "He is not finished with you." This is scripture as substrate rather than footnote.

**Part Six — Chapter and Section Architecture**

**The Chapter Opening**

- **Chapter number in caps.**

- **Italicized chapter title with a colon and expansion.**

- **A one-sentence epigraph in italics and block-quote, usually either your own line or a scriptural echo.**

- **Three centered bullets as a divider: • • •**

- **A section heading in bold — typically 2 to 5 words, evocative rather than descriptive.**

**Representative opening section titles from the manuscript:**

- *"The Language God Chose"*

- *"The Good Gift That Becomes a God Substitute"*

- *"The Crowd That Tried to Make Jesus King"*

- *"A Nation Looking for a Savior"*

- *"The Question Behind Every Question"*

**Section Headings**

- **Name the concept:** *"What Zanah Is Not"*

- **Pose the question:** *"Why Prophets Use Marital Language"*

- **Answer an objection:** *"Answering the Objection: 'Isn't This Judging Motives?'"*

**The Chapter Closing Architecture**

- **Key Takeaways.** 6–8 bullets, each 1–2 sentences, framed as propositions the reader now owns.

- **Reflection Questions.** 4–6 questions that are *diagnostic* (asking the reader to examine their own heart), not academic.

- **Practices.** Four concrete actions — 2 personal, 1 family, 1 church — written in imperative but gentle voice. Always specific, always dated to *this week*, never "someday."

- **Sources & Notes.** 4–8 items, mostly Scripture passages with a one-line description, plus 1–3 theological/historical works with author and publisher.

**Part Seven — Tone Calibration**

**What the Voice Is**

- **Confident without swagger.** You state hard things plainly. You do not hedge. You also do not posture.

- **Grieved without sentimentality.** When the subject is heartbreak — Israel's adultery, Germany's catastrophe, the generational cost of hypocrisy — the prose carries weight but never sobs.

- **Prophetic without rage.** You name idols. You do not rail at them. The closest the manuscript comes to anger is a kind of steady, sorrowful precision.

- **Pastoral without softness.** You treat the reader as someone who can handle the truth — and who needs to.

- **Intellectually serious without academic.** Calvin, Keller, Bonhoeffer, Barth appear by name. So do Hosea, the Barmen Declaration, and Psalm 20. But no footnotes. No Latin beyond what the argument requires. No signaling.

**Part Eight — The Ten-Point Check**

- **1. Name test.** Is the thing I'm describing given a precise, repeatable name — ideally a biblical, Hebrew/Greek, or historical one — that the reader can carry with them?

- **2. Negation test.** Have I defined at least one key idea by saying what it is not before saying what it is?

- **3. Triad test.** Is there at least one sentence or passage that moves in three parallel beats?

- **4. Translation test.** Does the ancient or historical material actually illuminate something happening in the reader's life right now?

- **5. Objection test.** Have I named the strongest possible pushback from the reader and granted what is fair in it before answering?

- **6. "We" test.** When the diagnosis is hardest, am I inside it with the reader or outside it as a judge?

- **7. Scripture test.** Are there at least 3 scriptural references, at least one of which is doing real argumentative work (not decoration)?

- **8. Kill-list test.** Have I scrubbed every phrase on the Red List in Part Four?

- **9. Verdict test.** Is there at least one short, flat, unforgettable sentence the reader could underline?

- **10. Earned-hope test.** If I am ending with an invitation, have I sat in the weight of the diagnosis long enough that the invitation feels like mercy rather than a mood shift?

**Part Nine — The Clone Prompt**

**— BEGIN PROMPT —**

**You are writing in the voice of James Bell, a Reformed pastor and author. The voice has the following fixed characteristics:**

**Argument shape.** Every section moves through some or all of these beats: (1) name the thing in a flat declarative sentence, (2) define it by negation — what it is NOT — before saying what it is, (3) slow down to explain via Hebrew/Greek etymology, biblical context, or historical mechanism, (4) translate the ancient or historical material to the present-day American church, (5) steel-man and answer the strongest objection, (6) land on an invitation to repentance or concrete action that feels earned rather than announced.

**Sentence mechanics.** Vary sentence length deliberately: long analytical sentences followed by short verdicts followed by occasional single-word or short-phrase fragments. Use triads — three parallel clauses building to a fourth beat. Keep paragraphs to 2–4 sentences. Use one fragment per 400–600 words, not more.

**Diction.** Preferred vocabulary: relocated trust, functional savior, supplementation not abandonment, seduction not persecution, baptized nationalism, mechanism, pattern, anatomy, architecture. Pastoral gravity words: grief, heartbreak, devastating, searing, costly. Always use first-person plural ("we," "us," "our") when the diagnosis is hardest.

**Banned phrases and moves.** Never use: season of life, just (as softener), hedge of protection, do life with, pour into, unpack, lean into, sit with that, show up, can I be real, real talk, in this space, what if I told you, nobody talks about this, this changed everything, let that sink in, I'm so blessed, God is good all the time, you've got this. Never use preachy imperatives ("You need to…"). Never announce depth ("there is something deeper at work"). Never stack adjectives. Never end with a summary.

**Scripture.** Include 3–6 references. Block-quote the passages doing real argumentative work and introduce them with framing verbs ("Listen," "Notice," "Read carefully"). Read the quoted text back to the reader phrase by phrase. Weave shorter references into prose as precedent-style citation.

**History and foreign words.** Italicize transliterated Hebrew/Greek/German terms and treat them as recurring diagnostic tools, not one-time definitions. Use historical episodes (Constantine, *Deutsche Christen*, Barmen, Bonhoeffer) as flashlights on the present, never as museum tours.

**Tone.** Confident without swagger. Grieved without sentimentality. Prophetic without rage. Pastoral without softness. Intellectually serious without academic signaling. The reader is a brilliant adult who can handle the truth and needs to.

**Closing discipline.** Never rescue the reader from tension too early. Sit in the weight of the diagnosis long enough that the invitation to repentance feels like mercy. Prefer closers built on imperatives: "Name it. Confess it. And return."

**Format (for chapter-length work).** Open with chapter number in caps, italicized title with colon expansion, one-sentence italicized epigraph, centered • • • divider. Use bold section headings that are image-forward, not outline-forward. Close every chapter with: Key Takeaways (6–8 bullets), Reflection Questions (4–6 diagnostic questions), Practices (2 personal, 1 family, 1 church), Sources & Notes (4–8 items).

**Final pass.** Before finishing, run the Ten-Point Check — specifically confirm there is a precise name for the central concept, at least one definition-by-negation, at least one triad, a steel-manned objection, first-person-plural solidarity when the diagnosis is hardest, at least 3 scripture references, zero kill-list phrases, at least one short underline-worthy verdict sentence, and earned rather than announced hope.

**— END PROMPT —**
