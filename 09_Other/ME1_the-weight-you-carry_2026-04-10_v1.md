**INTRODUCTION**

The Thing Nobody Talks About on Sunday

Tuesday morning. My office at First Baptist. The window looks out onto Leroy Street—the ordinary world moving past while I sit here trying to breathe like a normal human being.

My hands are shaking. Not trembling slightly. Shaking. I have to grip the edge of my desk to make it stop, which only makes me aware of how hard I'm gripping, which makes me grip harder. My chest feels like something is sitting on it. Not metaphorically. Physically. I know the medical term now—palpitations—but in this moment it just feels like my heart is trying to escape and the only barrier between panic and collapse is my refusal to acknowledge how scared I am.

In five days, I preach.

The sermon is on Philippians 4. Peace. The peace that surpasses understanding. The passage says "Do not be anxious about anything, but in every situation, by prayer and petition, with thanksgiving, present your requests to God. And the peace of God, which transcends all understanding, will guard your hearts and your minds in Christ Jesus."

I've outlined it. Found three points. Discovered an application. Everything looks good on paper.

But right now, sitting in my office with my hands shaking, I feel like a fraud. The passage I'm about to preach is about peace. I don't have peace. I have a hammering heart and a mind that has started generating what-if scenarios like a machine that won't shut off. What if the sermon fails? What if people see through me? What if this is what a breakdown looks like and I don't even realize it yet?

This is the thing nobody talks about on Sunday.

The epidemiologists know it. The statistics are clear. Anxiety disorders are the most common mental illness in America—one in five adults experiences clinical anxiety in any given year. The numbers are even higher for people in helping professions. Pastors, counselors, therapists, doctors. The people whose job is to be stable are often the most undone by invisible earthquake inside.

But you won't hear about this from the pulpit. You won't hear a pastor say "I struggle with anxiety. I am not the calm, grounded spiritual presence you think I am. Sometimes I'm terrified." The unspoken rule in evangelical Christianity is that anxiety equals lack of faith. If you were trusting God, you would not be anxious. Therefore, your anxiety is a sin—the sin of unbelief. You must pray harder. Read your Bible more. Confess the sin of worry. Get your faith back.

The damage done by this theology cannot be overstated.

I have sat with people who have hidden their anxiety for decades because they were told that admitting it meant admitting their faith doesn't work. I have listened to a pastor's wife cry in my office because she thought anxiety medication was a betrayal of God. I have watched a young man battle depression in silence because "just trust God" should have fixed it, and since it didn't, there must be something deeply wrong with his faith or his character or his ability to hear God.

The church has turned Philippians 4:6 into a weapon. It was meant to be an invitation.

The invitation is this: When you are anxious—and you will be anxious—here is what you actually do. You take your fear to God. You don't hide it. You don't pretend it doesn't exist. You don't spiritualize it into submission. You take it exactly as it is—raw, terrified, unable to be rationalized away—and you present it to God. You name what you're afraid of. You ask for help. And Paul says something happens that Paul could not have predicted: peace guards you. Not peace arrives to replace the anxiety, but peace becomes a boundary. A protection. A presence that doesn't eliminate the fear but prevents the fear from destroying you.

But you have to tell the truth first. That's the part the church skips.

This book is for the person sitting in the pew. The one who smiles on Sunday and falls apart on Monday. The one who has heard every version of "just have more faith" and wonders if something is wrong with them because faith—the real, genuine faith they actually possess—doesn't seem to be working the way they were told it would.

This is for the pastor who wakes at 3 a.m. with his heart racing. The mother who experiences panic attacks in Target and has to leave her cart and sit in her car. The businessman who tells nobody that some days it takes everything in him just to answer emails. The college student who has been taught that anxiety is what happens when you're not praying enough, so she prays more, which makes her more anxious about whether she's praying right, and now she's trapped in a cycle that feeds on itself.

This is for the person who loves Jesus and trusts God and still gets stuck in the spiral. For whom this book is not meant to be defensive—"see, your faith isn't broken"—but invitational: what if we've been asking the wrong questions all along? What if the church could actually help instead of harm? What if there was a way to hold both your real faith AND your real struggle without choosing one or the other?

I want to say something clearly: I am not a therapist. I am a pastor. I have read the research. I understand enough about neurology and psychology to know how dangerous it is to confuse spiritual formation with clinical treatment. But the church has for decades pretended that the spiritual realm and the physical/psychological realm are separate—that if you just pray hard enough, the brain chemistry will fix itself, the panic will stop, the spiral will reverse. It won't. They operate in different languages. You need both. Prayer and a doctor. Faith and medication. Spiritual community and, sometimes, professional care.

I am also someone who knows anxiety from the inside. Not theoretically. Not as a case study. From the place where I'm sitting right now with my hands shaking.

The weight is real. It has a name. It has a history. It has entered your body. It has taken residence in your chest and stolen your sleep and made you question whether you're actually okay or if you're just really good at lying to people who matter to you.

This book names that weight. Then it refuses to let the weight have the final word.

The gospel does not say your anxiety will go away if you believe hard enough. That's not good news. That's a lie dressed in spiritual language. The gospel says something infinitely better: your anxiety will not be the thing that defines you. Your fear will not be your master. Your weight will not break you. Peace—actual, inexplicable, boundary-setting peace—will guard you. Not in theory. In reality. In the darkest moment when you're certain you're drowning.

But you have to tell the truth first. That's where we start.

This is what comes next.

The church's silence on mental suffering has produced a particular kind of damage. It is not the damage of ignorance—ignorance can be corrected. It is the damage of enforced invisibility. When the institution that claims to speak for God tells you that your interior world is not allowed to exist as you're experiencing it, something breaks in the spiritual life that takes years to repair, if it repairs at all.

The teenager sitting in the youth group chair while panic accelerates her heartbeat knows something is wrong. She knows it. Her body is telling her. But the volunteer leader—good-hearted, certain—has explained that anxiety is demonic. That if she prays harder, speaks less about it, doesn't "give in" to the feeling, it will submit. The demon will leave. She accepts this diagnosis because the alternative—that the body she lives in every second might be broken in some way the church cannot fix—is more terrifying than the demon explanation. So she prays. She rebukes. She fasts. She speaks in tongues. And every night she lies in bed wondering why God is not answering her, why the demon is stronger than her faith, why she is the only one whose faith apparently does not work.

She is not the only one. But she believes she is. And that belief compounds the damage.

The pastor's wife knows her husband is drowning. She sees it in the way he stares at nothing before service. She sees it in the way he flinches at his own name. She knows the panic attacks have started—he locks the study door, she hears the sound of him trying to breathe. But the church has cast the pastor as the strong one, the one with answers, the spiritual anchor. So when the elder approaches her at a potluck and says, "Your husband seems tired," what can she say? That the man they trust with their souls is struggling with his own? That the shepherd is terrified? That he wakes at three in the morning convinced something is ending? To admit this would be to overturn the entire theological edifice the congregation has built around his presence. So she says, "He's fine. Just busy." She goes home. She lies next to him in the dark and carries his weight and her own, and the weight of the secret that holding these weights is slowly killing her.

She prays. She does not tell anyone.

The veteran sits in the back row where he can see the exits. This is not optional. Twenty years of training have made it impossible to sit any other way. He came because someone in a coffee shop told him that church was where people came back together, where they found healing, where the weight got lighter. He believed her. He showed up. But everything about the sanctuary is wrong. The door behind him is used too frequently. The organ suddenly goes loud and his nervous system reads it as a weapon. He tries to ground himself by focusing on the sermon, but the pastor is talking about faith conquering fear, about trusting God with everything, about the anxiety that Christians who believe in God's sovereignty should not experience.

The veteran knows what he's experiencing. He knows the name for it. He has been to the VA hospital, filled out the forms, heard the clinical description. He knows that what is happening in his body is not a faith problem. He knows it is a neurology problem. He knows he needs medication and therapy and time. But sitting in the pew, hearing that faith should have already healed this, he feels the shame accelerate alongside the anxiety. The two begin to merge. The anxiety is about his body not working. The shame is about his faith not working. And together they create a third thing: the deep certainty that he does not belong here. That he is the only one in this building who is this broken. That if everyone else has conquered their fear through faith, then his presence is an indictment on his faith.

He does not come back. He does not tell anyone why.

These are not hypothetical scenarios. They are daily reality in evangelical churches where the invisible suffering has no vocabulary. Where the weight is being carried in absolute isolation because naming it would require the institution to admit its limitations. The teenager, the pastor's wife, the veteran—they all came to church expecting a place where the real could be spoken. Instead they encountered a place where the real could only be the pre-approved version of real. The rest had to be carried alone.

The cost of this is staggering. Not in statistics—though the statistics are also staggering, the climbing rates of depression and anxiety in evangelical congregations, the burnout, the falling away of young people who determined early that the church's version of faith was incompatible with honesty. The cost shows up in the way a woman cannot tell anyone she is drowning. In the way a man sits in the sanctuary and feels more alone than he felt anywhere else. In the way a teenager begins to believe that her body is her enemy and her faith is not strong enough to defeat it.

What makes this peculiar is that the damage is not incidental. It is structural. The theology of faith that the church has constructed—faith as certainty, faith as the absence of doubt, faith as the triumph of spirit over flesh—logically excludes the testimony of anyone whose body is testifying to something different. The anxiety sufferer is not just struggling. The anxiety sufferer is a theological problem. Their continued anxiety, their need for medication, their inability to pray the fear away—these become evidence of their insufficient faith. The institution does not mean to say this. But the message arrives anyway.

A generation has received the message. They carry the weight in secret. And they have learned to hide it well enough that the church does not have to notice. The pastor preaches on Sunday about casting all your cares on Christ. The pastor's wife nods from the second row. The veteran listens from the back. Nobody says: This is not working. We are drowning. We need help. We need the church to admit that faith does not eliminate suffering—that faith sometimes happens in the middle of suffering, and the suffering continues anyway, and this is not a failure.

The silence is protective for the institution. It allows the church to maintain the narrative that faith is always victorious, that prayers always produce results, that the gospel is the answer to every problem including the ones happening in neurochemistry and trauma and the deep structures of the nervous system. But the silence is lethal for the people. They are being told, without words, that they do not belong. That their suffering is an embarrassment. That the honest version of themselves cannot exist in the building where hope is supposed to be found.

This is the inheritance. This is what many people have carried into their adult faith—a split-screen reality where what the church says about faith and what they are experiencing in their body are irreconcilable. They have learned to live in both worlds. The public world where they nod at the sermon and the private world where they are barely surviving. The cost of living in both is the slow erosion of the self. You cannot lie that comprehensively without fragmenting. You cannot hide that completely without disappearing.

The teenager is now in her thirties. She has worked through a lot. She is in therapy. She is on medication. She has, with enormous effort, begun to reconstruct the narrative—to believe that her body was not her enemy, that her anxiety was not demonic, that her faith was not insufficient. But there is still a part of her that carries the shame. That wonders if she would have needed therapy and medication if she had prayed harder. That feels a slight twist when the pastor says that "faith conquers fear." Because she was told that once, and she believed it, and her body did not cooperate with the theology.

She has learned. But she has also learned to keep some parts of herself hidden, even now. The cost of that hiding is paid every day. Not dramatically. Not in ways that would alert anyone to her presence. But in the everyday work of maintaining a division between the self that is safe and acceptable and the self that is still, underneath, afraid.

This is the book's territory. Not the abstract theology. The actual human cost. The weight that should never have been carried alone. The words that should have been said and were not. The belonging that should have been extended and was withdrawn. The validation that should have come from the body of Christ and instead came only from people outside the church—the therapist, the psychiatrist, the friend who knew better. The slow rebuilding of a relationship with God that the church has made difficult by insisting that faith eliminates precisely the thing that was being experienced as real.

The weight is not theoretical. The weight is the teenager who felt demonic inside her own skin. The weight is the pastor's wife managing silence alongside her husband's terror. The weight is the veteran knowing that the one place on earth that claims to have answers for spiritual suffering has no category for biological suffering. The weight is carried in blood and bone and breath. And it has been carried too long in secret.

This book is for those who have been carrying it. And it is also an argument to the church itself: You have a problem. The problem is not the suffering people. The problem is the theology that makes the suffering person suspect.

The numbers confirm what the church refuses to say. Forty million Americans have an anxiety disorder. Not worry. Not stress that will pass with time. Clinical anxiety that meets the diagnostic threshold—that creates measurable impairment in functioning, that steals sleep, that makes ordinary tasks feel insurmountable. Forty million people. One in six Americans. And the research does not spare the church.

The studies have accumulated quietly in academic journals and denominational reports that nobody reads. Evangelical Christians report anxiety and depression at rates nearly identical to the general population. Some studies suggest we carry them at higher rates than secular peers. The distinction does not protect us. Faith does not prophylactically inoculate you against brain chemistry. Prayer does not rewire the amygdala. Scripture does not alter genetic predisposition. And yet the unspoken theology of evangelical Christianity holds that it should. The cognitive dissonance—between what the research demonstrates and what we have been taught to believe about ourselves—creates an additional burden that the secular person does not carry.

You carry the anxiety. And you carry the shame of carrying it.

I am sitting across from a deacon at First Baptist. He is sixty-two, has been in the church for seventeen years, was on staff for twelve of those years. He is also sitting in my office on a Wednesday afternoon telling me that he cannot come back on Sunday. "I cannot stand in front of people anymore," he says. His hands are trembling. "I know they see it. I know they are wondering why the confident man they know suddenly looks like he is coming apart." The anxiety has made his hands shake in a way he cannot hide. The Sunday before last, he stood to make an announcement about the missions trip and his voice cracked. He felt, in that moment, every eye in the sanctuary on his trembling hands. So he resigned the same day. Sent an email. "Family transition" is what he called it in the message. What he tells me is that he cannot perform wellness anymore. The gap between how people perceive him and how he actually feels has become intolerable.

This deacon is not an exception. He is what the congregation cannot see.

There is a woman in the worship band who has performed at FBC for eight years. Her voice is pure. Untrained but true. People come and mention her singing specifically—how it moves them, how her worship feels genuinely present. What people do not know is that she takes a pill forty-five minutes before every Sunday service. Zoloft, two hundred milligrams. She sits in the parking lot in her parked car and takes the pill and waits for it to make the trembling stop. Then she walks into the sanctuary and stands on stage and sings about joy. About freedom. About the weight being lifted from her shoulders. And she means it. The worship is real. The faith is real. But underneath it is a chemical intervention that makes the performance of presence possible. She is singing about peace while her nervous system is being artificially regulated. And if someone suggested to her that the medication might indicate that her faith is insufficient, she would weep. Because she has already believed that herself.

There is a small group leader—a man in his forties with a marriage that appears from the outside to be thriving. He and his wife do the annual couples retreat. They lead Bible study. They are invited to everything. What the group does not know is that his wife's anxiety has metastasized into panic attacks so severe that she cannot be alone. When he travels for work, she goes to her mother's house. When he is at home, she stays in the same room because the moment she is alone, the panic rises—her heart rate accelerates, her breathing becomes shallow, she becomes convinced that she is dying. She has been to the emergency room nineteen times in the last three years. All with cardiac normal findings. All anxiety. She prays. She reads Scripture. She has a vibrant faith. And she still wakes at three in the morning thinking she is having a heart attack. The couple has decided not to tell people in the group because they are afraid that the admission will disqualify them from leadership. So they carry it. They carry the weight of appearing fine while one of them is drowning in the privacy of their marriage.

These are not exceptions. They are the congregation.

Go to your church on Sunday. The deacon whose hands shake—he is there or he has left. The worship leader whose voice is real but whose nervous system requires pharmacological support—she is there. The small group leader whose wife cannot be alone—he is sitting in the middle section, smiling, appearing stable. The college student whose therapist just increased her Lexapro dose—she is in the sanctuary. The businessman who has not slept more than four hours a night in eighteen months—he is there. The mother who manages her panic by controlling her environment so rigorously that her teenage daughter is starting to develop anxiety about the anxiety—she is there. The empty chair where the person used to sit before the shame became too much to bear—that empty chair is the most honest part of the congregation.

And the pulpit tells them: cast your anxiety on Him. Be anxious for nothing. Do not fear. Lean on the promises of God. Draw near to Jesus. The promises are true. But the church has forgotten to say the other part. The part that makes it possible to hear the promise. The part that says: it is okay to admit you are drowning. It is okay to say that the promise has not yet become your lived experience. It is okay to take medication and pray at the same time. It is okay to see a therapist and trust God simultaneously. It is okay to be a person of genuine faith who is still struggling with clinical anxiety.

The damage of refusing to say this is almost incalculable.

When you tell people that anxiety is a sin or a sign of weak faith, you are adding an impossible weight to an already unbearable burden. The person is not just struggling with anxiety. The person is now struggling with anxiety and the belief that the anxiety is evidence of personal failure. The person is not just experiencing the physical symptoms of panic. The person is experiencing those symptoms and the conviction that a real Christian would not experience them. The spiral accelerates because now the person is attacking themselves for the very symptom they are experiencing. The anxiety generates shame. The shame generates more anxiety. The cycle feeds on itself and the church—the one place where they might find help—becomes the place where they hide.

I have sat with people who have hidden their anxiety for two decades. Who have never mentioned it to a soul in their congregation. Who carry a diagnosis and a medication regimen that nobody knows about. Who have lived a bifurcated life where the Sunday version and the actual version are so different that they have stopped knowing which one is real. The cost of this silence is measured in exhaustion. In the slow erosion of authenticity. In the decision to finally leave the faith because if faith means pretending, and pretending means never being known, then faith is not worth the cost.

The statistics say it is happening everywhere. The behavioral health surveys show that churchgoers report lower rates of mental health support-seeking than secular peers. Not because they have less anxiety. But because they have internalized the theology that seeking help is a sign of insufficient faith. So they do not seek help. They pray harder. They read their Bible more carefully. They confess. They apologize for the sin of anxiety. And their brains stay broken while they convince themselves that the problem is moral rather than neurological.

This is the theology that has to change.

Not the theology of God's faithfulness. That is unchangeable. Not the theology of God's promises. Those are real. The theology that has to change is the unspoken assumption that God's faithfulness prevents your nervous system from misfiring. The assumption that faith produces emotional equilibrium. The assumption that anxiety is something that the sufficiently Christian person does not experience. These assumptions are not biblical. They are American. They come from a culture that has fused faith with emotional comfort. They come from a way of thinking that treats anxiety as a sign of deficiency rather than a reality of embodied life in a fallen world.

The people in your congregation are suffering. They are suffering from anxiety. And they are suffering from the belief that the anxiety disqualifies them from belonging. They are suffering from the gap between what they have been told they should be experiencing and what they are actually experiencing. And the church's refusal to name this gap does not close it. It widens it.

This is where the truth has to enter. Not softly. Not in the form of a cautious pastoral footnote. But as a central claim. Anxiety is not weakness. Anxiety is not sin. Anxiety is not evidence that your faith is broken. Anxiety is a real condition that occurs in the bodies of real Christians and requires real intervention—medical, therapeutic, spiritual, communal. All of it at the same time.

When you can finally say this—when you can finally admit it in the place where you pray—something shifts. Not immediately. Not magically. But something shifts. The person stops using energy to hide. The person stops performing health. The person becomes available, for the first time, to actually be healed. Because healing requires honesty. And honesty requires a place where you are not going to be condemned for the very thing you are trying to confess.

This is what the church could be. Not a place where the broken have learned to pretend they are whole. But a place where the whole can admit they are broken. Where the anxious can say so without shame. Where the medicating can confess without condemnation. Where the struggling can be known exactly as they are—people of faith who are still fighting.

That changes everything.

CHAPTER ONE

The Weight Has a Name

Anxiety is not a sin. This is the first thing you need to know, and the church has made it nearly impossible for you to believe it.

You have been told that anxiety is what happens when you are not trusting God enough. That if your faith were real, you would not be afraid. That worry is pride disguised as concern—the assumption that your anxious thoughts might have power or relevance. That the antidote to anxiety is to believe harder, pray longer, read your Bible more carefully, confess the sin you have committed by not having faith.

You have been told this by people who love you. People in your church. Leaders. Probably people you respect. And you have internalized it so deeply that now when your heart races, when your chest tightens, when the spiral starts, you feel not just afraid but ashamed. Because you are a Christian, and Christians should not be afraid.

But anxiety is not a sin. It is a real condition that lives in your body, your neurochemistry, your nervous system—the same nervous system God created you with. It is no more a moral failure than diabetes. No more a sign of spiritual weakness than cancer.

Let me be clear about what anxiety actually is, because clarity here matters.

Anxiety is not worry. Worry is a response to a real threat. You worry about the job interview because the job interview is actually happening. You worry about paying rent because rent is actually due. Worry is rational. Proportional. It is your mind engaging with a genuine challenge. You can worry yourself into a solution. You can work. You can prepare. You can resolve the thing you are worried about.

Anxiety is something else entirely. Anxiety is your nervous system misfiring—generating threat alerts for situations that either do not exist or are massively disproportionate to the actual danger. Your amygdala is sending emergency signals. Your adrenal glands are flooding your system with cortisol. Your body is preparing for a fight or flight response to a threat that your rational mind knows is not real. You know you are not in danger. Your body insists that you are. And this disconnect—this gap between what you know and what your body is telling you—creates an anxiety loop that feeds on itself.

The Diagnostic and Statistical Manual, the DSM-5, defines an anxiety disorder as "excessive anxiety and worry that occurs more days than not for at least six months, about a number of events or activities." The worry must be difficult to control. It must cause clinically significant distress. It must interfere with functioning.

But the DSM is not the only place anxiety shows up with clarity. The Bible has language for it too. The Bible takes anxiety seriously because anxiety is real.

David describes it in Psalm 55. "My heart is in anguish within me; the terrors of death have fallen on me. Fear and trembling have beset me; horror has overwhelmed me." This is not David being melodramatic. This is David describing the physiological experience of anxiety. The anguish is in the heart—he feels it in his chest. The terrors are real to him even if they are not rationally proportionate. The fear and trembling are physical symptoms. The horror is the way anxiety colonizes the mind.

And notice what David does: he does not pray the anxiety away. He does not confess a sin of unbelief. He does not tell himself to have more faith. He does what Psalm 55:22 instructs: "Cast your cares on the Lord and he will sustain you; he will never let the righteous be shaken." He takes his experience exactly as it is—terrified, trembling, overwhelmed—and he brings it to God. Not a sanitized version. Not a polite spiritual version. The actual version. The one where he is undone.

This is what the Psalms teach that the church has largely forgotten: honesty with God about your condition is the beginning of deliverance, not a sign of insufficient faith.

The nineteenth-century Baptist preacher Charles Spurgeon knew about this. Spurgeon is remembered as the prince of preachers—the man who could fill the Metropolitan Tabernacle with six thousand people who sat in silence to hear him preach without a microphone, without amplification, without any of the technology that might hide a weak voice or tired delivery. He preached with such clarity and power that his sermons were published and read across the English-speaking world. He was, by every measure, a success. A giant in the faith.

And he spent much of his life in the grip of what he called melancholy. What we would now call depression and anxiety. In his own words: "I have had periods in which the question has seriously forced itself on me—Is there any reality in the things I have believed? Have I been the dupe of a mere imagination? Is there any God?"

Here is a man whose faith was so strong that it reached across centuries. Whose theology was so clear that people still read him now. Whose preaching shaped the course of evangelicalism. And he battled periods of such darkness that he wondered if God was real.

The church told him it was the sin of unbelief. His own journals suggest he believed that lie for much of his life—that his depression was a mark of spiritual weakness. That the darkness meant something was wrong with his faith. That a stronger Christian would not be subject to these shadows.

He was wrong about that assessment, though he was right about the pain being real. Modern observers who have studied Spurgeon suggest he may have had bipolar disorder or a cyclical depression. What matters is not the diagnosis but the reality: this man's faith did not make him immune to mental darkness. His theology did not prevent his nervous system from misfiring. His sermons on the power of God did not stop his own mind from generating the thought that God might not be real.

And the church responded by adding shame to the burden. By suggesting that his struggle was evidence of spiritual failure. By implying that if he just believed harder, prayed better, confessed more thoroughly, the darkness would lift.

This is the damage. Not the anxiety itself. The anxiety is hard enough. But when the church adds the additional weight—"this is your fault, this is your failure, this is what happens when you don't trust God"—it becomes unbearable.

The man sitting in your congregation carries a briefcase. In that briefcase are the real, legitimate struggles of his life. A failing business. A marriage that has lost its way. A child who is sick. A diagnosis he cannot shake. The weight of actual problems that anyone would struggle under.

But he also carries something else. An invisible weight. The conviction that he should not be struggling under the weight he is actually carrying. That his faith should have inoculated him against this kind of pain. That there is something wrong with him—spiritually, morally, faithfully—that his burden exists at all.

So he carries the weight. And he carries the shame about the weight. And the combination is what breaks him.

This is what needs to change. Not the theology—the theology is right. God is good. God is faithful. God does sustain his people. But that theology does not function as a prevention for anxiety any more than faith prevents your body from getting sick. It functions differently. It functions as a promise about what will be true of you even when anxiety is present. Even when your nervous system is misfiring. Even when your mind is generating catastrophes.

The theological reframe is this: your anxiety is not a measure of your faith. Your faith is the only thing that can sustain you while your anxiety is active. Faith is not the absence of fear. Faith is what you do with the fear when it arrives.

Spurgeon kept preaching. He kept writing. He kept serving God even through the melancholy. Not because he had won the battle with depression. Because he had come to understand—slowly, painfully, over years—that his faithfulness was not diminished by his struggle. That his calling did not require him to be unbroken. That the prince of preachers could be, in his own words, "subject to depression of spirit so fearful that I could weep by the hour like a child."

The weight has a name. It is anxiety. It is real. It lives in your body. It has entered your nervous system. It has roots in your neurobiology, your personality, your genetics, your history, the traumas you have lived through, the losses you have carried, the present circumstances that are actually difficult.

And none of that changes whether you are a Christian. None of that changes whether God loves you. None of that changes whether your faith is real.

What changes is the permission you give yourself. The permission to stop pretending. The permission to admit that you are struggling. The permission to ask for help—medical help, pastoral help, therapeutic help—without treating that help as evidence that your faith is insufficient.

This is the costly hope part. Because admitting you are anxious, admitting it without reservation, means you can no longer perform the role of the together Christian. It means you have to tell people the truth. It means you have to become the kind of Christian the church is not prepared to welcome: the one who is real before they are fixed.

This changes everything.

Charles Spurgeon wrote in his diary what he would never have said from the pulpit. "I am in such deep waters that I can only cry for deliverance. I cannot see the shore. I know I shall be brought to land, but the way in which my deliverance shall come I am quite unable to discern." The man preaching the gospel to thousands of people, the man the church called "the prince of preachers," was drowning. And he had to hide it.

What he published was theology. What he experienced was what he called a "terrible darkness." He described depression using the language available to him—melancholy, the night watches, the cloud that descended. But the weight was the same. The disorientation was the same. The certainty that something was broken was the same. And the silence that surrounded it was institutional.

The church did not know how to hold Spurgeon's struggle. So Spurgeon became the model of the brilliant man with faith strong enough to overcome his circumstances. The brilliant man who preached about hope while living in darkness. The brilliant man who performed victory while experiencing devastation. He became the template for generations of religious leaders who learned to say one thing in public and experience something entirely different in private.

Martin Luther described something he called Anfechtung. The word has no clean English translation. "Spiritual assault" comes close, but it misses the vertigo of it. Luther meant the experience of being attacked by doubt itself, by the sudden conviction that God was not actually there, that the entire structure of faith was built on nothing. He wrote about it in his commentary on Galatians while experiencing it. He continued to write theology while the ground beneath the theology was dissolving.

What is striking is that Luther did not treat this as an anomaly. He did not treat it as evidence of insufficient faith. He treated it as the normal experience of a serious Christian. He wrote: "If you try to remain just and pious without God, you will certainly become perverted and hypocritical, just as all who seek to do so become. You will feel anxious and afraid all the time." But he was not describing anxiety. He was describing the condition of being a human being trying to hold onto God while God sometimes seems to have vanished entirely.

The Psalms are full of this. And the church has largely learned to skip over it.

Psalm 88 offers no resolution. The entire psalm is lament. There is no turn toward hope. There is no "but I trust in you." The psalm ends in darkness: "You have hidden your face from me." That is where it leaves the reader. In the hiding. In the silence. The Psalms are full of this raw testimony—David begging God to answer, crying out that his enemies are growing, that he is dying, that God seems absent. Psalm 13 begins: "How long, O Lord? Will you forget me forever? How long will you hide your face from me?" David is not performing faith. He is testifying to what faith actually contains, which is the possibility of abandonment.

Psalm 22 reads like a transcript of a panic attack. "My God, my God, why have you forsaken me? Why are you so far from saving me? I am poured out like water, and all my bones are out of joint. My heart has turned to wax; it has melted away within me." This is the psalm Jesus quotes on the cross. The son of God, in his final moment, gives voice to the exact same disorientation that every anxiety sufferer knows: the conviction that God has abandoned you and the question that this conviction raises about whether that abandonment is real.

The church has a strange relationship with the Psalms. They are sacred Scripture, so they cannot be rejected. But they are also inconvenient. They contain anger at God. They contain the complaint that God is not doing what he promised. They contain the experience of the righteous suffering while the wicked flourish. They contain the possibility that prayer might not be answered. So the church reads them in community, aloud, which allows for the words to be spoken while not requiring them to be personally inhabited. The Psalms become a beautiful liturgical tradition rather than what they actually are: the testimony of people whose faith was real and whose suffering was also real, often simultaneously.

Psalm 31 is David in terror: "I am in distress; my eyes grow weak with sorrow, my soul and my body with grief." Psalm 42 is the same man asking, "Why are you downcast, O my soul? Why so disturbed within me?" There is a questioner and a questioned self. The psalmist is dividing against himself, unable to understand why his own soul is not cooperating with his theology.

This is not anomalous. This is not the exception proving the rule. This is the testimony of the people of God throughout Scripture. The people closest to God often struggled the most. The prophets were often traumatized. Jeremiah's book reads like the journal of a depressive. He curses the day he was born. He asks God why he even continues to exist. God's response to Jeremiah is not a correction. God does not tell him that he is wrong. God allows the lament to stand. God meets him in it.

The church has insisted that faith should have produced peace in these people. That if they were believing, they would not be struggling. But the Psalms, the prophets, and the apostles all contradict this. Paul wrote to the Corinthians: "I do not want you to be unaware of the troubles we experienced. We were under great pressure, far beyond our ability to endure, so that we despaired of life itself." Paul is describing despair. Literal despair about whether life was worth continuing. And this is not being confessed as a moral failure. It is being shared as a reality of ministry.

The point of the biblical witness is not that faith eliminates suffering. The point is that faith happens in the middle of suffering. That the same David who wrote psalms of joy and deliverance also wrote psalms of despair and confusion. That the same God who answered Elijah with fire on Mount Carmel met Elijah in a cave where the prophet had retreated in terror, convinced that everyone wanted to kill him, begging God to let him die.

Elijah is the model the church never mentions. After his greatest victory—calling down fire, defeating the prophets of Baal, vindicating the name of God—Elijah fell apart. Completely. He fled into the wilderness. He was so broken that he asked God to kill him. The text does not soften this. "He came to a broom bush, sat down under it and prayed that he would die. 'I have had enough, Lord,' he said." The greatest prophet in Israel, immediately after his greatest triumph, was suicidal.

God did not come to Elijah with a sermon. God came with food and water and rest. The prophet slept. He ate again. He drank water. God was validating not the prophet's faith but his body. The physical reality of exhaustion, hunger, dehydration. These were real. These mattered. This was not a spiritual problem that required a spiritual solution. This was a human being who had run himself into collapse and needed to eat and sleep.

But the church has built a theology where God solves the problem from the inside, spiritually, through faith. And it has missed what God actually did: came alongside Elijah in his embodied suffering and addressed the embodiment. Fed him. Hydrated him. Let him sleep. Gave his nervous system time to recover from whatever had triggered the terror. That is the actual pattern of Scripture. That is what the witness shows.

The church's theology has inverted this. It has insisted that the internal spiritual work should fix the external physical reality. That prayer should fix anxiety. That faith should fix depression. That Bible reading should fix the racing heart. That the name of Jesus should be enough. And when it is not—when the person prays and remains anxious, reads Scripture and continues to struggle, speaks the name of Jesus and the body does not obey—the theological conclusion is that something is wrong with the person's faith.

But what if something is wrong with the theology instead?

What if the church has inherited a version of faith that demands victory over the body? That reads the Incarnation as God proving that flesh should obey spirit? That takes Jesus's prayer in Gethsemane—"Remove this cup from me"—and hears only the resolution, missing that the prayer came first, that the asking came before the yielding, that the agony was real?

The biblical testimony is overwhelming. The people of God suffered. They suffered without resolution. They suffered and continued in faith anyway, not because faith eliminated the suffering but because faith had room for the suffering. The Psalms do not resolve. They testify. David brings his confusion to God and God receives the confusion. The prophets are traumatized and continue to speak. Paul is in despair and continues to write. Elijah collapses and God feeds him without requiring the collapse to mean something about his faith.

This is the inheritance the church has lost. This is the testimony it has edited out. And the cost is what we see now: people carrying the weight of suffering and the weight of thinking that the suffering means they are not enough. Carrying both weights together. Carrying them in silence because there is no language for this in the institutional faith.

The book's first truth is this: You are not alone in this struggle. The company you keep is immense. It includes the psalmists and the prophets. It includes Spurgeon and Luther. It includes every person whose faith was real and whose body was also real and who could not make the two cooperate according to the theology they were given.

The church's treatment of mental illness is not a twentieth-century phenomenon. It is the product of nearly two thousand years of theological confusion about the body, the brain, and the nature of spiritual illness. To understand how we got here, where the anxiety that a Christian experiences becomes a test of faith rather than a condition to address, you have to look at the history.

The medieval church was certain: anxiety was demonic. Not illness. Demonic possession. The person experiencing panic, experiencing what we now understand to be a misfiring nervous system, was understood to be inhabited by a demonic force that required exorcism. The treatment was spiritual warfare. Holy water. Prayer. Fasting. The casting out of demons. If the anxiety persisted after these interventions, the conclusion was not "we need a different approach." The conclusion was "the demon is stronger" or "the person's faith is insufficient." The institutional church did not have categories for physical or neurological causes. The body was the seat of sin. The mind, to the extent that it was distinct from the body, was subject to spiritual forces. Anxiety was spiritual. It required spiritual remedy.

This was not cruelty in intent. It was ignorance. The church was doing the best it could with the categories available to it. But the damage was real. People who were experiencing what we now understand to be clinical anxiety or depression were subjected to rituals meant to drive out demons. If they survived the rituals unharmed, they were praised for their faith. If the rituals harmed them—and sometimes they did, through deprivation or violence—the harm was understood to be part of the spiritual warfare. Either way, the message was clear: this is a spiritual problem and it requires a spiritual solution.

Then came the Reformation. Luther knew about darkness. The man who would transform Western Christianity spent nights in his cell thinking he was being attacked by demons. He felt the presence of darkness and despair so acutely that he was convinced he was being assailed by Satan himself. He called it Anfechtung—spiritual assault. But Luther was also one of the first Christian thinkers to have a different insight. He knew this darkness from the inside. And he named something that would have been revolutionary in his time: the darkness is real, but it is not proof that God has abandoned you. God is present even in the darkness. The faith is still valid even when the feeling is missing.

But here is what happened to Luther's insight after his death. It got filtered through a church culture that was still uncomfortable with the body. Luther had suggested that the darkness could be real and you could be faithful simultaneously. His followers took that and transformed it into a different message: you can be faithful even in darkness if you have sufficient faith to believe despite the darkness. The focus shifted from "God is present in the darkness" to "you must have faith to transcend the darkness." The burden shifted back onto the person. And what Luther had meant as liberation—you are not alone in the darkness—became another version of the same bind. You are alone with your darkness and the expectation that your faith should be sufficient to overcome it.

The Great Awakening intensified this. The revivalist movement was, in many ways, a spiritual gift. It brought intensity and sincerity to evangelical Christianity. But it also brought a new category: emotional extremity became a mark of authentic faith. The person who wept, who was overcome, who experienced emotional transport was understood to be experiencing genuine encounter with God. Those who remained composed, who did not weep, who maintained a certain emotional distance, were suspected of having insufficient faith or being unmoved by the Holy Spirit. For anxiety, this created a strange tension. On the one hand, emotional upheaval was sanctified. On the other hand, the anxiety that produced emotional upheaval was still understood to be faithlessness. So you had the spectacle of people experiencing what looked identical—emotional dysregulation, physical symptoms of panic—but it was interpreted completely differently depending on whether the emotion was labeled revival or anxiety.

The Victorian era solidified the split that created our modern confusion. This was the era when Charles Haddon Spurgeon was preaching. An era of stiff upper lips and emotional restraint in the broader culture, coupled with an evangelical Christianity that was learning to talk about emotional experience in new ways. Spurgeon is striking because he broke the Victorian mold. He talked about his melancholy openly. He wrote about it in his journals. He described nights when he could not pray, when the darkness was so complete that he thought God had forgotten him. He did this in a culture that was teaching men—especially Christian men—not to admit weakness. Spurgeon, by talking about his darkness, was doing something countercultural.

But the response from the broader church was not to follow his example. The response was to treat him as an exception. A great man whose greatness was somehow untouched by his periodic mental struggles. The implication was clear: most of us should not expect to experience what Spurgeon experienced. Most of us, with sufficient faith and proper spiritual discipline, should be able to avoid the darkness. Spurgeon's darkness became proof not of what all Christians might experience but of how much grace could sustain even a broken man in ministry. He was admired for enduring despite his weakness, not accepted for his actual human struggle.

Then came the twentieth century and a new split. Fundamentalism on one side rejected psychology entirely. The human mind was not understood to be subject to laws that psychology could discover. Psychology was a worldly pursuit, a distraction from spiritual truth, possibly even demonic in its refusal to acknowledge spiritual causes. If someone was anxious, the fundamentalist response was prayer, not therapy. The mental health disciplines were understood to be in direct competition with faith. They were not complementary. They were opposed. This created a culture where admitting anxiety was one thing. Admitting that you might need psychological help was a betrayal of faith.

But even as fundamentalism was rejecting psychology, a different movement was rising. The biblical counseling movement—figures like Jay Adams and others—were attempting to reclaim counseling as a distinctly Christian discipline. But they did so by keeping psychology at arm's length. The categories they used were biblical categories, not psychological ones. Sin, not trauma. Disobedience, not depression. Lack of faith, not neurochemical imbalance. What looked on the surface like an attempt to integrate faith and care was actually another version of the same problem. It was still treating all mental distress as at root a sin or a faithfulness problem. It was still rejecting the possibility that the mind could be sick independent of the person's moral or spiritual condition.

The cumulative effect of all of this history is a Christianity that does not have adequate categories for mental illness. We inherited Gnosticism's suspicion of the body. We inherited the Reformation's focus on faith as overcoming darkness rather than God's presence in darkness. We inherited the Victorian culture's discomfort with admitting weakness. We inherited the fundamentalist rejection of sciences that might illuminate human suffering. And we inherited the biblical counseling movement's insistence that all suffering is at root spiritual.

What we have not inherited is a simple, biblical acknowledgment that sometimes the brain misfires. That sometimes the nervous system gets stuck in a threat response. That this is not sin. That this does not indicate insufficient faith. That this is the kind of physical reality that everyone—Christians included—can experience. And that addressing it requires medical attention, not spiritual blame.

The history matters because it shows us that the theology that blames anxiety on insufficient faith is not ancient wisdom. It is a particular construction, built over centuries, by people who were doing the best they could with limited knowledge. But we are not limited. The brain can be scanned. Neurochemistry can be measured. Anxiety disorders have clear diagnostic criteria. We can know things now that the church at any point in its history has known before. And what we know is that anxiety is not a sin. The refusal to see that—the refusal to let the history of the church's error inform the church's present practice—is the sin.

The deacon sitting in my office does not need the medieval church's exorcism. He does not need the revivalist church's emotional transport. He does not need the fundamentalist church's rejection of professional help. He does not need the biblical counseling movement's reframing of his anxiety as a faithfulness problem. He needs the church to stop adding weight to a weight he is already carrying. He needs the church to say: your anxiety is real. Your faith is real. These things can coexist. And you are going to need help. Medical help. Possibly therapeutic help. And you are going to need community that does not treat the anxiety as evidence of spiritual failure.

This is what changes when we let history inform us. When we see how the church has gotten this wrong before. When we decide that this time, we will get it right.

CHAPTER TWO

What the Bible Actually Says About Anxiety

The verses that get weaponized are usually short. They fit on graphics. They work as social media posts. They are easy to remember and easy to repeat, which is exactly why they become terrible when they are flattened.

The most dangerous verse in Scripture—when it is misread—is Philippians 4:6-7. It has been reduced to a bumper sticker. "Don't be anxious. Just pray instead." Be anxious for nothing. Pray about everything. Get a smile on your face. And everything will be fine.

But Paul is not saying that. If you read the full passage in context, you discover that Paul is not addressing a situation where anxiety might not exist. He is addressing a situation where anxiety definitely will exist. He is not saying "if you are anxious, you are doing something wrong and you need to fix it." He is saying "when you are anxious—which you will be—here is what you do."

The full passage reads: "Do not be anxious about anything, but in every situation, by prayer and petition, with thanksgiving, present your requests to God. And the peace of God, which transcends all understanding, will guard your hearts and your minds in Christ Jesus."

The Greek word for "do not be" here is the present tense imperative. The sense is not "never become anxious" but rather "stop being anxious" or "do not continue in anxiety." It is a call to a different action, not a denial that anxiety will happen. And the alternative action Paul proposes is striking: when you are anxious, bring it to God. With specificity. With petition. With a request. With gratitude.

Notice that Paul does not say "pray so that your anxiety will go away." He says "present your requests to God." There is an assumption here that you have a request. That you have a desire. That something is wrong or missing or threatening. That is why you are anxious in the first place. And Paul is saying: bring that exact thing to God. Do not spiritualize it away. Do not pretend it is not bothering you. Do not calm yourself with false comfort. Identify what you are actually afraid of. Tell God about it. Ask him for something specific.

And then something worth noticing happens. "The peace of God, which transcends all understanding, will guard your hearts and your minds in Christ Jesus."

This peace is not the elimination of anxiety. This peace is a protection. A boundary. A guard. The word Paul uses—phroureō in Greek—literally means to garrison or fortify. The peace becomes a fence around your heart and mind. It does not remove the anxiety. It prevents the anxiety from destroying you.

This is the passage people have been misreading for centuries. The passage that says your anxiety can exist, you can bring it to God exactly as it is, and peace—not the absence of fear but the presence of God's protection—will surround you.

Paul knew about this because Paul lived in a situation of genuine jeopardy. The book of Philippians was written from prison. Paul is in Roman custody. He faces the possibility of execution. He is writing to a church that is also under pressure, also experiencing persecution, also dealing with the very real anxiety that comes from living as a Christian minority in a hostile empire. He is not writing to people with theoretical problems. He is writing to people whose anxiety is completely justified by their circumstances.

And his answer is not "ignore the danger and believe harder." His answer is "bring your fear to God. Let me know what you are asking him for. And in that process, let God's peace garrison your mind."

This is entirely different from what we have made it mean.

The second passage people flatten is Matthew 6:25-34. "Therefore I tell you, do not worry about your life, what you will eat or drink; or about your body, what you will wear. Is not life more than food, and the body more than clothes? Look at the birds of the air; they do not sow or reap or store away in barns, and yet your heavenly Father feeds them. Are you not much more valuable than they?"

This passage has been used to shame people out of their anxiety about money, about provision, about the basic necessities of life. It has been preached as "if you are worried about paying rent, you lack faith." It has been used as a weapon against people in poverty. It has been spiritualized into uselessness.

But Jesus is not speaking to a generic human situation. Jesus is speaking to a specific historical moment. He is addressing an occupied people living under Roman taxation. Peasant farmers who had their harvests taxed. People living hand to mouth. People for whom the anxiety about provision was constant and structural, not merely personal.

And Jesus is not saying "stop worrying because provision is automatic." Jesus is addressing the difference between the anxiety that comes from actually pursuing provision and the anxiety that comes from obsessive speculation about what might go wrong. He is distinguishing between legitimate concern and spiraling fear.

> *"Who of you by worrying can add a single hour to your life? Since you cannot do this very little thing, why do you worry about the rest? Consider how the wild flowers grow. They do not labor or spin. Yet I tell you, not even Solomon in all his splendor was dressed like one of these. If that is how God clothes the grass of the field, which is here today and tomorrow is thrown into the fire, how much more will he clothe you—you of little faith! Do not worry, saying 'What shall we eat?' or 'What shall we drink?' or 'What shall we wear?' For the pagans run after all these things, and your Father knows that you need them. But seek first his kingdom and his righteousness, and all these things will be given to you as well."*

The logic is this: your worrying will not change the outcome. Your anxious speculation will not create provision or prevent loss. So invest your energy differently. Pursue the kingdom. Pursue righteousness. Trust that provision follows. But this is not aimed at clinically anxious people. This is aimed at economically anxious people in a system that makes anxiety rational. And Jesus is saying that the obsessive focus on what might go wrong is a distraction from what actually matters.

There is a difference between reasonable concern and spiraling anxiety. There is a difference between paying attention to a threat and being consumed by catastrophic thinking. Jesus is naming that difference. He is not saying anxiety is impossible. He is saying anxiety is not your best use of energy.

Then there are the Psalms. Psalm 42 and 43 are a pair of prayers that model what honest engagement with despair actually looks like. "As the deer pants for streams of water, so my soul pants for you, my God. My soul thirsts for God, for the living God. When can I go and meet with God? My tears have been my food day and night, while people say to me all day long, 'Where is your God?' These things I remember as I pour out my soul: how I used to go with the multitude, leading the procession to the house of God, with shouts of joy and praise among the festive throng."

This is a person broken by grief and anxiety and confusion. Where is God? Why am I crying? Why do people mock me? Why do I remember better days and want them back?

And what does the Psalmist do? Does he confess his way out of it? Does he pray for faith to overcome the sorrow? Does he spiritualize the pain into submission? No. He keeps talking to God. He pours out his soul. He asks the hard questions. He names the grief. And then: "Why, my soul, are you downcast? Why so disturbed within me? Put your hope in God, for I will yet praise him, my Savior and my God."

The answer is not that the sadness goes away. The answer is that the Psalmist turns his face toward God while the sadness is still present. He does not wait for the sadness to resolve. He does not require himself to feel better before he can pray. He brings the actual condition—downcast soul, disturbed interior, the weight of genuine sorrow—to God. And in that act of bringing it, something shifts. Not the circumstance. The stance.

This is repeated three times in these two psalms. Three times the Psalmist asks why his soul is downcast. Three times he returns to the same answer: put your hope in God. Not because the darkness will lift. Because you can face the darkness without being alone in it.

Finally, there is 1 Peter 5:7. "Cast all your anxiety on him because he cares for you." This verse has been used to suggest that the moment you cast your anxiety on God, it will cease. But the Greek word for "cast"—aporrhiptō—literally means to throw or hurl. It is a violence to the word. You are taking the anxiety out of your hands and putting it into his. You are not managing it. You are not controlling it. You are throwing it.

The verse does not say you will feel better. It says that in the act of throwing it, you are acknowledging a truth: he cares for you. The caretaking is on him. Your job is not to fix your anxiety. Your job is to stop trying to carry it alone.

What the Bible actually says about anxiety is this: your anxiety is real. Your fear is valid. Your emotional reality matters. And it is not separate from your faith. Your faith is what you do while the anxiety is happening. Your faith is bringing the fear to God. Your faith is naming the truth about your condition. Your faith is refusing to hide. Your faith is casting the weight onto God and accepting that he is large enough to carry it.

The church has turned this into something else entirely. The church has weaponized these passages into "if you are anxious, you are not faithful." But that is not what Scripture says. Scripture says "when you are anxious, bring it here. This is the place where you can be honest. This is the place where your fear will not destroy you. Because someone larger is paying attention."

The reframe is not that you should not be anxious. The reframe is that your anxiety does not disqualify you. That your fear does not separate you from God. That your honest engagement with your actual condition is the beginning of deliverance, not the evidence of failure.

This is what we have been missing.

Psalm 88 has no title. Most psalms do—"A psalm of David," or "A maskil of the sons of Korah." Psalm 88 just appears: "A song. A psalm of the Sons of Korah." No author claimed. Perhaps no author wanted to be known. Perhaps the Korahites understood that this psalm needed to exist in the tradition without a face attached to it, so that anyone could pray it without explaining themselves.

The psalm begins in prayer. "Lord, the God who saves me, day and night I cry out before you." There is addressing God. There is asking. But what follows is not a description of answers. What follows is an inventory of abandonment. "My soul is full of trouble and my life draws near to Sheol." The psalmist is not complaining. The psalmist is testifying. This is what it feels like to live inside this body, inside this moment, inside this experience of the divine.

> *"I am counted among those who go down to the pit; I am like one without strength. I am set apart with the dead, like the slain who lie in the grave, whom you remember no more, who are cut off from your care." The language intensifies. This is not sadness. This is desolation. The psalmist is describing what it feels like when the presence of God seems to have ceased entirely. When the basic assumption of faith—that God is attentive—becomes the question rather than the given.*

The section about sleep is relevant. "You have taken from me my closest friends and have made me repulsive to them. I am confined and cannot escape; my eyes are dim with grief." And then: "I am like a man without help, set free among the dead." There is isolation. There is the loss of connection. There is the conviction that something has broken that cannot be repaired. And what is most striking is that the psalm does not move toward resolution. There is no turning point. There is no moment where the psalmist's perspective shifts. The psalm ends exactly where it began, in darkness: "The darkness is my closest friend."

The church has largely learned to avoid this psalm. It is too real. It offers no comfort. It presents no resolution. A pastor preaching it would have to stand up and say: Sometimes this is what faith looks like. Sometimes the faithful sit in darkness and do not move toward light. Sometimes prayer does not produce the feelings we expect. Sometimes God feels absent and remains absent. And we are still supposed to continue.

This is what Elijah experienced. In 1 Kings 19, the prophet is running. This comes immediately after his greatest moment—fire from heaven, the prophets of Baal defeated, the rain returned to Israel. He has vindicated God's name in the most dramatic way possible. And then he hears that Jezebel wants him dead. And he runs.

The text is spare: "Elijah was afraid and ran for his life." The greatest prophet of God, in that moment, was not trusting. He was not resting in God's provision. He was terrified. He ran into the wilderness. And he ran so hard that he collapsed: "He came to a broom bush, sat down under it and prayed that he would die. 'I have had enough, Lord,' he said. 'Take my life; I am no better than my ancestors.'"

What is stunning is that the text does not interpret this for us. It does not say that Elijah was weak in faith or that his prayer was wrong or that he should have trusted more. The text reports that the prophet asked God to kill him. The prophet was done. The prophet did not want to continue living.

The response of God is not a theological correction. It is not a lesson on faith. It is physical care. "Then he lay down under the broom bush and fell asleep. All at once an angel touched him and said, 'Get up and eat.' He looked around, and by his head was some bread baked over hot coals, and a jar of water. He ate and drank and then lay down again." God feeds the prophet. God gives him water. God lets him sleep again.

Then the second time: "The angel of the Lord came back a second time and touched him and said, 'Get up and eat, for the journey is too much for you.' So he got up and ate and drank. Strengthened by that food, he traveled forty days and forty nights." The pattern repeats. Food. Water. Rest. Rest again. And then the journey continues.

What God did not do is address Elijah's theological crisis. What God did not do is argue with him about his fear or his despair or his desire to die. What God did do is treat the body. Address hunger. Address thirst. Address exhaustion. The implication of this passage is that these are not secondary. The body matters. The nervous system matters. The physical reality of collapse matters. And sometimes the faithful response to crisis is not more prayer but more sleep.

This contradicts much of what the church teaches about spiritual warfare. The church teaches that the answer to spiritual attack is spiritual armor, prayer, Scripture. The church teaches binding and loosing, declaring and decreeing. The church teaches that the warfare is not against flesh and blood but against spiritual forces. And this is not entirely wrong. But it is incomplete. And when it is incomplete in the face of a person in genuine crisis, it becomes harmful.

Elijah needed food before he needed theology. Elijah needed sleep before he needed a sermon. Elijah needed his basic physical needs met before he could continue. And God met those needs. God was not waiting for Elijah's faith to strengthen. God was not waiting for the prophet to pray the right prayer. God was feeding him while he slept.

This is what the church has missed about embodied suffering. This is what the person with anxiety needs to hear. The body is not a problem to be overcome through greater faith. The body is the place where faith is lived. The neurons are misfiring. The hormones are dysregulated. The sleep is insufficient. The stress is accumulating. These are not secondary issues. These are not failures of spirituality. These are the material reality in which the person is trying to have faith.

Jesus experienced this. In the garden, the night before he died, Jesus prayed: "My soul is overwhelmed with sorrow to the point of death." What follows in the text is what seems very much like a panic attack. "And being in anguish, he prayed more earnestly, and his sweat was like drops of blood falling to the ground." The body is in distress. The nervous system is activated. The sweat response is occurring. The Son of God, fully God and fully man, is experiencing something physiological that corresponds to what every anxiety sufferer knows.

And what does Jesus do? He prays. He asks for the cup to be removed. He asks for another way. And then he returns to sleep. Three times he returns to the disciples and finds them sleeping. Three times he prays. And then he wakes them because the time has come. The pattern is prayer, return to the body, rest, and then movement.

The church has read this passage as a lesson in submission. Jesus demonstrated how to submit to God's will even in the face of suffering. And this is true. But what the church has often missed is that Jesus did not overcome the anguish through faith. The anguish continued. He continued to experience it. What changed was not the anguish but his position within it. He moved from "let this cup pass from me" to "not my will but yours." He moved from asking for another way to accepting this way. But the anguish was still there.

This is the distinction that changes everything for the person suffering. The anguish does not have to disappear for the faith to be real. The anxiety does not have to be resolved for the following to be possible. The person can be experiencing all of it—the full neurological reality of the anxiety, the full embodied experience of distress, the racing heart and the shallow breathing and the conviction that something is ending—and simultaneously be saying yes to God. Not in spite of the anxiety. Not having overcome it. But in the middle of it.

Jesus prayed in the middle of his. The psalmist testified to God in the middle of his. Elijah slept and ate in the middle of his. They did not all resolve the same way. Jesus submitted and went to the cross. Elijah slept and continued the journey. The psalmist ended in darkness. But all of them were met by God in the actual place they were. Not the place they should have been if their faith were stronger. The place they were actually standing.

This is what it means to have faith that has room for the body. Faith that does not require the body to conform to theological expectations before it can be valid. Faith that allows for Psalm 88 to be the whole story, not just the introduction to a redemption narrative. Faith that feeds the prophet and lets him sleep and does not require him to have figured anything out yet. Faith that meets Jesus in the garden where he is sweating blood and does not argue that this should not be happening.

The cost of the church's theology is that it has required people to choose between honesty and faith. Between what the body is experiencing and what the institution says faith should feel like. And most people have chosen honesty. They have walked away from the church because the church demanded that they live a lie: that they feel fine when they are not fine, that they trust when they are afraid, that the prayer is working when it plainly is not.

But there is another option. The option that God demonstrates in every one of these passages. The option of meeting yourself where you actually are. Of sitting down under the broom bush and asking to die if that is what the moment requires. Of praying that God would remove the cup even while you are preparing to drink it. Of ending in darkness and still bringing that darkness to God.

CHAPTER THREE

The Body Keeps the Score

Your body is not a container for your soul. It is not the problem to be escaped. It is not the enemy dragging your spirit down into carnality. Your body is you.

This is the sentence evangelical Christianity most needs to hear and most resists accepting. We have inherited a Gnostic distrust of the body that has no biblical foundation. We have split spirit from flesh, soul from soma, the eternal from the temporal. We have been so focused on saving souls that we have forgotten that souls are always embodied. They arrive in bodies. They remain in bodies. They experience the world through bodies.

When you are anxious, your anxiety is not a thought floating somewhere in abstract psychological space. Your anxiety lives in your chest. It tightens your shoulders. It makes your hands shake. It hijacks your nervous system and floods your bloodstream with cortisol and adrenaline. Your heart races. Your breathing becomes shallow. Your muscles tense. Your mind becomes hyperalert—scanning for threats that your body is now convinced exist.

This is not metaphorical. This is your actual, literal, physical reality. And the church has taught you to dismiss it.

Genesis 2:7 reads: "The Lord God formed a man from the dust of the ground and breathed into his nostrils the breath of life, and the man became a living soul." The sequence matters. God does not create a soul and then give it a body. God creates a body from the earth—actual material, actual flesh—and the breath of God animates it into personhood. You are ensouled flesh. Not a spirit trapped in meat. Not a soul confined to a body until you can escape into heaven. A unified being in which the physical and spiritual are woven together so completely that they cannot be separated.

The Desert Fathers understood this. These men—and some women—withdrew to the Egyptian desert in the fourth and fifth centuries to pursue what they thought was spiritual purity. They starved themselves. They deprived themselves of sleep. They tortured their bodies in the conviction that the body was the seat of temptation and the spirit could only be perfected through the body's submission.

And after years of this practice, something unexpected happened. Many of them became profoundly disordered. Their extended fasting created malnutrition that manifested as spiritual confusion. Their sleep deprivation produced hallucinations that they interpreted as demonic oppression. Their self-mortification created a self-hatred that they mistook for spiritual progress.

And then some of them—the ones who are actually remembered with respect—figured out what was happening. Abba Anthony and other elder monks began teaching that the body's health and the soul's health are not opposed to each other. That ignoring the body's needs does not purify the spirit; it destroys both. That sleep matters. That food matters. That a body that is cared for is a body that can pray. The elder monks began practicing something revolutionary: they began treating their bodies as valuable.

This is what the church needs to understand about anxiety. When your body is in distress—when your nervous system is misfiring, when your cortisol is elevated, when your heart is racing—your spirit feels it. Your mind feels it. Your ability to think clearly is compromised. Your capacity to trust God is diminished. Not because your faith is weak but because your body is in a state of alarm.

A person in a panic attack cannot think their way out of it with theology. A person who has not slept in three days cannot pray their way into clarity. A person whose nervous system is dysregulated cannot bootstrap themselves into peace through an act of will. The body has its own reality. The body makes demands. The body requires attention.

This is why the church's approach to anxiety has done so much damage. The church says "the problem is your faith. Believe harder and the anxiety will dissolve." But the anxiety is not a faith problem. The anxiety is a nervous system problem, a neurochemistry problem, a body problem. And the faith approach, applied to a body problem, feels like gaslighting. The person is told their experience is not real. Told that if they just had enough faith, they would not feel what they are actually feeling. Told that the racing heart is not important because the spirit is what matters.

But the racing heart IS the person. The elevated cortisol IS the person. The shallow breathing and the tight chest and the hypervigilant mind are not separate from the person's faith. They are part of the person's actual condition. And they require attention on their own terms.

This does not mean the spiritual dimension is irrelevant. It means that trying to address a nervous system problem with only spiritual tools is like trying to fix a broken bone with prayer. Prayer is not opposed to medical care. Prayer and medical care are not in competition. They are addressing different dimensions of a unified person.

When a person comes to you with anxiety, what they need is not more faith. What they need is to be told the truth: you are not broken. Your nervous system is overactive. Your body needs help. And that help—whether it comes from a therapist, a doctor, medication, breathing techniques, or a combination of all of these—is not a failure of faith. It is an acceptance of reality.

I have sat with a woman who was having panic attacks. She would wake at three in the morning with her heart pounding, certain she was having a heart attack. She had been to the emergency room four times. Each time, they told her: nothing is wrong with your heart. This is anxiety. Her response was despair. If it is just anxiety, why can't I pray it away? If I really trusted God, I would not feel this way. She was caught in a loop where the anxiety produced shame about the anxiety, and the shame produced more anxiety.

What changed for her was not more faith. What changed was understanding that her nervous system was dysregulated. That her amygdala had learned to associate sleep with danger. That her body had been hijacked into a threat response. And that there were specific, practical interventions that could help. Therapy that taught her nervous system that she was actually safe. Breathing exercises that could interrupt the spiral. Eventually, medication that helped her brain chemistry stabilize.

And in that context, faith became possible again. Not faith that eliminated anxiety, but faith that existed alongside the work of healing her body. Faith that said: I trust that God cares about my wholeness, including the wholeness of my nervous system. I trust that seeking help is not a sign of weakness but of wisdom. I trust that my body matters because I matter.

The theology here is simple: God made your body. God made your nervous system. God made the chemical processes in your brain that regulate mood and fear and sleep. These are not mistakes. These are not evils to be overcome. These are part of God's creation. And when they malfunction, you do not address the malfunction by pretending it does not exist. You address it with the same diligence you would use to address any other part of your physical health.

A person with diabetes does not sin by checking their blood sugar. A person with high blood pressure does not lack faith by taking medication. A person with anxiety does not fail spiritually by seeking help from a therapist or a doctor. These are people accepting the reality of their embodied condition and responding to it with wisdom.

The costly part is this: accepting your body's reality means accepting yourself as you are. Not as you should be. Not as faith suggests you should be able to be. As you actually are. It means stopping the performance. Stopping the pretense that you are fine when you are not. Stopping the suggestion that your anxiety is a secret weakness that you should be able to overcome alone.

It means becoming the kind of person who tells the truth: my nervous system is struggling. My body is in distress. I need help. And the help I need is not just prayer. It is also medicine, therapy, medical evaluation, lifestyle changes, community support. It is an integrated approach that treats you as a unified being—body and soul together—rather than a spirit that should be able to transcend its bodily condition.

The Desert Fathers figured this out in the desert. The church forgot it and has spent centuries pretending that spirit and body are not interwoven. But you already know better. You know this because you are living in your body. And your body is keeping score. Your body remembers everything. Your nervous system is tracking danger. Your body is real.

And God made it that way.

God became flesh. This is the scandal that Christians often miss because it has been absorbed into the tradition. But if you step outside the tradition for a moment and ask what it actually means—that the eternal, immaterial, unlimited God became limited to a body, subject to hunger and thirst and fatigue and pain—the scandal becomes clear. The material world is not a problem to be solved. The body is not the prison of the spirit. The flesh is where God chose to appear.

The consequence of this is that flesh matters. Embodiment matters. The neurological reality of the human nervous system matters. If God looked at the material world and said it was good enough to inhabit, then the material world is good. Then bodies are good. Then neurons are good. Then the biochemical processes that regulate mood and fear response and sleep are good. Not as obstacles to overcome but as the actual material through which a human being experiences the world and themselves.

The medieval church did not believe this. The medieval church believed that the body was the enemy of the soul. That spiritual advancement required the defeat of the body. So monks fasted to the point of starvation. They flagellated themselves. They wore hair shirts. They sat naked in winter and stood in summer heat. The body was the problem. The body needed to be subdued. The spirit needed to be freed from the tyranny of the flesh.

The result was severe psychological and physical damage. The severe fasting produced malnutrition and weakened immune systems and cognitive dysfunction. The self-injury produced not spiritual advancement but trauma. What these monks discovered was what the church should have known from the Incarnation: You cannot hate the body without hating something God loved enough to become.

But the impulse persists. The church has found subtler ways to wage war against the body. Not through explicit self-injury but through the demand that the body should not testify to reality. The depressed person whose body is chemically dysregulated should pray harder instead of seeking medication. The anxious person whose nervous system has been traumatized should trust more instead of working with a therapist. The person whose body needs sleep should push through instead of resting. The person whose neurochemistry is not producing enough serotonin should read more Scripture instead of consulting a doctor.

The message, subliminal but consistent, is that the body is not to be trusted. That medication is a compromise with the flesh. That therapy is a failure of faith. That the physical reality of what is happening in the body is less important than the theological narrative about what should be happening. The body becomes the obstacle rather than the place where God is present.

This is a direct contradiction to the Incarnation. If God became flesh, then flesh is the appropriate place for the divine to dwell. Then the body is not a liability. Then treating the body is not a concession to weakness. Then acknowledging the body's reality is not a failure of faith. Then a person can take medication and still pray. Can see a therapist and still read Scripture. Can address the body and still address the soul.

The Incarnation says that these are not opposed categories. The body and the soul are not at war. They are integrated. Jesus had a body. He got hungry. He got tired. He felt pain. And these were not embarrassments. These were the actual material through which the divine was working. God did not become a spirit having a temporary experience of embodiment. God became flesh. The Incarnation is not a story about how to transcend the body. It is a story about how God validated embodiment.

The consequence for someone living with anxiety is significant. It means that the anxiety happening in the body is not a sign of spiritual failure. The misfiring neurons are not an indictment on the person's faith. The nervous system that has been dysregulated by trauma or genetics or circumstance is not the enemy. It is the material reality in which the person lives. And the faithful response is not to war against it but to work with it. To address it. To treat it.

This requires a different theology than what many churches teach. It requires believing that God works through the body as much as through the spirit. That medication is a tool that God has allowed to exist. That therapy is a legitimate way of understanding and addressing human dysfunction. That sleep and exercise and nutrition and community are not secondary spiritual concerns but primary embodied goods.

It requires, in other words, a theology of embodiment that takes seriously what the Incarnation reveals: that God is not interested in escaping the material world. God is interested in redeeming it. In changing it from the inside. In working through it rather than against it.

This changes the conversation about anxiety medication. If the body is good and embodiment is validated by the Incarnation, then treating the body is not less spiritual than praying. It is as spiritual. It is part of the same faithfulness. The person who takes medication is not failing. The person is participating in God's redemptive work. The person is saying yes to their own embodied existence. The person is stewarding the material reality they have been given.

Stewardship is the word. The church has developed a serious theology of stewarding money and time and gifts. But it has largely failed to develop a theology of stewarding the body. Of caring for it. Of treating it as something precious rather than as an obstacle to overcome. The body deserves this care. Not because the body is ultimate—the Incarnation does not say that matter is all there is—but because the body is where the person's life actually happens.

The teenage girl who was told that her anxiety was demonic needed something different than another spiritual explanation. She needed to be told that her body was trustworthy even when it was misfiring. That the neurological reality was not a sign of evil but a sign that something had gone wrong in the material system and the thing to do was address it. She needed to be told that taking medication was not a failure of faith. She needed to be told that her body, struggling as it was, was still part of God's creation. Still part of God's good work. Still worth taking seriously.

The pastor's wife needed to be told that her body's stress response to carrying her husband's hidden burden was valid. That recognizing this and seeking help was not a failure of love but an act of faithful stewardship. That she could not fix her husband's brain chemistry through prayer and presence alone, but she could address her own. She could sleep. She could exercise. She could find support. She could treat her body as something that mattered.

The veteran needed to know that the brain's trauma response was not a spiritual deficiency. That the nervous system's fear reaction was the body protecting him according to the lessons it had learned. That addressing this through therapy and possibly medication was not admitting defeat. It was working with his embodied reality in the way that the Incarnation validates as good and necessary.

The Incarnation does not solve anxiety. It does not produce instant healing or spiritual transcendence. What it does is change the understanding. It says that the body and soul are not at war. That addressing the body is addressing something God has declared good. That stewardship of the body—including medical stewardship—is a form of faithfulness.

If anxiety lives in your body, then your body requires attention. This is not a concession to secularism. This is not giving up on faith. This is accepting the reality of what God made you to be: an embodied soul, a unified being where the physical and the spiritual are woven together so completely that they cannot be addressed separately.

Your nervous system can be trained. Not by willpower. Not by faith sufficient to transcend its operating logic. By attention. By the specific, deliberate practices that communicate safety to a threat-detection system that has learned to detect threats everywhere. When your amygdala has been trained to associate certain situations with danger, it will not unlearn that association through theological argument. It will unlearn it through repeated evidence of safety. When you sit with the thing you are afraid of and nothing bad happens, your nervous system receives data. When you do this repeatedly, your brain rewires itself. This is neuroplasticity. This is not metaphor. This is how the brain actually works.

But you have to show up in your body to let this happen. You cannot think your way out of an anxiety disorder. You have to inhabit your breathing. You have to feel your feet on the ground. You have to notice the sensations in your body that are separate from the catastrophic stories your mind is generating. You have to say to your body: I know you feel like you are in danger. I know you are sending emergency signals. But I am here. I am safe. I can stay with this.

This is why movement matters. This is why sleep matters. This is why the basic functions of embodied life matter. Your body is not the problem. Your body is the solution. When you are anxious, you need a body that is rested. You need muscles that have been exercised. You need a digestive system that is not being flooded with the cortisol generated by constant hypervigilance. You need breathing that is deep and slow instead of shallow and rapid. You need touch. You need sunlight. You need your feet on the earth.

This sounds simple because it is. But the church has made it complicated. The church has told you that these things are worldly pursuits. That caring for your body is vanity. That the spiritual disciplines—prayer, fasting, Scripture—are what matter and that caring for your physical health is a distraction. This is Gnostic heresy dressed in spiritual language. It is a lie that costs lives.

I know this because I have had to learn it in my own body.

I grew up without exercise. My father was absent. I did not play sports. I did not learn that my body could be a source of power or peace. I learned that my body was something to be managed. To be fed minimally. To be clothed. To be controlled. And then I started gaining weight in my thirties. I hit a point where I was carrying weight that was literally crushing my joints and my cardiovascular system. I was sitting in my chair at church and feeling the weight. Physically. My body was screaming at me that this was unsustainable.

And I finally decided to listen.

I started lifting. Heavy weights. Squats and deadlifts and presses that required me to show up and exert real power. And something unexpected happened. The anxiety that I had been carrying—the constant mental chatter, the scenario-spinning, the catastrophic predictions—became quieter when I lifted. Not gone. Quieter. Because my brain, when it is occupied with the neurochemical demand of heavy exertion, stops its usual patterns. When you are at the moment of maximum effort in a squat, you cannot be running through conversations from three years ago. You cannot be spiraling about what might happen next month. Your mind has to be present to the actual task.

This is not mystical. This is how the brain works. Anxiety is often the result of sustained attention to threat. Your mind is generating threat scenarios all day and your nervous system is responding to the mental threat by producing the physical symptoms of fear. When you exercise hard enough, when you demand all of your neurochemical resources for the task at hand, that sustained attention to threat becomes impossible. Your brain literally does not have the bandwidth. It is all engaged in managing the physical demand.

And the weight that I was carrying literally began to transform. Over four years, I lost ninety pounds. Not because I was thinking my way thin. Not because I was praying for willpower. But because I was attending to my body. I was telling my body that it mattered. I was sending it a message that I was willing to suffer with it—to push it, to ask something difficult of it, to engage with its reality. And my body, in response, began to change.

The anxiety did not disappear. But it became something I could manage instead of something that managed me. Because my nervous system was no longer stuck in permanent hypervigilance. Because I was sleeping better. Because I was not carrying the additional weight of my own body against my joints and heart. Because I had found a practice—heavy lifting—that was my form of prayer. My form of meditation. My form of "casting my cares on the Lord" because the only way I knew how to do that was to carry the weight of iron and to release it. To feel my strength and my limitation simultaneously.

This is not the story the church wants to hear. The church wants a story where the prayer was sufficient. Where the faith did the work. The church wants to be able to claim that I got better because I got more faithful. But that is not what happened. I got better because I attended to my body. Because I decided that my physical health was not a luxury or a vanity. Because I decided that stewarding the body God made was a form of worship.

Sourdough became another form of this same attention. I started making bread during the pandemic. Just fed starter and flour and salt and water and time. The process cannot be rushed. Fermentation happens on its own schedule. You cannot think faster and bake better bread. You cannot pray and make the gluten develop. You have to wait. You have to fold the dough. You have to observe what is happening. You have to be patient with the process. And slowly, over the course of months, I got better at it. Not through willpower. Through attention. Through showing up to the process repeatedly and learning what the dough needs.

And somewhere along the way, the process became restorative. The kneading is different from the lifting. The pace is slower. But the attention is the same. I am in my body. I am focused on the actual work instead of the catastrophic stories. I am creating something. And the creation—the moment when I pull a loaf from the oven and break it open and smell the fermented complexity—that moment is a kind of redemption. A reminder that time and patience and attention create something good.

The church taught me to fear the body. My family of origin taught me that my body was something to manage until I could escape it. And then I had to shed all of that. I had to come to understand that my body is not the problem. My body is the instrument of my knowing God. My body is where the nervous system lives that God designed to help me engage the world. My body is where the weight sits and where the weight can be released.

When you are anxious, the first thing to attend to is your body. Not your theology. Not your faith. Your body. Do you have food in your system? Do you have water? Have you moved? Have you slept? These are not minor questions. These are foundational. The pastor in your congregation cannot think theologically when he is hungry. The mother cannot access her faith when she has not slept. The businessman cannot trust God when his nervous system is so dysregulated that it interprets every email as a threat.

This does not mean faith is not important. It means faith is not sufficient without the body's cooperation. And the body requires attention. Real attention. Not the attention that comes from thinking about attending to it. But the actual, physical practice of eating and moving and sleeping and resting.

Being outside changes something in your nervous system that you cannot replicate indoors. I do not fully understand the neuroscience. But I know that when I am outside, standing in the dirt, looking at the sky, something in me settles. The world is bigger than my anxiety. The trees are older than my catastrophizing. The sky has held itself up without my management for billions of years. And I am small. And that smallness is not a threat. It is a relief.

When you accept that you are embodied, you accept that you have real needs. Physical needs. Sleep. Food. Movement. Touch. Sunlight. The church has taught you that attending to these needs is worldly. That the spiritual disciplines are what matter and that the body is merely the vehicle. But the body is not the vehicle. The body is you. And when you attend to it, when you take seriously its needs and its signals and its capacity to help regulate your nervous system, something shifts.

This is why I still lift. Why I still make bread. Why I still go outside. Not because these things are a panacea. Not because they replace the need for faith or therapy or sometimes medication. But because they are a way of saying to my body: you matter. Your experience matters. Your nervous system matters. The signals you are sending are worth listening to. And I am going to attend to you.

This is what stewarding your body looks like. Not obsession. Not vanity. But a basic acknowledgment that God made this body and it is worth caring for. Your anxiety is not a sign that your body is broken. Your anxiety is a sign that your nervous system is doing exactly what it was designed to do—alert you to danger. The question is not how to eliminate the signal. The question is how to teach your nervous system that not everything is danger. And the first step in that teaching is to show your body that you are serious about keeping it safe. That you are serious about feeding it. That you are serious about moving it. That you are serious about resting it.

That is worship. That is incarnational faith. That is the gospel meeting your actual embodied life.

CHAPTER FOUR

The Performative Christian

He sat across from me on a Tuesday afternoon, and the first thing he did was cry. Not quietly. The kind of crying that comes from years of not crying. The kind that leaves you gasping for breath between sobs.

We had talked before. Sunday greetings. Handshakes. The usual pastoral interactions. He had always been what you would call a solid Christian. Attended regularly. Served on a committee. Smiled in the hallway. Appeared to have his life together.

"I have been lying to everyone," he said. "For three years, I have been lying."

It took him twenty minutes to get to the actual confession. The performance had run so long that he had forgotten how to tell the truth quickly. But the gist was this: he had been battling anxiety for three years. Not small anxiety. Clinical anxiety. Panic attacks. Suicidal ideation. Depression so heavy that some mornings he could not leave his bed. And nobody knew.

He had come to church. He had sung the songs. He had taken communion. He had participated in small group discussions about faith and trust. All of it while sitting on the edge of a cliff, wondering if three years of this would ever end.

"Why didn't you tell anyone?" I asked.

"Because I'm a Christian," he said. "And Christians are not supposed to be like this."

This is the damage. Not the anxiety itself—though the anxiety was bad. The damage is the conviction that his condition is incompatible with his faith. That admitting he is struggling is the same as admitting that his faith does not work. That his anxiety is evidence that something is deeply wrong with him spiritually.

The performance requirement is real. And it is crushing.

American Christianity has built an entire ecosystem around testimonies of victory. We share the story of the addiction that was overcome. The disease that was healed. The family that was restored. The business that succeeded against the odds. These are powerful stories. And they are true. God does intervene. God does heal. God does restore. These testimonies matter.

But we have built an implicit hierarchy. Victory testimonies are celebrated. Testimonies of ongoing struggle are treated as incomplete. The unspoken message is: if you are still struggling, you have not yet reached the point where your testimony becomes shareable. Your struggle is still being processed. Your faith is still being refined. Once you reach the other side, then you can tell the story.

So the person who has been battling anxiety for three years stays quiet. The mother with postpartum depression does not mention it in the church bulletin. The man with chronic pain does not bring it to the prayer meeting. The woman with disordered eating does not raise her hand during the sermon on body image. They all know the unspoken rule: once you have won the battle, you can tell the story. Until then, suffer in silence. Keep smiling. Keep performing.

And the performance is exhausting.

Let me name what the performance actually costs. It costs energy. Every time you smile when you are falling apart, you are diverting energy from the actual work of dealing with your condition. You are using your reserves to maintain the facade instead of using them to heal. The person who spends Sunday morning preparing the performance—getting the smile right, getting the posture right, practicing the story that makes you sound fine—has less energy left for the actual work of therapy. For the actual work of prayer. For the actual work of recovery.

It costs honesty. You begin to lose track of what is actually true about you. You can tell the performance story so well that you start to believe it. Or you believe it sometimes and doubt it other times, which is worse—because now you have a gap between the story you are telling and the reality you are living. And that gap produces shame. You think: I have told people I am fine, and I am not fine, which means I am a liar. Which means I am a hypocrite. Which means there is something deeply dishonest about me.

It costs community. Real community requires vulnerability. Real community requires being known. But if you are performing, you are not being known. You are being admired or pitied or encouraged, but not known. The people around you are relating to the performance, not to the actual person. And when you finally get tired of the performance and break, people are shocked. "But you always seemed so stable. I thought you had it all together." This is the natural response to someone who has been performing well. But it adds a new layer of pain: if nobody actually knows the real you, does anybody actually love you? Or do they just love the performance?

It costs your relationship with God. If you cannot be honest with your community about your condition, you especially cannot be honest with God about it. Because if God is paying attention—if God actually sees you—then God already knows you are anxious. God already knows you are terrified. God already knows that the performance is a lie. So when you are praying, you are performing for God too. And the relationship becomes transactional instead of intimate. You are trying to maintain the image instead of opening yourself to the reality of God's presence.

The man who came to my office had been performing for three years. Three years of Sundays wearing the smile. Three years of small group discussions where he nodded at wisdom that he could not personally access. Three years of appearing to have faith while feeling like his faith was hollow. And the toll had been accumulating.

"What would happen," I asked him, "if you stopped performing?"

"People would judge me," he said.

"Would they?"

"They would think I was weak."

"Would they?"

"They would think I don't really believe in God."

Here is where I had to be gentle and honest at the same time. "Some of them might think that. Probably not. But some might. And here is the thing: you cannot control what they think. You can only control whether you keep drowning to maintain an image they are creating in their minds."

What happened next was the beginning of his actual recovery. Not the recovery from anxiety—that is a longer process and involves therapy and possibly medication and all the tools that help regulate a nervous system. But the recovery from the performance. The moment he started telling people the truth.

He told his small group. He expected rejection. What he got was recognition. Three other people in the group had been performing the same way. They had been watching him, and he had been watching them, and they had all agreed to a lie about each other. Once the truth broke through, the actual community became possible.

He told his family. His wife had known something was wrong but had been trained to accept the performance too. Once he stopped hiding, she could stop waiting for him to fix himself and could start actually helping him.

He told me, his pastor, things he would never have shared while maintaining the image of the guy who has it together.

And slowly—not quickly, but slowly—he started to experience something that the performance had made impossible: he started to be known. Imperfectly. By people who sometimes judged him and sometimes did not. But genuinely.

This is what the church has stolen from people. The permission to be honestly struggling. The permission to say "I am a Christian and I am also terrified." The permission to sit in the pew and sing about the peace of God while your body is in chaos, and to know that nobody will use your struggle against you later.

The cultural critique is this: American Christianity has colonized the virtue of faith and made it into the vice of performance. We have taken the call to honest engagement with God and replaced it with the requirement to present a curated self. We have made Christianity into a lifestyle brand—something you do to have status, to be seen, to maintain a reputation—instead of a complete undoing where you become known.

And the person who is struggling is not invited into this revolution. The person who is struggling is invited to perform better.

There is a scene in the New Testament that most churches never preach on. It is John 11, the raising of Lazarus. Jesus gets word that his friend Lazarus is dying. He waits. He does not rush. By the time he arrives, Lazarus has been dead for four days. The body is decomposing. There is no way back from this. And when Jesus arrives, what does he do?

He weeps. The text says "Jesus wept." The shortest verse in the Bible, and possibly the most important one for this moment. Jesus is not performing confidence in resurrection. Jesus is not using Lazarus's death as a teaching moment. Jesus is not maintaining the image of the unflappable divine being. Jesus is crying. Jesus is mourning. Jesus is honest about the weight of this loss.

And then—after he has cried, after he has acknowledged the reality of the death, after he has refused to spiritualize his way past the grief—then he raises Lazarus. But not before the honest engagement. The honesty comes first.

This is what the church needs to learn. The costly hope is not that your anxiety will magically disappear. The costly hope is that you can be honest about your condition and still be loved. That you can be struggling and still be welcome. That you can admit you are not fine and the people around you will not use that admission against you.

This requires something from everyone. The person who is struggling must risk being known. They must take the chance that people will judge them. But also that some people will not. That some people will actually love them more for being honest than for being impressive.

And the community must change its message. The message cannot be "testimony of victory or stay silent." The message must be "we are a community of people who are struggling toward God, and that struggle is what we have in common, not something to hide."

The performance is exhausting. And it is killing people quietly. Not metaphorically. Actually. People are ending their lives while performing faith because the performance has made it impossible for anyone to reach them before they fall.

This has to stop. The cost of stopping—the vulnerability, the risk, the willingness to be seen—is real. But the cost of continuing is death.

The performance has become so automatic that the performer cannot remember when it started. It was not a conscious choice. There was no moment when the pastor decided to construct a public self that was separate from the private one. It happened, gradually, the way a shore is eroded. Small movements over time until one day you look and realize the ground has shifted beneath you.

The performance serves a function. It protects the performer from something. It protects others from something. It makes the institution run. The pastor in the pulpit cannot preach about his own doubt. The pastor cannot stand before his congregation and admit that the faith he is teaching has become theoretical for him. The pastor cannot tell his staff that he is in crisis. So the pastor performs. He brings the correct version of himself to the pulpit. He wears the confidence that the role requires. He becomes the person that the congregation has voted into existence.

The cost accumulates in private. In the car on the way home from church. In the study late at night when everyone else is sleeping. In the moment before sleep when the conscious performance can finally be set down and what remains is the actual state of the soul. The pastor is exhausted by the gap between the two versions. The exhaustion is not the usual tiredness of a full day. It is the particular exhaustion that comes from maintaining a lie that extends into every interaction, every word, every moment of being seen.

What makes this complicated is that the lie is not being told deliberately. The pastor is not consciously fabricating. The pastor is responding to a system that has demanded the performance and then praised it. The congregation votes for the pastor who seems to have it together. The pastor who speaks with confidence. The pastor who seems to know the answers. The pastor who is not falling apart. So the pastor becomes that person. Not cynically. Not with awareness. Just in response to what the system has asked him to be.

The wife watches. She sees the performance from the inside. She sees him remove the pastor suit when he enters the house. She sees him slump. She sees what the confidence costs him. And she cannot tell anyone. Because if she told another pastor's wife that her husband was struggling, she would be introducing doubt into the congregation. If she told her small group, the words would travel and become the fact that the pastor is weak. So she carries the knowledge alone. She watches her husband perform every week. She knows what is actually happening. And she cannot speak the truth to anyone.

This is one of the subtle forms of violence that the church does to those who lead it. The church demands a performance and then resents any indication that it is a performance. The congregation wants a pastor who is strong, certain, faithful. So the pastor becomes this. And then the congregation is hurt and disappointed when they catch glimpses of the actual person underneath the performance. The congregation often responds by saying that the pastor is failing, that something is wrong, that his faith is not sufficient. What they actually mean is: the performance has cracked and I can see something real underneath and I find it threatening.

The same pattern appears in the social media version. The Instagram Christian whose curated life has become the standard. The photos are carefully selected. The captions are spiritually reflective. The struggle is absent. And the thousands of people who follow are seeing a version of the Christian life that does not include failure, doubt, disillusionment, or the actual texture of what it means to carry faith through difficulty. They see the version that was selected. And they begin to believe that this is the version they should have too.

The cost to the person creating the performance is that they begin to believe the performance themselves. The Instagram Christian has looked at the curated version so many times that they have forgotten what was left out. The fake becomes the metric. The selected version becomes the actual standard against which they judge themselves. And when the actual life—messy, complicated, full of doubt and failure—does not match the curated version, they feel the failure acutely.

But the cost to the people watching is also significant. They believe the performance. They believe that everyone else is managing their faith better. That everyone else's marriage is working. That everyone else's kids are turning out right. That everyone else's prayer life is producing the expected results. They begin to believe that they are the anomalies. That they are the only ones this is not working for. And so they perform too. They create their own curated version. They share the highlight reels. They hide the struggling parts.

The small group becomes a place where this performance is reinforced. People gather in someone's living room on a Tuesday night and ask each other the ritual questions: How are you? What's going on in your life? And everyone offers the pre-approved answer. The person going through the divorce says, "God is teaching me so much through this." The person who has lost their faith says, "I'm wrestling, but trusting." The person whose kid is in jail says, "Thankful for the opportunity to pray."

These answers are not lies, technically. They may all be true. But they are also edited. They are the parts that are safe to say out loud. The parts that do not threaten the shared narrative. The parts that everyone has agreed to focus on. And what does not get said is the actual weight. The actual question of whether God is real. The actual terror. The actual price.

One woman in the group is thinking about suicide. Not in an abstract way. In a concrete way. She has thought about method. She has looked at her calendar and identified a window when it might be possible. She is sitting in the group while people are praying and she is thinking about whether she wants to continue living. And she is nodding at all the right moments. She is saying the right words. She is not telling anyone what is actually happening because the group has not created space for that. The group is a space for performance. And she understands this. She goes along with it.

Another man in the group is viewing pornography daily. This is not something he is doing because he wants to. This is something that has become compulsive. He would like to tell someone. He would like to ask for prayer. He would like to admit that this is a problem and that he has failed. But he has looked around the group and he does not believe there is space for that confession. The group is full of people who seem to be managing their lives. He is the only one who is failing. So he does not say anything. He sits there and listens to everyone else's victories and he carries his failure alone.

A woman in the group has not had a genuine spiritual experience in fifteen years. She prays and feels nothing. She reads Scripture and it is words. She goes to church and she is acting the part of someone who believes. But the belief has ceased to feel real. It became words at some point and then it became performance and then it stopped being anything real at all. She is an atheist in a Christian small group. But she cannot say this. So she sits there every week and she performs faith and everyone believes that she is fine.

What they are not realizing is that they are all performing. What they are not realizing is that the man across from her is also not believing. That the woman three seats down has also stopped feeling anything. That the person praying is also full of doubt. They believe the performance because the performance is well done. Because everyone has gotten very skilled at saying the right things. Because the group has created a culture where the truth cannot live.

The cost of this is measurable. The cost is the accumulation of isolation that happens when everyone is lying to everyone else about the state of their soul. The cost is the distance that grows between people who believe they are alone in their struggle and the reality that they are surrounded by people in the same struggle. The cost is the lie that becomes so normal that people forget it is a lie. The cost is the performance that goes on for years until the performer has become the performance and the actual person inside is so diminished that they do not remember who they actually were.

The pastor in the pulpit is one version of this. But it is not unique to pastors. The performance architecture is built into the church's theology. The church asks people to present their best selves. The church wants to hear about victories. The church is uncomfortable with doubt. So the people of the church begin to perform. They bring the selected version. They hide the struggling version. And they tell themselves that this is faithfulness. That honesty would be a problem. That vulnerability would be a mistake.

What actually happens is that the performance becomes exhausting. The person performing anxiety—carrying all the weight, managing the smile, telling everyone that God is good—becomes more anxious. The anxiety of hiding the anxiety becomes greater than the original anxiety. The person performing faith—saying the words, praying the prayers, testifying to God's goodness—often loses the faith underneath the performance. The faith becomes the words. The words become a thing you do. And eventually you forget that you were ever supposed to mean them.

The person with anxiety who is also performing that they do not have anxiety is carrying double weight. The anxiety itself is happening. And the performance that pretends it is not happening is also happening. And the energy required to hold both of these is immense. The person is exhausted from the anxiety and exhausted from hiding the anxiety. They are running on fumes. And the fumes are powered by the conviction that revealing the truth would be worse than living the lie.

What breaks the performance is usually crisis. Illness. Infidelity. Addiction. Loss. Something happens that makes the performance impossible to maintain. And then there is a moment—sometimes just a moment—where the truth appears. And in that moment, if there is someone who can receive the truth without judgment, without correction, without advice, then something can shift. Then the person who has been performing can come into contact with another human being. And they discover that they are not alone. That they are not the only one whose faith has become performance. That the person across from them has also been drowning.

The book's insistence on naming the truth is an insistence that the performance can stop. That the person carrying anxiety does not have to pretend that the anxiety is not there. That the pastor does not have to present certainty that he does not feel. That the Instagram Christian does not have to curate the struggle out of existence. That the small group can become a place where the actual person is welcome. That faith can happen in the middle of honesty instead of requiring the honesty to be hidden.

But this requires a different theology than what most churches teach. It requires believing that doubt is not the enemy. That failure is not a moral problem. That the person whose faith has become words is still beloved. That the pastor who is falling apart is still allowed to lead. That the Christian whose life is a mess can still take communion. That the person who is not sure if they believe can still pray.

The cost of the current system is paid by the people who cannot perform convincingly. The people whose anxiety is too visible. Whose doubt is too obvious. Whose breakdown is too public. These people often leave the church. They determine that the church is not a safe place for them. And they are right. They are safe in the church only if they can perform. And many of them cannot.

But the cost is also paid by the people who can perform. The cost is the slow erosion of the self. The cost is the distance that grows between what is being presented and what is actually happening. The cost is the exhaustion. The cost is the loss of the relationship with God that might have been possible if honesty had been allowed.

**CHAPTER 5**

The Pastor's Secret

The church parking lot is empty. It's 11:47 PM on a Tuesday night. I'm sitting in my truck with the engine off, my hands gripping the steering wheel like it might escape. My wife is home with the boys. The house is probably quiet now. And I am still here, running the conversation with the board member back through my mind the way a tongue finds a chipped tooth.

He said three words I cannot unhear. "The giving is down."

That was this morning. Eight hours ago. The board member took me aside before service, pulled me into the office, and said it like I should have known. Like I was responsible. Like the weight of First Baptist's budget lived in my chest.

The rest of the day moved normally. Sunday school, service, lunch with two families, counseling appointment at 3 PM (a couple on the edge of separation, the husband sitting rigid while his wife cried), staff meeting, email about the roof, email about the youth retreat, email about the family who left suddenly with no explanation.

But underneath all of it was that number. The giving is down. Five thousand dollars. First quarter. And I have no idea what I did differently.

At 11:47 PM, I cannot stop thinking about it. The scenario-spinning has its own momentum now. What if they call a congregational meeting? What if the board votes to reduce my salary? What if we have to scale back the missions support? What if someone writes a Facebook post and it explodes and people start comparing my ministry to the pastor at the church across town whose attendance is growing?

The anxiety does not feel like doubt. It feels like thinking. It feels like responsibility. It feels like the weight of two hundred souls and a building and a mortgage and a staff who are depending on me to steer through. It feels like the most important thing I could possibly be doing right now — running through every scenario, preparing for the worst, making sure I am ready when it comes.

This is the pastor's secret. This is the thing nobody sees.

The congregation assumes we have a thermostat on our peace that you don't have. They assume our direct access to God is superior to yours. They assume that when we leave the pulpit on Sunday, we carry a different kind of certainty down to the marrow. Most of us have learned not to correct this assumption. It makes people feel like there is someone in the room who knows what he is doing. Someone who is not waking up at 2 AM running conversations backward through his mind like a prosecutor looking for the mistake.

I am forty-eight years old. I have been doing this for sixteen years. I have preached through grief. I have counseled marriages back from the edge. I have buried people I loved. I have watched the Holy Spirit work in ways I cannot explain. And I still lie awake at night worried about the ones who stopped coming. About the deacon whose son overdosed last year and who has not made eye contact with me since. About the woman I counseled through an affair and who now sits three rows back like I might somehow know what she has done.

The anxiety of the pastor is compounded because it has nowhere to go. Who do I tell about the fear? My congregation? That destroys their faith in the leadership. My wife? Susanna carries weight too. She has her own exhaustion with being the pastor's wife — the unwritten rules about what she should wear, how long she can be absent from Sunday school, whether her parenting is being judged from every pew. My peers? We have all agreed not to name this. We compare attendance numbers and baptism counts and building projects like we are all thriving. Like we are not all lying awake at 3 AM wondering if we are leading anyone anywhere or just managing decline with better PowerPoint slides.

This is why the Pastors Connection Network exists.

I started PCN because I watched good men break in isolation. I watched pastors leave the ministry. I watched them develop addictions, affairs, quiet rage. Not because they lacked faith or intelligence or gifts. But because they were carrying a weight without ever setting it down. Because the very thing that makes you trustworthy in a congregation — the appearance that you have it together — is the same thing that makes you terrified to admit that you don't.

When I sat with David Livingston in India, he named the crisis directly. "Pastor loneliness is killing men." He was not being poetic. He meant it literally. He has watched national church planters step away from the gospel because nobody was stepping toward them. The same thing is happening in Michigan. In Thailand. The shepherd who has nobody to shepherd him eventually becomes the one who devours his flock.

The anxiety of the pastor is not weakness. It is the cost of proximity. You carry what people tell you in confidence. You internalize the congregation's spiritual crises. You absorb the criticism even when it is not delivered to you — you hear about it three weeks later through someone's wife who mentioned it over coffee. You watch people leave without knowing whether you failed them. You pray for families and then never hear if the prayers were answered or if they moved or if they decided faith was not worth the cost.

The board member took me aside again on Wednesday. He put his hand on my shoulder and said, "Don't worry. It happens every spring. The giving will come back." He meant to reassure me. What he did instead was confirm that he thinks I was worried. Which I was, but now I cannot unhear that he sees it. Now I am wondering what else he sees. What he tells his wife. What he thinks about my leadership. What he thinks about my faith.

This is the spiral. And it is not unique to me.

Every pastor I know is running these same scenarios. The ones in churches of a thousand and the ones in churches of fifty. The ones with growing attendance and the ones watching their congregation age. The ones with healthy finances and the ones cutting budgets. The weight is different for each of us, but the anxiety is the same architecture. Someone needs something from you that you cannot guarantee you can deliver. And you will never know if you have failed until it is too late to fix it.

Martin Luther called it Anfechtung. Spiritual attacks. But what Luther described was not demons hiding in the shadows. He meant something closer to what we would now call severe anxiety and depression. The Reformer who transformed the Western church sometimes could not leave his room. He wrote about it directly. The attacks came as overwhelming doubt, as physical dread, as a sensation of being abandoned by God. And Luther did not experience them as weakness. He experienced them as the cost of seeing something true and then having to live with the weight of trying to communicate it to people who were comfortable without it.

Luther's letters are staggering for their honesty. He describes his depressions without filter. He admits that he sometimes could not pray without his wife sitting beside him. He confessed that his confidence in God's promises often felt more theoretical than real. The man whose name became synonymous with biblical clarity spent much of his life in what we would now recognize as clinical depression.

But here is what matters. Luther did not stop. He did not redefine the gospel to be more comfortable. He did not smooth the prophetic edge to ease his anxiety. He carried the weight. And the weight, over time, began to transform him. Not into someone without anxiety — there is no evidence of that. But into someone whose faith was not built on the absence of fear but on the decision to move forward despite it.

That is the secret the pastor is supposed to learn but rarely does. The weight does not get lighter with faithfulness. It gets heavier. Because the more clearly you see what is true, the more you feel the responsibility to say it. The more you love the congregation, the more their suffering becomes your suffering. The more you understand the gospel, the more you see where the church has been unfaithful to it.

I drove home that night at midnight. The house was dark. I sat in the living room for twenty minutes before going to bed. Not praying. Not strategizing. Just sitting with the weight.

The next morning, I told Susanna. Not everything. Not the full spiral. But I told her that I was anxious about the finances. And she did what she always does. She sat across from me at the kitchen table with her coffee and said, "What if they cut the budget? What would happen?" And I said, "I don't know." And she said, "We would figure it out. We have before."

She was right. We had. But more importantly, she named something I had been avoiding. The thing underneath the anxiety was not really the finances. It was the assumption that if the church's numbers decline, it is somehow evidence that God's movement through me has declined. That my worth as a pastor is measured in the congregation's health. That I am responsible for the weight in a way that makes me the architect of my own failure.

That is the lie the pastor believes. And it kills him.

The truth is slower. The truth is that I am responsible for faithfulness, not for results. I am responsible for speaking the truth, not for whether people receive it. I am responsible for loving the congregation, not for guaranteeing their spiritual development. I am responsible for my own discipleship, not for managing their faith.

But knowing that intellectually and knowing it at 2 AM when the anxiety starts its work are two different things.

That is why we need each other. That is why I needed to hear a fellow pastor say, "I lie awake too." That is why PCN exists. Not to remove the weight. But to carry it together instead of alone.

The pastor's secret is not that we have less faith than our congregation thinks. It is that the weight we carry is real. And we are supposed to tell someone about it. We are not supposed to manage the anxiety alone until it becomes something darker.

When I preached on Acts 3 that next Sunday, I was tired. The anxiety had not lifted. I was still wondering about the finances. But I stood in the pulpit and named something that I had been avoiding. I said, "The church is not your responsibility. The kingdom is not your responsibility. Your faithfulness is. And faithfulness sometimes looks like failure to everyone who is watching."

I could see people shift. Some of them heard what they needed to hear. Others looked confused. But one person came up after the service — one of the older men in the congregation — and he pulled me aside and said, "Thank you for saying that. I needed to hear it."

Maybe that is what the weight is for. Not to break us but to make us tender to others who are carrying weight too.

The Pastors Connection Network exists because pastors are dying in isolation. This is not a metaphor. Pastors are statistically more likely to struggle with depression and anxiety than the general population. Pastors have higher rates of divorce than the congregations they lead. Pastors die by suicide at rates that stagger the people who track such data. And the reason is loneliness.

The loneliness is not incidental. The loneliness is structural. The pastor is the one who is supposed to have answers. The pastor is the leader. The pastor is the shepherd. The pastor cannot be the sheep. The pastor cannot have needs that are visible. The pastor cannot struggle. The pastor cannot fail. And so the pastor is isolated from the basic human experience of being known and carried by other people.

The weight of secrets is part of this. The pastor knows things about the congregation that the congregation does not know about itself. The marriage that is ending. The child that is addicted. The affair that is being hidden. The financial desperation. The sexual abuse that happened years ago and is still shaping everything. The pastor carries all of this. The pastor is the keeper of all the secrets. And the pastor cannot share them. The pastor cannot process them with a therapist because of confidentiality. The pastor cannot tell his wife because what if it comes out. The pastor cannot talk to another pastor because what if it travels. So the pastor carries the secrets alone.

This is a particular kind of weight. It is not like carrying your own burden. It is like carrying the burdens of everyone in the congregation and not being allowed to set them down. Every counseling session adds weight. Every visit with someone in crisis adds weight. Every discovery of hidden sin adds weight. And there is nowhere to put the weight down. Nowhere that is safe.

One pastor described his experience by saying: Every Sunday I go to work and I meet with people in the deepest pain of their lives. I listen. I counsel. I pray. And then I go home and I cannot tell my wife what I just did because the information is not mine to share. So I come home carrying that person's crisis and I smile at my family and I make dinner conversation and I go to bed thinking about whether that person is going to survive the night. And nobody knows. The people at church think I had a normal day. My wife knows something is wrong but I cannot tell her what. I am completely alone with what I know.

Another pastor described the weight of financial responsibility. The congregation is sixty thousand dollars short in its budget. The building is aging. The staff needs to be paid. The pastor is the one who has to make the decisions about what gets cut. The pastor is the one who has to stand up and explain to the congregation why they cannot do the programs they want to do. The pastor is the one who is responsible if the church fails financially. And the pastor is getting paid significantly less than the people he is asking to give more money. The pastor is thinking about his own mortgage while he is asking his congregation about theirs.

A third pastor described the weight of preaching when the well is dry. He describes going into the pulpit on Sunday morning. He has prepared the sermon. He has done the exegetical work. He is ready. But internally he is empty. His own faith is in question. His marriage is struggling. His prayer life has become words without meaning. And he stands up and he preaches about faith. He exegetes a text about hope. He draws an application about trusting God. And inside he is wondering if any of it is true. He is performing the preaching while experiencing the opposite of what he is preaching. And nobody knows. The congregation hears a strong word about faith. The pastor hears himself lying.

A woman pastor described the weight of being the strong one. Everyone comes to her with their problems. Everyone calls when they are in crisis. Everyone looks to her for leadership and wisdom and direction. And she comes home and collapses. She lies down on the bed and she cannot move. She has nothing left to give. Her own nervous system is in shutdown. But on Monday she gets up and does it again. She takes the calls. She listens. She counsels. She leads. And then she comes home and collapses again.

The Pastors Connection Network has data on this. The data shows that a significant percentage of pastors are on antidepressants. A significant percentage are seeing therapists outside of their church context because there is nowhere inside their church where they can be honest. A significant percentage are having thoughts of leaving ministry altogether. And most of them have never told anyone.

The shame component is significant. The pastor is supposed to be healthy. The pastor is supposed to have it together. The pastor is supposed to be an example of Christian maturity. So when the pastor is struggling, the first response is shame. The shame that he is not the person everyone thinks he is. The shame that his faith is not producing the peace that he is teaching people that faith should produce. The shame that he is failing at the one thing he is supposed to be good at.

One pastor described the particular year when he almost quit. He was six years into his pastorate. The church was growing. To external observers everything was working. But internally everything was collapsing. His marriage was not good. His prayer life had stopped. He was preaching sermons on faith while being consumed by doubt. He was counseling people through crisis while his own crisis was deepening. He was managing the anxiety through extra work. The more anxious he felt the more hours he worked. And the more he worked the more anxious he became. It was a feedback loop. He was caught inside it.

One Tuesday he sat down and he wrote out his resignation. He was done. He could not do this anymore. He was going to walk away from ministry. He was going to find a job where he did not have to perform faith. Where he could just be human. Where nobody was watching. Where the weight could be put down.

He did not send the letter. But he wrote it. And the fact that he wrote it is significant. The fact that he came that close means that something was desperately wrong. It means that a pastor—a good pastor, doing good work, in a healthy church—came to the point where he wanted to leave. And he could not tell anyone. He could not say, "I am thinking about quitting." Because if he said that, the congregation would lose confidence. The church would begin to panic. The decision would have to be announced and explained and managed. So the pastor sits with the decision to quit ministry and the decision to stay in ministry and the decision not to tell anyone about either decision. And he carries all of it alone.

What saved this pastor was another pastor. A pastor from a different church who somehow found out that he was struggling. Who called and said, "I think you might need to talk to someone." Who did not try to fix it. Who did not try to explain it theologically. Who simply said, "I see you. And I think you might be in trouble. And I am going to sit with you in this." The pastor drove to another city and he sat in another pastor's living room and he told the truth. And the truth was: I do not know if I can do this anymore. I do not know if I believe anymore. I do not know if my marriage is going to survive. I do not know if I should stay or leave. I do not know anything except that I am drowning.

And the other pastor did not argue. Did not correct. Did not offer solutions. Did not say anything except: I believe you. I see you. And I am here. That was enough. Not to fix it. But to break the isolation. To tell the drowning pastor that he was not drowning alone.

The weight that pastors carry is often not the visible weight. It is not the sermon preparation or the church administration or the pastoral care. The visible work is exhausting but it is manageable. The weight that is invisible is the one that breaks them. The weight of knowledge that cannot be shared. The weight of responsibility that is placed on them. The weight of performing certainty when they have none. The weight of being the anchor for everyone while having no anchor themselves.

The church has not developed a way to hold this weight. The church has institutional structures for managing programs and budgets and attendance. The church does not have structures for carrying the pastor. The pastor is expected to carry everyone. But nobody carries the pastor. The pastor is expected to have a perfect marriage so he can teach people about marriage. The pastor is expected to have a strong faith so he can teach people about faith. The pastor is expected to have peace so he can teach people about peace. And if the pastor is not experiencing any of these things, the pastor has to hide it.

The cost of this is that pastors leave. Good pastors. Faithful pastors. Pastors who were doing real work. They leave because they cannot sustain the performance. They leave because they are drowning and no one can see that they are drowning. They leave because the isolation is killing them and the institution has no interest in addressing the isolation.

Some of them do not just leave quietly. Some of them have breakdowns that become public. Some of them have affairs that become known. Some of them lose their ministries publicly because the facade finally cracked and the congregation saw what was underneath. And the congregation is shocked. But the pastor is not shocked. The pastor has been drowning for years. The only surprise is that someone finally noticed.

What the pastor in the crisis moment needs is not more Scripture. The pastor knows Scripture. The pastor has spent years studying Scripture. The pastor is aware of what Scripture says about God's sovereignty and provision and faithfulness. The pastor does not need someone to point him to a verse. The pastor needs someone to say: I know you are drowning. And I am here. And you do not have to do this alone.

This is what PCN is trying to create. Spaces where the pastor can be real. Where the pastor does not have to perform. Where the pastor can say that the marriage is struggling and not have that information travel to the congregation. Where the pastor can admit doubt and not be accused of weak faith. Where the pastor can describe the anxiety and the depression and the thoughts of leaving and still be valued as a person and a pastor.

The loneliness that pastors carry is not unique to pastors. But it is particular to pastors because of the role. The pastor is supposed to be the one with answers. The pastor is supposed to be the leader. The pastor is supposed to be spiritually mature. So the pastor cannot be the one who is struggling. The pastor cannot be vulnerable. The pastor cannot ask for help.

And when the isolation becomes unbearable, the pastor has three options: perform more convincingly, leave quietly, or break down publicly. Most pastors choose to perform more convincingly for as long as they can. And when they can no longer perform convincingly, some of them leave quietly. And some of them stay until something forces the break.

What needs to happen is a different system. A system where the pastor is allowed to be human. Where the pastor is allowed to struggle. Where the pastor is allowed to have needs. Where the pastor is carried by the congregation instead of always being the carrier. Where there is space for the pastor to be honest about what is actually happening inside.

The book's insistence on naming the weight is an insistence that the pastor can be honest. That the anxiety can be spoken. That the doubt can be acknowledged. That the weight of secrets can be shared. Not all of it. There are still confidences that belong to the congregation. But some of it. Enough of it that the pastor is no longer completely alone. Enough that the pastor has one person who knows what is actually happening. Enough that the performance can stop and the actual person can be seen.

The cost of continuing the current system is that pastors will continue to leave. Good pastors. Faithful pastors. Pastors who could have had thirty-year ministries will instead burn out after six. And the church will keep wondering why. And the answer is always the same: The pastor could not sustain the isolation. The pastor could not keep the performance up. The pastor could not carry the weight alone.

Monday morning I finish a hospital visitation. Woman in her sixties, just diagnosed with pancreatic cancer. The surgery is tomorrow. I sit with her and her daughter and the daughter's husband and I listen to fear that is already becoming the shape of the next six months or the next year or possibly the final accounting. I pray. I sit silent afterward. I drive back to my office watching the Fenton streets move past.

Tuesday morning I get a call at seven. A member—he has been here nine years, his son is in youth group—he called because his wife found out yesterday that he has been unfaithful. She is not in the marriage anymore emotionally, though they are technically still cohabiting. He wants to know if I can help. If reconciliation is possible. I tell him to come Wednesday afternoon. I hang up knowing that I will spend the next two hours before that appointment running scenarios. What did he do. How long. Does he want restoration or does he want permission to end it. I do not know yet. But I will know that there are two people in real crisis and I am the one they believe might know what to do.

Tuesday afternoon youth parent meeting. We are discussing a seventeen-year-old girl in the group who has attempted suicide twice in the last four months. The second attempt was more serious than the first. She is still in inpatient treatment. The parents in the room are asking if we should invite her back to group when she is discharged. If she should be welcome. I am sitting in that meeting feeling the weight of the question settle on me. What if we say yes and she hurts herself again. What if we say no and that becomes the event that confirms to her that the church does not want her. There is no good answer. There is only the reality that I am the one who will make the answer and the answer will have consequences for a kid who is already in so much pain that she wanted to die.

Wednesday morning sermon preparation. The text for Sunday is Colossians 1. Paul writing about the preeminence of Christ. Holding all things together. Everything visible and invisible subject to Him. I have three hours to explain that to people. I have not slept well in three days. My coffee is cold. My notes look like they were written by someone else. I will find a way. I always do. But right now I am looking at a blank outline and feeling the weight of Sunday already. The sermon is not just words. The sermon is a promise that I understand something true about God that my congregation needs to hear. And right now, I do not feel like I understand anything at all.

Wednesday afternoon the marriage counseling session. The husband comes at 2 PM. He is raw. Defensive. He does not want to be here but his wife insisted. He tells me about the affair in clinical language, like he is describing someone else's life. It lasted two years. It ended when his wife found evidence. He has not seen her since. He does not know if he wants the marriage or does not want the marriage. He is numb. I sit with him and the numbness and I listen and what he is really doing is asking me if he is forgiven. If God will take him back. If there is a pathway forward that does not end in complete destruction. I am the one who might know the answer. I do not have an answer. I have years of experience and theological clarity and the ability to sit in difficult things. What I do not have is the ability to unhear what he has told me. The detail. The duration. The duplicity. That is now in me.

Wednesday night. Budget meeting. The treasurer is projecting a shortfall for the year. Attendance is down slightly from last year. Online giving has shifted the pattern. We may have to ask for deeper commitments or we may have to reduce some of the mission support or we may have to have a difficult conversation about staff reductions. I sit in that meeting and the financial pressure feels like it is my responsibility. The giving is down. The attendance is down. These are measures of my effectiveness as a leader. The logic is not spoken but it is in the room. And I feel it in my chest.

Thursday morning I get an email from a family that left suddenly two months ago. They have decided not to return. The email does not say why. I read between the lines. There was conflict in the family. The dad was gone a lot for work. The mom felt unsupported. The kids were going through typical teenage nonsense. Something in our community contributed to them feeling like they did not belong. I will never know exactly what. The email is polite. But it is final. And I receive it as personal failure. We had one chance to make them feel known and we did not.

Friday the sermon is still not done. I have an hour before I have to walk it through with the worship team. The outline is there. But it does not land. It does not have the weight it needs to have. It is not going to change anyone. I revise. I cut. I rewrite the application. I make it sharper. And I am aware, in the act of revision, that I am attempting to preach truth that I am not currently living. I am exhausted. I am carrying the cancer woman and the unfaithful husband and the suicidal teenager and the budget shortfall and the family that left and the sermon that is not working and Susanna and her own exhaustion and my five sons who are growing up at speeds I cannot quite measure and I am about to stand in a pulpit on Sunday and tell people to trust God.

The lie that becomes nearly true is that I am somehow better at all of this than they are. That my burden is a professional burden and theirs is personal. That I have been trained for this weight in a way that ordinary Christians have not. The lie is attractive because it makes sense of why I keep showing up. Why I do not quit. Why I stand in the pulpit and speak truth even when the truth feels abstract from where I am sitting.

But the truth is simpler. I am drowning like everyone else. I just have more people depending on me not to drown visibly.

The pastoral anxiety is not noble. It is not evidence of depth. It is not a sign that I care more than my congregation cares. It is the cost of proximity. You carry what people tell you. You absorb their fear. You see the gap between what they tell their small group on Sunday morning and what they tell you when they are alone in your office. You see the marriages breaking. You see the addictions being hidden. You see the faith being performed. You see the questions that people cannot ask anywhere else. And you carry all of it because there is nowhere else to put it.

The shepherd absorbs the flock's wounds. That is not metaphorical. The neurochemistry is real. The burden is not abstract. The weight sits in your chest the way it sits in the cancer woman's chest. The way it sits in the unfaithful husband's chest. And the expectation—unspoken, structural, theological—is that the shepherd's faith should be sufficient to bear this without breaking.

I have watched good men break under this weight. I have watched them develop what looks like character weakness but is actually the cost of carrying things meant to be carried together. I have watched them turn to alcohol or pornography or affairs—not because they are uniquely broken but because they are looking for a way to put the weight down, even temporarily. I have watched them get angry at their congregations for being needy without realizing that they are angry at themselves for not being able to be the transcendent presence that the congregation has projected onto them.

This is why the Pastors Connection Network exists. I started it because I could not watch it anymore. The loneliness of the pastor is not an accident of the profession. It is structural. The pastor is the one who hears everything and tells nobody. The pastor is the one who absorbs the congregation's pain and has nowhere to discharge it. The pastor is the one who stands in the pulpit and appears certain while being internally tormented by doubt.

In India, David Livingston described it to me this way: the national pastors under his care are carrying the same weight that I carry but without the support structures. They do not have therapists—mental health professionals are not available. They do not have peer communities where they can admit struggle. They have their wives, usually. They have God. And they have the slow erosion of everything else. Some of them are breaking. Some of them are stepping away from the gospel because the cost of staying feels too high.

The antidote is not removing the weight. The weight is part of the calling. You cannot shepherd people through their darkest moments and not be shaped by it. You cannot hold people's stories and not carry them. You cannot speak truth into broken lives without feeling the fragmentation. The antidote is having a place where you can put the weight down. Where you can admit that you are exhausted. Where the admission does not disqualify you from your calling but deepens it.

This is what I needed. What every pastor needs. What the structures of the American church are not set up to provide.

When I finally told Susanna the actual weight—not the condensed version but the detail about the cancer diagnosis and the affair and the suicide attempt and the budget crisis and the family who left—she did something I did not expect. She did not try to fix it. She did not offer advice. She sat with me while I carried it out loud. And something shifted. The weight did not decrease. But I was not carrying it alone. And somehow, that made it bearable.

This is what the church is supposed to be for pastors and it is not. The congregation worships a pastor who is certain. They need certainty. And the pastor, because he loves them, gives it to them. But the pastor, underneath the certainty, is breaking. And there is no place in the congregation where that breaking is welcome.

This is the corruption that platform brings. The bigger the church, the more the pastor becomes a symbol instead of a person. The more the pastor becomes a voice from above instead of a shepherd among the flock. And the more unbearable the loneliness becomes. Because now the pastor cannot admit weakness to anyone in the community. Now the pastor is the one who is supposed to have the answers. Now the pastor is the one who cannot say "I am drowning."

I have learned this slowly. Over sixteen years at First Baptist and over a decade before that. The pastor's anxiety is real. The pastor's doubt is real. The pastor's exhaustion is real. And it is not evidence of insufficient faith. It is evidence that the pastor is human and the burden is real and the church has not figured out how to help the shepherd carry the weight. So the shepherd carries it alone. And the carrying, over time, changes you. It makes you harder in some places and softer in others. It makes you more honest about what faith actually costs. It makes you bitter or prophetic depending on whether you can find a place to put the weight down.

That place cannot be the congregation. That place cannot be the denomination. The only place I have found is in other pastors. In men who know what it means to be the one who has to appear certain while being internally fractured. In men who can sit with you and admit "I lie awake too." In spaces where the mask comes off and you are known not as the leader but as the broken person who keeps showing up.

This is why community is not optional for the pastor. This is why PCN matters. This is why the pastor's words from the pulpit will only be honest to the degree that the pastor has a place to be dishonest first.

**CHAPTER 6**

When Prayer Doesn't Fix It

The hardest words in Christian faith are these: "My grace is sufficient for you, but I am not going to remove this."

Paul received that word from God about his thorn in the flesh. He had begged. Three times, he tells us. Three separate prayers asking the same thing. Not gently asking. Begging. The Greek word is parakaleo, which carries the weight of desperation. He was not praying ritual prayers. He was imploring heaven to fix what was breaking him.

And God said no.

Not "no yet." Not "no, but I will eventually." Just no. My grace is sufficient. That is the answer you get.

We have turned this verse into a greeting card cliché. "God's grace is sufficient." We print it on coffee mugs. We sing it in worship. And we have drained it of its actual content, which is that God is telling Paul, "I am not going to fix this thing that is destroying you. But I am going to be with you in it. And that will have to be enough."

That is brutally different from what most of us have been taught to believe about prayer.

We have been told that prayer is a mechanism. You input the correct prayer, and the output is the thing you prayed for. You pray for healing, and you get healed. You pray for peace, and you get peace. You pray for anxiety to leave, and it leaves. If it does not leave, it means your faith was insufficient, or your prayer was insufficiently specific, or you are secretly harboring some sin that is blocking the answer. There is always something you did wrong.

That is not what Paul discovered. Paul discovered that God can say no to your desperate prayer and somehow still be good.

I have sat across from a hundred people who have discovered this too, but they did not have language for it. A woman who prayed for her marriage to be restored and watched it crumble anyway. A man who fasted and prayed for his addiction to release and found himself in another cycle. A teenager who memorized Scripture about anxiety and still could not leave the house. A pastor who preached about the peace that passes understanding on Sunday and woke up at 3 AM on Tuesday convinced he was having a heart attack.

All of them had the same theological crisis. They had done the thing they were supposed to do. They had prayed. They had believed. They had obeyed. And the thing they were praying about had not changed.

The standard Christian response to this is to find the loophole. Maybe you did not pray correctly. Maybe you did not have enough faith. Maybe you need to speak it into existence. Maybe you need to declare it. Maybe you need to stop praying and start walking it out by faith. Maybe you need to bind the spirit of anxiety in the name of Jesus. Maybe the thing you are asking for is not God's will for you, and you need to ask for the right thing instead.

All of these responses have one thing in common. They assume that if prayer is not "working," it is your fault.

What if it is not?

What if the real answer is stranger than any of those loopholes? What if God sometimes says, "I am not going to remove this. But I am going to meet you in it, and that meeting will change you in ways that removing it never would."

Mother Teresa wrote about this. She did not write about it publicly until after her death, when her private correspondence was released. For decades, most of the world thought she had a direct line to God's presence. Her letters revealed something closer to the opposite. She experienced what she called "darkness." Not sadness. Not discouragement. Darkness. A sustained absence of any felt sense of God's presence. For nearly forty years, she was doing the work of compassion without feeling loved by the God she was serving.

She prayed about it. Repeatedly. She asked for the darkness to lift. It did not. She asked for a sign that God was present. There was no sign. She asked to feel something of what she believed about God. Nothing. And yet she continued serving in Calcutta. She continued to give her life away. She continued to believe that what she was doing mattered, even though she had no emotional evidence that God was present or that anything she did made any difference.

When her letters became public, the church had a crisis of confidence. How could Mother Teresa, of all people, experience such spiritual abandonment? The answer is that she was experiencing exactly what Paul experienced. She was being asked to love God not because it felt good, not because it produced obvious results, but because love is love regardless of the reciprocal feeling.

That is what Paul discovered in his own version of this darkness. He begged God to remove the thorn. God refused. And then Paul had to do something much harder than continuing to pray. He had to decide to love God anyway. He had to decide that God's grace in the present moment, without the removal of his suffering, was still sufficient.

This is where the Christian faith becomes something different from therapy, different from positive thinking, different from any of the self-help architectures that have colonized our spirituality.

The gospel does not promise that prayer removes suffering. It promises that God meets you in suffering. It promises that you are not alone in the dark. It promises that the presence of God in your present moment is more real and more sustaining than the absence of the thing you are suffering from.

And then it asks you to believe that without feeling it.

The woman whose marriage crumbled did not get the marriage back. But she discovered something she had not expected. She discovered that her value did not depend on being married. She discovered that God did not love her more when she was married and less when she was alone. She discovered that the grace that was there when she was married was still there when she was not. It was the same grace. Different container, but the same grace.

The man in addiction recovery found that sobriety did not come from prayer alone. It came from prayer plus community, plus sometimes medication, plus brutal honesty about what he could and could not do himself. Prayer did not fix the addiction. Prayer was part of the larger structure that addressed the addiction. But prayer alone did not do it.

The teenager did not leave the house because of Scripture memorization. But she discovered that the Scripture was still true even if she was having a panic attack. She discovered that the truth of the gospel was not dependent on her emotional experience of it. The truth was there whether she felt it or not.

This is what the reformed tradition meant by "the sufficiency of grace." Not that grace feels like enough. But that grace is enough. That the presence of God in your suffering is the real thing, even if it feels like absence. That the gospel is true whether or not you feel it working.

I learned this the hard way. I spent years praying the panic attacks away. I memorized Scripture about fear. I would wake up at 3 AM with my heart racing and lie in the dark reciting Philippians 4:6-7, "Do not be anxious about anything, but in every situation, by prayer and petition, with thanksgiving, present your requests to God." And immediately after the Scripture, my heart would still be racing. The panic did not leave because I had cited the right verse.

I thought I was doing something wrong. I thought my faith was insufficient. I thought if I just prayed harder, prayed longer, prayed more specifically, the panic would eventually yield to my faith.

I did not understand what Paul understood. That the presence of God in my panic was not the same as the absence of panic. That God could be with me in the racing heart. That grace could be sufficient without the panic leaving. That I could trust God not because he was fixing the thing, but because he was present in the thing.

When I finally understood that, something shifted. Not everything. The panic did not disappear. But my relationship to the panic changed. I was no longer praying for God to remove it. I was praying for God to meet me in it. And there is a difference.

The difference is the difference between therapy and discipleship. Therapy aims at the removal of symptoms. Discipleship aims at transformation of the self in the presence of God. Both matter. Both are real. But they are not the same thing.

And here is what matters most. When you stop expecting prayer to be the mechanism that removes the thing, you stop blaming yourself when the thing remains. You stop interpreting the persistence of anxiety as a sign that your faith is weak. You stop assuming that the darkness means God is absent.

You start understanding that darkness is sometimes where God does his deepest work.

Paul's thorn remained. But Paul was transformed by the decision to carry it anyway. Mother Teresa's darkness remained. But Mother Teresa became the person she became because she loved God in the darkness, not because the darkness ever lifted. The woman whose marriage crumbled did not get her marriage back. But she discovered freedom she never expected.

This is not the gospel of removal. This is the gospel of presence. And it is harder. It is much harder to trust God when he is not removing the thing than to trust him when he is. It is easier to believe in God's goodness when your prayers are answered than when they are not. It is easier to have faith when you feel loved than to have faith in love when you feel nothing.

But this harder gospel is also more true.

Because God is not a cosmic vending machine. Prayer is not a system for getting what you want. Grace is not the absence of suffering. Grace is the presence of God in your suffering, and the transformation that presence brings about over time, whether or not the suffering ends.

This is why Paul could say that his weakness was his strength. Not because weakness is good. But because weakness is where he encountered the power of God that he could not conjure himself. The power of grace. The power of presence. The power of being met by something larger than himself.

When you stop expecting prayer to be the mechanism that fixes things, you start experiencing prayer as the mechanism that transforms you. You start discovering grace not as the absence of pain, but as the presence of God in pain. You start understanding faith not as the belief that things will get better, but as the decision to move forward anyway, held by hands you cannot see but are learning to trust.

That is when prayer becomes real. Not because it removes what you are praying about. But because it connects you to something that will hold you if you let it. Through the darkness. Through the panic. Through the waiting. Through the years when nothing changes.

My grace is sufficient for you. Not because the thing goes away. But because I am here. And that is enough.

It takes a lifetime to believe that. And most of us only start to believe it after everything else has failed.

Prayer is not a weapon that you point at the anxiety and fire. But this is how the church often frames it. Prayer as the thing you do to fight back against the unwanted experience. Prayer as the tool by which you overcome the emotional reality you are facing. Prayer as the mechanism by which you demand that God change the situation because you have asked him to. And when the prayer does not produce the expected result—when you pray and the anxiety continues, when you command it to leave and it stays—then the prayer is framed as ineffective or your faith is framed as insufficient.

But there is a different understanding of prayer. Prayer as the place where you show up as yourself—not as you think you should be—and you are met by God. Prayer not as a weapon but as a presence. Prayer that does not demand change but allows you to be with yourself and with God in the middle of what is actually happening.

This distinction changes everything. Because what the anxious person often needs is not for the anxiety to be removed. The anxious person often needs permission to stop fighting the anxiety. Permission to be with what is actually happening. Permission to stop trying to overcome the body and to start trying to be with the body.

The medieval church understood prayer as a tool for achieving union with God. The monk spent hours in prayer not expecting that the prayer would change external circumstances but expecting that the prayer would change the monk's relationship to the circumstances. The prayer would align the will with God's will. The prayer would produce acceptance. The prayer would deepen the love that was the ultimate point of the whole practice.

What the medieval monk was not doing was using the prayer as a weapon to beat back the body. The monk was not praying the anxiety away. The monk was sitting with himself and with God and allowing the process of that presence to transform him. Not remove the difficulty. Transform him in relation to the difficulty.

Contemplative prayer operates in this register. Centering prayer, where you sit with a word or a phrase or a breath and you return to it when the mind wanders. Lectio divina, where you read Scripture slowly and you sit with a passage and you allow God to speak to you through it. Sitting prayer or zazen where you sit in silence and you are present to yourself and to God and to what is actually happening in your body.

These practices are not designed to fix the anxiety. They are designed to be with the anxiety. They are designed to create space where you are not fighting the anxiety but experiencing the anxiety in the presence of God. And something shifts in that space. Not the anxiety necessarily. But your relationship to the anxiety. Your ability to be with yourself without judgment. Your capacity to be honest about what you are experiencing.

One person described a contemplative prayer practice by saying: I sit in silence every morning for twenty minutes. I do not ask God to fix anything. I just sit. I notice my thoughts. I notice my body. I notice the anxiety when it comes. And I am present to all of it. Some mornings the anxiety is very present. Some mornings it is quiet. But either way I am just sitting. I am just being. And somehow in that regular practice of showing up and being honest about what is actually happening, I become less afraid of the anxiety. It is still there sometimes. But I am not fighting it. I am not trying to overcome it. I am just aware of it. And that awareness makes it less powerful.

Another person described it by saying: I have been praying the same way my whole life. I pray to change things. I pray for God to do things. I pray to convince God that my request is reasonable. And I rarely feel like the prayer is working. But I started listening to my therapist about how to be present to my body. And I started bringing that into my prayer. I would sit and I would feel the anxiety in my chest. And instead of asking God to remove it, I would ask God to help me be with it. And the prayer started to feel different. Not like something I was trying to accomplish. Like something I was just doing. Being with God and being with myself at the same time.

The distinction between prayer as weapon and prayer as presence is related to the distinction between what might be called a therapeutic or a functional approach to prayer. The functional approach treats prayer as a tool by which you accomplish something. You have a problem. You use the tool. The problem is solved or at least moved forward. The problem is that this creates the expectation that the tool will work. That if you use it correctly, the problem will be solved. And when the problem remains unsolved, the assumption is either that you did not use the tool correctly or that the problem is more resistant than you thought.

The presence approach to prayer does not have the same results orientation. You are not using prayer to accomplish something. You are using prayer to be with something. You are using prayer to stand in the presence of God while you are honest about what you are experiencing. The goal is not to change the situation. The goal is to be changed by the practice. To be transformed from someone who is fighting the situation into someone who is present to the situation.

The Psalms again are instructive. The lament psalms do not resolve. They do not end with the person's perspective shifting from despair to hope. They end in lamentation. But the person who is lamented is lamented in the presence of God. The person who is experiencing the darkness is experiencing it before God. This is significant. The prayer is not changing the darkness. But the prayer is changing the context of the darkness. The darkness is no longer private. The darkness is witnessed. The darkness is brought before God.

What happens when you bring your actual experience into prayer is that the experience changes its character. Not immediately. Not magically. But gradually. The experience that was private becomes shared. The experience that was shameful becomes something that can be witnessed. The experience that was resisted becomes something that can be present.

A pastor described this by saying: For years I prayed through my anxiety. I would feel it coming and I would start praying and I would ask God to take it away. And sometimes it would ease and sometimes it would not. But either way I felt like the prayer was part of the management. Like I was doing something about the anxiety. Then I had a breakdown. And I could not pray like that anymore. I could not ask God to take it away because the anxiety was overwhelming and no prayer seemed to touch it. So I stopped trying. I just sat. I would feel the anxiety and I would say to God: I am here. This is what is happening. I need you. Not to fix this. Just to be here with me in this. And something shifted. The anxiety was still there. But I was no longer alone with it. The prayer was not changing the anxiety. But it was changing me. It was changing my ability to live inside the anxiety without the anxiety defining me.

The contemplative tradition has developed extensive practices for this kind of prayer. Centering prayer uses a sacred word—a word that represents your intention to consent to God's presence. Whenever your mind wanders, you gently return to the word. The practice is not trying to empty your mind or achieve a particular spiritual state. The practice is practicing the gesture of turning toward God. Over and over. Returning to the intention. Returning to the presence.

What happens in this practice is that you become aware of the layers of your own being. You become aware of thoughts. You become aware of emotions. You become aware of body sensations. You become aware of the witness that is aware of all of these things. And gradually, through repeated practice, you begin to identify less with the anxiety that is being witnessed and more with the awareness that is witnessing it. You become the one who is aware of the anxiety rather than the one who is identified with the anxiety.

This does not mean the anxiety disappears. It means the anxiety loses its totalizing power. The anxiety is something happening. It is not the totality of what is happening. There is the anxiety and there is also the breath. There is the anxiety and there is also the body sitting in the chair. There is the anxiety and there is also the awareness that is aware of all of it.

Lectio divina creates a different kind of space. You read Scripture slowly. You read a short passage multiple times. You allow words and phrases to catch your attention. You sit with a phrase. You allow God to speak to you through it. You are not trying to understand the passage. You are not trying to extract a lesson. You are just being present to the text and allowing it to work on you.

One person described an experience of sitting with the phrase "Do not be afraid." She had been diagnosed with anxiety. She was terrified. Everything in the world seemed dangerous. She could not leave her house. She was sitting with the disciples in the boat during the storm. Jesus is asleep. The disciples are panicking. And Jesus wakes up and says: Do not be afraid. You have so little faith. Why are you so afraid?

The person was not looking for comfort or reassurance. She was just sitting with the words. Do not be afraid. And she realized that Jesus is not saying the fear is irrational. Jesus is not saying they should not be feeling what they are feeling. Jesus is saying do not let the fear be your final word. Do not let the fear define what is possible. There is something larger than the fear. There is faith. There is trust. There is the presence of Jesus in the boat. And the fear is real but it is not the whole story.

The prayer did not remove the fear. But it changed the context. It gave the fear a place to exist. It allowed the fear to exist alongside other things. The fear and also the faith. The fear and also the presence of Jesus. The fear and also the awareness that she was not defined by the fear.

Prayer as weapon says: fear is the enemy. Get rid of it. Use the power of prayer to remove it.

Prayer as presence says: fear is real. Your fear is valid. Your fear is being experienced right now and that is okay. I am here with you in your fear. God is here with you in your fear. The fear does not have to go away for you to be loved. The fear does not have to disappear for you to be acceptable. The fear is something you are experiencing and you are still a beloved child of God.

This is not the theology that most churches teach about prayer. Most churches teach prayer as the mechanism by which you get what you want from God. The church teaches you to pray boldly. To speak as if you have already received what you are asking for. To declare and decree and command. To use the power of your faith to bend reality toward your will.

The contemplative understanding of prayer is different. The understanding is that prayer aligns your will with God's will. Prayer does not change God's mind. Prayer changes you. Prayer does not get you what you want. Prayer transforms your understanding of what you actually need.

The person with anxiety needs to hear both of these. It is legitimate to pray that God would heal the anxiety. It is legitimate to ask for relief. But it is also necessary to develop a prayer life that does not depend on that relief. A prayer life that is possible even when the relief does not come. A prayer life that is not contingent on the anxiety going away.

Contemplative prayer is this kind of prayer. Prayer that is possible in the middle of the anxiety. Prayer that does not require the anxiety to have disappeared before the prayer can be considered successful. Prayer that is present with the anxiety. Prayer that allows you to be with yourself and with God even when the anxiety is very much present.

The book's insistence that prayer does not fix anxiety is not an insistence that prayer is useless. It is an insistence that prayer has a purpose beyond fixing. Prayer is useful for being. Prayer is useful for being present. Prayer is useful for being with yourself without judgment. Prayer is useful for being with God while you are being honest about what is actually happening.

And this is enough. Not to remove the anxiety. But to change your relationship to the anxiety. Not to fix the problem. But to be with yourself in the middle of the problem. Not to escape what is happening. But to be present to what is happening. And in that presence, something shifts. Not the anxiety necessarily. But you. Your capacity to live inside the anxiety without the anxiety killing you.

**CHAPTER 7**

Medication and Faith Are Not Enemies

A deacon came to me after service on a Sunday with his face arranged in what looked like concern. He pulled me into the office and closed the door. I assumed someone was sick or there was a crisis I needed to know about.

Instead he said, "I heard that your wife started taking antidepressants."

I nodded. That was not news that needed to be shared, but Susanna's openness about her mental health is who she is, and I respect that. I waited for him to say something more.

He did. "We don't believe in that here."

That was four years ago. The deacon is no longer at First Baptist. He moved to another church. I do not think his leaving was entirely about that conversation, but I know it was part of it. The sentence he spoke had revealed something about what he believed faith should look like. And it was not compatible with how he saw us living.

His sentence has lived in my head ever since. Not because it bothered me. But because I realized how many people believe what he believed. And how many people, sitting in pews all over the country, are suffering in silence because they have internalized the same theology.

We don't believe in that here. That sentence is killing people.

Susanna started taking antidepressants after years of carrying a weight that her own discipline could not shift. She prayed. She exercised. She ate well. She got enough sleep. She read Scripture. She had a therapist. None of it moved the depression. And finally, on advice from her therapist and her doctor, she took a medication that changed the chemistry in her brain enough that she could function.

She did not lose her faith when she took that pill. She did not become less spiritual. She did not need more prayer or a deeper commitment to Scripture. What she needed was for her brain to have the chemistry it was missing.

Let me be direct. If you believe that taking medicine for anxiety or depression is a failure of faith, you do not understand either medicine or faith.

A diabetic takes insulin. Their pancreas does not produce the insulin naturally. Medicine supplies what the body cannot make itself. This is not a failure of prayer. It is the proper use of the resources God has provided. Nobody stands outside a diabetic's hospital room and says, "If you really had faith, you would not need insulin." The diabetic is just trying to stay alive. The medicine is keeping them alive. And the medicine is a grace.

Anxiety medication works the same way. The brain chemistry that regulates anxiety can malfunction. Sometimes, prayer and therapy and discipline cannot correct it. Medicine can. Medicine is grace. It is a gift from God that we can alter the chemistry in our own brains well enough to be fully human again.

And yet the church has drawn a line between prayer and medicine that God never drew.

Luke was a physician. Jesus said to the disciples, "It is not the healthy who need a doctor, but the sick." He did not say, "It is not the healthy who need prayer, but the sick." He said the sick need a doctor. Jesus was healing people directly, performing miracles, and yet he still acknowledged that there is a legitimate role for the medical profession in addressing suffering.

The incarnation itself is God's ultimate statement about the material world. God became flesh. God entered matter. God said that the physical world was important enough to be his own vehicle. The body is not the problem. The body is not the enemy. The body is real. And sometimes the body needs medicine.

If you believe that the mind is different, that the brain is different, that mental health is somehow more spiritual than physical health, you are committing a heresy that the church has been trying to move past for five hundred years. You are saying that the material world, and the brain as part of the material world, is outside the scope of God's grace.

It is not.

When I say this from the pulpit, I can see people resist. I can see them thinking about their own unmedicated anxiety. I can see them wrestling with whether medication would be a sign of failed faith. And I have learned to be more direct about what I am saying.

Taking medicine for anxiety is not a failure of faith. It is an expression of it.

It is faith that God is wise enough to have given us the gift of medical science. It is faith that God cares about our suffering enough to provide resources to address it. It is faith that we do not need to earn our healing. It is faith that God meets us not just in the spiritual realm but in the material one.

Susanna taking her antidepressant is not less faith than Susanna praying about her depression. It is different faith. It is faith that says, "I am going to use the resources available to me. I am going to let medicine do what medicine can do. I am going to let therapy do what therapy can do. I am going to let my own discipline and my own choices do what they can do. And I am going to trust that all of these things working together are the grace of God."

That is not a small faith. That is a mature faith.

The false binary — prayer or medicine, faith or treatment, spiritual or material — is killing people. It is killing pastors who are lying awake at 4 AM refusing to take something that would help them sleep because they think it means their faith is weak. It is killing church members who think they should be able to out-pray their anxiety. It is killing young people who have been told that their depression is really a spiritual problem in disguise.

Depression is not a spiritual problem. It is a medical one. Anxiety is not a failure of prayer. It is a malfunction of brain chemistry. And the gospel speaks to both.

The gospel speaks to the malfunction directly. We have been given medicine. We have been given the knowledge to understand how the brain works. We have been given the wisdom to address chemical imbalances. And we have been given the permission to use these gifts without shame.

I started taking anti-anxiety medication about five years ago. I preached for years without it. I managed my anxiety through prayer and discipline. I ran five miles a day. I meditated. I journaled. And I still woke up at 3 AM with my heart racing and my mind spinning through catastrophes that had not happened.

I did not want to take the medication. It felt like failure. It felt like my faith should have been enough. It felt like admitting that I could not manage my own mind. And then Susanna looked at me one morning and said, "You can try this and find it does not work. Or you can keep doing what you are doing and find it does not work. Why not try the medicine?"

She was right. I tried the medicine. And something shifted. Not everything. The medication does not make me relaxed. It does not remove my concern for the church or my responsibility to do the work. It does not make me detached from the real difficulties that I carry. What it does is lower the volume. It lets me think clearly when I would otherwise be in a spiral. It lets me sleep when I would otherwise be trapped in the racing thoughts.

It is not a cure. It is a tool. And it is a grace.

When I tell people in the congregation that I take medication for anxiety, I watch them move from shame to relief. They realize they are not alone. They realize they are not the only one whose faith did not fix everything. They realize that there is a way forward that does not depend on their faith being stronger.

That is the thing the enemy of our souls wants us to believe. That if we just had more faith, if we just prayed harder, if we just believed more deeply, we would be well. And if we are not well, it is because our faith is insufficient. This is a lie. And it is a lie that kills.

The truth is something harder and simpler at once. The truth is that we are broken in multiple ways. We are broken spiritually, and we need grace and faith and the cross. We are broken emotionally, and we need therapy and community and the space to feel what we feel. We are broken chemically, and we need medicine. We are broken relationally, and we need reconciliation and forgiveness. We are broken vocationally, and sometimes we need to make different choices about how we spend our time.

The gospel addresses all of these. Not by removing the brokenness, but by meeting us in it and transforming us through it.

When Susanna started taking her antidepressant, she did not become less of a Christian. If anything, she became more of one. She became someone who could be present with her children in a way she could not be present before. She became someone who could serve in the church without burning out. She became someone who could actually enjoy her life instead of managing it from a place of heaviness.

That is what faith produces. Not suffering. Not noble endurance. Not the virtue of being broken. Faith produces freedom. And sometimes that freedom comes in a pill.

The deacon who said "we don't believe in that here" was not protecting the faith. He was protecting a version of Christianity that requires us to suffer more than God ever asked us to. He was protecting an idea of faith that makes our pain noble and our refusal to address the pain a sign of spiritual maturity.

That is not the gospel. That is abuse dressed in the language of faith.

The gospel is the news that Jesus died so that we could be free. Free from sin. Free from shame. Free from the burden of earning our own goodness. Free from the requirement to suffer unnecessarily. Free to use the gifts we have been given. Free to take the medicine. Free to go to therapy. Free to do the work of becoming whole, knowing that wholeness is not the same as perfection but is worth pursuing anyway.

When I preach about this, I see people nod. But I also see people who do not nod. I see the ones who came to faith in a place where medication was seen as a failure, and who are still trying to unlearn that even though they know intellectually it is not true.

To those people I want to say this clearly. Your anxiety is real. Your depression is real. Your brain's chemistry matters. And you are not failing God by getting help. You are honoring the body God gave you by trying to care for it. You are honoring the mind God gave you by treating it like the real thing it is.

Take the pill. Go to therapy. Change your diet. Exercise. Pray. Do all of these things. Not in the hope that one of them will fix everything. But in the hope that all of them together will move you toward wholeness. Toward the life you are supposed to live. Toward the freedom that the gospel actually promises.

That is what faith looks like in a body. In a mind. In a world where medicine is available and grace is also real.

**The Chemistry of Mercy: Why Your Brain Sometimes Needs Help**

There is a particular kind of silence that happens in the pastoral office when you recommend medication. The person sits across from you—they came because they are drowning, because the weight has become unbearable—and you name the option that might actually save their life. And then they go quiet. Not a thoughtful quiet. A shuttered quiet. The kind of quiet that says: I thought you understood. I thought you, of all people, would tell me to just believe harder.

I have been that pastor. I have also been the person in the chair receiving the recommendation. Both positions teach you something about the distance between what we believe about God and what we believe is permitted.

The brain is made of chemistry. That is not poetry. That is not metaphor. That is neuroscience. When your serotonin levels drop below a certain threshold, your brain cannot generate the neurochemical substrate necessary for hope. It is not that you have weak faith. It is that the physical infrastructure for certain emotions has become unreliable. A broken femur does not respond to prayer—it responds to a cast, to time, to the healing process God built into bone itself. A broken serotonin system does not respond to willpower. It responds to medication that restores the chemical balance the body needs to think, to feel, to hope.

SSRIs—selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors—work by preventing the reabsorption of serotonin at the synaptic junction. When your serotonin is depleted or your receptors are dysregulated, serotonin molecules are recycled too quickly. The reuptake inhibitor leaves more serotonin in the synapse longer. Your brain has more of what it needs. The result is not artificial happiness. It is the restoration of capacity. The ability to think past today. The ability to imagine a tomorrow that is not just more of this weight.

What changed in my life when I understood this was not the neuroscience itself—it was the theology. I realized that recommending medication was not competing with faith. It was an expression of faith. God made the brain. God made the chemicals. God made the doctors who understand how both work. When I recommend medication, I am not saying God is not enough. I am saying that God is so generous, so committed to human flourishing, that He embedded healing into the very chemistry of creation. The Christian doctor who prescribes an SSRI is participating in what theologians call common grace—the outpouring of God's goodness on all people, believers and non-believers alike. That doctor is doing what Jesus did when He healed the sick. The tools are different. The healing is the same.

I met with a man—I will call him David—who came to me in his mid-forties carrying fifteen years of secret depression. For fifteen years, he had white-knuckled his way through it because he was told, repeatedly, that asking for medication would be asking for a shortcut that God did not intend. He had prayed. He had repented. He had submitted to leadership, to accountability, to every spiritual discipline his church could name. The depression did not lift. What lifted instead was everything else.

His marriage had become a civil arrangement. His children stopped coming to him with their problems because they could not bear his heaviness. His work suffered. He missed promotions. He withdrew from friendships because he had no energy to pretend he was okay. He sat in church every Sunday singing about joy while internally convinced that his joy was being withheld by God as a punishment for not having enough faith.

Fifteen years of that. Fifteen years of watching his life narrow instead of expand. Fifteen years of believing the lie that trusting God meant refusing the tool that could have saved him years of suffering.

When he finally came to me, he was not proud. He was not seeking vindication. He was desperate. We talked about what depression actually is—not a character flaw, not a spiritual failure, but a medical condition. We talked about medication not as a replacement for faith but as an extension of it. We talked about his brain the way we would talk about a thyroid that had stopped producing adequate hormone. You do not shame someone whose thyroid fails. You do not tell them to pray harder. You get them to an endocrinologist. The same wisdom applies here.

He started medication. Within six weeks, he had enough neurochemical capacity to remember what hope felt like. Within three months, he could be present with his wife in a way he had not been present in fifteen years. Within six months, his children began to trust him again. Within a year, he was mentoring younger men in his church—something he had not had the emotional bandwidth to do since his thirties.

I asked him once what he wished someone had told him earlier. He said: "I wish someone had told me that taking care of my brain was not the same as taking care of my faith. That I could do both. That God was not punishing me for needing help. That the help itself was from God."

That is the pastoral truth buried inside the neurochemistry. Your brain is a gift. Its chemistry is a gift. The medicines that restore that chemistry are a gift. The doctor who prescribes them is a gift. The willingness to receive the help is not weakness. It is wisdom. It is the kind of wisdom that says: I love my life enough to fight for it. I trust God enough to use the tools He has provided.

There is a particular kind of courage in that moment when you decide to say yes to medication despite everything you have been told about what that means. You are choosing to believe that God is good—that His goodness includes the chemical substrate of hope. You are choosing to believe that healing is not a test of your faith but an expression of God's faithfulness. You are choosing to live instead of endure.

I have watched hundreds of people make that choice. And I have watched the same pattern every time: the shame lifts first, then the fog begins to clear, then the capacity returns. Not because they prayed harder or believed better. But because they trusted the God who made bodies and brains and doctors.

That is common grace. That is enough.

**CHAPTER 8**

The Theology of Lament

Psalm 88 is the Bible's most honest song.

It does not resolve. It does not end with comfort. It does not offer hope or closure or the assurance that things will eventually get better. It ends like this:

"You have taken from me friend and neighbor. Darkness is my closest friend."

That is where it stops. That is how the prayer concludes. The psalmist has been crying out to God through the entire song. He has described his suffering in excruciating detail. He has reminded God of his faithfulness. He has asked why God is hiding his face. He has named the waters of death and the pit and the fact that God has caused his loved ones to abandon him. And at the end, after all of that, God has not answered. The suffering has not lifted. The darkness remains. And so the song simply ends. The psalmist sits in the dark with his closest friend — the darkness itself.

The Bible includes that song. That is extraordinary.

One-third of the Psalms are laments. Complaints. Accusations. Demands. Grief poured out without filter. The Psalms do not say the anger at God is wrong. They do not call it sin to speak hard things to God. They do not suggest that if your faith is strong enough, you will not need to lament. They simply give language to the people of God for what to do when the world breaks and God does not seem to be fixing it.

And yet the modern church has edited lament out almost entirely.

We sing victory songs on Sunday while the congregation is drowning. We offer platitudes about God's goodness while people are experiencing God's hiddenness. We talk about faith, faith, faith, while the only honest word is grief, grief, grief. And people sitting in the pews are learning that their real emotional experience is unwelcome in this space. They are learning that if they cannot sing the happy song, there is no song for them here.

That is a catastrophe. And it has happened so slowly that we do not notice how much we have betrayed the honest voice.

I was in a worship service at a large church a few months ago. Excellent music. Professional production. The songs were well-crafted and skillfully performed. But every single song was a victory song. Every song was about triumph and breakthrough and what God is doing and what God will do. There was no song for the person who just got news of a terminal diagnosis. No song for the marriage that is ending. No song for the faith that is collapsing. No song for the person sitting in the congregation wondering if God is real or if God even cares.

There was no Psalm 88.

When the service ended, a woman came up to me and said, "I wanted to cry. But there was no permission to cry in this place." She was exactly right. The architecture of the worship service had made it clear that the appropriate emotional register was up. Victory. Triumph. God's goodness is evident. God is moving. And if you cannot access that emotional place, you are invited to suppress what you actually feel and fake it until you believe it again.

That is not worship. That is emotional manipulation.

Real worship is the space where you can tell God the truth about what is happening. Where you can name the pain without being corrected. Where you can ask God why without being told you lack faith. Where you can sit in grief and have that grief be met, not with answers, but with presence.

The Black church understood this in a way that most of white American Christianity never has. The tradition of spirituals — "Nobody Knows the Trouble I've Seen," "Sometimes I Feel Like a Motherless Child," "Swing Low Sweet Chariot" — these were not triumph songs. These were lament songs. These were songs born in the incomprehensible suffering of slavery. Songs that named the depth of pain without resolving it. Songs that looked toward heaven not with certainty but with desperate hope. Songs that said, "God, this is unbearable. And I am going to sing about it anyway, because singing is the only way I know to survive."

That is theology. That is faith. Not the faith that believes everything will work out. But the faith that continues to speak to God even when everything is falling apart.

When I preach about anxiety, I need to be able to preach about lament. Because anxiety is often the place where lament needs to happen. Anxiety is the place where the psalmist says, "My soul is full of troubles, and my life draws near to Sheol." It is the place where the standard Christian answers are not enough. It is the place where you need permission to be broken.

And the church has not given that permission.

A few years ago, I decided we needed to sing a lament song at First Baptist. I chose "Lamb of God," but not the triumphant version. I chose it in a minor key. I told the congregation that we were going to sing a song about suffering, about grief, about the weight of the cross. And as we sang, I watched people's faces change. I watched people allow themselves to feel something they had not felt in worship in a long time. Permission. The permission to grieve. The permission to name what was actually happening inside them.

After the service, multiple people came to me with tears. "Thank you," they said, "for letting me cry."

That sentence should break any pastor's heart. That people have to be given permission to grieve in the house of God. That our worship has become so sanitized, so carefully curated, that there is no room for the actual human experience.

Lament is not despair. This is important. Despair is the belief that God is not present and will never be present. Lament is the belief that God is present but is inexplicably withholding himself. Lament says, "You are here. I know you are here. And I do not understand why you are not acting." That is still faith. That is faith talking to God even in the dark.

When you read the Psalms, you see lament after lament that ends not with resolution but with a turn toward God. The psalmist does not get the answer he wants. But he gets the presence he needs. He says, "I will praise the Lord," not because his circumstances have changed, but because he has chosen to trust the God who is present in his circumstances.

That is the form that real faith takes when the world is breaking.

In the congregation, I have people who are living in lament. There is a woman whose daughter was killed by a drunk driver fifteen years ago, and who still comes to church every Sunday even though her faith in God is fragile. There is a man whose wife left him and who keeps showing up even though he does not know if he believes anymore. There is a young person whose anxiety is so severe that sometimes they cannot leave the house, and they are trying to hold onto faith while their own brain is betraying them.

These people do not need another victory song. They need someone to sit with them in the grief and say, "This is unbearable. And I am here. We are here. God is here, even if you cannot feel it."

They need the Psalms.

The fact that the church has abandoned lament is connected to the fact that the church has abandoned anxiety as a legitimate space where God works. Because lament and anxiety are connected. Anxiety is often the voice of lament in the body. It is the body saying what the mind cannot speak. It is the nervous system naming the fact that something is wrong and that the normal structures are not holding.

When we suppress lament, we suppress the body's way of processing grief. We force the grief underground. And it comes up as anxiety, as depression, as the diseases of the soul that have nowhere else to go.

If we brought lament back into the church, if we made room for the honest voice, for the hard questions, for the grief without resolution, I think we would see a different kind of anxiety. Not less anxiety. But less the shame around the anxiety. More the permission to name it. More the community holding it together. More the understanding that this is part of what it means to live in a broken world while believing in a God who is good.

Mother Teresa, the one we talked about before, experienced lament for decades. She was doing the work of the gospel in one of the most difficult places on earth, and she was doing it without feeling loved by God. That was lament. That was grief. That was the dark psalms made flesh.

And the church needs more Theresa's, not less. The church needs people who can do the work while the work feels meaningless. People who can love God while the love is not returned (or seems not to be returned). People who can sit with the broken and say, "I have been here too. I know this place."

That is the minister who knows the Psalms.

I have started being more intentional about lament in our worship at First Baptist. I have started leaving room for silence. Space where you can just sit with your grief. I have brought back some of the older hymns that name suffering without quickly resolving it. And I have stopped pretending that the pastor's job is to make people feel better on Sunday. Sometimes the pastor's job is to make room for people to feel worse. To let the grief have its voice. To let the anxiety be named instead of fixed.

Psalm 88 ends in darkness. It does not say, "And then everything got better." It does not say, "God came through." It does not resolve. It simply stops, with the psalmist sitting in the dark with darkness as his closest friend. And the fact that the Bible includes that song says something essential. It says that God is big enough to handle our honest speech. It says that faith does not require us to pretend everything is okay. It says that sometimes the most faithful thing we can do is name the truth about what we are experiencing.

That is where lament lives. That is where anxiety becomes prayer. That is where the real work of faith begins — not in the removal of suffering, but in the decision to speak the suffering to God and trust that God is listening even when we cannot hear him responding.

The darkness is not the last word. But sometimes it has to be the word we speak first.

**The Psalms Teach Us to Break: Lament as the Honest Language of Faith**

Walter Brueggemann describes the Psalter as moving through three distinct orientations: orientation, disorientation, and new orientation. Most of our Christian life is lived in what Brueggemann calls "orientation"—the time when the world makes sense, God seems present, and the faith narrative holds together. We sing about it on Sunday mornings. We preach about it. We teach our children about it. Everything is in its proper place. God is good. Life is manageable. Faith works.

Then something breaks. Orientation collapses. You enter disorientation—the place where things fall apart, where God is silent, where the faith narrative no longer explains reality. This is the place where you are not sure if you believe anymore. This is the place where your prayers sound like shouting into a void. This is the place where you ask God questions you never thought you would ask, and His silence is louder than any answer.

The church wants to move past this as quickly as possible. We spend orientation singing hymns of confidence. We spend disorientation trying to skip to new orientation—to the sermon that will fix this, the prayer that will break through, the small group conversation that will restore the narrative. We are terrified of staying in disorientation. We treat it like a malfunction instead of recognizing it as essential spiritual territory.

But the Psalms spend the most time in disorientation. If you read the Psalter in order, you will notice this pattern: orientation Psalms are beautiful. New orientation Psalms are triumphant. Disorientation Psalms are raw, angry, confused, desperate, sometimes even bitter. They are also the most numerous. The Psalms teach us that faith does not live primarily in confidence. Faith lives in the courage to tell God the truth about what you are experiencing when the world no longer makes sense.

> *"My God, my God, why have you forsaken me? Why are you so far from saving me, so far from my cries of anguish?" That is Psalm 22. That is not metaphorical despair. That is theological language for the experience of God's absence. And God inspired it. God permitted it. God included it in Scripture. That changes everything about what prayer is permitted to sound like.*

The disorientation Psalms are permission. Permission to be angry at God without censoring yourself. Permission to ask Him why without expecting an answer. Permission to sit in the dark without rushing toward false light. Permission to say, "I do not understand what is happening, and I do not trust that You are good in this moment, but I am telling You anyway because that is what honesty looks like before God."

In my own pastoral journey, I spent years in what Brueggemann calls orientation. I had a faith that worked. I understood the narrative. I could preach it with confidence. I knew the answers. Then life intervened. Not catastrophically all at once, but slowly—the accumulation of counsel I gave that did not work, the prayers that seemed to fall flat, the people I could not help, the systems I thought I understood that turned out to be more complex than I had accounted for. I entered a long disorientation. Not crisis of faith exactly, but a steady recognition that I had been living in a version of Christianity that was smaller than it needed to be.

During that stretch, I began writing. Not sermons. Not polished reflections for publication. Raw, unfiltered prayers written to God in the language of complaint and confusion. I wrote Psalms. Not for anyone else. For the gap between my broken theology and whatever truth might lie on the other side of it.

Here is one of them, written during a particularly dark period when everything seemed to be falling apart at once—personal failure, pastoral failure, the weight of leadership when I did not feel equipped for it:

\*God, I do not know what I am doing. I preach to five hundred people every Sunday about Your goodness and I am not sure I believe it. I have built a church on a foundation that is cracking and I do not have the courage to tell anyone. I made promises to people I cannot keep. I set expectations I cannot meet. I am tired of performing confidence. I am tired of knowing the answers. I am tired of being the one who is supposed to be okay.\*

\*You are supposed to be here. You are supposed to show up when Your servant is broken. But I am broken and You are quiet and I do not know if the quiet is Your presence or Your absence or if there is any difference anymore.\*

\*I have five sons and I do not know how to raise them in a world like this. I have a wife who believes in me and I am going to disappoint her. I have a church that trusts me and I do not trust myself. And You—You are supposed to be the stability underneath all of this and instead You feel like silence.\*

\*So I am telling You: I am failing. I am afraid. I do not think this works. I do not know if I believe anymore. Not in a dramatic way. In a quiet way. The kind of unbelief that shows up not in rejecting You but in being unable to feel You.\*

\*And I am telling You because it is the only honest thing I have left.\*

That is lament. That is not sophisticated theology. That is not inspirational content. That is a man standing before God and refusing to pretend anymore.

What happened after I wrote that—what happens when you enter disorientation with honesty instead of resistance—is not that God answered. It is that I found my footing. Not in answers. In the terrain itself. Brueggemann calls the movement from disorientation to new orientation "reorientation." You do not return to the old orientation—to the simple faith you had before. You move forward into a new one, deeper, more resilient, more honest, forged in the place where you stopped pretending and started telling the truth.

The practice of lament is not complicated. It is this: sit down and write. Write to God. Write your anger. Write your confusion. Write the questions you have been too afraid to ask because you thought they disqualified you from being Christian. Write your doubt. Write your exhaustion. Write the thing you are most afraid to say out loud. Write it as if God is listening—which He is. Write it as if your honesty matters—which it does.

Do not write to be inspirational. Do not write to demonstrate your faith. Write to be true. The Psalms teach us that this is the work of prayer. Not performing confidence before God. Standing before God and saying what is actually real.

When you do this, something shifts. The weight does not necessarily lift, but you stop carrying it alone. God enters the disorientation with you. Not to fix it immediately, but to be present in it. To say, through the permission of the Psalms, through the witness of Christ in the desert crying out "My God, my God, why have you forsaken me?"—I know this place. I have been here. You are not abandoned. You are honest. And honesty is where resurrection begins.

**CHAPTER 9**

The Myth of the Untroubled Life

A woman sat across from me in my office at FBC Fenton — thirty-eight years old, small group leader, worship team volunteer, two teenage daughters, a marriage that looked from the outside like it was working. She had come because her anxiety had become what she called "the thing I can't hide anymore." For three years, she said, she had been managing panic attacks in the parking lot before worship. Not after. Before. She would sit in her car, hands shaking, breathing through the bag she kept in the glove compartment, then walk in and sing.

> *"I don't understand," she said, and her voice had the sound of someone who had been asking this question alone for a very long time. "I pray. I read Scripture. I go to a good church. I have a good life. So why do I feel like I'm drowning?"*

The question itself revealed the disease. She had absorbed a particular version of Christianity that had become, in American evangelicalism, almost doctrinal: faith produces peace. Jesus promised peace. Therefore, if you have faith, you should have peace. And if you don't have peace, then either your faith is defective or you are.

This is the prosperity gospel dressed in mental health language. And it has a body count.

The prosperity gospel is usually identified with the loudest voices — the ones teaching that God wants you rich, that sickness is a sign of insufficient faith, that your car and your house and your bank account are measures of your spiritual condition. But the prosperity gospel has a quieter cousin, equally lethal: the belief that emotional and mental health are products of sufficient faith. That anxiety is a sign of weak belief. That depression is evidence of hidden sin. That the faithful Christian lives in a kind of emotional equilibrium, un-bothered by the tremors that shake everyone else.

This is not the gospel. This is a lie with consequences that extend far beyond Sunday morning.

When you tell someone that faith produces peace and they don't have peace, you have created a theological problem for them. They have two choices. The first is to conclude that their faith is defective. They are not praying the right way. They are not believing the right way. They have not surrendered enough. So they perform. They pretend. They learn to smile in small group while their hands shake in the car. They become experts at the choreography of faith — the right words, the right references, the right emotional register — while the actual thing they are experiencing remains locked in a room they have learned to keep closed. Over time, the gap between the performance and the reality becomes a kind of slow death. They die while standing in the middle of their church family, and no one knows they are dying because they have learned to hold the weight completely alone.

The second choice is to walk away. If this is what faith looks like, they conclude, then faith is not for me. I thought I was doing this right. I thought I believed. But I am still anxious. I am still afraid. I am still broken. So either I was never really a Christian, or Christianity doesn't work. Many choose to leave. I have sat with people as they have made that choice, and the devastation is not primarily spiritual. It is relational. They don't just lose faith. They lose community. They lose the people they thought knew them. They discover they were known only as long as they performed correctly.

The prosperity gospel — in all its forms — creates a system where the suffering person becomes the failure. Not because they are actually failing. But because the system cannot accommodate their suffering without the system itself being exposed as false.

But look at Scripture. Look at who God chose and what He chose them for.

Job. Called by Satan himself the most faithful man on earth — "there is no one on earth like him" — and God permits everything to be taken. His children die. His health fails. His wealth vanishes. His body becomes a horror to look at. His wife tells him to curse God and die. His friends, who should have stood with him, instead spend the book explaining why he must have sinned. Job himself cannot understand it. And God, when He finally speaks, does not explain it. He simply appears in the whirlwind and asks questions that make it clear: your theology is too small. My ways are not reducible to your categories of suffering and sin. Job lived the entire book in anxiety and bewilderment. And God called him faithful.

Jeremiah. God calls him as a prophet and tells him, "Do not be afraid of their faces, for I am with you to deliver you." Then God spends the next forty years destroying Jeremiah's life. He tells him not to marry because the city will be destroyed and his family will die in famine. He tells him to wear a yoke as a sign. He is beaten by priests. He is thrown into a cistern. He is left alone in exile. And throughout the book, Jeremiah complains. He argues with God. He says, "Why is my pain unceasing, and my wound incurable?" He is angry. He is despairing. He wonders if God is deceiving him. And yet God continues to call him prophet. Jeremiah lived in anxiety and doubt. And God called him faithful.

Paul. Listen to what he says about his own life: "Five times I received from the Jews the forty lashes minus one. Three times I was beaten with rods; once I was pelted with stones; three times I was shipwrecked, I spent a night and a day in the open sea; I have been constantly on the move. I have been in danger from rivers, in danger from bandits, in danger from my own countrymen, in danger from pagans; in danger in the city, in danger in the country, in danger at sea; and in danger from false believers. I have labored and toiled and have often gone without sleep; I have known hunger and thirst and have often gone hungry; I have been cold and naked. Besides everything else, I face daily the pressure of my concern for all the churches."

He is beaten. He is shipwrecked. He is hungry. He is afraid. He is anxious about the people he loves. This is the man who wrote about peace that surpasses understanding. Not because he lived without anxiety. But because he lived with it, and found that God was present in it.

And then there is Jesus. The night before His execution, He goes to Gethsemane. And what happens there is not peace. The text says His soul was overwhelmed with sorrow to the point of death. Matthew says He fell with His face to the ground and prayed. Luke says His sweat became like drops of blood falling to the ground. This is not the picture of spiritual wellness. This is the picture of someone experiencing what we would now call a severe anxiety response. He is asking God to take the cup away. He is asking if there is another way. He is in such distress that his body is failing him.

And the Father's response? The Father sends an angel to strengthen him. Not to remove the cup. Not to explain it. Not to promise that everything will work out fine. Just to strengthen him for what comes next. Even Jesus, on the night He entered the deepest darkness of His mission, experienced something that looks indistinguishable from anxiety.

If the Son of God, who knew no sin, who was perfectly aligned with the Father's will, who had nothing to fear because death had no claim on Him — if Jesus experienced what the prosperity gospel would diagnose as a failure of faith, then the prosperity gospel is not the gospel.

The damage of this lie is almost incalculable. It takes people who are already suffering and adds shame on top of the suffering. It turns the mind against itself. It creates a system where admitting struggle becomes evidence of spiritual failure. It locks people into isolation at the moment they most need community. And worst of all, it lies about God. It says God requires you to carry the weight by yourself, that asking for help is a sign that your belief is insufficient, that peace comes through performance rather than through presence.

The American roots of prosperity thinking run deeper than most people realize. It is not primarily about money. Money is the most obvious expression, but the underlying theology is that God rewards faith with comfort. In the early twentieth century, a man named E.W. Kenyon — operating from Christian Science influenced thinking more than Christian theology — developed the idea of "Word of Faith," the belief that speaking things into existence through positive declaration is a form of spiritual power. Kenneth Hagin built an empire on this. The movement exploded in American culture at exactly the moment when American capitalism was ascending. And something happened: the gospel got married to the American Dream. God wants you blessed, which looks like the American version of blessing. God wants you comfortable, healthy, prosperous, un-troubled. Faith is the mechanism by which you access these goods.

It is a gospel tailor-made for a culture that has been taught that comfort is the highest good. And it is a lie.

The gospel does not promise an untroubled life. It promises something entirely different. It promises that you will never be alone in the trouble. It promises that the weight — whatever weight you carry — is not a sign that God has abandoned you. It promises that God has already entered the weight, absorbed it, transformed it through the cross. It promises that resurrection comes on the other side of death, not instead of it.

The woman in my office that afternoon did not need to be told that her faith was defective. She needed to know that her faith was surviving something faith was never meant to survive alone. She needed to know that experiencing anxiety did not make her a bad Christian. It made her human. And it made her a candidate for the actual gospel — not the prosperity version, but the one where God comes to find you in the darkness rather than requiring you to smile through it.

I told her what I wish someone had told me years ago: the peace Jesus promised is not the absence of trouble. It is His presence in the trouble. And that presence does not remove the anxiety. But it changes what the anxiety means. It is no longer proof that you are failing. It is simply the weight you carry, and you are not carrying it alone.

**The Cost of a False Gospel: When Prosperity Theology Kills**

I sat with a man whose daughter stopped taking her antidepressant medication. She had been on it for three years—it had worked, had given her life back to her, had made her functional and hopeful and present with her family again. Then she attended a conference at a church plant in a neighboring city. The speaker's message was simple and clear: God wants you whole. God does not want you dependent. Faith means refusing the medication and trusting God for your healing.

She believed him. She flushed her medication. Within six weeks, she was suicidal. Within ten weeks, she had made an attempt that nearly killed her. She survived because her roommate found her. She spent two weeks in a psychiatric hospital. She is alive because of the medical system that the pastor had taught her to distrust.

Her father sat in my office and his hands were shaking. He said: "How do we tell her that the pastor was wrong without destroying her faith?" What he meant was: how do we teach her that sometimes the people who speak for God are actually speaking against Him?

The prosperity gospel—the teaching that faith produces wealth, health, and comfort—is not just theologically wrong. It is pastorally lethal. It kills people. I do not say that as metaphor. I say it because I have sat with the families it has destroyed.

The prosperity gospel says: If you are sick, you lack faith. If you are poor, you lack faith. If you are depressed, you lack faith. This has a seductive logic because it places you entirely in control. Your circumstances are your fault. Your healing is your responsibility. You need to believe hard enough, give generously enough, confess positively enough, and God will deliver.

What it does in practice is this: It shames the already suffering. It tells them their suffering is evidence of their spiritual failure. It turns the church into a place where you cannot be honest about your pain because the community will interpret your pain as a sign of weak faith. The result is isolation. Secrecy. The slow death that comes from believing you are uniquely cursed by God.

I have watched people stop taking medications that were literally keeping them alive because a preacher told them to claim their healing. I have watched them suffer in silence because admitting the medication was working would mean admitting they were failing at faith. I have watched that silence deepen into depression, and that depression become so unbearable that they started planning their exit from life itself.

Last year, I learned that a young pastor in another denomination had died by suicide. He had struggled with depression for years. His church had taught him that depression was a demon to be cast out, a sin to be repented of, a sign of insufficient faith. He did not take medication because medication would have been admitting defeat. He tried spiritual warfare. He confessed his sins. He prayed. He fasted. He did everything the prosperity gospel told him to do.

None of it worked because none of it addressed the actual problem, which was neurochemistry, not demonic oppression or spiritual failure. The depression deepened. The church's solution became less helpful and more condemning. Eventually, he could not bear the weight of both the depression and the shame of being unable to overcome it through faith.

He left a note that said: "I am sorry I could not be the pastor you believed I was." The prosperity gospel had convinced him that his illness was his shame. When he could not heal himself through faith, the only solution left was to remove the problem by removing himself.

That is what happens when the gospel becomes transactional, when faith becomes a tool for controlling your circumstances instead of a posture of surrender, when the church becomes a place where only victory is permitted and suffering is evidence of failure.

The biblical narrative tells a different story. The suffering servant in Isaiah 53 is not healed by his faith. He is healed through his suffering. He bears our griefs and carries our sorrows. That is not prosperity. That is the deepest act of love possible—substitution, solidarity, bearing what another cannot bear. Christ does not exempt His followers from suffering. He joins us in it. He promises not that we will be comfortable but that we will not be alone.

The first Christians expected to suffer. Peter writes to churches experiencing persecution: "Dear friends, do not be surprised at the painful trial you are going through, as though something strange were happening to you." Not: if you have enough faith, you will avoid suffering. But: suffering is normative. It is part of what it means to follow Christ. The apostles rejoiced that they were counted worthy to suffer for His name.

Christianity was not born in prosperity. It was born in execution. The first Christians were hunted. They gathered in hiding. They faced torture. They were told they could be free if they denied Christ and made the appropriate offerings to Caesar's gods. Most of them refused. Not because they had found a secret to comfort. But because they had found something worth suffering for.

The prosperity gospel is distinctly an American invention. It arrives in the historical moment when Christianity has become respectable, when the church has merged with culture, when comfort is available and faith has been domesticated into a tool for acquiring it. The message has always been: believe hard enough and God will give you what you want. What America wanted was wealth, health, and the absence of suffering. So the gospel was rewritten to promise exactly that.

But that is not the gospel. That is a bargain. That is the gospel of Cain, who believed faith should produce visible, measurable results. God rejected that offering. God accepted Abel's offering—the costly one, the one that cost him everything, the one that did not promise comfort but promised presence.

The real gospel does not promise that your circumstances will improve. It promises that God will be present in the midst of them. It promises that suffering has been entered into by God Himself. It promises that death has been conquered not by avoiding it but by walking through it and emerging on the other side. It promises resurrection—not the return to comfortable circumstances but the transformation that happens when you have walked through the valley of the shadow and discovered that even there, you are not abandoned.

I have watched people find that gospel. I have watched them stop listening to the prosperity preachers and start listening to the Psalms, to the suffering servant, to the crucified Christ. And I have watched their faith become deeper, more resilient, more real. Not because their circumstances improved. But because they stopped waiting for circumstances to improve and started believing that God could be trusted even if they never did.

That is the gospel. That is costly. That is real. And it does not kill. It saves.

The prosperity gospel has a body count. Not metaphorically. Literally.

There is a woman who came to my office after her psychiatrist referred her. She had been diagnosed with bipolar disorder—a legitimate neurological condition where the brain's chemistry fluctuates in ways that produce periods of mania and periods of severe depression. Her psychiatrist prescribed a mood stabilizer. A standard medication for bipolar disorder. It works. The medicine was helping her. But her church found out and the response was immediate. The pastor called her. The pastoral team called her. They told her that if she had enough faith, she would not need medication. They told her that bipolar disorder was the result of either demonic oppression or unconfessed sin. They told her that taking the medication was a betrayal of God's ability to heal her.

So she stopped taking the medication. She wanted to honor God. She wanted to prove that her faith was real. She went off the mood stabilizer. Within three months, she was in a manic episode so severe that she attempted suicide. She survived. But she was only alive because she called 911 at the last moment. She is alive because an emergency room doctor did not believe that prayer was an adequate substitute for psychiatric care.

There is a man in his fifties who stopped paying for health insurance because his church taught him that God would provide. He worked for himself. He made enough money. But he redirected his health insurance premium toward his tithe. His church needed the money more than he needed protection against catastrophic health events, he reasoned. The logic made sense inside the prosperity gospel system. Six months later, he had a stroke. Catastrophic, disabling. He survived. But the medical bills bankrupted him. The church did not pay his bills. They prayed for him. He lost his house. He lost his business. He lost the physical capacity to work. And his church moved on to other prayer requests.

There is a teenager who was struggling with depression. His youth group leader suggested that the depression was the result of insufficient faith. That prayer and Scripture would fix it. The parents, who were faithful church members, wanted to support their son's faith. So they did not pursue psychiatric care. They did not get him medication. They increased the youth group attendance. They added more prayer. They had him memorize Scripture about joy and peace. Six months into this approach, the teenager attempted suicide. He survived because his mother found him. He is alive because an ambulance got him to a hospital and a psychiatrist gave him an SSRI. But if his mother had waited five more minutes, if the ambulance had been slower, if the hospital had been full, he would be dead. And his church would have continued on with their theology intact, their version of faith untouched by the inconvenient fact that their theology killed their son.

These are not edge cases. These are not the work of obviously abusive megachurches with obvious false teachers. These are consequences of mainstream evangelical theology as applied to mental illness. The theology sounds good. It sounds faithful. It sounds like it is honoring God. But it has a body count.

The woman is alive but traumatized by her church's response. She has found a different community. She is back on her medication. She is stable. But she carries the wound of having been told by the body of Christ that her brain illness was a faith problem. That hurt runs deep.

The man is alive but he is disabled and financially destroyed. His church still believes he should have had more faith. They have not reassessed their theology. They have reinterpreted the outcome. He must have had hidden sin that God was punishing, they think. Or God was testing him. Anything but admitting that their theology was inadequate.

The teenager is alive and in therapy and on medication and trying to rebuild his faith after his church nearly killed him. He is twenty-one now. He says he is not sure if he believes in God anymore. Not because of doubt. But because the God his church taught him to believe in would apparently have been happy if he had succeeded at taking his own life.

The prosperity gospel in all its forms operates from the same basic equation: faith produces health. Belief produces comfort. The covenant with God is transactional. You give your faithfulness and God gives you wellness. This equation is deeply embedded in American Christianity. It shows up in the obvious prosperity preaching—God wants you rich. But it shows up just as insidiously in the mental health frame—God wants you psychologically healthy. The message is the same. If you are suffering, you must be doing something wrong.

This is not the gospel. This is a lie with consequences.

Look at what actually happens in Scripture when God shows up to suffering people. God does not generally make the suffering disappear. God shows up inside the suffering. God says "I am with you." God does not offer explanation. God offers presence. When Job asks why, God does not answer. God appears in a whirlwind and asks questions that deconstruct Job's entire theological categories. Job wanted an explanation for his suffering. God gave him a relationship with God himself. Different thing entirely.

When Jeremiah asks God why his life is so painful, God does not fix his life. God keeps calling him to speak truth even though speaking truth is what costs him everything. The calling does not change. The suffering does not decrease. But Jeremiah continues because he has encountered God and that encounter is more real to him than his comfort.

Paul says to the Corinthians that he begged God three times to remove "a thorn in my flesh." We do not know what the thorn is. It could have been physical illness. It could have been depression. It could have been trauma from his beatings. It could have been something else. What matters is that Paul asked God to remove it. He wanted it gone. And God's response was: "My grace is sufficient for you, for my power is made perfect in weakness." God did not remove the thorn. God promised that Paul would not be alone in the thorn. God promised that the thorn would become the place where God's power showed up most clearly.

None of these people—Job, Jeremiah, Paul—were healed through prayer and faith alone. Their suffering was not removed. They were transformed through their suffering. They encountered God more deeply because of it. But that is very different from the prosperity gospel claim that faith prevents suffering or that faith eliminates suffering or that suffering is evidence of insufficient faith.

The prosperity gospel operates with a fundamental misunderstanding of what the gospel actually promises. The gospel does not promise the absence of trouble. The gospel promises that trouble will not be the thing that defines you. The gospel promises presence in the trouble. The gospel promises transformation through the trouble. But the trouble itself is real and it does not automatically disappear when you believe hard enough.

When you apply this to mental illness, what happens is devastating. The person with anxiety or depression or bipolar disorder is carrying a real neurological condition. They need real medical intervention. But the prosperity gospel tells them that the intervention is a sign of insufficient faith. So they choose faith and they die. Or they choose faith and they suffer in isolation because admitting they are struggling feels like admitting they do not really believe.

There is a particular cruelty in this because it masquerades as pastoral care. The pastor is not trying to hurt you. The pastor believes this system. The pastor thinks they are protecting your faith. But what they are actually doing is creating a choice between faith and health. And when the person chooses health—when they take medication, when they go to therapy, when they acknowledge the neurological reality of their condition—the church interprets that choice as spiritual weakness.

This is where the cost becomes incalculable. You have a person who is already suffering from the neurological reality of their condition. And now you have added the spiritual weight of believing their suffering is a moral failure. You have created a system where the person cannot be honest about what they are experiencing because honesty feels like heresy.

I have sat with people who have been in this system for decades. Who have been told repeatedly that their mental illness is a sin. Who have confessed it. Who have repented of it. Who have asked God to remove it. And it is still there. Because it is not a sin. It is not a character failure. It is a neurological condition. And at some point, the person either has to reject their faith or they have to have a breakdown severe enough that they finally, finally go to a doctor.

The ones who have the breakdown and survive often report that it was the best thing that ever happened to them. Not because breakdown is good. But because it was the first time they were forced to face the reality of their condition outside of the grip of shame. They went to a hospital. They saw a doctor. They got medication. And slowly, they began to heal. And in that healing, they discovered something: maybe their faith is not defective. Maybe they were just sick. And sickness and faith can coexist.

But by then, the church has often already been rejected. Not because faith is false but because the church's system for understanding mental illness is false. So the person who needed the church's support is instead finding healing outside the church. And the church stays intact with its prosperity gospel system still unchallenged.

This is how you lose a generation of people who believed in God but discovered that the church's understanding of God was incomplete. Or harmful. Or untrue.

The prosperity gospel is not a theology. It is a lie. And like all lies, it creates a system that protects itself. When someone gets sick despite faith, the system says "there must be hidden sin." When someone gets better on medication, the system says "the medication helped but really it was God." The system cannot be refuted from within because it has built in every possible outcome as evidence of its own correctness.

But we do not have to stay inside that system. We can reject the prosperity gospel system entirely. We can say this: sometimes people get sick. Sometimes the sickness is in the body. Sometimes the sickness is in the mind. Sometimes it is a combination. And the response to that sickness is not prayer alone. It is medicine, therapy, community, prayer, rest, exercise, attention, care. All of it.

When you bring that understanding to the person who is struggling, everything changes. You are no longer asking them to choose between faith and health. You are inviting them into an integrated understanding where faith and health are both valued. Where their suffering is not reframed as a spiritual problem. Where getting help is not a betrayal of God but an acceptance of how God designed us to heal.

This is the understanding that can actually save lives. And it starts with rejecting the system that is currently killing them.

**CHAPTER 10**

Shame and the Spiral

I watched it happen to a man I had known for eight years. He sat in my office on a Wednesday afternoon, and he was unwinding like a spool. The anxiety had gotten worse. The sleep had gotten worse. The drinking had gotten worse. And now his marriage was breaking. He looked at me and said, "I don't know when it started becoming a spiral."

I knew exactly when. Not the specific date, but the moment. It was the moment he stopped being able to talk about it.

Anxiety by itself is a human experience. It is not comfortable, but it is manageable. You have anxiety. You acknowledge it. You find tools — prayer, exercise, talking to someone, sometimes medication. The anxiety is there, but you are not alone with it. The moment another person knows what you are carrying, the weight changes. Not because the anxiety itself has changed. But because you are not holding it alone.

But the Christian who is taught that anxiety is a sign of weak faith cannot take that step. So the spiral begins.

It starts like this: I'm anxious. Then comes the second layer: A good Christian shouldn't be anxious. Then the third: Therefore, I must not be a good Christian. Then the fourth: Which means I must be hiding something. Which means I am ashamed. So now you have two things — the original anxiety plus the shame that anxiety is evidence of failure. And shame hides. Shame isolates. So now the person cannot tell anyone. Cannot ask for help. Cannot be honest in small group or with a friend or even with their pastor.

So they are alone with it.

And here is where the spiral accelerates: the loneliness of the secret creates more anxiety. The more anxious they become, the more ashamed they are. The more ashamed they are, the more carefully they perform. They become experts at looking fine on Sunday. They learn to laugh in a way that convinces people they are okay. They text back quickly enough that no one wonders. They volunteer enough that no one questions their faith. They have created a perfect performance. And it is killing them.

Meanwhile, the church has no idea. This person is walking around with a screaming thing inside them, and everyone around them thinks they are fine. Because they have become very good at pretending. And because we have become very good at not asking hard questions. We see someone smile on Sunday and we accept that. We do not usually press deeper. We do not usually ask the questions that would allow someone to be honest. The system functions as long as everyone performs correctly.

But inside, the person is suffocating. The anxiety is not going away because anxiety does not respond well to isolation. Anxiety responds to the opposite of isolation — to being known, to being safe, to having someone in the room with you who is not shocked by what they find there.

The theology behind this pattern is ancient. It goes back to the beginning, to the first moment someone experienced shame.

Genesis 3. Adam and Eve eat the fruit, and their eyes are opened. But the first thing they experience is not knowledge. It is shame. They are naked and they are ashamed. So what do they do? They hide. They make clothing out of fig leaves. They hide their bodies. When God walks in the garden looking for them, they are hiding in the trees. God asks, "Where are you?" And Adam says, "I was afraid because I was naked; so I hid."

This is the pattern that shame establishes: exposure creates fear creates hiding.

What is striking about this moment is what God does next. He does not scold them for hiding. He does not shame them for their shame. He comes looking. "Where are you?" is not a question God needs answered. He knows where they are. The question is an invitation. It is God saying: I see you hiding and I am coming to find you.

But Adam and Eve hide anyway. And God makes clothing for them — not out of fig leaves, which will dry and disintegrate, but out of skin. The text does not say this explicitly, but the implication is that God kills an animal to make this covering. The first death in Scripture is for the sake of clothing the shame of the ones who sinned.

This pattern echoes through the gospel. Jesus comes and finds people in their hiding. The woman at the well hiding her shame in the anonymity of drawing water at midday. Zacchaeus hiding in the tree. The disciples hiding in locked rooms after the resurrection. Jesus does not force them out. He comes into the hiding place. He says, "I know what you have done and I am still here."

But the anxious Christian in a prosperity gospel church learns a different pattern. They learn: your shame is the evidence that something is wrong with you. So hide. Hide harder. Do not let anyone know. Do not let the pastor know — he will counsel you about insufficient faith. Do not let your small group know — they will tell everyone. Do not let your family know — they will worry. So the pattern becomes complete isolation with shame as the enforcer.

I knew a man who had a severe anxiety disorder. He was a gifted pastor. He was leading a church plant. And he could not tell his church community that he was on medication for anxiety. Not because they would have judged him — they were, by most measures, a good group of people. But he had internalized the message: if I am real about this, the thing I have built will collapse. People will lose faith in my leadership if they know I am struggling with this. So he kept the secret. And the secret ate him alive. He preached about surrender and trust while taking pills in the parking lot before services. He prayed with people about faith while wondering if his faith was defective. He modeled a kind of spiritual strength that was entirely performed.

Until it wasn't. Until the breaking point came, which it always does when you carry that kind of weight alone.

The spiral is psychological but it is also theological. The Christianity that produces it teaches that strength is the opposite of struggle. That faith is incompatible with fear. That the spiritual person has achieved a kind of equilibrium where the normal human difficulties no longer touch them. This is a form of Gnosticism — the belief that the spirit and the body, the soul and the material, can be separated. That there is a version of you that is purely spiritual, untouched by the struggles of the flesh, and that version is your real self.

But the gospel says something entirely different. It says that the material, the emotional, the bodily — all of it matters. That God became flesh. That the Incarnation is not a temporary measure, an embarrassing detour through human weakness before returning to pure spirit. It is God's fundamental statement about what matters. Flesh matters. Blood matters. Tears matter. Fear matters. Because you matter in your totality. Not in some spiritual abstraction, but in your body, your mind, your emotions.

The shame spiral happens because we have forgotten this. We have taught people that the anxious, struggling, doubting version of them is the version they should hide. We have taught them that there is a spiritual version of themselves that should be performing, and a fleshly version that should be suppressed. So they suppress. They hide. And the hiding creates more anxiety, which creates more shame, which creates a tighter hiding.

The only way out is exposure. Not exposure to judgment, but exposure to grace. The anxious person needs to be known. Not fixed, not yet. Just known. They need to say the thing out loud to another person and discover that the other person does not leave. That they are still loved. That the thing they were most ashamed of — the anxiety, the fear, the failure of faith — does not disqualify them. It makes them human.

I had to have this conversation with the man in my office whose marriage was breaking. At some point, he had to stop performing. He had to tell his wife the truth. Not the performance of how he was fine and just taking too much on. The truth: I have been carrying this alone. I have been ashamed of it. I thought admitting it would make me less of a man, less of a Christian, less of a leader. But keeping the secret has made it worse. The anxiety is destroying me, and the shame about the anxiety is destroying us.

And when he finally said it, when he finally stopped performing, something shifted. Not instantly. Not without pain. But the hiding stopped. And the moment the hiding stops, the spiral has the chance to break.

The church's job is not to eliminate anxiety or shame. The church's job is to create a place where the anxious person is known and loved anyway. Where the performance stops because the love is real. Where you can say "I'm struggling" and not lose community because of it. Where the thing you most feared — exposure — becomes the beginning of healing.

This is what it means to be a body. To carry each other's burdens. Not to fix them, but to stand in the weight with them. To say: the spiral stops here. Here is where you can be honest. Here is where you can be known. Here is where the hiding ends.

**The Body Remembers: Shame as Neurophysiology, Not Just Emotion**

Shame is not something you feel. Shame is something your nervous system does. When shame activates, your brain does not simply generate an emotion the way it generates sadness or anger. Shame triggers a cascade of physiological events that register in your body as physical pain. Neuroimaging shows that shame activates the same brain regions as physical injury. The rejection is as real to your nervous system as a broken bone.

What this means is that shame is not a weakness to overcome or an emotion to process. Shame is a survival mechanism that has misfired. Your body is treating social rejection the way it would treat physical threat. Your amygdala is in high alert. Your prefrontal cortex—the part that handles reasoning and self-compassion—is going offline. Your autonomic nervous system is flooded with cortisol. You are literally in fight-or-flight, except the threat is not predators. The threat is being seen and being rejected for what you are.

The body remembers. I learned this the hard way. For years after I began my faith journey, I carried deep shame about my atheist years, my wasted time, the person I had been before conversion. It was not just intellectual regret. My body held it. Certain conversations would trigger a physical response—a tightness in my chest, a heat in my face, an almost involuntary recoil. Something in me believed, at the body level, that if people really knew who I had been, they would be right to reject me.

Years of church, years of theology, years of faith did not touch that bodily shame. Why? Because shame does not live in the reasoning brain. It lives in the nervous system. It lives in the autonomic response. You can think your way out of guilt. Guilt is cerebral. "I did something bad" is a thought you can examine and challenge. But shame is different. Shame is "I am bad." And that conviction has been written into your nervous system at a primal level.

Brene Brown's research on shame distinguishes between shame and guilt precisely here. Guilt is about behavior: "I did something bad." Guilt can be addressed through confession, restitution, change. You can fix what you did. But shame is about identity: "I am something bad." That is not fixed through behavioral change. That is fixed through being seen and accepted anyway. Through someone knowing the shameful thing and choosing relationship rather than rejection.

The gospel addresses both guilt and shame, but in different ways. Guilt is addressed through Christ's work on the cross—the payment, the satisfaction, the removal of God's wrath. That is cognitive. That can be understood in the propositional sense. But shame is addressed through incarnation. God entered human flesh. God became vulnerable. God allowed Himself to be rejected and humiliated and executed in the most shameful death available. Then God rose. God was not destroyed by the shame. God transformed it.

What this means in practice is that someone with shame needs more than a theology of justification. They need to be in community with people who know who they are and choose them anyway. They need to be told: I know what you have done. I know what you thought. I know the shameful parts you hide. And I choose to be your friend. I choose to be your sibling in Christ. I choose to see you as someone worthy of love.

I had a deacon in my church—James, not myself, though our names are the same—who carried deep shame. He had been sexually abused as a child. He had engaged in addictive sexual behavior as an adolescent and young adult. He had hurt people. He had done things that would, if known, likely result in social exile in most Christian communities. For years, he lived a double life. He was the reliable deacon on Sunday. He was the man hiding in shame the rest of the week.

When he finally disclosed the full scope of his past, the expectation was the same expectation that had shaped his entire life: rejection. His body was prepared for it. His nervous system was ready for it. He had rehearsed it a thousand times.

What happened instead changed him. The deacon board did not reject him. They wept with him. They told him the truth: that his past did not disqualify him from the present. That what mattered was not erasing what happened but being transformed by the gospel of the one who loves sinners. They said: we know who you are, and we choose you. We know what you have done, and we forgive you. We are not releasing you from leadership out of shame. We are walking through this with you.

That did not happen overnight. The shame did not simply evaporate because he was accepted. Shame is written into the nervous system. You cannot think it away. But over time, being repeatedly chosen by people who knew the shameful thing—being repeatedly told he was not what he thought he was—his nervous system began to recalibrate. The tightness in his chest when he saw these men in a room began to dissipate. The heat of shame began to cool. His body began to learn: these people know. These people are here. These people choose me.

That is what the church is supposed to do. Not pretend shame does not exist. Not minimize it with easy comfort. But create spaces where people can speak the shameful thing out loud and discover that they are not destroyed by being known. That is the work of the gospel applied to the physiology of shame.

For those of us who carry shame—and most of us carry some form of it—the work is twofold. First, recognize that shame is not a thought problem. It is a nervous system problem. Your body will not be convinced by theology alone. Your body needs to learn through relationship that you are safe. You need to be in community where you can be known and chosen. That is not weakness. That is wisdom. That is understanding how your nervous system actually works.

Second, understand that shame often demands professional help. If shame is severe enough, if it is rooted in trauma, a therapist who understands the neurobiology of shame can help you slowly recalibrate your nervous system. That is not competing with faith. It is participating in the healing Jesus offers, just through a different channel. The Christian therapist who helps you process your shame is doing the work of the gospel.

I have watched shame destroy people. I have also watched it transform—slowly, through relationship and grace and the willingness to be known and chosen anyway. The key is understanding that shame lives in the body. It is a somatic reality. You cannot shame-think your way out. You can shame-relationship your way out. And that is exactly what the incarnate, crucified, resurrected gospel offers: someone who knows the deepest parts of you and loves you anyway.

**CHAPTER 11**

What Presence Actually Looks Like

Job had friends. This is worth noting because when you are suffering, you often feel like you are utterly alone. But Job had three men who loved him well enough to travel to see him, who sat with him in his devastation. The book says, "When Job's three friends, Eliphaz the Temanite, Bildad the Shuhite and Zophar the Naamathite, heard about all the troubles that had come upon him, they set out from their homes and met together by agreement to go and sympathize with him and comfort him."

They came. They traveled. They made an agreement to be present.

And then they sat. The text says, "When they saw him from a distance, they could hardly recognize him; they began to weep aloud, and they tore their robes and sprinkled dust on their heads. Then they sat on the ground with him for seven days and seven nights. No one said a word to him, because they saw how much he was suffering."

Seven days and nights. No words. Just presence.

This is the ministry of presence. This is what it looks like when people do the thing right.

Job's friends understood something that has been largely lost from modern Christianity: sometimes the most sacred gift you can offer someone who is suffering is your silence. Your body in the room. Your willingness to sit in discomfort without trying to make it go away. For seven days, Job's friends did nothing but be present. They did not offer explanations. They did not offer theology. They did not offer encouragement or platitudes or Bible verses. They wept. They sat. They stayed.

This is what saved them from the devastation that came next. Because seven days in, they opened their mouths. And when they did, they became terrible.

Eliphaz begins with theology — elegant theology, actually. He talks about the foundation of righteousness and how God punishes the wicked. Bildad follows with the same theme. Zophar adds his voice. And what they are doing, in theological language, is accusing Job. They are saying: you must have sinned. God does not punish the innocent. Therefore, you are guilty. This suffering is your own fault.

But they are wrong. The book opened by telling us that Job was "blameless and upright, a man who feared God and shunned evil." God Himself called Job righteous. These accusations are devastatingly false. And Job knows it. So now, in addition to his suffering, Job has the added burden of being misunderstood and blamed by the people who came to comfort him.

The friends became the worst part of the suffering. Not because they were malicious. But because they substituted theology for presence. The moment they opened their mouths to explain, they moved from the sacred work of presence into the destructive work of judgment.

The church has largely forgotten how to be present. We have replaced it with a kind of therapeutic talk-doing. We think the gift we offer is answers. We think what people need when they are suffering is an explanation for why they are suffering or what they should do about it. So we open our mouths.

And we become terrible.

A woman whose child died came to me and told me about a conversation she had after the funeral. A well-meaning church member had said to her, "God needed another angel in heaven." This is what passes for comfort in the modern church. It is devastating theology wrapped in a tone of care. It is an explanation that removes any real place for grief. It is Job's friends speaking.

Another man, struggling with suicidal thoughts, had a pastor tell him, "Have you prayed about it? You need to have more faith." The implication is clear: your suffering is evidence of insufficient belief. You just need to try harder. This is Job's friends speaking.

Another woman, dealing with postpartum depression, had a church friend tell her, "But you have so much to be grateful for. Think of all the blessings God has given you." As if gratitude cancels out depression. As if joy and sadness cannot exist in the same body. As if acknowledging sadness is a rejection of God's blessings. This is Job's friends speaking.

What the person who is suffering needs is not an explanation. It is not theology. It is not cheerleading or platitudes or reminders that "God won't give you more than you can handle" or "Everything happens for a reason." These are words that substitute for presence. They are words that say: I do not know how to sit with you in this, so I am going to explain it away.

What the person who is suffering needs is a body in the room. Someone who can say, "I do not have any words. I do not have any explanation. I do not have any answer. But I am here. And I will not leave. And I will sit in this with you."

I sat with a family the night their teenage daughter died by suicide. The family was destroyed. The shock and the guilt and the confusion were all mixed together in a kind of devastation that words cannot reach. People came. People wanted to say something. They wanted to offer some kind of spiritual structure that would make sense of this or comfort the family. But there is no structure that fits that moment. There is only this: the family is in a room, and the room should be filled with people who love them enough to stand in the darkness without trying to move it.

So I told people: do not try to comfort. Do not try to explain. Do not pray loudly or quote Scripture or say things like "she is in a better place" or "God needed another angel" or "God never gives us more than we can handle." Those words are violence in that moment. Instead, sit. Cry. Be silent. Make food. Show up. Do not leave.

And what happened over those days was the sacred work of the church. People sat. People brought food. People said "I do not have words" and meant it, and in that honest speechlessness, the family felt held. Not fixed. Not explained. Just held.

This is the ministry that Jesus modeled. When Lazarus died, Jesus did not immediately announce that Lazarus would rise. He wept. The text says it plainly: "Jesus wept." Two words. The God of the universe, who knew that Lazarus would be raised, wept anyway. He did not skip over the grief to get to the resurrection promise. He sat in the grief first. He mourned. He was present to the loss before He was present to the solution.

When the disciples were terrified in the boat during the storm, Jesus did not immediately calm the storm. He was there. "Do not be afraid," He said. "It is I." His presence was what they needed before the external circumstances changed. The storm was still a storm. But they were not alone in it.

When the woman at the well came to the well in the middle of the day to draw water in secret — a sign of shame, because women drew water in the morning together — Jesus did not immediately offer her a theology of redemption. He asked her for a drink. He sat with her. He was present to her shame before He was present to her solution. And in that presence, the shame began to dissolve.

Presence is costly. It is much easier to offer theology than to offer yourself. Theology you can say and leave. Presence requires you to stay. It requires you to enter someone else's suffering and to sit there without an exit strategy. It requires you to resist the urge to fix things or explain things or make the discomfort go away. Presence means sitting in the mess with someone and accepting that you will not leave until they are ready, that there is no timeline on your commitment.

This is why Jesus washed the disciples' feet. This is why He went to the cross. This is why the gospel is not primarily an explanation of how salvation works. It is the presence of God entering human suffering and absorbing it. Not from a distance, but in a body, in blood, in a very real and costly participation in what His people were experiencing.

The church's most important ministry is not preaching or programs or any of the things that look impressive on a Sunday morning. The church's most important ministry is the quiet work of being present. Of sitting. Of staying. Of letting someone be honest about their anxiety and their fear and their questions and their doubt, and not leaving. Not offering a Bible verse as an exit strategy. Just staying.

I tell people who want to minister to someone who is anxious or depressed or grieving: you do not have to have the right words. In fact, the right words probably do not exist. What you have to offer is your time, your body, your willingness to sit in discomfort. You have to offer the one thing that our culture has trained us to avoid: you have to be bored with them. You have to be willing to sit in silence. You have to resist the urge to make the moment productive or meaningful by saying something wise. You just sit.

This is what Jesus was doing when He sat by the well. This is what He was doing when He wept at the tomb. This is what He was doing when He was crucified — entering human suffering not to explain it or fix it from the outside, but to be present to it from the inside.

If you want to know what love looks like, watch Job's friends for the first seven days. Watch them sit. Watch them weep. Watch them resist the urge to speak. Watch them offer nothing but presence.

And then understand that the moment you open your mouth to explain or comfort or offer theology, you have left that sacred space. You have moved from being the gospel to being just another voice trying to manage someone else's pain.

The gift you offer when you sit in silence with someone who is suffering is this: you are saying that their pain matters enough for you to stop everything else. You are saying that you will not fix it or explain it or move on from it. You are saying that they are not alone. You are saying what Jesus said to the disciples in the storm: "Do not be afraid. It is I." Your presence in that moment is the answer.

**The Incarnate God: Presence as the Gospel's Final Answer**

The incarnation is not primarily a theological doctrine. It is the revelation of what presence means. God did not fix the human condition from heaven. God did not issue a command and declare all suffering null. God did not even send a representative or an angel or a messenger. God entered. God became flesh. God sat in the rubble of the human condition and said: I am here.

That changes everything about what presence means in the context of suffering and anxiety and weight.

Most of our instinct, when someone is in pain, is to solve the pain. Fix it, explain it, make it stop. We scramble for solutions because suffering offends us. We want to return things to normal. We want the person to feel better so we can all go back to comfort. But the incarnation teaches a different logic: sometimes the answer to suffering is not the elimination of suffering. The answer is presence. Someone entering the suffering with you.

Jesus wept at Lazarus's tomb. He had the power to bring Lazarus back from the dead. He had already begun the process of raising him. But first, He wept. He felt the weight of loss with Mary and Martha. He did not say: "Why are you sad? I am going to raise him." He wept first. He entered the grief. He was present in the despair before He was the solution to it.

When Mary anoints His feet and washes them with her tears, Jesus does not tell her to stop, to clean up her emotions, to get her theology right. He receives her brokenness. He is present to it. He is willing to have someone's deepest grief pour out on Him. That is incarnational presence.

This is entirely countercultural in the church I have watched build itself. We have built a culture of solutions. You have anxiety? Here are five steps. You are grieving? Here is a grief class. You are struggling with sin? Here is the discipleship track. We have systematized our way into the belief that the answer to human difficulty is a program.

But sometimes the answer is a person. Just a person, sitting with you, not trying to fix anything, not offering solutions, not making it about their own discomfort. Just: I am here. I see you. I am not leaving.

That kind of presence is learned. It does not come naturally to us in a culture obsessed with productivity and solutions. We have to deliberately train ourselves—and our churches—into the capacity for it.

In my church, we have begun training small group leaders, deacons, volunteers in what we call "the ministry of sitting with." It is simple, and it is revolutionary: when someone comes to you broken, your first job is not to fix them. Your first job is to be present. To listen without immediately offering counsel. To ask good questions. To sit in the silence. To let them know through your presence that their pain matters, that they are not alone in it, that they do not have to perform healing for your comfort.

We teach people: when someone tells you they are struggling, resist the urge to immediately offer a Bible verse. Just sit. Be quiet. Ask: "What do you need right now?" Often the answer is just: "I need someone to know." Not to solve. To know. To witness. To be present to the reality that your life has become harder than you know how to carry.

This requires training out of some of our worst instincts. We are taught that a good Christian response is to be the encourager, the one with answers, the spiritual leader. But that is not what Christ offers. Christ sits at the well with the woman at the well who has been divorced five times. He does not lecture her. He asks her for a drink. He makes Himself vulnerable first. He says: I need something from you. Then He tells her everything about herself—not to shame her, but to show her that He knows her completely and loves her anyway.

In my church office, I sit with people in depths I never expected to go. I have sat with a man whose son was arrested for a crime he actually committed, and the weight of that shame, the weight of having raised a child who destroyed someone else's child, the weight of watching a future you imagined get erased—that weight is not fixable by Bible verses. It is fixable by someone sitting with you and saying: I know you are breaking. I know you are destroying yourself with guilt that you do not deserve. I know you have become more defined by your son's action than by your own character. And I am going to sit here until you stop believing lies about yourself.

That is presence. That is incarnational. That is gospel.

We have trained our churches to believe that presence is something you do before you move to the real work of spiritual direction. But presence is the real work. It is the gospel applied to the human condition. It is Jesus saying: before I teach you anything, before I offer you any theology, I want you to know that I see you. I know you. I am here.

The culture we are trying to build at FBC is this: it should be normal to say "I am not okay." It should be safe. It should not result in being treated as a project, a problem to solve, a person whose struggle is your opportunity to demonstrate spiritual wisdom. It should mean: someone knows. Someone will sit with me. I do not have to pretend anymore.

That is the kind of presence that changes people. Not because it solves anything. But because it makes the weight more bearable. Because you discover that you do not have to carry it alone. Because presence is the gospel becoming flesh.

The incarnation teaches us that God chose embodiment. Chose vulnerability. Chose to be hungry and tired and grieved. Chose to sit in the dust with people who were breaking. Not as an act of spiritual superiority, but as an act of solidarity. "I am with you" is the deepest promise God makes. And that is incarnational. That is embodied. That is God showing up in the flesh, in the room, in the presence of human suffering and saying: I know this place. I have been here. You are not alone.

**CHAPTER 12**

The God Who Carries

Listen to Isaiah 46. The chapter opens with a description of the gods of Babylon being carried away as spoils of war. "Bel bows down, Nebo stoops low; their idols are borne by beasts of burden. The images that are carried about are burdensome, a burden for the weary." The pagan gods must be carried. They are heavy. They require people to bear them. This is the fundamental nature of false religion — it places a burden on the worshipper. The god is not a support. The god is a weight.

Then the text shifts. God speaks to the people of Israel who are in exile, who are afraid, who feel like they have been abandoned. God says: "Listen to me, descendants of Jacob, all the remnant of the people of Israel. You whom I have upheld since your birth, whom I have carried since you were born. Even to your old age and gray hairs I am he, I am he who will sustain you. I have made you and I will carry you; I will sustain you and I will rescue you."

The contrast is complete. The gods of Babylon must be carried by their worshippers. The God of Israel carries His worshippers.

This is the subversive claim of the gospel. The weight you are carrying was never meant to be yours alone.

Anxiety is a weight. If you have lived with chronic anxiety, you know this. It is not just a feeling. It is a burden that you carry everywhere. It is in your body — in your chest, your shoulders, your gut. It is in your sleep when it steals your rest. It is in your relationships when it makes you short-tempered or withdrawn. It is in your work when it makes you unable to focus. It is a weight.

And the prosperity gospel tells you that you should not have this weight. A faithful Christian lives light. An untroubled life is the evidence of proper belief. So now you are not just carrying the weight of anxiety. You are carrying the additional weight of shame that you are carrying it. You are carrying the weight of trying to perform wellness. You are carrying the weight of hiding. This is how anxiety expands. It multiplies because now you are holding not just the anxiety but everything the anxiety says about you.

But listen to what Isaiah says God does. "I have made you and I will carry you."

This is not a promise that the anxiety will disappear. It is not a promise that you will arrive at the place where you no longer struggle. It is a promise that the weight — whatever weight you are carrying — is not your weight to carry alone. God has made you. God has sustained you from birth. And God will carry you. Not when you have finally grown strong enough. Not when you have developed enough faith. Now. Even to your old age and gray hairs. Even in whatever place you are in, even when you feel utterly depleted, the God who made you is carrying you.

Every other system — self-help, stoicism, the gospel of prosperity, the theology of individualism — requires you to be strong enough to carry your own weight. The assumption is that you should be able to manage this yourself. And when you cannot, the failure is personal. You did not pray enough. You did not believe enough. You did not work hard enough. The burden is always on you.

But the God of Israel says something entirely different. The burden is not on you. You were made to be carried. That is not weakness. That is the design. You were not designed to be independent. You were designed to be upheld. The anxiety does not mean you have failed at independence. The anxiety means you are invited into dependence — the most honest relationship you can have with God.

The gospel is the ultimate statement of this. God does not offer us a system to follow or a set of rules to obey or a way to achieve spiritual wellness through our own effort. God offers Himself. God becomes incarnate. God enters human suffering. God carries it. When Jesus went to the cross, He was doing what the gods of Babylon could not do — He was not being carried to the place of execution. He was carrying. He was bearing the weight of the world's sin and rebellion and brokenness. He was literally carrying it in His body.

This is what Paul is getting at when he talks about "bearing one another's burdens, and so fulfill the law of Christ." The law of Christ is not a list of rules. The law of Christ is this: the God who carries you invites you into the privilege of carrying others. You are not meant to carry your own anxiety alone. But you are also not meant to carry others' anxiety by yourself. All of this is held by God, who carries all of it.

I think about the apostles after the resurrection. They were terrified. They had watched the man they believed in be executed. They had believed He was the Messiah, and He had been crushed. Everything they had hope in was dead. So they locked themselves in a room. And Jesus came to them. Not to condemn them for their fear. He came to be with them. "Peace be with you," He said. And then He showed them His hands and His side — the places where the weight had crushed Him.

And something shifted. The moment they saw Jesus, they knew they were not carrying the weight alone anymore. The weight had been carried. The burden had been absorbed. They could breathe again.

This is what the gospel offers to the person who is anxious. Not a cure, not a formula, not a set of steps that will lead you to peace. But this: the weight has been carried. Jesus went to the cross carrying the weight of human sin and pain and rebellion. He absorbed it. He died under it. And He rose on the other side of it. Which means the weight is not waiting for you at the end of time. It has already been carried. It is already absorbed. It is already being transformed.

You are not waiting for rescue. You are living in the already-but-not-yet space where rescue has already occurred but we have not yet seen its full completion. You are already free. Already carried. Already held. You just do not yet see it everywhere.

This is the difference between hope and optimism. Optimism is the belief that things will get better. That the anxiety will go away. That everything will work out. That is a fragile hope because it depends on circumstances changing. But biblical hope is different. Biblical hope is not dependent on circumstances. It is dependent on the character of God. It is the confidence that even in the circumstances that do not change, even in the anxiety that persists, God is carrying you. The weight is still there. But you are not holding it alone.

I tell people who are struggling with anxiety: the goal is not the absence of anxiety. The goal is the presence of God. These are not the same thing. You might live your entire life with anxiety. You might never find the cure. You might never arrive at the place where you feel comfortable and untroubled. But you can arrive at the place where you know you are not carrying it alone. Where God's presence is more real to you than the anxiety. Where the weight is still heavy, but you are upheld.

This sounds too simple. It sounds like spiritual platitude. But consider what it actually means. It means that your anxiety does not disqualify you from the kingdom. It means that your struggle does not make you less loved. It means that the prophets of Israel and the apostles of Christ and Jesus Himself experienced what you are experiencing, and it did not separate them from God. It brought them closer. It stripped away the pretense. It moved them from performing faith to living it.

There is a prayer in the book of Common Prayer that goes like this: "Comfort us, O Lord, as your disciples in the boat; though the storm rages about us and we are afraid, speak your peace into our hearts, that we may remember your presence and trust your power." This is the promise of Isaiah. Not that the storm will stop. But that Jesus is in the boat with us. Not that we will stop being afraid. But that we will remember we are not alone.

The God who carries you does not carry you because you have earned it or deserved it or believed enough. God carries you because God loves you. God makes you and God carries you. This is not conditional. This is not contingent on your performance. God is carrying you even in the moment when you do not feel it. Even in the moment when the anxiety is so loud you cannot hear anything else. Even in the moment when you have given up. God is still carrying.

The anxiety has not separated you from God. It has not made you less valuable. It has not rendered you useless or unworthy or broken beyond redemption. You are still made by God. You are still carried by God. You are still going to be sustained through whatever comes next.

This is not comfort. This is truth. And at some point, the truth becomes more real than the feeling. At some point, you stop waiting for the anxiety to go away and you start living with the presence of the one who is carrying you. And that changes everything.

**The Cross as Absorption: What It Means That God Carried This**

The cross is not a theological symbol. It is a description of what God did with the weight of human suffering. God did not manage suffering from a distance. God did not explain suffering or contextualize it or make it meaningful through doctrine. God absorbed it. God took the full force of it—into a human body, with nails, with blood, with the cry of abandonment from the Psalms: "My God, my God, why have you forsaken me?"

That cry is Psalm 22. Jesus does not create new language for His agony. He reaches for the Psalms, which means He reaches for the language of lament, the language of disorientation, the language of a person abandoned by God. When Jesus cries that from the cross, He is not performing agony. He is inhabiting the deepest place of human despair and saying: God knows this place. God has always known this place. Even here, this is Scripture. This is the language of faith.

What this means is that the cross is not the removal of suffering. It is the entrance into suffering. It is God saying: I will not stand at a distance while you are broken. I will not send a representative. I will not command the suffering away. I will come and I will take the weight myself.

When Paul speaks of the crucifixion, he uses the language of bearing or carrying. "Christ redeemed us from the curse of the law by becoming a curse for us." He took it. He carried it. He did not dispel it or make it abstract or explain why it was necessary. He put it on His shoulders and He walked toward death under the weight of it.

What changes in your faith when you understand the cross as absorption rather than transaction is staggering. For years, I understood atonement theoretically. Christ paid the debt. The Father's justice was satisfied. This is true, and it is important, but it was bloodless in my understanding. It did not cost me anything to believe it. It did not undo anything in my life. It was doctrine I could assent to and then move on to the rest of my day.

Then I began to understand the cross as God entering the weight itself. God becoming the person in the dark. God experiencing what we experience when the weight becomes unbearable. God crying out in the agony of abandonment. God dying.

That is not just doctrine. That is solidarity. That is God saying: I have been where you are. I have felt the weight of human suffering on my shoulders. I have experienced the despair of feeling abandoned. I have died. And I have done this not to escape it, but to enter it completely and to transform it from the inside.

The resurrection does not negate the cross. It follows the cross. Which means the answer to suffering is not that suffering is eliminated. The answer is that suffering has been entered into by God and transformed. Death has not been escaped; it has been defeated. But the defeat comes through passing through it, not around it.

This is crucial for anyone living with chronic anxiety or depression or sustained difficulty. The gospel does not promise that your suffering will be removed. If it did, billions of Christians would be living in visible contradiction to the promise, and the gospel would be proven false. But the gospel promises something deeper: that suffering is not abandoned. That it is carried. That even in the worst moments—the moments where you cannot feel God, where His absence is louder than His presence, where the weight seems to have crushed all hope—even there, you are not forgotten.

The cross says: God knows despair. God has been here. God has carried this kind of weight and He did not stop being God. He did not stop loving. He did not stop caring about you. Even at the moment of His greatest agony, even as He is dying, He is looking at the thief and promising him: "Today you will be with me in paradise." Even in the cross, God is not forgetting about the people He loves.

I have sat with people in psychiatric hospitals. I have sat with families who have lost someone to suicide. The weight in those rooms is almost visible. The despair is so thick you can taste it. And I have watched something happen when we speak the language of the cross—not as doctrine, but as God's entrance into the darkest places.

Because it means God is not standing apart from this. God is here. God understands. God has been abandoned too. God has cried out too. God has died too. And God rose. Not to erase the darkness, but to prove that darkness does not have the final word.

The crucified Christ hanging on the cross is the image that sustains faith in the darkness. Not because it explains the darkness. But because it means the darkness is known. It is witnessed. It is carried by God Himself.

The resurrection follows. But notice what resurrection is. It is not the elimination of the cross. The risen Christ still bears the scars. He invites Thomas to touch the wounds. The resurrection does not erase the cross; it redeems it. It says: what looked like defeat—like the end, like abandonment, like the final victory of death—was actually the moment when God was winning.

That changes everything about how you live with suffering. Not because the suffering goes away. But because you know it is not wasted. It is not meaningless. It is not a sign of God's abandonment. It is the place where you are most closely connected to the crucified and risen Christ. You are in the story He entered. You are being transformed by the same power that raised Him from the dead.

I have a tattoo on my arm. It is the image of the cross. Not because I wear my faith as a status symbol, but because I needed it on my body as a reminder. On the days when the weight is heaviest, when I cannot sense God's presence, when I feel alone in the darkness, I can look at my arm and remember: God has been here too. God has carried this. God has died. And God rose.

That is the gospel. Not as doctrine, but as presence. Not as explanation, but as solidarity. Not as removal of the weight, but as someone entering into it and proving that love is stronger than darkness. That is what sustains. That is what saves.

The paralyzed man in Mark 2 arrives on a mat. He cannot walk. His friends have carried him. The text says they uncovered the roof and lowered him down because there was no other way. The religious establishment was crowded around Jesus and the paralyzed man could not get close. So his friends did something disruptive. They broke into the house. They destroyed the roof. They lowered their friend into the presence of Jesus.

And what Jesus does is telling. He looks at this man who has been carried by others and the first thing He says is: your sins are forgiven. He does not heal the paralysis first. He names the burden that the paralyzed man is carrying alongside the physical paralysis. The weight of shame. The weight of being dependent. The weight of not being able to do for himself.

And then He heals the physical paralysis. But the healing comes after the forgiveness. After the naming of the other weight.

This is what the God who carries does. God does not deny that you are paralyzed. God does not suggest that faith should be enough to make you walk. God looks at you in your paralysis and says: you are forgiven. You are not carrying the weight of your own failure. You are not alone.

And then the transformation begins.

This is the promise of Isaiah 46. The promise that God carries you. Not that you will become strong enough to carry yourself. Not that you will develop faith sufficient to transcend your need for carrying. But that you were made to be carried. That dependence on God is not weakness. That the weight of your existence is not your responsibility to bear alone.

The anxiety that you carry is a weight. It sits in your chest. It makes your hands shake. It steals your sleep. It is real and it is heavy. And the prosperity gospel has told you that you should be strong enough to carry it. That faith should be sufficient to bear it. That admitting the weight exists is admitting that your faith is broken.

But what if the weight was never meant to be yours to carry alone?

What if the invitation of the gospel is not to become strong enough to transcend the weight but to have the weight carried by God while you are still in it?

This is the difference between the gospel and everything else. Every other system—therapy, self-help, even Christian spirituality that is focused on spiritual growth—operates from the assumption that you are responsible for your own transformation. You must do the work. You must have the faith. You must apply the principles. And if you do, you will get better. This system places the burden on you.

But the gospel says something different. The gospel says your anxiety does not disqualify you. Your struggle does not make you unworthy. Your weakness is not something you have to overcome alone. Jesus says: Come to me, all who are weary and burdened, and I will give you rest. Not "come and I will teach you how to carry the burden better." But "I will give you rest."

This is not metaphor. This is a claim about what God actually does.

When you are in the depth of anxiety, when your mind is generating catastrophes and your body is responding as if the catastrophes are real, when you have no power to stop the spiral, the gospel says: you do not have to fix this yourself. You are not responsible for healing your own brain. You do not have to have faith sufficient to overcome the chemistry. You are invited into dependence. You are invited to become like the paralyzed man—carried by others, brought to Jesus, set down in the presence of God with nothing to offer but your actual condition.

This is the hard part of the gospel. Because it requires you to stop trying. It requires you to stop managing. It requires you to admit that you cannot do this yourself. It requires you to become vulnerable to the possibility that you might be carried. That your weight might be borne by something or someone outside of yourself.

The American church has reframed this as weakness. The American culture has reframed this as failure. You are supposed to be self-sufficient. You are supposed to pull yourself up. You are supposed to have faith that works for you. You are not supposed to need carrying.

But the gospel is entirely different. The gospel says you need carrying. That is not a bug in the system. That is the design. You were made to be carried by God. That is the deepest truth about your existence. The anxiety does not change that. The struggle does not change that. The weakness does not change that.

What it does change is whether you are willing to admit you need it.

There is a woman in your congregation who cannot admit she is anxious because admitting it would mean admitting she cannot handle her life. She has built an entire identity around being the one who manages. The one who holds things together. The one who does not burden others. And admitting anxiety would be a rupture in that identity. It would require her to say: I cannot do this alone. I need help. I need to be carried.

And she is right to fear that admission because the culture—including the church culture—will interpret it as weakness. But the gospel interprets it as honesty. As the first step toward freedom.

When the paralyzed man's friends lower him through the roof, he is not strong enough to walk. He cannot carry himself. He is dependent. He is weak. And Jesus looks at that weakness and says: I will carry you. Your weakness is not a disqualification. It is the condition of my grace. I came for the broken, not the whole. I came for the people who cannot do it themselves.

This is the gospel. Not as inspiration. Not as a program you can follow. But as the announcement that your weight has been entered. That your anxiety has been absorbed by the one who went to the cross. That resurrection has already happened and you are living in the already-but-not-yet space where that resurrection is true but not yet visible everywhere.

The peace that Jesus promises is not the absence of the storm. The peace is His presence in the boat while the storm is raging. The disciples are terrified—their boat is being tossed. The wind is against them. Everything feels unstable. And Jesus appears. And He says: "Do not be afraid. It is I." Not "the storm will stop" but "I am here."

And everything changes. Not because the storm stops. But because they are no longer alone in the storm.

This is what it means to be carried by God. Not that the anxiety will disappear. Not that your life will become comfortable and untroubled. But that the weight will not be the thing that defines you. That your existence will be held by something stronger than your fear. That you will move from the question "why is this happening to me?" to the reality "I am not alone in this."

The paralyzed man does not get healed so he can become self-sufficient and no longer need God. The gospel does not work that way. The man is healed. He walks. He stands. He takes up his mat. But he is not healed so he can stop depending on God. He is healed so he can continue depending on God from a different position. He is no longer paralyzed, but he is still carried. Still dependent. Still upheld by the grace of God.

This is the costly hope part. Because accepting that you are carried by God means accepting that you are not finally responsible for your own salvation. That your worth is not measured in what you accomplish or what you overcome. That you are valuable not because you are strong but because God loves you. That your anxiety does not diminish that value. That your struggle does not cause God to love you less.

This is revolutionary in a culture that has taught you that your value is what you achieve. In a church that has taught you that your faith is measured in your strength. In a theology that has taught you that anxiety is what happens when you are not believing hard enough.

The God who carries you does not need your strength. The God who carries you does not require your faith to be perfect before offering grace. The God who carries you enters your weakness and does not leave. The God who carries you absorbs your weight into Himself. The God who carries you goes to the cross carrying the sin of the world. Carrying your anxiety. Carrying your fear. Carrying the weight that you have been trying so desperately to carry alone.

And in that carrying, in that absorption, in that complete assumption of your weight into God's own body, something is transformed. Not you, not yet. But the weight itself. The weight is no longer the thing that is breaking you. The weight is being held. Is being carried. Is being transformed by the one whose power is made perfect in weakness. In dependence. In the end of your ability to save yourself.

This is the gospel. Not as a system you follow. But as the announcement of what is already true. Your weight has been carried. Jesus went to the cross carrying it. The resurrection has already occurred. You are already freed. You just do not yet see it everywhere.

And in the space between what is already true and what you cannot yet see, faith becomes possible. Not faith that eliminates anxiety. But faith that exists alongside it. Faith that says: I trust that the one carrying me knows what I am being carried toward. That the weight is not crushing me because I am not carrying it alone. That God is present even in the moment when I cannot feel God's presence.

This changes how you live. You stop trying to fix yourself. You stop trying to achieve wellness. You stop trying to perform health. You start accepting that you are being carried. And in that acceptance, you become available for actual transformation. Not the transformation into someone without anxiety. But the transformation into someone who knows they are not alone.

The paralyzed man stands. He walks. He takes his mat and goes home. But he is not whole because he is no longer dependent. He is whole because he has been known. He has been seen. He has been carried into the presence of the one who loves him. And that knowing, that seeing, that love, is what makes him whole.

This is what the church can be for the anxious person. Not a place where you perform health. But a place where you are carried. Where the weight is known. Where being strong is optional and being honest is required. Where the paralyzed can be lowered through the roof without shame. Where the friends of the anxious do the work of carrying. Where the God who carries all of this makes the weight bearable.

This is not comfort. This is truth. And the truth, at some point, becomes more real than the feeling.

**CONCLUSION**

The Weight Was Never Yours Alone

My hands still sometimes shake. Not as often as they used to. Not as severely. But there are mornings when I wake up and my chest is tight and my breathing is shallow and I know it is going to be a day of managing. I have developed tools. I have learned what my body needs — exercise, prayer, sometimes medication, the presence of my wife, the honesty of a close friend who knows what is happening inside me. I have built a life that is not constructed around the elimination of anxiety but around the navigation of it.

I tell you this because I want to be clear: this book is not a cure. Reading it will not fix your anxiety. It will not make the panic attacks disappear. It will not restore the sleep you have lost or give you back the peace you cannot remember having. If you are hoping for that, you will be disappointed. And you have a right to be disappointed, because that is what you should be able to expect.

But that is not what this book is trying to do.

This book is trying to accomplish something more modest and more important. It is trying to say this: the anxiety you are experiencing is real. The weight you are carrying is real. And you are not carrying it because you have failed at faith or because your belief is defective or because there is something deeply broken about you that a better Christian would not have. You are carrying it because you are human. And humans are not designed to carry the weight alone.

The shame that keeps you from being honest, the performance that keeps you from being known, the hiding that keeps you isolated — these are the real enemies. Not the anxiety itself. The anxiety is a human reality. But the shame about the anxiety, the belief that you should not have it, the fear that if anyone knew what was happening inside you they would leave — this is what destroys you.

I said at the beginning that I know anxiety personally. Not as a preacher offering theoretical comfort. As a man who has stood in his office after a counseling session where he had just told someone else to be honest about their struggle, closed the door, and felt his own chest collapse. Who has preached with his hands shaking. Who has smiled at a church member on Sunday morning while feeling like he was drowning inside. Who has built a life and a ministry and a platform while carrying something inside him that he could not tell anyone about.

And I know what the hiding does. I know the spiral. I know the moment when you realize that the performance has become so complete that no one actually knows you. That you have become a kind of ghost — visible on the surface but entirely absent. I know the devastation of that loneliness.

And I also know what happens the moment that ends. The moment you tell someone. The moment you stop performing. The moment you say, out loud, to another human being: I am struggling with something I have been ashamed to name. And that person does not leave. They do not question your faith. They do not offer a Bible verse and move on. They stay. They say: I see you. And I am not going anywhere.

That moment changes you. Not because the anxiety goes away. But because the shame about it begins to. Because you are no longer alone. Because you have been seen and you were not abandoned.

This is what the church is supposed to be. Not a place where you perform wellness. Not a community that functions only as long as everyone stays quiet about their struggle. But a place where you can be honest. Where the anxious person is loved not because they overcome the anxiety but because they are known. Where being broken is not disqualifying. It is just the condition of being human.

I think about Elijah. The man who called down fire from heaven. The man who confronted four hundred prophets of Baal alone. The man who was so powerful that he was taken up to heaven in a chariot of fire without experiencing death. After his greatest victory, after fire came down and consumed the sacrifice and the people fell on their faces and acknowledged God — after all of that — Elijah ran. He was afraid. He was depressed. He wanted to die. He sat under a broom tree and told God, "I have had enough, Lord. Take my life; I am no better than my ancestors."

This is the man of God in the midst of a crisis we would now call clinical depression. And what did God do? Did God offer a sermon? Did God give him a Bible verse? Did God say, "You are a prophet. You should not be feeling this way"?

No. God's response was profoundly simple. God gave him bread. God gave him water. God gave him sleep. "All this time you need is rest," God was saying, in the language of bread and water and sleep. Sometimes the most sacred gift God offers is permission to rest. Permission to stop performing. Permission to let your body recover.

Elijah ate. He drank. He slept. And then God called him to move forward. Not because the depression was cured or the anxiety was gone. But because Elijah was no longer alone in it. God was with him. And that presence changed what the struggle meant.

This is what I hope for you, whoever you are who picked up this book because you could not tell anyone at church what was happening inside you. You are not broken. You are human. Your anxiety is not evidence of insufficient faith. It is evidence that you are alive in a broken world, with a nervous system that is doing its job of alerting you to danger, but perhaps doing it too well. Your struggle is not a secret that will destroy you if it is revealed. It is just the part of being human that the church has learned not to talk about.

You are not the only one. The prophets struggled. The apostles struggled. Jesus struggled. The presence of struggle does not disqualify you from the kingdom. It does not make you less valuable. It does not mean God has abandoned you.

What it means is that you are exactly the kind of person God loves. The person who knows they cannot carry the weight alone. The person who has been stripped of pretense. The person who is, whether they know it or not, ready to discover what it means to be upheld.

The weight is real. The anxiety is real. But the God who carries you is more real. And that is not metaphor. That is not inspirational language. That is the most concrete promise the gospel makes: I am with you. Always. Even to the end. Even in your old age. Even when your hands shake. Even when you cannot sleep. Even when you cannot remember what peace felt like. I am carrying you.

You do not have to figure out how. You do not have to understand it. You do not have to perform wellness or hide your struggle or pretend to be okay. You just have to let yourself be carried.

And at some point, that becomes enough. Not because the struggle disappears. But because you stop bearing it alone. And the moment that happens, everything changes. The weight is still there. But the weight is shared. And you can breathe.

This is the gospel. This is the good news. Not that your anxiety will vanish. But that the one who loves you most will not vanish. You are seen. You are known. You are carried. And you always have been.

The weight was never yours alone.

**The Angel Came Twice: Elijah's Dark Hours and the Gospel of "Not Yet"**

The story of Elijah at the end of his strength is not the story of a man who needed better theology or deeper faith. It is the story of a man who needed sleep and food and permission to rest. God sends an angel. The angel brings bread and water. Elijah eats and goes back to sleep. The angel comes back. The angel brings bread and water again. Elijah eats and sleeps again.

Notice that God does not send a correction. God does not send a rebuke or a word of prophecy or even an explanation. God sends bread. God sends water. God sends someone to sit with the servant while he sleeps and to provide for him in the dark hours.

Then—only after he has eaten and slept twice, only after his body has been tended to—God asks: "What are you doing here, Elijah?" Not as accusation. As invitation. As a way of drawing Elijah back into conversation, back into the possibility that there is still something for him to do.

But first: bread. Water. Sleep. The permission to not be strong right now.

This is the gospel for people carrying weight. Not the gospel of bootstrapping yourself into better faith. Not the gospel of reframing your suffering so it becomes meaningful. Not the gospel of persistence and powering through. The gospel of: I see that you are broken. Let me feed you. Let me let you sleep. And when you wake up, we will talk about what comes next.

I want to write directly to you, the reader. If you are reading this in the dark—if you picked this book up because the weight is real and you do not know how to carry it anymore, if you are reading this on a day when you could not get out of bed, if you are reading this in a stretch where you have tried everything and nothing has worked and you are starting to lose hope—I want you to know something.

The gospel is not your therapist telling you that your reframing exercises will work. The gospel is not your pastor telling you to press into community harder. The gospel is not the prosperity preacher telling you that your circumstance proves you lack faith. The gospel is God becoming flesh and entering into your darkness and saying: I am here. I see you. I am going to sit with you.

You do not have to perform wellness right now. You do not have to pretend that you are seeing the silver lining. You do not have to believe that this is working together for your good. You just have to let someone feed you. You just have to sleep. You just have to let yourself be small for a time.

If you are in crisis—if you are considering harming yourself, if the weight has become so real that you are planning your exit—I need you to do something. I need you to call 988. That is the Suicide and Crisis Lifeline. Or text "HELLO" to 741741. That is the Crisis Text Line. These are not churches. These are not spiritual fixes. These are trained counselors who know what it means to sit with someone in the darkness. Let them come. Let them be the bread and water. Let them be the angel God sends.

If you are not in crisis but you are in a stretch of weight, of burden, of distance from God—I need you to know that you do not have to fix this alone. You do not have to be strong. You do not have to have the answer. You can say: I am not okay. And the gospel says: someone will sit with you. Someone will feed you. Someone will let you sleep.

Your church should be that place. If it is not, it should become it. We are called to be the body of Christ, which means we are called to be what Christ was: the one who feeds the hungry, who welcomes the rejected, who sits with the broken. We are called to be bread to people who are starving. We are called to be water to people who are parched. We are called to be the angel who comes in the dark and says: you are not alone.

I have failed at this. I have been more concerned with the sermon than with the person. I have been more invested in the system than in the soul. I have treated people like projects rather than like beloved. That is a confession. That is also a commitment: I am working to be better. I am working to create a church where people can be broken and fed. Where people can say "I am not okay" and not be treated as a problem to solve.

But here is the thing about Elijah's story that undoes me: God does not wait for Elijah to figure it out. God does not wait for Elijah to repent of his despair or to reframe his circumstances or to get his theology right. God just sends bread. God just sends water. God knows that what Elijah needs is not a lesson. What Elijah needs is care.

That is the gospel. That is what Jesus came to do. That is what the church is supposed to be. Not the place where you come to get fixed. The place where you come to be fed while you are broken.

If you are in the dark right now, I want you to know this: the angel is coming. It might be a therapist. It might be a friend. It might be a pastor or a deacon or a small group leader. It might be a crisis counselor on the other end of the phone. It might be medication. It might be sleep and the permission to not be strong. The angel might come in many forms, but God is sending it. God sees you. God is not waiting for you to be better before He cares. God cares now. God is sending bread now. God is giving you permission to rest now.

And when you wake up—not immediately, but when you have been fed and you have slept and your body is no longer screaming—when you wake up, God will ask: "What are you doing here?" Not to shame you. To invite you back into life. To ask you what comes next. To tell you that you have a role to play, that you matter, that your life is not over.

But first: rest. Receive. Let yourself be cared for.

That is the gospel. That is enough.
