# Table of Contents

# INTRODUCTION: WHY THIS DOCUMENT EXISTS

The Life Arc tells you what to build. The Operating System tells you how the building actually happens — Monday morning, Tuesday at 10 AM, Friday at 4 PM, the first Monday of every month, the last weekend of every December. Every hour of execution time is either on the calendar or it does not happen.

You have a completion problem. It is not a talent problem or a discipline problem. It is an architecture problem. The architecture is in this document. You will not need to memorize it. You will calendar it, automate it, post it on the wall behind your desk, and run it until the muscle is built.

This is the coffee-with-a-friend version of “here is how you actually finish what you start.” There is no padding here. Every section is meant to be printed, used, referenced, revised.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

# PART 1: THE DAILY OPERATING RHYTHM

Your default day has a shape. When you deviate, you deviate on purpose, not by drift.

## 5:30 AM — Wake, Water, Word

Out of bed by 5:30. Glass of water immediately. No phone for the first forty-five minutes. Before anything else: Scripture and prayer for twenty minutes in the chair by the window with your Bible and a single cup of coffee. You are not reading for sermon prep. You are reading to remain a man of God before you become a pastor, writer, or network builder. Use the M’Cheyne plan or whatever reading plan you are running, but the discipline is: the Word before the work.

At 6:00 AM, the workout happens. Thirty minutes, four days a week minimum — three lifts and two conditioning sessions on rotation. This is not optional. It is the physical anchor of the 100-pound weight-loss goal and the muscle-definition goal, and it is also the cognitive primer for the deep work block that follows.

## 6:30–8:30 AM — Deep Work Block

This is the most important two hours of your day. Everything else is arranged around it.

- No email open.
- Phone on Do Not Disturb, face down, across the room.
- Door closed.
- One document open: the active primary project.
- Timer set for two 50-minute blocks with a ten-minute break between.
- The only question: did the primary project move forward today?

This block feeds the books, the major articles, and the Substack pipeline. This is where *The Monster in the Mirror* gets finished. Where *When God Bless America Replaces Thy Kingdom Come* gets drafted. Where the body of work is built.

The rule: you do not break this block for anything short of an actual emergency. Emails will wait. Staff will wait. Texts will wait. Susanna knows. The kids know. The staff knows. The church office knows the rule: before 8:30 AM, interrupt only for emergencies.

## 8:30–9:00 AM — Family Breakfast Window

Coffee refill, breakfast with Susanna and whichever sons are home. Phone still off. This is the transition from creative work to family presence to ministry day. The window is short. Use it fully.

## 9:00–12:00 PM — Church Morning Block

Email triage (no more than 30 minutes), staff check-ins, sermon prep for Sunday (Tuesdays and Wednesdays especially), counseling sessions by appointment, administrative work, ChurchTrac entries, board/deacon communication. This is the operational heart of the pastoral work.

## 12:00–1:00 PM — Lunch

Either home with Susanna or a strategic lunch (staff mentoring, board member relationship, PCN peer). Never eat alone at the desk — that is signal you have over-packed the day. If you find yourself repeatedly eating at the desk, something in the operating system has broken.

## 1:00–4:00 PM — Ministry Afternoon Block

Hospital visits, home visits, deeper counseling, PCN work, LiveWell communications, team development meetings, external partnerships. Three afternoons a week should have real pastoral contact with people. One afternoon is PCN-focused. One afternoon is LiveWell platform maintenance. Flex as needed.

## 4:00–5:00 PM — Finish-and-Triage Hour

Close the open loops from the day. Final email pass. Prep tomorrow morning’s deep work document (open the file, leave it ready). Log the day’s work in the weekly review log. Write tomorrow’s top three priorities on a card and leave it on the desk.

## 5:00 PM — Hard Stop for Work

Walk out. Home. Dinner is with family. The church does not have you from 5:00 PM on except for Wednesday evening and Sunday.

## Evening — Family First, Reading Second

Dinner with the family. Conversation, not screens. After dinner: presence with Susanna and the boys. A walk. A game. Homework help. Bedtime rhythms for whoever is still at home. At 8:30 or 9:00, when the house settles, thirty to sixty minutes of reading — theology, history, philosophy, the authors you already love. This is not optional reading. It is the slow, long-haul formation of the mind that the voice requires. Without it, you run out of depth in a year.

## 10:00 PM — Wind Down

Phone on the charger across the room. No screens in bed. Brief prayer with Susanna. Asleep by 10:30 at the latest. The 5:30 AM wake happens on autopilot only if the 10:30 PM sleep happens on autopilot.

## Daily Non-Negotiables Checklist

Seven items. Print the card. Check each one before bed.

- [ ] Word and prayer (20 min minimum)
- [ ] Workout or walk (30 min minimum, four days/week for full workout)
- [ ] Deep work block (2 hours, primary project moved forward)
- [ ] Time with Susanna (meaningful, not incidental)
- [ ] Time with at least one son (meaningful, not incidental)
- [ ] Evening reading (30 min minimum)
- [ ] Phone off by 9:30 PM

If any one of these is missed, it is not a catastrophe. If more than two are missed more than two days in a row, the system needs an intervention.

# PART 2: THE WEEKLY CADENCE

The week has a shape. Each day has a dominant purpose.

## Monday — Planning and Church Week Start

- 7:00 AM: weekly planning session at the desk. Twenty minutes. Review calendar, confirm deep work blocks, confirm the one finishable goal of the week.
- Morning deep work: primary project.
- Afternoon: staff meeting (typical time 10:00 AM or 1:00 PM, whichever pattern the staff currently holds), sermon work, pastoral appointments.
- Evening: family dinner, reading, bed.

## Tuesday — Deep Writing + Sermon Prep

- Morning deep work: primary project.
- Mid-morning: sermon exegesis (core exegetical work on the passage — Greek, Hebrew, historical context).
- Afternoon: pastoral appointments, counseling, hospital visits.
- Tuesday is the day you do not schedule external meetings unless they are unavoidable. Tuesday is where the sermon gets its spine.

## Wednesday — Writing + Ministry + Wednesday Night

- Morning deep work: primary or secondary project.
- Mid-morning: sermon outlining and drafting.
- Afternoon: prep for Wednesday night (Awana lesson, youth teaching, adult prayer gathering coordination).
- 6:30 PM: Wednesday night at the church.
- Evening: late, home, shorter family time, skip the reading block if needed.

## Thursday — Sermon Polish + LiveWell/PCN Block

- Morning deep work: secondary project (Thursdays flex toward LiveWell writing or PCN prep).
- Mid-morning: sermon polish, manuscript completion, slides.
- Afternoon: LiveWell platform work (Substack drafting/editing, email newsletter, social scheduling) OR PCN monthly work (international communication, summit planning, member outreach).
- Evening: family + reading.

## Friday — Weekly Review + Final Sermon Pass

- Morning deep work: primary project OR Substack article completion depending on cadence needs.
- 1:00–1:30 PM: Weekly Completion Review. Recurring calendar event. Non-negotiable.
- Afternoon: final sermon pass, service order confirmation, worship team check-in if needed.
- Evening: date night with Susanna. Every Friday. Not every other Friday. Every Friday.

## Saturday — Sermon Rest Polish + Family

- Saturday morning: last sermon touch-up, pray through the sermon, print the manuscript or outline. Thirty to sixty minutes only.
- Rest of day: family — projects with the boys, Susanna, house maintenance, cooking sourdough, long walks, time outside.
- No book writing on Saturday. The primary project rests. Your mind rests.
- Evening: family dinner, no church prep, bed early.

## Sunday — The Lord’s Day

- 8:00 AM: at the church. Prayer. Setup.
- 9:00 AM: Sunday School.
- 10:30 AM: worship and preaching.
- Noon: fellowship with congregation, pastoral conversations.
- Afternoon: rest. Actual rest. Nap if needed. Reading. Family. No devices if possible.
- Evening: family dinner. Short review with Susanna about the week ahead.

## Weekly Non-Negotiables Checklist

- [ ] Five deep work blocks (ideally Monday–Friday, minimum four)
- [ ] Five sermon prep hours (exegesis Tuesday, outline Wednesday, polish Thursday, final Friday, pray-through Saturday)
- [ ] Friday weekly review completed
- [ ] Date night with Susanna
- [ ] At least one hour with each son who is home
- [ ] One workout session of at least 45 minutes (long one, in addition to the shorter daily sessions)
- [ ] Sunday preaching delivered
- [ ] One Substack essay shipped (if biweekly, every other week; otherwise, one drafted toward biweekly)

# PART 3: THE MONTHLY OPERATING CEREMONIES

Four touchpoints. Each one is calendared recurring. Each one is short.

## First Monday, 10:00 AM — Monthly Ship Audit (30 min)

What did I ship last month? List it. Specifically. What am I shipping this month? Write the three to five expected completions. What on the active list is stalled? Why? What adjustment is needed? What idea file items need killing this month?

Output: a one-page Ship Log entry added to the master Ship Log file.

## Second Wednesday, 7:30 PM — Money Meeting with Susanna (60 min)

Joint review. Coffee on the table. Both laptops open.

- Review the four accounts (checking, savings, retirement, platform revenue).
- Confirm month’s spending against budget categories.
- Review savings progress against Medicaid-bridge strategy and long-term wealth plan.
- Review platform revenue against targets.
- Confirm next month’s planned major expenses.
- End with prayer for provision.

## Third Thursday, 2:00–4:00 PM — PCN Monthly Block (2 hrs)

- David Livingston check (India) — a Zoom or a written update, alternating.
- Yupho check (Thailand) — written update typical, Zoom monthly.
- Messenger group pulse — read the threads, respond to what needs response, note patterns.
- Summit planning progress.
- Trip logistics (May/June Thailand trip, currently).
- Upcoming member outreach — who am I calling this month?

## Last Friday, 3:00 PM — Inner Life Check (45 min)

Alone. Phone off. A notebook.

- State of my walk with Christ?
- State of the marriage — honestly?
- State of the father-son relationships?
- What am I avoiding?
- What am I fearing?
- What do I need to confess or reconcile?
- What do I need to say yes to next month?
- What do I need to say no to next month?

This is the pastoral check on your own soul. It is the single most important monthly ceremony. Without it, everything else drifts into performance. Do not skip.

# PART 4: THE QUARTERLY CYCLE

Four quarters. Four ceremonies per quarter — one heavy, three light.

## Quarter Start: Kill and Promote Session (90 min)

Held the first business Monday of January, April, July, October.

- Review the active list.
- Review what shipped last quarter.
- Promote from idea file to active list if a slot is open.
- Kill at least three items from the idea file.
- Set quarterly completion targets (three to five specific things).
- Tell Susanna the targets. Tell one PCN peer the targets.
- Write the quarter’s targets on an index card. Tape it to the desk.

## Mid-Quarter Check (30 min)

Held mid-quarter (roughly February 15, May 15, August 15, November 15).

- Are the quarterly targets on track?
- What has changed?
- What adjustment is needed?

## Quarter Close: Retrospective (60 min)

Held the last business day of March, June, September, December.

- What shipped?
- What did not ship, and why?
- What started that wasn’t planned?
- What went better than expected?
- What needs different architecture next quarter?

## Quarterly Marriage and Family Weekend

Once per quarter, a weekend set aside for the family. Could be a short trip, a home project, a retreat, a camping weekend, a day away with Susanna. On the calendar before the quarter starts. Non-negotiable. Too often, the family gets the scraps of what is left after ministry demands take the good hours. The quarterly weekend is the structural protection against that drift.

# PART 5: THE ANNUAL PATTERN

## Late December — Annual Roadmap Day (4 hours)

Away from home. A coffee shop or a borrowed space. No interruptions.

- Review the year that ended. What shipped? What failed? What surprised?
- Define the coming year’s three to five major completions.
- Sequence them by quarter.
- Identify what will NOT happen in the coming year.
- Update the Life Arc document if necessary.
- Write a one-page year-ahead summary.
- Tell Susanna the summary that evening.

## First Week of January — Year Launch

- Calendar all quarterly ceremonies for the year.
- Set up all annual deadlines for book projects.
- Review and refresh the three living documents (Active List, Idea File, Kill List).
- Communicate the year’s plan to staff, board, PCN peers, Substack audience.

## Birthday / Anniversary Rhythms

- Your birthday: personal half-day of reflection. What did the last year teach me? What am I becoming?
- Susanna’s birthday: a dedicated day for her. Planned in advance.
- Wedding anniversary: annual overnight minimum. An actual trip every five years.
- Each son’s birthday: a dedicated evening with just that son.

## Summer Reset Week

One week in June or July — after *The Monster in the Mirror* ships this year. Off the grid. Reading, rest, recalibration. This is not vacation in the tourism sense. It is sabbath in the structural sense. The Life Arc is a marathon. The summer reset keeps the runner intact.

# PART 6: THE THREE LIVING DOCUMENTS

These three documents run the execution architecture. They live in a shared cloud folder (iCloud or Dropbox) as plain markdown files, syncing across phone and laptop, always accessible.

## Document 1: Active List (`Active-List.md`)

Only three projects. Ever. Short file. Visible at a glance. Updated weekly.

Template:

    # ACTIVE LIST — Updated [date]

    ## PRIMARY
    **Project:** [title]
    **Definition of Done:** [specific, measurable, external]
    **Ship Date:** [specific date]
    **Public Commitment:** [who knows]
    **Current Status:** [on track / at risk / off track]
    **Next Action:** [the very next thing]
    **Last Moved Forward:** [date]

    ## SECONDARY
    [same structure]

    ## TERTIARY
    [same structure]

## Document 2: Idea File (`Idea-File.md`)

A running list. Every new idea goes here, with date of capture and a one-line note of why it’s interesting. Ideas sit here until they are either promoted to active or killed.

Template:

    # IDEA FILE — Updated [date]

    ## BOOK IDEAS
    - [YYYY-MM-DD] [Title] — [one-line why]

    ## ARTICLE IDEAS
    - [YYYY-MM-DD] [Working title] — [one-line why]

    ## SERMON SERIES IDEAS
    - [YYYY-MM-DD] [Book/passage/theme] — [one-line why]

    ## COURSE / CURRICULUM IDEAS
    - [YYYY-MM-DD] [Concept] — [one-line why]

    ## BUSINESS / PLATFORM IDEAS
    - [YYYY-MM-DD] [Concept] — [one-line why]

    ## PCN IDEAS
    - [YYYY-MM-DD] [Concept] — [one-line why]

    ## MISCELLANEOUS
    - [YYYY-MM-DD] [Concept] — [one-line why]

## Document 3: Kill List (`Kill-List.md`)

What has been killed and when. Honored, closed, final.

Template:

    # KILL LIST — Updated [date]

    ## KILLED THIS QUARTER
    - [YYYY-MM-DD] [Title / concept] — [one-line reason]

    ## HISTORICAL KILLS
    - [YYYY-MM-DD] [Title / concept] — [one-line reason]

Starter files for all three are provided in this system package.

# PART 8: THE SOFTWARE AND TOOL STACK

The right tools, configured once, used daily. This is the complete stack. Do not add more than what is here without a clear reason. Less is more.

## Calendar

**Google Calendar** (already in use).

Configuration: - Four calendars: Personal, Family, Holidays, FBC Fenton. - Add a fifth: “Deep Work” — color-coded distinctly. - All deep work blocks pre-scheduled 90 days out, recurring. - Weekly review, monthly ceremonies, quarterly sessions all recurring. - Shared view with Susanna for Family and Personal calendars.

## Task and Project Management

**Todoist** (recommended) OR **Things 3** (if on Apple ecosystem only).

Configuration: - Three projects matching the Active List (Primary, Secondary, Tertiary). - One “Idea Inbox” project that dumps new ideas for later triage. - One “Operational” project for pastoral, church, and admin tasks. - One “PCN” project. - One “LiveWell” project. - One “Family” project. - Tags for deep-work-required vs. operational.

Use case: todos on the phone, same todos on the laptop. Voice capture to Todoist works on iPhone and Android.

## Writing — Books and Long-Form

**Scrivener** for book manuscripts.

Configuration: - Separate project file per book. - Chapter-per-folder organization. - Use the Scrivenings mode for full-draft reading passes. - Compile to DOCX for editor handoff.

## Writing — Articles, Essays, Substack

**Ulysses** (Mac/iOS) OR **iA Writer** (cross-platform) OR directly in **Substack editor**.

Recommendation: draft in Ulysses/iA Writer, paste into Substack for scheduling. Drafting in the Substack editor directly invites constant tinkering; drafting in a distraction-free markdown editor ships faster.

## Living Documents (Active List, Idea File, Kill List, Reviews)

**Obsidian** (recommended) OR plain markdown files in **iCloud Drive / Dropbox**.

Configuration: - One vault called “Bell OS.” - Folders: `/01-Active`, `/02-Ideas`, `/03-Kills`, `/04-Reviews`, `/05-Templates`, `/06-Ship-Log`. - The three living documents sit at the top of `/01-Active` and `/02-Ideas` and `/03-Kills`. - Templates folder has the six review templates, ready to copy. - Ship Log is one running file — additions only, never deletions.

Obsidian syncs to phone via Obsidian Sync (\$5/month) or via iCloud Drive if single-user.

## Note Capture (voice and text, fast)

**Drafts** (iOS — best-in-class for instant capture) OR **Apple Notes** with widgets OR **Todoist voice capture**.

Configuration: - One-tap voice note that transcribes automatically. - Actions configured to send captures into the Idea File automatically (Drafts supports this natively). - Lock screen widget for sub-two-second capture.

Every idea you have gets captured here first. Nothing in your head. Everything on the page. Review the Drafts inbox at the Friday weekly review and triage into the Idea File.

## Focus / Distraction Blocking

**Freedom** (cross-platform) OR **Cold Turkey Blocker** (more aggressive) OR **iOS Focus Modes**.

Configuration: - “Deep Work” mode: blocks email, social media, news sites, Slack, Facebook, YouTube. - Scheduled to activate automatically 6:15 AM to 8:45 AM weekdays. - iOS Focus Mode “Deep Work” mirrors this on phone. - One-tap override only with a 30-second delay (enough to stop yourself).

## Email

**Gmail** (already in use) with aggressive rules.

Configuration: - Turn off all push notifications. - Check email only at 9:00 AM, 1:00 PM, and 4:00 PM. Three times a day. Not more. - Rules to auto-label and skip inbox for newsletters, promotions, automated notifications. - Starred only for actions due today. - “Inbox Zero by 5:00 PM daily” — unachievable most days, but the target shapes the triage.

Optional upgrade: **Superhuman** (\$30/month) if email is a chronic time sink — keyboard-first, fastest mail client going. Probably not needed at current volume.

## Substack

**Substack.com** publishing platform.

Configuration: - Scheduling enabled — draft in advance, schedule for publication at a consistent time (Thursday 7 AM works for most audiences). - Email newsletter settings: full post delivered to inbox. - Paid subscription tiers configured: monthly \$8, annual \$80, founding member \$160 (or adjust as platform matures). - Comments enabled, moderated.

## Social Media Repurposing

**Buffer** OR **Later** (simpler, cheaper) for scheduling Facebook and Instagram.

Configuration: - Batch social posts on Thursday afternoon for the coming week. - Sermon-to-social pipeline: every sermon produces 3–5 social clips with quote cards. - Use the social-media-repurposing skill for the conversion. - Auto-post mid-morning (9:00 or 10:00 AM) when engagement is highest.

## Podcast (if/when launched)

**Riverside.fm** for recording. **Descript** for editing and transcript generation. **Captivate** or **Transistor** for hosting and distribution.

Only activate this when the Substack cadence has held for six months. Podcast is a forcing function amplifier, not a starting project.

## Voice Transcription

**Otter.ai** OR **Whisper via MacWhisper app**.

Configuration: - Car voice memos transcribed automatically. - Sermon audio auto-transcribed for article repurposing. - Meetings (counseling excluded, always) transcribed for action items.

## Graphics and Branding

**Canva Pro** (already in use).

Configuration: - Brand kit loaded (orange \#E8600A, black \#1A1A1A, modern farmhouse aesthetic). - Templates pre-built for: Substack header graphics, Facebook quote cards, sermon series artwork, resource wall pieces, PCN materials. - Bulk social graphics batched on Thursday afternoons.

## Automation Glue

**Zapier** OR **Make (formerly Integromat)** for cross-app automations.

Configuration: see Part 9.

## Money

**Quicken** OR **YNAB** OR **Monarch Money** for household financial tracking. **Wave** or **QuickBooks Self-Employed** for LiveWell platform revenue and business expenses.

Monthly money meeting uses these as the data source.

## Church Management

**ChurchTrac** (already in use — no API).

Accept that ChurchTrac integration is manual. Do not try to force API integration that doesn’t exist. Instead, schedule 30 minutes Tuesday afternoons for ChurchTrac updates in batch.

## Cloud Storage

**iCloud Drive** OR **Dropbox** OR **Google Drive** — whichever one already has your files.

The Bell OS folder lives here. Synced across all devices. Backed up automatically.

## Password / Security

**1Password** (strongly recommended). Shared vault with Susanna for household and platform logins. Separate vault for ministry logins accessed by staff who need them.

## Physical Tools

- A pocket Moleskine or Field Notes for longhand capture when away from phone.
- Fountain pen or reliable everyday pen.
- Index cards for daily top-three and quarterly targets taped to the desk.
- A physical printed weekly schedule on the office wall.
- A physical whiteboard for sermon arc sketching — not for task management. The whiteboard is for thinking, not tracking.

# PART 9: THE AUTOMATION MAP

Automate the boring parts. Keep the soul parts manual.

## What Gets Automated

**1. Calendar invites for ceremonies.** Every ceremony is a recurring calendar event with a detailed description field that holds the relevant template or checklist.

**2. Template spawning.** Zapier/Make recipe: on Friday at 1:00 PM, create a new file in `/04-Reviews/weekly/` named `Week-of-[Monday date].md` with the weekly review template pre-loaded. Same for monthly, quarterly, annual templates.

**3. Idea capture → Idea File.** Drafts iOS app with an action that appends any new capture to `Idea-File.md` in Obsidian vault automatically, with timestamp.

**4. Sermon-to-article pipeline.** Recipe: when a new sermon audio file lands in a designated folder, auto-transcribe via Otter/Whisper, drop transcript into a draft folder, send notification to start the article repurposing step manually.

**5. Email triage.** Gmail filters for newsletters, promotions, receipts — auto-label and skip inbox. You see only what needs response.

**6. Substack scheduling.** Draft Wednesday, schedule Thursday 7 AM, publish automatically. You are not manually clicking publish.

**7. Social batching.** Buffer scheduled once a week on Thursday afternoons for the coming week. No daily social posting — it compounds distraction for no meaningful audience gain.

**8. Monthly ship-audit reminder.** Calendar event triggers 9:45 AM first Monday — notification fifteen minutes before the 10:00 AM ship audit, with the template file already open.

**9. Money meeting reminder.** Both you and Susanna get a calendar notification Wednesday 7:15 PM. Template file opens automatically on the laptop.

**10. Weekly review reminder + auto-template.** Friday 12:55 PM — phone notification, laptop opens the new weekly review file, desktop focus mode activates.

**11. Daily close-out.** End-of-day shutdown checklist: close email, close Slack, review tomorrow’s top three, log one sentence in the daily log. Automated reminder 4:45 PM.

**12. Book backups.** Scrivener auto-backups to Dropbox every 10 minutes during active writing. Secondary backup via iCloud. Third-level backup via weekly manual export of completed chapters to plain text in the Bell OS vault.

**13. Substack subscriber welcome sequence.** When a new paid subscriber joins, automatic email welcome with three curated starter essays sent over the first week. Configured in Substack native tools.

**14. PCN member check-in rotation.** Recipe: Sunday evening, a random PCN member’s name surfaces in your todo list as “reach out this week.” Simple randomizer script over the membership list ensures no one gets ignored for months.

**15. Annual deadline reminders.** Book ship dates, Thailand trip, summit, annual roadmap — all trigger a 60-day warning, 30-day warning, 14-day warning in your task manager.

## What Stays Manual

**1. Sermon writing.** Never automated. Sermon prep is prayer plus exegesis plus audience knowledge. No shortcut.

**2. Counseling and pastoral conversation.** Obviously manual. Never transcribed, never logged in systems that could be breached.

**3. The primary deep work.** The book gets written by you, one sentence at a time. AI can edit, research, suggest, format. The actual argument is yours.

**4. Decisions about killing projects.** Judgment call every quarter. Never automated.

**5. The inner life check.** A human moment in front of God. Not delegated to a template or a reminder (the reminder is automated, but the reflection is not).

**6. The marriage.** No automation. Phone away. Full presence. Date night, money meeting, weekly rhythm — they go on the calendar, but what happens during them is not automated.

**7. Birthday and anniversary planning for family.** Calendar reminders only. What you do with the time is not delegated to templates.

## What Gets AI-Assisted

**1. Research for sermons and articles.** Use Claude / ChatGPT to surface relevant church-history anecdotes, cross-references, cultural diagnostics — then verify, filter, and frame through your voice.

**2. First-draft outlines for articles and resource pieces.** Use the custom CLAUDE.md instructions you have built. Then rewrite in your voice.

**3. Editing passes on manuscripts.** Line-level suggestions, anti-AI-style enforcement, consistency checks.

**4. Repurposing sermons into social content.** Pipeline handled by the social-media-repurposing skill.

**5. Email drafting for routine communications.** Quick drafts for non-sensitive emails that you then edit lightly.

**6. Spreadsheet and data work.** Financial modeling, budget variance analysis, platform metrics.

**7. Content calendaring.** Mapping the five LiveWell content pillars across the year.

What AI does not replace: the voice, the prayer, the relationships, the judgment calls, the preaching, the counseling, the fathering, the marriage, the inner life.

# PART 10: THE ACCOUNTABILITY STRUCTURE

Systems alone do not hold. Humans hold.

## Susanna — Primary Accountability Partner

Tell her the active list. Tell her the quarterly targets. Tell her the annual roadmap in writing. Give her permission to ask, at any time, “how is \[project\] coming?” without it being a source of tension. Agree that when she says “you are overcommitting,” you will stop and actually listen.

Weekly touch: Sunday evening, 10 minutes, week-ahead review together. Monthly touch: the money meeting. Quarterly touch: you share the kill-and-promote results with her the same day.

## The PCN Peer Circle — Brotherhood Accountability

Three men from the PCN network who know your life and will check in honestly. Quarterly calls — one each week in the first month of the quarter, rotating. The question each of them asks: “What did you ship this quarter? What are you shipping next?”

No marketing. No networking. Brotherhood.

Suggested initial names (confirm with actual peers as relationships develop): - One nearby pastor (Michigan or near-midwest). - One longer-tenure PCN member who has shipped multiple projects. - One international partner (David Livingston in India, rotating).

## Editor / Publisher — Book Accountability

For each book under development, one person knows the ship date and will hold you to it. For *The Monster in the Mirror*, this person is your editor or publisher (confirm name). For *When God Bless America*, identify the right person now — either your editor, a trusted theological reader, or a publisher contact. Put the ship date in writing to them.

## Church Staff — Protected Time Accountability

Cody, Alyssa, Laurie, Bob, Levi all need to know: deep work time (6:30–8:30 AM) is protected. Staff meetings do not schedule during that window. Emergencies override — nothing else.

Staff meeting (weekly) includes a 5-minute “pastor’s creative status” item where you say what you are shipping this month. This normalizes completion as part of pastoral leadership.

## Board / Deacons — Organizational Accountability

Quarterly board update includes a one-paragraph “pastor’s writing and PCN progress” section. Not because the board governs your writing — they do not. But because visibility prevents drift. When the board knows a book is in progress and there is a ship date, a pastor who misses the date owes an accounting. The structure protects both you and the church.

## Substack Audience — Public Accountability

The audience is the largest and most diffuse accountability structure. When you announce a ship date publicly (“*The Monster in the Mirror* ships in July 2026”), the social cost of missing rises. Use this deliberately.

When a book does ship, the audience celebrates with you. That positive reinforcement builds the completion muscle.

## Sons — Modeling Accountability

Your five sons see what gets done. They see whether their father starts many things and finishes few, or starts carefully and finishes faithfully. This accountability is silent and long. It may be the most important of all.

# PART 11: THE ENVIRONMENT DESIGN

Your physical and digital environments shape your behavior more than willpower does.

## The Office (at the church or at home)

- Desk faces a wall, not a window. The wall has: the weekly schedule printed large, the quarterly index card, a photo of Susanna and the boys, and a printed copy of the daily non-negotiables checklist.
- Desk surface is clean. Laptop, notebook, pen, mug. Nothing else. All other materials go in drawers or shelves.
- A second monitor is useful if you are doing parallel-document writing. A third is overkill.
- Noise-canceling headphones for deep work blocks.
- A standing-desk option or at least a walking-meeting option to break up the sitting.

## The Home Writing Space

If deep work sometimes happens at home (early morning before the church office opens), have a dedicated corner with the same principles. One surface. Limited distractions. Bible and notebook within reach.

## The Phone

This is the single most important environmental lever.

- Grayscale mode enabled most of the day — reduces the dopamine pull of colored app icons.
- Home screen: only the five to seven apps you actually need daily (Phone, Messages, Calendar, Todoist, Drafts, Bible, Maps).
- Social media apps on the second screen or deleted from the phone entirely. Use desktop only.
- Email app — notifications off.
- Phone goes in a drawer during deep work blocks.
- Phone goes on a charger in the kitchen by 9:30 PM. Not in the bedroom.

## The Car

Audiobook or nothing. No news radio, no podcasts-as-noise, no constant input. The car is one of the last remaining thinking spaces in a modern life. Protect it.

Voice-to-text for idea capture while driving — Drafts app with “Hey Siri” trigger.

## The Wardrobe

Modern farmhouse aesthetic — you already know this. Simplify so dressing takes three minutes. Quality over quantity. Five shirts you love beat twenty you tolerate. Decision fatigue from wardrobe is decision fatigue you cannot spend on writing.

## The Kitchen

Sourdough continues. Cooking meals with Susanna and the boys continues. The kitchen is a ministry space as much as the pulpit is. Keep it central.

## The Digital Hygiene

- Zero social media on the phone (Facebook, Instagram, Twitter/X). Access only on desktop, batched, Thursday afternoons.
- YouTube: not on the phone. Desktop only. Use it for research, not for leisure scrolling.
- News: once a day, a single source, ten minutes, morning coffee. Not all day.
- Slack or equivalent: not on the phone. Desktop only, closed outside of check-in times.

# PART 13: WHAT HAPPENS WHEN THE SYSTEM BREAKS

Because it will. Not often. But sometimes.

## When You Miss a Weekly Review

Do not skip it — do it on Saturday morning instead. Late is better than never. The pattern of doing the review is what matters.

## When You Miss Deep Work for a Week

Get honest about why. Family emergency? Church crisis? Illness? Those are real. Novelty-chasing or distraction creep? That is different. For real emergencies — just resume next week. For creep — bring it to the inner life check and name it.

## When the Active List Won’t Move

If a project has had no movement for three weeks, it is either dead or stuck. Name which. If dead — kill it. If stuck — name the one specific blocker and solve it before the next deep work block.

## When a New Idea Is So Compelling You Want to Break the Finish-Before-Start Rule

Write it down. Sit with it for a week. If it is still compelling, bring it to Susanna and to one PCN peer. If both agree it is significant enough to replace a current active project, consider the swap. Almost never swap. Occasionally swap. The default answer is “no, this goes in the idea file.”

## When You Are Just Exhausted

Rest. The summer reset week exists for this. The sabbath exists for this. A tired pastor cannot write a prophetic book; a tired writer cannot preach a faithful sermon. The system is built to be sustainable. When it is not sustainable, that is signal — not to push through, but to rest.

## When the System Itself Becomes the Focus

This is the subtle failure mode. The system is a servant. It serves the work. If you find yourself spending more time managing the system than doing the work, prune. Fewer reviews. Simpler templates. Shorter checklists. The architecture should be almost invisible once it’s built. If it is still loud, it is not yet right.
